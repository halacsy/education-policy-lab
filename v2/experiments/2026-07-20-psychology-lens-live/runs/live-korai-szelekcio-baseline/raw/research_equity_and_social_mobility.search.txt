# Research Notes: Early Academic Selection via Hungary's 6- and 8-Year Gimnázium Tracks

## Scope and system description
Hungary's general secondary schools (gimnázium) offer parallel structures of differing length: 
in gimnáziums secondary education can last for 5, 6 or 8 years depending on how many years the student spent in primary school.
 This means selection into the academic track can happen at two points below the standard age-14 transition: 
tracking can partially start at Grade 5 (age 10-11) or at Grade 7 (age 12-13), when students can apply to an 8-year or 6-year general secondary school.
 The 8-year form is the historically dominant variant, while 
the other two options were introduced in the early 1990s.
 A structural side-effect noted by Hungary's own education research institute is that 
due to the development of the 6 and 8 years long general secondary schools or classes, the number of places at general secondary schools for the 14-year-old students has decreased,
 with vocational secondary schools absorbing displaced students: 
for the better students who fail to enter 'gimnázium', the vocational secondary school operates as a substitute.


## Finding 1 — Early tracking is independently linked to major inequality, not just a proxy for other factors
The most direct academic treatment of this exact question, Horn, Keller and Róbert's chapter "Early tracking and competition – A recipe for major inequalities in Hungary" (in *Models of Secondary Education and Social Inequality*, Edward Elgar, 2016), frames Hungary as a paradigm case of an early-tracking, high-competition system. 
Secondary school tracks are highly stratified by academic performance and family background, affecting later educational and career opportunities, with the grammar school track characterized by the most advantageous family background and best academic achievements relative to the mixed (vocational secondary) and vocational tracks.
 Related PISA-based work by Horn et al. (2006) is cited as showing that 
the huge differences in student performance among the three tracks in Hungary — academic, vocational secondary and vocational — are related to differences in the socio-economic status of students' families,
 and the authors 
claim that the huge differences in student performance in Hungary are due to the early tracking
 itself, not merely to prior ability differences. *Status: peer-reviewed causal/associational claim, widely cited in the Hungarian literature; the tracking effect is disentangled from SES in the authors' models but this remains one research lineage rather than an uncontested consensus.*

## Finding 2 — Selective ("tracking") schools raise scores of the selected but appear to depress outcomes for those left behind, and long-run labor-market gains are doubtful
A companion empirical strand (Horn 2013; Gurzó & Horn 2015) that evaluated the same Hungarian tracking policy found: 
Horn (2013) analyzed the same policy and evaluated its short-term impact on student achievement, finding that tracking schools have higher value-added than general schools, while they have a deteriorating effect on the test scores of students left in normal schools.
 Extending this to long-run outcomes, the follow-up thesis concludes that 
tracking is able to improve the short-term school achievement of the selected students on the expense of other children's achievement, but its impact disappears in the long run.
 *Status: this is a "sorting relocation" finding — it suggests gains for selected students are partly zero-sum (achieved at others' expense) and may not persist into adulthood/labor-market outcomes; based on a single empirical strand (CEU thesis + Horn 2013), not multiply replicated internationally.*

## Finding 3 — Hungary has one of the OECD's strongest socioeconomic performance gradients, and school-type (track) segregation is a major structural channel
Cross-nationally, Hungary is repeatedly flagged as an outlier on equity. In PISA's ESCS (economic, social and cultural status) analysis, 
in the 2022 PISA report, children's economic, social, and cultural status explained 12%−16% of the variance in reading, science, and mathematics on average across OECD countries, and in some countries — including Hungary — this value exceeded 20%.
 OECD's PISA 2018 volume further finds that 
in Argentina, Bulgaria, the Czech Republic, Hungary, Peru, the Slovak Republic and the United Arab Emirates, a typical disadvantaged student has less than a one-in-eight chance of attending the same school as high achievers.
 A World Bank analysis of Hungary's PISA data attributes much of this to the tracked structure: 
performance in PISA varies significantly by students' socioeconomic backgrounds — differing by more than three years of schooling between top and bottom quintile students, significantly more than elsewhere in the EU; performance also varies by type of school (general secondary, vocational secondary, and vocational); and 15-year-olds from disadvantaged backgrounds are disproportionately represented in vocational schools and vocational secondary schools, where quality is significantly below that of general secondary schools.
 The most recent OECD country profile confirms persistence of this pattern: 
in Hungary, the variation between schools in mathematics performance is among the largest compared to average total variation across OECD countries, and Hungary has one of the highest differences in mathematics performance between students in the top and bottom quarters.
 *Status: robust, multiply-replicated cross-national finding (OECD/World Bank), though PISA measures 15-year-olds already sorted into tracks, so it cannot by itself isolate the causal share attributable to the 6/8-year gimnázium mechanism specifically versus the general 3-way secondary tracking (gimnázium/szakgimnázium/szakképző).*

## Finding 4 — Selection operates on top of, and interacts with, ethnic/Roma school segregation, which is itself increasing
Separately from academic tracking, Hungary has documented rising between-school ethnic segregation that overlaps with SES sorting. Kertesi and Kézdi's long-run study finds 
ethnic segregation between Hungarian schools strengthened substantially between 1980 and 2011,
 with a pattern where 
segregation appears to have decreased between 2006 and 2008 and increased again afterwards, though noise in the data prevents firm conclusions.
 A companion town-level study finds that 
in Hungarian towns, free school choice diminishes the role of residential distribution because many students commute to schools of their choice, and towns where such commuting is more pronounced are characterized by stronger inter-school inequalities,
 and that 
local educational policies have, on average, somewhat segregationist tendencies, though there is substantial heterogeneity across towns.
 Recent quasi-experimental work (Hermann & Kisfalusi) on socioeconomically segregated ("high-poverty") primary schools finds that 
attending a high- or extreme-poverty school is associated with lower reading scores, a lower likelihood of completing secondary education, and a lower likelihood of obtaining the secondary school-leaving certificate.
 Because entry to 6/8-year gimnázium programs typically requires competitive entrance exams, application costs, and often urban location, this layer of selection is widely treated in the literature as compounding — not replacing — pre-existing residential/ethnic segregation, concentrating disadvantaged and Roma students in the primary schools and non-selective tracks left behind. *Status: the segregation literature (Kertesi/Kézdi, Hermann/Kisfalusi) is well-established and peer-reviewed; the specific causal link from 6/8-year gimnázium admission to Roma/ethnic segregation is inferential/compositional (i.e., an application of separately documented segregation dynamics to the tracking mechanism) rather than a finding from a single study measuring gimnázium admission's ethnic effects directly.*

## Finding 5 — Persistent early school-leaving and skills gaps concentrated among Roma and rural students, on the "losing" side of sorting
Current EU monitoring data underline who bears the downside of the sorted system. 
Among early school leavers, the most vulnerable groups are those living in rural areas, Roma and people with disabilities,
 and 
in 2023, 58.7% of Roma students (18–24-year-olds) were school dropouts, compared with 9.3% among non-Roma.
 The 2025 EU Education and Training Monitor notes that 
in the 2022 OECD PISA, the share of disadvantaged students who reach a good level in at least one of the basic skills (reading, mathematics and science) is 12.1%, considerably below the EU average (16.3%).
 *Status: factual/statistical (Eurydice/EU Monitor, Eurostat-based); these are system-wide outcome gaps, not proof that the 6/8-year gimnázium specifically (as opposed to the broader 3-track secondary structure) is the primary cause, since early leaving concentrates mainly in vocational tracks rather than gimnázium non-admission per se.*

## Finding 6 — Contested/mixed evidence on whether early starting age (a related but distinct selection mechanism) helps or harms low-SES children
A methodologically adjacent study (using the same research group's approach to age-based selection, distinct from track selection) found a counter-intuitive equity result: examining school starting age, the authors report that 
children generally gain from starting school one year later, and the effects are much stronger for students from low-educated families,
 a pattern the authors attribute to 
the better performance of kindergartens, as opposed to schools, in developing the skills of low-status children.
 *Status: this concerns delayed school entry rather than gimnázium tracking directly, but is relevant as evidence that not all "earlier is better/worse" assumptions about Hungarian selection mechanisms point in the same equity direction — selection timing effects are mechanism-specific, and researchers caution against assuming a single uniform effect of "early" interventions.*

## Finding 7 — No clearly identified current (2024–2026) government proposal to abolish or restructure the 6/8-year gimnázium specifically
Searches of recent Hungarian education-reform tracking (Eurydice national reform reports 2024–2026, Daily News Hungary coverage of pending 2026 parliamentary education reforms) surfaced substantial activity on teacher careers, salaries, assessment systems, AI policy, and vocational (technikum) pathways, but no discrete legislative or ministerial proposal specifically targeting the structural existence of the 6- or 8-year gimnázium track was found in these results — e.g., pending 2026 reforms discussed instead concern 
restoring powers previously removed from teaching staff, expanding the authority of school heads, strengthening the role of teachers' unions, and removing restrictions on teachers' right to strike.
 *Status: absence of evidence, not evidence of absence — this may reflect a gap in current public search results rather than confirmation that no debate exists; readers seeking the live political debate on abolishing 6/8-year gimnázium specifically should treat this as an open research gap requiring Hungarian-language primary-source searching.*

### Synthesis notes on the two framing questions (inference, not new empirical claims)

**Who gains/loses:** The cited literature converges on a distributional pattern: children who pass the age-10/12 entrance exams into 6/8-year gimnázium — disproportionately from higher-SES, higher-parental-education, urban families — appear to gain access to schools with stronger peer composition and (per Horn 2013) higher short-term value-added; children who remain in standard primary schools face a diminished-quality residual peer group and, per the segregation literature, a system where high-poverty/high-Roma-share schools are linked to worse downstream attainment. Long-run individual benefit to selected students is less clearly established (Finding 2) than the short-run relative advantage.

**Sorting reduced or relocated:** Based on the evidence above, the dominant inference in this research literature is relocation rather than reduction of sorting: early gimnázium selection adds an additional, earlier sorting layer on top of pre-existing residential/ethnic segregation and the later 3-way secondary track split (gimnázium/vocational secondary/vocational), rather than replacing or diminishing those other sorting mechanisms. This is an interpretive synthesis of Findings 1–4, not a direct empirical finding from any single cited source.
