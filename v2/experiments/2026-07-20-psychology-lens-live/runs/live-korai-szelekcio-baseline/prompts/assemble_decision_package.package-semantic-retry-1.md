PUBLIC QUESTION: Should Hungary keep its 6- and 8-year gimnázium tracks?

TRANSFORMATIONS:
TP-live-t1 — Status quo with monitoring (passive counterfactual)
Goal: Maintain current 6-/8-year gimnázium structure and admissions rules while tracking equity indicators, avoiding disruptive structural change absent political consensus.
Mechanisms: Existing centralized entrance-exam admissions continue unchanged, preserving current selection intensity; National ABC and PISA reporting infrastructure already requires schools to analyze outcomes by SES, providing a data feed without policy correction history
Evidence: strong
Risks: Persistent or worsening segregation given demographic contraction and geographic concentration of tracks in urban areas; Continued erosion of legitimacy as ministerial rhetoric acknowledging problems without action increases public distrust; PISA data has historically not translated into policy correction, suggesting monitoring alone is unlikely to alter trajectory

TP-live-t2 — Delay selection age to 14-16 (Polish/Finnish-style comprehensive extension)
Goal: Raise the age of first academic selection from 10 to at least 14-16, reducing the number of pre-adolescent sorting points and associated inequality of opportunity.
Mechanisms: Later selection reduces the number of school-type sorting points, and international comparative literature associates earlier selection age and more school-types with higher inequality of opportunity; Poland's 1999 reform delaying tracking to 16 produced improved PISA outcomes and labour-market gains (3pp employment, 4% earnings) via comprehensive middle-tier restructuring; Removing early selection reduces the 'brain drain' externality on ordinary schools' peer composition, though this specific effect remains unquantified
Evidence: moderate
Risks: Institutional durability risk: Poland's reform was substantially reversed after 2016 despite measured success, suggesting reform could be politically reversed; High symbolic salience already demonstrated in Hungary, where even non-abolition proposals were reframed as 'communist' unification, indicating strong opposition mobilization capacity; Teacher workforce (half aged 50+, only 7.2% under 30, low relative pay) may lack capacity for implementation fidelity

TP-live-t3 — Preserve tracks, reform admissions mechanism (Lannert-style procedural reform)
Goal: Keep the 6-/8-year track structure intact but replace performance/recommendation-based admissions with mechanisms less correlated with family background and private tutoring access.
Mechanisms: Redesigning entrance exams to reduce tutoring-sensitive content could weaken the SES-independent sorting advantage documented in Hungary versus other OECD countries; Administrative desegregation rules in the 2000s produced temporary reductions in urban segregation, indicating admissions-level intervention can shift outcomes even without structural abolition; Institution-by-institution accountability review based on competence-test results, as advocated by the National Pedagogue Chamber, could complement admissions redesign
Evidence: moderate
Risks: Church-maintained and private schools sit outside Klebelsberg Centre authority and may resist or opt out of admissions changes, limiting national coverage; Prior administrative desegregation measures 'stagnated mid-decade,' suggesting reform fatigue or reversal risk over time; Divided union positions (PSZ favoring phase-out vs PDSZ favoring broader segregation focus) may prevent unified implementation support

TP-live-t4 — Structural desegregation without touching track age (choice and zoning reform)
Goal: Address the structural drivers of segregation identified as independent of the tracking mechanism itself — school-choice commuting, maintainer exemptions, geographic concentration — while leaving the 6-/8-year track age and admissions criteria untouched.
Mechanisms: Geographic settlement patterns independently produce stratification (village pupils funneled to vocational schools regardless of academic tracking), suggesting zoning/network levers operate through a distinct causal channel from track admissions; Higher inter-school choice commuting correlates with stronger inter-school inequality, indicating that restricting or structuring choice/commuting could reduce segregation without touching track age; 2015 Curia exemption permitting segregated Roma-only schools under Church-maintainer status indicates a specific legal lever (review of anti-discrimination exemptions) distinct from admissions-exam reform
Evidence: moderate
Risks: Prior administrative desegregation rules from the 2000s only produced temporary reductions before stagnating, suggesting this lever alone may have limited durability; Legal challenge risk given the Curia's prior exemption ruling for Greek Catholic Church schools; Given demographic contraction and centralization under KLIK curtailing municipal planning autonomy, local adaptation capacity for zoning changes is constrained

TP-live-t5 — Targeted quota and outreach expansion for disadvantaged access to existing tracks
Goal: Preserve the 6-/8-year track structure and admissions exam but introduce mandated disadvantaged-student quotas, outreach, and exam-preparation support to change who is selected without changing the selection mechanism's timing or structural design.
Mechanisms: Since per-pupil financing is currently identical across elite and disadvantaged schools yet access depends on private tutoring, targeted supplementary funding for exam preparation for disadvantaged applicants could offset the tutoring-access gap without altering exam content; Quota mechanisms directly address the compositional imbalance (14% advantaged vs 1% deprived among top readers) by mandating minimum disadvantaged representation, independent of the admissions-design reform in T3; School starting age evidence shows kindergarten-stage interventions produce stronger equity gains for low-status children than later interventions, suggesting outreach should begin well before the age-10 selection point to be effective
Evidence: weak
Risks: Value-added gains attributed to selective tracks may be substantially explained by family background rather than institutional effect, meaning quota-admitted students might not experience the same benefit unless underlying school-quality issues are also addressed; Zero measured tertiary-education effect of track selection in at least one study raises the possibility that expanding access to a track with contested added value may not durably benefit admitted disadvantaged students; Quotas without broader distrust-of-local-schools resolution may not shift underlying parental sorting behavior driving demand for selective tracks

TP-live-t6 — Comprehensive lower-secondary pilot with opt-in enrichment (Finnish-inspired de-tracking pilot)
Goal: Test a voluntary regional pilot removing academic selection at grades 5/7 in favor of comprehensive lower-secondary schooling with within-school enrichment, evaluated against comparison regions before any national decision.
Mechanisms: Finland's comprehensive-schooling shift (1972-1977) was associated with rising performance and historically low between-school variance, though confounded by simultaneous teacher-training, curriculum, and welfare reforms — a pilot can help isolate Hungary-specific effects from these confounds; No significant measured efficiency losses from de-tracking are found in cross-national meta-analytic evidence, and countries increasing school heterogeneity showed higher student competency progress, supporting a testable hypothesis for the pilot; Finland's post-1990s experience shows school-choice deregulation can re-introduce segregation even without formal tracking, so the pilot must pair de-tracking with choice/zoning controls (linking to T4) to be a valid test
Evidence: moderate
Risks: Finnish experience shows de-tracking alone is insufficient against choice-driven segregation absent institutional guardrails, so a pilot without accompanying zoning controls may understate or overstate feasible national effects; Low institutional durability precedent (Poland's reversal after measured success) suggests even a successful pilot may not translate into lasting national policy; Pilot regions volunteering may not be representative, limiting external validity of results
