PACKAGE SUMMARY:
Hungary's 6- and 8-year gimnázium tracks concentrate a three-stage selection funnel (ages 10, 12, 14) that international comparison links to unusually high between-school variance and one of the OECD's largest socioeconomic performance gaps. Six directions occupy the decision space, differing in what mechanism they alter and what constraint they accept.

TP-t1 (status quo with monitoring) changes nothing structurally, adding measurement of segregation without action. Its leading mechanism is inertia: veto power held by church-maintained schools and middle-class families has historically blocked reform, and no discrete reform currently exists in the pipeline. Evidence for feasibility is strong, but the empirical record shows Hungary's segregation indicators (e.g., a 170-point reading gap between advantaged/disadvantaged deciles) are worsening, and existing assessment infrastructure (PISA, ABC testing) has a documented history of not translating into structural change—raising a value question about whether monitoring constitutes accountability or avoidance.

TP-t2 (delay selection to age 14, Polish-style) removes the two earliest entry points, closing the funnel to one transition, mirroring Poland's 1999 reform, which is empirically associated with reduced inequality and gains concentrated among previously lower-tracked students. This is the most structurally direct option and carries the strongest cross-national support, but also the highest legal burden (requiring statutory amendment and negotiation with church/private maintainers outside central authority) and the highest political risk—even narrower admissions reviews have been reframed as communist-style imposition. Constraints include teacher shortages, an aging workforce, and Poland's own reversal, which cost thousands of teacher placements and school closures. Whether this is judged worth the risk is a value choice about how much stability to sacrifice for structural change; the empirical uncertainty is whether displaced demand would relocate to church/private schools rather than disappear.

TP-t3 (reform admissions processes, preserve tracks) targets SES-linked bias in exams and private preparation access without abolishing tracks, aligning with the Minister's stated preference. It is procedurally lower-cost and more legally contained, but evidence suggests self-exclusion by disadvantaged families is driven by strategic parental decisions rather than exam design alone, weakening confidence that this lever reaches the actual behavioral driver. It also cannot bind church/private maintainers, and even this narrow framing has already triggered disproportionate political conflict—an empirical finding about political reception, not a value judgment.

TP-t4 (preserve tracks, invest in comprehensive-school quality) treats distrust in local schools as the root demand driver, sequencing quality investment before any structural change. This is the least politically contentious option and directly addresses documented teacher-shortage concentration in disadvantaged schools. However, it rests on an unproven causal claim: that improved quality reduces demand for selective tracks, when demand may also reflect status-signaling independent of quality. Demographic contraction (270 schools at risk of closure) may also overwhelm investment gains—an empirical uncertainty about whether resources can outpace population decline.

TP-t5 (cap only the age-10 entry point) is an incremental compromise removing the youngest, most controversial selection stage while retaining ages 12 and 14. It reduces political conflict scope and legal burden but has weak evidence overall; its central risk is mechanism displacement—demand may simply concentrate more intensely at age 12, leaving segregation levels largely unchanged. No comparable partial-detracking case exists internationally to validate this approach.

TP-t6 (fiscal conditionality) avoids direct admissions or structural mandates by tying funding premiums to equity outcomes, sidestepping legal conflict with maintainer autonomy. It has a plausible measurement backbone (existing ABC testing) but no evidence of causal effectiveness anywhere in the record, and risks becoming symbolic, or prompting maintainers to game non-SES criteria.

Across all options, a shared empirical gap is whether the track structure itself—versus residential segregation, free school choice, or church/private exemptions—causally drives Hungary's exceptional stratification; this determines whether structural, admissions, fiscal, or investment-based levers can work at all. Decision-makers must weigh: how much institutional stability and legal risk to accept for structural gains; whether to target the selection mechanism or the demand behind it; whether partial reform is a viable stepping-stone or merely displaces the problem; and whether monitoring without action is honest sequencing or credibility erosion given the Minister's own acknowledgment of harm. The research most likely to change this calculus concerns displaced-demand behavior under partial or full reform, realistic transition costs, and the actual causal share of track structure versus other segregation channels.
