# Research Notes: Early Academic Selection via 6- and 8-Year Gimnázium Tracks in Hungary

## Scope note
These notes address structural early tracking (age 10-12) via extended gimnázium programs, drawing on OECD, EU, Eurydice, and academic sources. No policy recommendation is offered; contested claims are flagged.

---

### 1. Structural facts: how the tracks work
Hungary's basic education is normally an 8-year single-structure school (ages 6–14), but 
tracking can partially start at Grade 5 (age 10-11) or at Grade 7 (age 12-13), when students can apply to an 8-year or 6-year general secondary school
. These parallel tracks were introduced relatively recently in the current system: 
since 1991 there have also been six- and eight-year secondary schools, and pupils can enrol in these programmes earlier, attending eight-year secondary schools from the fifth grade and six-year secondary schools from the seventh grade
. This creates an "early/late adopter" institutional patchwork rather than a uniform national system — 
8-year education is the most widespread of the extended options; the other two [6-year and 5-year] were introduced in the early 1990s
. As of 2024/25 data, 
secondary education is provided at 880 schools across Hungary, a dense network intended to ensure every student can find a secondary general school close to home, accessible by public transport in under an hour
, meaning the extended-track gimnáziums sit alongside, not instead of, a broad standard network.

### 2. Comparative scale of selection (OECD benchmark)
OECD's Hungary profile flags this as an outlier practice: 
around 85% of Hungarian students are in schools which can select students according to academic performance or recommendations from feeder schools, based mainly on their academic records, compared to the OECD average of 43% of students in such schools
. The same source frames the general mechanism, not just the 6/8-year gimnázium specifically, as a systemic equity risk: 
system-level policies such as early tracking, school choice, academic selection and grade repetition can hinder equity if not managed appropriately
, and notes 
compared to the OECD average (age 15), tracking starts early in Hungary: partially at Grade 5 (age 10-11) or later at Grade 7 (age 12-13)
. **Inference**: the 85% figure captures selective admission broadly (including standard 4-year gimnáziums and vocational tracks with entrance tests), not solely the 6/8-year cohort — the extended tracks are one visible instance of a wider selective-admission norm.

### 3. Equity/segregation evidence (contested magnitude, not contested direction)
Multiple EU/OECD sources converge on Hungary having comparatively severe socioeconomic school segregation: 
Hungary has the highest level of segregation of students from less and more advantaged socio-economic groups into different schools (Gutiérrez et al., 2020)
, and 
Hungary's segregation of the most and least disadvantaged students was the highest in the EU in the 2022 PISA study
. Roma-specific segregation is also documented as severe: 
the segregation of Roma pupils is the second highest in the region, with 62% of Roma children aged 6-15 attending schools where all or most pupils are Roma
. Academic literature on the tracking reform's causal effect is more nuanced/contested: a CEU thesis reviewing Horn's (2013) analysis found 
tracking schools have higher value-added than general schools, while they have deteriorating effect on the test scores of students left in normal schools
, and that 
tracking is able to improve the short-term school achievement of the selected students on the expense of other children's achievement, but its impact disappears in the long run
. **This is a specific causal finding, not universally replicated** — treat as one study's result, not consensus.

### 4. Governance mechanism that remains unchanged regardless of gimnázium-track reform: centralized admission/exam infrastructure
Regardless of whether 6/8-year tracks exist, entrance to upper-secondary schools generally runs through a nationally standardized process: 
the entrance examinations to upper-secondary schools are centrally organised
, and 
admission occurs via a centralized application process managed by the Ministry of Culture and Innovation, prioritizing primary school grades and entrance exams where applicable
. School maintenance itself was centralized in 2013: 
in 2011, the Hungarian parliament passed Act CXC on National Public Education, mandating state oversight of public school operations previously managed by local municipalities, and effective from January 1, 2013, this reform transferred governance, funding, and maintenance responsibilities to a centralized agency, initially the Klebelsberg Institution Maintenance Centre (KLIK)
. This means any reform of the 6/8-year track question would operate within — and not require altering — the existing centralized KLIK/Tankerület governance and centralized-exam architecture.

### 5. A distinct, largely unaffected selection channel: church-run and private school exemptions
Separately from state-track selection, church-maintained schools operate under different rules and have expanded, which is a governance feature that would persist independent of any 6/8-year gimnázium decision: 
educational institutions founded by national minority self-governments or churches, as well as private schools and kindergartens maintained by cities, may continue to operate, and in their case, the maintainer is to determine their own rules for operation
. Research has documented this channel as consequential for segregation specifically: 
this selective privatisation process in favour of Christian organisations has prompted new school choice patterns that have further enhanced social polarisation and Roma segregation in Hungarian education
. A widely cited legal episode illustrates the mechanism's durability: 
in April 2015, in the Nyíregyháza segregation case, the Curia overturned an earlier judgment and exempted the Greek Catholic Church from anti-discrimination provisions in law, allowing the Church to re-open and run a segregated Roma-only school
. **Contested framing**: NGO sources (ERRC) characterize this as government-enabled ("state-motivated church-assisted segregation," per 
Ercse (2018) called this phenomenon state-motivated church-assisted segregation
); this is an advocacy/academic interpretation, not a neutral institutional fact.

### 6. Locality-based segregation as a background condition independent of tracking policy
Even absent any academic tracking, geographic/residential sorting produces large performance gaps by settlement type, an older but illustrative PISA-based data point: 
pupils coming from small schools of small localities go in much higher ratio to vocational schools that do not provide school-leaving exams
, while 
since small villages operate one single primary school usually with one single class/grade, there is no possibility for segregation within school or between schools in these localities
. **Inference**: this suggests part of Hungary's segregation problem is driven by settlement geography and housing patterns rather than the 6/8-year gimnázium mechanism alone, meaning abolishing or modifying the tracks would not by itself resolve locality-driven stratification.

### 7. Outcome/attainment context (mixed signal, not directly attributable to tracking)
Hungary's overall PISA performance sits close to OECD averages — 
in mathematics 15-year-olds score 473 points compared to an OECD average of 472; in reading 473 versus 476; in science 486 versus 485
 — while disadvantaged-student outcomes lag sharply: 
in the 2022 OECD PISA, the share of disadvantaged students who reach a good level in at least one of the basic skills is 12.1%, considerably below the EU average of 16.3%
. Longitudinal cross-country equity research finds Hungary among lower-equity systems but with some improvement over time: 
countries with low equity levels, such as Hungary, Peru, Germany, Chile, and Bulgaria, managed to improve
 between 2000 and 2018 PISA rounds — this is a trend claim, not evidence the 6/8-year track mechanism specifically drove the change.

## Summary of what stays fixed under any gimnázium-track reform
- **Centralized governance** (Klebelsberg Centre/Tankerület system, ministry-run exam authority) is structurally separate from the tracking question and would persist under either scenario.
- **Church/private-school autonomy over admissions and internal rules** is a parallel selection channel outside the 6/8-year gimnázium debate.
- **Settlement/geographic segregation** operates independently of school-track policy.
- **The centrally organized entrance-exam infrastructure** (Oktatási Hivatal-administered) is the same mechanism used whether or not extended tracks exist — reform of tracks would not by itself require dismantling this apparatus.
