DILEMMAS:
- Structural abolition versus incremental compromise in delaying selection: Full elimination of early tracking (age 10 and 12 entry points) offers the strongest theoretical alignment with cross-national inequality-reduction evidence, but carries the highest political, legal, and implementation risk; a partial reform targeting only the age-10 entry point reduces conflict scope but may simply displace rather than resolve segregation pressure.
- Procedural fairness versus structural change as the locus of reform: Reforming admissions procedures preserves the existing track structure and is politically framed as the primary lever under consideration, but evidence suggests self-exclusion and segregation are driven by family strategic behavior and structural funnel effects that procedural reform alone may not reach, raising the question of whether targeting process over structure is a genuine solution or a symbolic substitute.
- Compensatory investment versus structural detracking as the primary equity lever: Investing in comprehensive-school quality is the least politically contentious and legally simplest option, and addresses documented resource disparities, but it does not touch the selection mechanism itself, and demand for selective tracks may be driven by status-signaling rather than quality perception, raising doubt about whether it can substitute for structural reform rather than merely complement it.
- Monitoring and accountability versus action under acknowledged harm: Continuing the status quo with monitoring requires no new legal authority and avoids provoking veto constituencies, but the Minister's own acknowledgment of significant social selection and family burden creates a legitimacy problem: choosing inaction after diagnosing harm is itself a value-laden choice about tolerable inequality, not a neutral default.
- Fiscal conditionality as an untested indirect lever versus direct structural or admissions reform: Tying funding premiums to equity outcomes avoids the centralized-admissions-versus-free-choice legal tension and could operate without direct mandates on maintainers, but its causal effectiveness on segregation is entirely unproven, and maintainers could game non-SES criteria in ways that harm other groups, raising the question of whether an indirect, lower-conflict lever is worth pursuing absent evidence of impact.
- Reference-group and self-concept effects: delaying selection versus preserving current stratification: Delaying selection to age 14 reduces the number of high-stakes sorting junctures implicated in big-fish-little-pond and goal-orientation dynamics, potentially benefiting self-concept and motivation broadly, but the transition process itself may introduce short-term cognitive load and uncertainty, and partial reforms that retain some early sorting points may concentrate rather than dissipate psychological pressure at the remaining transition.
