PROBLEM: Observers report that a growing share of children in Hungary are being formally identified as having special educational needs (SEN), alongside related categories such as BTMN (learning and behavioural integration difficulties), autism, and behavioural or safety concerns. Whether such a rise has actually occurred, how large and continuous it is, and what it means are all open questions that research must verify rather than assume. A measured change in recorded identification could reflect several distinct and possibly overlapping drivers: real changes in prevalence (biological or environmental), changes in diagnostic practice or category definitions, changes in access to assessment, or incentives embedded in finance, placement, and accountability rules. This brief bounds the problem so later research can test these premises and map — without prejudging — where policy attention might be warranted. It does not yet answer the question or endorse any response.
