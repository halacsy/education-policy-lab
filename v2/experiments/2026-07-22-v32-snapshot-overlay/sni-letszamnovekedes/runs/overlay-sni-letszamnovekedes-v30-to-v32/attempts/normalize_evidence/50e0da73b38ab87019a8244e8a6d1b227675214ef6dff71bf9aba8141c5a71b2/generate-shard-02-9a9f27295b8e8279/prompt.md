PUBLIC QUESTION: More children are being identified as having special educational needs — what should Hungary do?
PROBLEM: Observers report that a growing share of children in Hungary are being formally identified as having special educational needs (SEN), alongside related categories such as BTMN (learning and behavioural integration difficulties), autism, and behavioural or safety concerns. Whether such a rise has actually occurred, how large and continuous it is, and what it means are all open questions that research must verify rather than assume. A measured change in recorded identification could reflect several distinct and possibly overlapping drivers: real changes in prevalence (biological or environmental), changes in diagnostic practice or category definitions, changes in access to assessment, or incentives embedded in finance, placement, and accountability rules. The submitter's underlying concern connects this identification trend to whether the specialist workforce and support methods can meet demand, how mainstream classrooms and teachers experience integration, whether parents move children between schools or classes in response, and whether such mobility reshapes school composition in ways that deepen educational inequality. This brief bounds the problem so later research can test these premises and map — without prejudging — where policy attention might be warranted. It does not yet answer the question or endorse any response.
