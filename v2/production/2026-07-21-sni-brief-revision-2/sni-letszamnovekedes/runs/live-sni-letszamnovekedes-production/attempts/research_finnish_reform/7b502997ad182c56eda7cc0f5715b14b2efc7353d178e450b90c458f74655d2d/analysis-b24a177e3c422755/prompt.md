## A. What the Finnish comprehensive-school experience implies

**1. Finland's own reform was a direct policy response to a prior rise in identification — this is documented, not assumed.**
FACT: 
A decade ago, the Finnish education system underwent a major reform concerning the provision of special education, and the reasons for the reform were the continuing trend in the increase of students receiving special education and the need to provide more systematic support for all students.
 This shows Finland faced essentially the same "rising SEN numbers" pattern Hungary now reports, and treated it as a system-design problem rather than only a clinical one.

**2. The 2010/2011 reform replaced a two-tier with a three-tier, non-categorical support model.**
FACT: 
The Finnish education system changed in 2010, when a three-tiered support system replaced the two-tiered system by adding the concept of intensified support between general and special support.
 
The Finnish approach emphasizes inclusion, early intervention, and individualized support based on student needs rather than diagnoses.
 Tiers are General (universal, Tier 1), Intensified (Tier 2, pedagogical assessment + learning plan), and Special (Tier 3, most intensive) 
Intensified support (Tier 2) activates when general support proves insufficient. This level involves a pedagogical assessment by teachers, resulting in a learning plan with more targeted interventions. Students receive more individualized attention, small-group instruction, and part-time special education while remaining in their regular classroom.


**3. Post-reform data show growth concentrated in the new "middle" category, while the top-tier "special support" share stayed comparatively flat — an important measurement point.**
FACT: 
The intensified support was a new type of support implemented in connection to the reform in 2010–2011 and the pupils receiving intensified support largely increased from 2.8% in 2011 to 11.6% in 2019. The special support has been quite constant since the reform and in 2019 approximately 8.5% of the pupils received this tier of support.
 By 2020, 
12.2% of the students in Finnish comprehensive school received intensified support and 9.0% received special support
. INFERENCE: this pattern is consistent with reclassification/redistribution across a newly created category more than with a continuously accelerating "special support" population — a distinction Hungarian data (which currently lacks an equivalent tiered breakdown) cannot yet test.

**4. There is direct, quasi-causal evidence that funding design shapes identification rates, independent of underlying need.**
FACT (specific empirical study, Pulkkinen et al. 2020): 
The special education funding reform introduced in Finland in 2010 changed the funding system from non-census-based to mainly census-based… this reform influenced special education by incentivising municipalities to reduce the share of students in full-time special education, where extra funding was discontinued after the reform.
 More generally, 
non-census funding systems are related to increases in the provision of special education, whereas census-based systems do not offer a fiscal incentive to identify students as having SEN.
 This is a load-bearing finding for the Hungarian question: it demonstrates that *identification counts can rise or fall with financing rules, holding presumed underlying prevalence constant* — directly relevant to whether Hungary's rise reflects incentive structures embedded in its own finance/placement rules, as the brief hypothesizes.

**5. Even after reform, sizable and persistent regional/municipal variation in identification and provision remained — a possible equity marker in itself.**
FACT: 
Special needs education has not been available equally in different parts of the country and for all age groups. The support provided for a pupil affected by similar learning difficulties varied from one municipality to another. In some cases, these differences were relatively major.
 Statistically, 
the share of pupils having received special support in all comprehensive school pupils varied between 4% and 13% by region.
 INFERENCE: such variation across otherwise similar administrative units suggests that a meaningful share of any national "increase" figure may be locally driven by practice/administrative differences rather than uniform underlying change — a caution for interpreting any single national Hungarian SNI percentage.

**6. Finland's "inclusion" label conceals a smaller degree of actual full mainstream inclusion than headline tier statistics suggest — a documented gap between rhetoric and practice.**
FACT/CONTESTED (critical literature): 
Most of the Tier 3 level students placed in general education schools are studying in general education classroom only part-time: at the national level, only 23% of Tier 3 level students (1.9% of all comprehensive students) are fully included in general education classes.
 Also, 
a majority of the target pupil group in Finland receive their SNE, whether full or part time, in a special class,
 which is a Finland-vs-Norway comparative finding, marked by the authors as a divergence between the two Nordic systems worth further scrutiny. INFERENCE: rising identification numbers do not by themselves indicate rising *mainstream* integration; the composition of placement (part-time pull-out, special class, special school) matters independently of the headline percentage.

**7. Growing workforce strain and role ambiguity accompanied the tiered system, even where teachers broadly endorsed it — a workforce-capacity signal relevant to the submitter's concern.**
FACT: Teachers in the tiered system 
generally... had a positive assessment of the system, which they saw as a natural part of their regular job. Nevertheless, they expressed facing challenges, such as the extended documentation and too little time for supporting all pupils in the class.
 Separately, an OAJ (teachers' union) concern was that 
at that time, a 'three-tiered support' system was introduced… with the aim of ensuring better access for pupils to the learning support they needed… but placing pupils with special needs in a general education group without support is the worst possible situation and yet unfortunately common.
 CONTESTED: the same union release also alleged 
the practice had, in some municipalities, been driven by cost-saving
-motivated implementation rather than pedagogical rationale — an interpretation not independently verified in the cited material.

## B. Later erosion / school-choice effects that matter

**8. Finland's celebrated equity was itself historically produced by de-tracking, and that equity gain has partially reversed alongside rising school choice — a documented trend, though causal attribution is contested.**
FACT: 
Since the release of the results from PISA 2000, Finland has been lauded as a high-performing, high-equity country. This success has been attributed in part to an egalitarian 9-year comprehensive school created by dramatic de-tracking reforms in the late 1960s and early 1970s.
 However, 
SES achievement gaps declined after de-tracking reforms but have increased more recently.
 Separately, 
not only has Finland's average performance fallen in recent cycles of PISA, but inequality in achievement appears to be increasing.
 INFERENCE/CONTESTED: authors link the reversal to rising parental choice and selective classes, but this is a correlational association across a period with multiple co-occurring changes (immigration, urbanization, curriculum changes), not an isolated causal test.

**9. A specific, documented mechanism: parental/school choice enables selective sorting by prior performance, concentrating advantage and disadvantage across schools.**
FACT: 
The rise of selective school classes and parental choice are leading to a system in which 'capable' parents make school choices for selective school classes for their children – who often are the ones with better school performance.
 This is described as producing fragmentation: 
the comprehensive education system, once considered equal and egalitarian, is fragmenting in an urban European setting.
 Helsinki-specific longitudinal analysis similarly finds 
segregation has begun to increase and appears to operate through the trends of middle-class avoidance and the decline of the underprivileged groups in urban schools and neighbourhoods.


**10. There is direct research explicitly connecting support-tier assignment with school-choice/enrolment mechanisms — the closest Finnish analogue to the submitter's concern about SEN status and family mobility.**
FACT (title/topic of a specific Finnish study, Turku): a 2016 paper is titled (translated) "The dilemma of inclusion and school choice — pupils' support-tier level and lower-secondary schools' intake in Turku" 
Lempinen, Berisha, & Seppänen (2016). Inkluusion ja kouluvalinnan dilemma – Oppilaan tuen taso ja yläkoulujen oppilaaksiotto Turussa.
 This indicates Finnish researchers have already operationalized the exact linkage the Hungarian brief flags — whether a pupil's support/SEN tier interacts with which school they can enter under choice-based enrolment. (Only the citation/title was retrieved in these notes; the paper's findings were not directly quoted and would need separate full-text review.)

**11. Broader Nordic/OECD framing: rising socioeconomic/ethnic residential segregation is transmitted into schools partly through school-market dynamics that municipalities must manage, not simply choose to enable or prevent.**
FACT: 
Strongly increasing socio-economic and ethnic residential segregation in many Finnish cities has had a marked effect on schools since residential patterns are a central factor in school segregation and over one third of Finnish school children live in larger cities.
 Local authorities are described as 
operat[ing] in the intersection between the imperative of school markets and the aversion to inequality,
 implying that even nominally neutral choice/enrolment rules can have distributive consequences that require active municipal management.

**12. A 2023 synthesis by Finnish researchers explicitly ties declining national learning outcomes to rising segregation, though this is an interpretive/advocacy synthesis rather than a single controlled causal study.**
CONTESTED (large expert consensus statement, not a single RCT/quasi-experiment): 
As previously celebrated for its success in PISA rankings, Finland is now seeing a decline in learning outcomes, with researchers attributing this to the growing trend of segregation among schools and students.
 This reflects a broad expert view (over 50 named authors) but should be read as an interpretive synthesis of multiple studies rather than a single definitive causal estimate.

## C. Grounding note: Hungary's own recorded SNI trend (for comparison, not analysis of cause)

FACT: Hungarian Central Statistical Office (KSH) figures cited in press show continuous multi-year increases, though reported totals differ by source/scope and should be treated as needing reconciliation:
- 
In the 2019/2020 school year the number of SNI pupils reached nearly 57,000, or 7.8% of primary pupils, most of them (72%) in integrated education.

- 
In 2023/2024 the number rose further to about 63,000, or 8.8% of primary pupils.

- 
According to KSH, 9.7% of primary pupils are now SNI, and this share rises continuously by grade, from 8.7% in first grade to over 11% by eighth grade.

- Regional variation is substantial: 
the most SNI pupils are in Békés county, where 14% of primary pupils are SNI, and the fewest in Borsod-Abaúj-Zemplén (5.4%) and Hajdú-Bihar (5.7%).

- Gender gap: 
girls are only 6.1% SNI while boys exceed 11%, meaning on average one in ten boys is SNI.


CONTESTED explanations offered in Hungarian press (expert quotes, not consensus): one KSH-sourced account attributed a sharp one-year jump partly to 
modernization of diagnostic assessment tools
, while a clinical psychologist countered that 
this cannot explain an increase of this size, and a much larger role may be played by compulsory school enrollment from age six and stricter school-readiness screening, which may have pushed immature children into school without developmental support.
 This is a direct, unresolved dispute in the Hungarian source material itself between "measurement/diagnostic-practice" and "systemic/policy-timing" explanations — precisely the ambiguity the research brief says must be tested rather than assumed.
