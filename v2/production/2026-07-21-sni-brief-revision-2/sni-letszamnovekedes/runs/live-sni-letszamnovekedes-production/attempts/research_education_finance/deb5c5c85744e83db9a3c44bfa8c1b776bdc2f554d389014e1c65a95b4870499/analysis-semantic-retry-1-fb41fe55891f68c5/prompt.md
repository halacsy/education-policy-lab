# Research Notes: SEN/BTMN Identification Trends in Hungary — Public Finance & Education Economics Perspective

## 1. Magnitude and continuity of the SNI rise (fact, quantified)

The KSH data show a 40 percent increase in the number of SNI students in Hungary over the past 20 years, and in the 2024/2025 school year there were already 112,000 SNI children in some form of public education institution
. 
In the 2023–2024 school year 104,865, and in 2024/2025 already 112,007 SNI children were registered in kindergarten, primary and secondary school; currently nearly 10% of primary school students qualify as SNI, rising to over 11% by 8th grade
. This confirms a real, multi-year upward trend in *recorded* identification, though the source (Raabe.hu, an education-publishing outlet) is a secondary aggregator of KSH data rather than the primary statistical release itself.

## 2. Composition of the SNI caseload

Among SNI children, the most common diagnosis is severe learning, attention, or behavioral-regulation disorder (about 66% of cases), followed by autism spectrum disorder
. This indicates the SNI increase is not driven by a single category but concentrated in psychological/behavioral-regulation and neurodevelopmental categories rather than sensory/physical disabilities.

## 3. Autism-specific trend (large, but contested in exact magnitude and interpretation)
Multiple outlets citing KSH report a roughly threefold increase in registered autism-spectrum cases in a decade: 
data recorded since 2001 show that in 2013, 3,319 children lived with autism spectrum disorder, a figure that rose to 9,737 by 2023
. A different source using overlapping KSH data reports a near-fourfold increase with different base figures: 
Hungarian KSH data show the number of autistic children nearly quadrupled over the past ten years (3,061 in 2013/14; 11,685 in 2023/24)
. **These two KSH-sourced figures disagree on both base and endpoint values** (3,319→9,737 vs. 3,061→11,685), indicating either different reference populations (calendar year vs. school year) or data-vintage inconsistency — a methodological caveat for any quantitative claim about autism prevalence trends.

Expert interpretation in these same sources leans toward **diagnostic-practice change rather than true prevalence change**: 
the increase is likely linked to the spread of diagnostic methods, greater awareness, and greater acceptance
, and one expert is quoted stating 
"there is no scientific evidence that the number of people with autism is increasing significantly"
, attributing the "increase" to 
more effective diagnostics and growing "autism knowledge," meaning parents and professionals (e.g., kindergarten teachers) detect symptoms earlier and refer children for proper assessment
. This is an **inference/expert claim, not a settled empirical finding** — no epidemiological (population-representative screening) study is cited to adjudicate between true-prevalence and ascertainment explanations.

## 4. BTMN is a distinct, softer, more Hungary-specific category with different age-profile than SNI

BTMN is internationally difficult to interpret because it is a Hungarian category, essentially milder than SNI: symptoms are similar but less severe, so they cannot be classified as disabilities
. 
Learning disorders under the SNI category involve some neurological/nervous-system deviation, whereas BTMN involves no such deviation, but rather pedagogical/psychological factors
. Age-profile data (last publicly available BTMN breakdown) show a striking jump from preschool to school age: 
in the 2018/2019 school year, 6,779 of 326,588 preschool children (2.1%) were classified as BTMN, versus 69,477 of 728,706 school-age children (9.5%) — a nearly fivefold higher prevalence among school-age children than preschoolers
. This gap is consistent with (but does not prove) the hypothesis that school-based academic/behavioral demands, rather than underlying developmental differences, drive a substantial share of identification.

## 5. Data-transparency gap: most granular BTMN statistics are several years stale

The last publicly available national education statistics, from 2018/2019, were used for the BTMN prevalence figures above
 — meaning that as of the most recent sources found (2025), researchers were still relying on data roughly six years old for detailed BTMN breakdowns, even though aggregate SNI totals are reported more currently (2024/2025). This is a factual observation about data availability, relevant to policy evaluation capacity.

## 6. Specialist workforce capacity is documented as strained, with assessment activity crowding out treatment/therapy time
A 2022 investigative account of pedagógiai szakszolgálat (educational counseling service) staff describes a shift of scarce clinical time toward paperwork: 
professionals' capacities are increasingly tied up by expert-committee assessments — the examination of integration, learning, and behavioral difficulties (BTMN)
, at the expense of individualized therapy. A related account from service-staff themselves states: 
they try to direct children diagnosed with ADHD, autism, developmental disorders, depression, anxiety, and compulsions toward the educational services for therapeutic care, including self-harming and suicide-risk children, but conditions for support are often not in place
, and 
overall, fewer and fewer specialists are available per growing numbers of children, so they do not receive adequate care
. This is a **resource/opportunity-cost finding**: rising identification volume appears to consume assessment capacity, with a possible trade-off against direct therapeutic service delivery — though these are journalistic/advocacy sources (petition-based reporting), not administrative capacity statistics.

## 7. Special-education teacher training pipeline shows a supply-demand mismatch, independent of headline application numbers

In the 2024 higher-education admissions, 2,944 applicants listed special-education teacher training (gyógypedagógus) as their first choice, making it the eighth most popular program that year
. However, a teachers'-union representative cautions that 
the relevant question is not how many apply but how many actually complete the program and enter the profession, since current statistics show only a fraction of applicants reach a diploma, and only about one-fifth go on to work in the field
. Applying that ratio, one source estimates that 
even if a fifth of the 2,944 admitted students (about 588 people) enter the workforce after the 8-semester program, still fewer will specialize specifically in autism spectrum disorder and work in state institutions rather than only private practice
, concluding 
there are not enough available specialists, so a special-education teacher typically has many more already-diagnosed children than would be ideal, and despite the visible increase, many affected individuals still do not receive a diagnosis
. This suggests apparent "excess demand" for specialist training is not translating into proportional workforce growth — a distinct finding from the raw identification-rate trend.

## 8. Historical account of a funding-linked incentive structure tying enrollment protection to SNI classification (older/pedagogical-training source, needs independent verification)
An education-training document (studocu lecture notes, not a peer-reviewed or official source) describes a mechanism whereby declining overall school enrollment interacts with SNI-linked normative (per-capita) funding: 
enrollment changes affect whether integration or segregation occurs — if a mainstream school needs more children it integrates SNI students, and if there are too many children institutions offload the "surplus"; as regular primary-school-age population declines, schools take in SNI students to avoid closure, drawing on normative financing multipliers (2x, 3x) that generate extra revenue usable for other purposes, avoiding the need to dismiss teachers or close the school
. **This is a lower-quality/unverified source (course material, undated institutional attribution, no citation to statute or budget data)** and should be treated as a hypothesis about incentive structure rather than a confirmed causal finding; it flags a testable proposition — that per-capita SNI funding multipliers may interact with demographic decline to create classification incentives at the school level — but does not itself establish that this occurs at scale or currently.

## 9. Segregation and school-choice dynamics are well documented in Hungarian education economics generally, though the SNI/BTMN-specific channel is less directly evidenced in the sources found
Extensive prior economics literature (Kertesi & Kézdi and related work) analyzes 
school segregation, school choice and educational policies in 100 Hungarian towns
, and a 2020 CEU working paper on social selection describes how 
a new channel for segregation was created by the Hungarian government through the establishment of so-called "Bridge classes," which in theory are second-chance opportunities for pupils who drop out before mandatory schooling ends but in practice often function merely as repositories for Roma pupils without meaningful educational opportunity
. This establishes that Hungary has documented precedent for administrative/categorical mechanisms (not itself SNI/BTMN) interacting with school choice to produce segregation — a structurally analogous risk pathway, but the search did not surface a study directly quantifying parent-driven school/class mobility specifically triggered by SEN or BTMN labeling.

## 10. International oversight has flagged special-needs protection and segregation as linked, unresolved policy concerns (institutional/normative claim, not quantitative)
Following a 2025 country visit, the UN Special Rapporteur on the right to education 
called for a review of curriculum and assessment, greater protection for students with special needs, clear and effective policies to tackle segregation, equitable funding for all types of school, and greater involvement of students, teachers and parents in decision-making
, and warned in the report's framing of a 
"hollowing out" of Hungary's public education system, deepening inequalities
 (per the OHCHR press release title). This is an authoritative but qualitative/advocacy-oriented source; 
the Rapporteur's full report was to be presented to the UN Human Rights Council in June 2026
, meaning more detailed findings may since have become available beyond what this search captured.
