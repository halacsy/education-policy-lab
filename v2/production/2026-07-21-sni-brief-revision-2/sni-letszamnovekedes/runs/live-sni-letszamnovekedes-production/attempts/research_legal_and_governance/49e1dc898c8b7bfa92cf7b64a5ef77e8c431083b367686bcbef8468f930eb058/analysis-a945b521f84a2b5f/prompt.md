# Research Notes: SEN Identification, Legal Authority, and Governance in Hungary

**1. The recorded SNI population has grown continuously since at least 2012/13, with a documented acceleration from 2021/22 (fact, statistical trend; causal driver contested).**
Hungarian Central Statistical Office (KSH) data compiled by Telex show that 
KSH data indicate that in the past year alone the number of children with special educational needs in education grew by more than two thousand, and from the 2012–2013 school year onward there have been more such special-needs students every year
. More recent reporting confirms the trend continued and accelerated: 
preliminary KSH figures show 112,007 SNI children in kindergartens and primary/secondary institutions in the latest school year, up from 107,421 in the same period the prior year
, and 
extraordinary growth was already visible in the 2021/2022 school year and did not stop in 2022/2023
. A separate figure set (differing scope/definition) reports 
the number of SNI children/students rose to 104,865 in the 2023/2024 school year, 5,277 more than a year earlier
, and one report frames this as 
a 40 percent rise in the number of SNI children/students over the past 20 years
. *Note: absolute totals differ across sources (63k for primary-school-only vs. ~105–112k for all levels), indicating scope/definition inconsistency across reporting rather than a single agreed figure — a methodological caveat for any policy analysis.*

**2. Category composition: the growth is concentrated in "severe learning/attention/behavioral regulation disorder," not classic disability categories (fact).**
Per KSH-based reporting, 
66% of SNI students have severe learning, attention, or behavioral-regulation disorders, and 12% have autism spectrum disorder
, with 
roughly one in ten boys nationally classified as SNI, versus 6.1% of girls
. Regional disparity is large: 
Békés county has the highest share (14% of primary pupils), while Borsod-Abaúj-Zemplén (5.4%) and Hajdú-Bihar (5.7%) have the lowest
. Most SNI students remain in mainstream settings: 
73% of SNI students are taught in integrated classes
.

**3. A 2017–2018 legal reform ("lex Taigetosz") restructured incentives between the BTMN and SNI categories, and is cited by commentators (contested inference) as a driver of SNI growth.**
The 2017 amendment to the Public Education Act 
ended, from 1 September 2018 in a phase-in system, the exemption from grading/classification and the option of narrative assessment for BTMN students; the law's wording changed "student" to "student with special educational needs," so only SNI students retain the exemption
. Civil-liberties group TASZ formally objected that 
the bill would restrict exemptions exclusively to SNI students, excluding BTMN students, removing exemptions from BTMN students for whom this could be pedagogically justified and even necessary for their development
. A 2024 news analysis explicitly links this legal tightening to the subsequent statistical jump: 
KSH data support that there may be a connection between the tightening and the rise in SNI numbers — the number of SNI children rose much more sharply from the 2021/2022 school year onward than previously
. *This causal link is asserted by journalists/commentators, not established by a controlled study — flagged as contested inference.*

**4. Diagnostic/placement authority is legally centralized in a two-tier expert-committee system, separate from medical diagnosis (fact, governance structure).**
Under the governing EMMI decree 15/2013 (pedagógiai szakszolgálati intézmények), 
SNI status can only be established by county (vármegyei/fővárosi) expert committees of the pedagogical service institutions, while BTMN status is established by district-level (járási) committees, which cannot establish SNI
. Authority is explicitly institutional, not medical: 
a medical diagnosis alone does not confer entitlement to special educational provision — SNI status must be established separately, and while a BNO code is a medical code, SNI/BTMN codes are issued by the expert committee
. This creates a bifurcated accountability structure: healthcare professionals identify impairment, but only an educational-administrative body confers the legal status carrying entitlements and placement consequences.

**5. Parental consent is the default trigger for assessment, but institutional/hatósági (administrative-authority) override exists when welfare is at risk — creating an autonomy-vs-child-welfare conflict zone (fact).**
Ordinarily, 
a parent, kindergarten/school, or other professional can initiate an assessment request to the district expert committee, but parental consent is required in every case
, and 
if a teacher proposes an assessment but the parent disagrees, the parent has the right to refuse to initiate the committee assessment
. However, this autonomy is not absolute: 
if a parent does not cooperate and this endangers the child, an assessment can proceed via an official administrative procedure without parental consent — the locally competent school-district center (tankerületi központ) is authorized to conduct this procedure, and the designated institution's director must initiate proceedings if the parent fails to participate in provision through their own fault
, potentially involving child-welfare services. Governing regulation similarly provides that 
in the case of initiating assessment for a multiply-disadvantaged child, absent parental agreement, the initiating institution requests the Office (Hivatal/school district) to commence administrative proceedings
.

**6. A formal appeal/review mechanism exists but is narrow and short-fused, raising due-process questions (fact).**
Parents can challenge outcomes: 
the right of appeal is governed by EMMI decree 15/2013 §39(10) — the requester may, within fifteen days of receiving the expert opinion or the notice rejecting an assessment, initiate a review of the expert opinion or the assessment's rejection through an administrative procedure
. Reviews of established SNI status are also periodic rather than solely parent-triggered: 
the expert committee conducts review proceedings ex officio, upon authority request, parental request, or institutional request, and — unless otherwise specified — the expert opinion must be reviewed ex officio during the first educational/school year following implementation
. The short 15-day window and reliance on administrative (not judicial) review channels are structural features relevant to accountability analysis, though no direct critique of their adequacy was found in available sources beyond the general segregation-related ECtHR criticisms below.

**7. Funding architecture ties per-pupil financing to SNI classification, creating a potential (unconfirmed) structural incentive toward over-identification (fact on mechanism; inference on effect).**
Historically, 
institutions educating SNI students received higher normative funding — 224,000 HUF/pupil/year in 2012/2013
, until 
normative financing was replaced by task-based financing (feladatfinanszírozás), under which SNI pupils are counted as two or three pupils, depending on classification, when calculating the average-salary-based subsidy used to fund additional teacher posts
. This means SNI classification directly increases an institution's staffing budget eligibility — a mechanism that could theoretically incentivize schools to seek SNI labeling for struggling pupils, though no source in this search set provides causal evidence that this financial incentive (as opposed to genuine need, diagnostic-practice changes, or the BTMN/SNI legal asymmetry above) drives the aggregate rise. This should be treated as a structural feature to examine, not a proven cause.

**8. Specialist workforce (gyógypedagógus) shortages are geographically uneven and coincide with a historic low in overall teacher numbers, creating a capacity-demand gap (fact).**
Research cited in an academic analysis found 
in several northeastern districts no special education teachers were employed at all despite rising numbers of children with special needs, a shortage compounded by the concentration of teacher-training institutions in urban centers, which limits the return of qualified professionals to peripheral areas
. Concurrently, 
while SNI-classified (including learning-disordered) children have increased, the number of teachers/educators employed in public education and vocational training reached its lowest point since the turn of the millennium in the 2023/2024 school year
, having 
fallen by about 1,600 compared with the 2022/2023 school year
. Even where diagnosis occurs, provision may fail: 
SNI-designated institutions sometimes cannot provide the development/therapy prescribed in the expert opinion due to staff shortages, leading parents to seek private providers — in some cases even reimbursed informally by the maintaining authority, a practice explicitly noted as not lawful
.

**9. Hungary has an unresolved, decade-plus legal-accountability record on discriminatory misclassification of Roma children via the special-needs assessment system (fact — binding international rulings; ongoing enforcement gap).**
In *Horváth and Kiss v. Hungary* (2013), the ECtHR 
found that two Roma applicants diagnosed with mild mental disabilities as children and placed in a remedial school had suffered ethnic discrimination in their enjoyment of the right to education, in violation of Article 2 Protocol 1 combined with Article 14 ECHR
, holding that 
in light of the recognised bias in past placement procedures, the State has specific positive obligations to avoid the perpetuation of past discrimination or discriminative practices disguised in allegedly neutral tests
. Enforcement has stalled: 
over the past decade the Committee of Ministers monitoring implementation has not been able to close the case, because the Hungarian government has not been able to convincingly demonstrate elimination of the discriminatory practices
, and 
the European Commission launched infringement proceedings against Hungary in 2016 over Romani school segregation, with no publicly available update on the investigation's status
. A 2023 follow-on judgment, *Szolcsán v. Hungary*, found 
that education of Roma children in segregated schools without adequate corrective measures was incompatible with the State's non-discrimination duty, and required Hungary to adopt measures not only to end segregation at the specific school but to develop a policy ending segregation in education nationally
. A 2024 Rosa Parks Foundation study found continuing practice problems: 
existing practices result in an unjustifiably high proportion of Roma children being diagnosed with mild intellectual disability, and the diagnostic process leaves wide scope for bias, subjective diagnosis, and misdiagnosis while excluding Romani parents from participation, with no guarantee rules ensuring assessment gives due regard to children's rights and disability rights
.

**10. UN and Ombudsman data (2023–24) corroborate that special-needs classification remains entangled with unresolved ethnic-segregation complaints, an accountability conflict distinct from (but overlapping) the general SEN-growth question (fact).**
A UN Special Rapporteur report noted 
an estimated 35% of Roma students attend majority-Roma schools, and complaints about segregation accounted for 69% of those received by the Ombudsman for Fundamental Rights in 2023
, and identified 
systemic over- and misdiagnosis of Roma children with learning disabilities, leading to placement in special education without proper justification, as a major concern exacerbating their educational disadvantage
. The Rapporteur further recommended 
systematic collection of ethnically-disaggregated data to track segregation patterns
 — data Hungary does not currently collect in this domain, per the same reporting stream.

**11. School-choice/catchment-area discretion creates a separate legal-governance tension around parental mobility and school composition (fact, from litigated case).**
In *Szolcsán*, domestic courts had upheld a school's refusal to enroll a Roma student outside its catchment area even though 
schools in Hungary have the discretion to enrol a child from outside the catchment area
; the ECtHR ultimately ruled this refusal, combined with the resulting segregated placement, violated the applicant's rights 
in conjunction with his right to non-discrimination (Article 2 Protocol 1 taken with Article 14)
. This illustrates a governance conflict directly relevant to the submitter's concern about parental mobility reshaping school composition: catchment/enrollment discretion sits with individual school administrations, while its cumulative, segregative effects are adjudicated only ex post at the level of individual litigation, not through any systemic ex ante safeguard identified in these sources.
