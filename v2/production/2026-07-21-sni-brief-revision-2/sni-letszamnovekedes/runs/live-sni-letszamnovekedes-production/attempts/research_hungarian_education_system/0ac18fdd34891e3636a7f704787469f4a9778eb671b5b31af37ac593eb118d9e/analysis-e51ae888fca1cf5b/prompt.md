# Research Notes: SEN/BTMN Identification Trends and Hungarian Education Governance

*Scope: This brief synthesizes verifiable statistical trends, legal/institutional architecture, and documented rights disputes relevant to the rising identification of special educational needs (SNI), BTMN, autism, and related categories in Hungary. Facts are cited to sources; interpretive or causal claims are explicitly flagged as inference or contested.*

---

## 1. The quantitative trend is real but its size and continuity require care

**Fact:** 
The number of children diagnosed with special educational needs (SNI) grew from 92,800 to 97,300 in one year, according to Central Statistical Office (KSH) data for the 2021/2022 school year, and while the number diagnosed with SNI increases every year, the rise that year was larger than in any of the preceding twelve years.
 
In the 2023/2024 school year the number of SNI students continued to grow, reaching about 63,000, or 8.8% of primary-school pupils
, per KSH data reported by eduline.hu. Over a longer horizon, 
statistical data show the number of SNI children/students rose by 40% over the last 20 years
 (Világgazdaság, 2024). Continuation into 2022/2023 is also reported: 
the rapid growth of SNI students and the shortage of special-education teachers is an increasingly serious problem, with extraordinary growth visible already in 2021/2022 that did not stop in 2022/2023.


**Caveat:** These figures come from journalistic aggregation of KSH/Oktatási Hivatal releases rather than a single continuous official time series in these search results; base-year definitions and category boundaries may have shifted (see §3).

## 2. Composition of the rise is uneven by disorder type, sex, and region

**Fact (disorder type):** 
The largest proportional increase was among children with severe learning disorders: in the 2021/2022 school year there were 45,300 such children, 12% more than the previous year.
 In 2023/2024, 
among integrated SNI students, 66% have a severe learning, attention, or behavioral-regulation disorder, and 12% have autism spectrum disorder.


**Fact (sex disparity):** 
Only 6.1% of girls are classified SNI, versus more than 11% of boys — meaning on average one in ten boys is SNI.
 This is a stable, large sex gap requiring explanation (biological, diagnostic-bias, or referral-pathway hypotheses are all plausible; none is confirmed here).

**Fact (regional disparity):** 
The county-level variation is substantial: Békés county has the highest share of SNI primary pupils (14%), while Borsod-Abaúj-Zemplén (5.4%) and Hajdú-Bihar (5.7%) have the lowest.
 This range (2.6x) is far larger than plausible true prevalence variation, suggesting local diagnostic/administrative practice differences — **inference, not confirmed in sources**.

## 3. BTMN is a distinct, softer legal category with its own — even larger — growth signal, and definitional ambiguity

**Fact:** 
BTMN (beilleszkedési, tanulási, magatartási nehézség — integration, learning, behavioral difficulty) is defined in Act CXC of 2011 on National Public Education as a "special treatment"-requiring child/student category, distinct from SNI, requiring designation by the expert committee.
 
BTMN is internationally difficult to interpret because it is a specifically Hungarian category, essentially milder than SNI: symptoms are similar but less severe, so it is not classified as a disability.


**Fact (last public data cited by researchers):** 
In the last publicly available public-education statistics (2018/2019), among preschool-age children (n=326,588) the number with BTMN was 6,779, or 2.1% of enrolled children, while among school-age children (n=728,706) the number classified BTMN was 69,477, or 9.5% of the school-age population.
 
The BTMN prevalence among school-age children is almost five times that registered among preschoolers.


**Inference flagged by researchers:** The jump between preschool and school-age BTMN rates is "striking" per the source, consistent with either (a) genuine emergence of difficulties once academic demands begin, or (b) school-entry screening/referral practices being much more sensitive/incentivized than preschool screening. The source does not adjudicate between these.

## 4. A contested causal hypothesis: 2020 school-readiness rule change

**Fact/reported hypothesis:** 
The increase in SNI numbers may have several causes, for example that specialists now have access to better diagnostic methods than before; but it may also be connected to the government's tightening, from January 2020, of rules on school-start deferral and school-readiness determination, which may have resulted in many six-year-olds entering school unready, instead of an additional year of kindergarten.


**Status:** This is explicitly presented in the source as one of several possible, non-exclusive explanations — **contested/unverified causal claim**, not a settled finding. It matters for policy because it implies part of the "rise" could be an artifact of an unrelated regulatory change (school-entry age policy) rather than changed prevalence or diagnostic generosity per se.

## 5. Governance mechanism that is unchanged: the expert-committee gatekeeping model

**Fact:** 
Under current law, only the expert committee of the pedagógiai szakszolgálat (Educational Guidance/Counseling Service) is authorized to determine BTMN status, and the manner, form, and location of provision and the areas requiring development; the child's institution is then obliged to organize developmental support based on the expert opinion.
 The same body performs the SNI determination and the integrated-vs-segregated placement recommendation: 
after assessment the expert committee proposes whether the child can be cared for in an integrated or segregated setting — integrated meaning attendance at a mainstream-curriculum institution together with peers, segregated meaning attendance limited to a special (gyógypedagógiai) institution or class, separated from non-SNI children.


**Relevance to the two framing questions:** This single-gatekeeper architecture (one expert committee system nationwide) is the core institutional interface through which any rise in identification is *processed* — it has not been restructured even as caseloads have risen, meaning capacity constraints in this body directly shape observed identification rates (see §7) independent of "true" need.

## 6. Governance mechanism that is unchanged: free school choice and the market/voucher-type model

**Fact:** 
Under the National Public Education Act (Nkt.), school choice is free — in theory parents may enroll their child at any school, and non-state actors may also provide public education services; this constitutes a market-type service mechanism, more precisely a voucher-financed model, in which the state provides the funding while the consumer freely chooses where to use it among state-recognized providers.


**Fact (asymmetric obligation):** 
Compulsory schooling places asymmetric burdens on parent and school: parents are obliged to enroll a school-age child somewhere and may freely choose any school, while the school responsible for the child's residential catchment area must in any case accept the child.


**Relevance:** This mechanism — a core piece of Hungarian school governance predating the SEN/BTMN rise — is left untouched by recent identification trends and by the 2023 anti-segregation law (see §9), and is the structural channel through which parental responses to classroom composition (including SEN concentration) can reshape school intake, as posited in the public question.

## 7. Specialist workforce and service-capacity constraints are documented and persistent

**Fact (shortage, current):** 
The shortage of special-education teachers (gyógypedagógus) is largest in Budapest, where 45 such positions are advertised, and smallest in Transdanubia — 2 each in Veszprém and Zala, 3 in Baranya.


**Fact (training pipeline responding):** 
Applications to the state-subsidized special-education degree program rose from 3,270 to 3,768 first-choice applicants for 2026, more than a 15% increase over the prior year.
 Whether this addresses the actual regional/functional shortage (e.g., concentrated in Budapest, specific specializations) is not established in these sources — **inference gap**.

**Fact (assessment/therapy bottleneck):** 
Waiting lists for diagnostic assessment run to several months, and therapeutic care capacity is absent or very limited
, per a 2022 open manifesto from pedagógiai szakszolgálat professionals reported by HVG; 
children diagnosed with ADHD, autism, developmental disorders, depression, anxiety, and compulsions are referred to these services for therapy "where the conditions for helping them are not ensured either in staffing (missing capacity, constant turnover), expertise and experience... or the necessary medical background."


**Note on source type:** This is practitioner/advocacy testimony (a public manifesto), not an independent audit — treat as documented professional concern rather than confirmed system-wide metric.

## 8. Legal-rights history: SEN/segregation categorization has been an established discrimination vector for Roma children, per binding rulings — status contested/unresolved

**Fact (2013 ruling):** 
In Horváth and Kiss v. Hungary (2013), the ECtHR ruled that two applicants placed in a special school for children with mental disabilities had been discriminated against, in violation of the European Convention on Human Rights.
 
The Court called on Hungary to modify its tests and provide safeguards against discrimination, misdiagnosis, and misplacement of Roma children.


**Fact (2023 ruling, ongoing):** 
In Szolcsán v. Hungary (application no. 24408/16), the ECtHR ruled that education of Roma children in segregated schools breaches the prohibition of discrimination and that Hungary must develop a policy to end segregation in education.
 
Hungary remains subject to an ongoing infringement procedure by the European Commission for systemic segregation of Romani children in education
 (as of the March 2023 press release).

**Contested/unresolved implementation:** 
The government has not provided data requested by the Committee of Ministers on the impact of new diagnostic procedures on Romani students
, and an NGO submission alleged 
culturally biased diagnostic protocols are still in use nationwide, with no effective supervision or monitoring of expert panels
 — **this is an advocacy-sourced claim, not independently verified here, and should be marked as contested/disputed rather than established fact.**

## 9. The 2023 anti-segregation law reportedly leaves the key selection mechanisms (school choice, elite tracks) untouched — a critic's assessment, contested

**Fact (critics' claim):** Regarding the December 2023 anti-segregation law, 
commentators emphasized problems posed by free school choice, which enables segregation strategies
, while 
the underlying problem — the system's selectivity — only strengthens as children progress through public education, made worse by six- and eight-grade gimnáziums, which the law does not even address, and which "cream off" children from better family backgrounds.


**Status:** This is a critical/opposition-aligned assessment (Mérce, citing Együttnevelés Szövetség and Tanítanék Mozgalom), not a government self-assessment — **flag as contested framing**, though the underlying legal fact (the law's silence on secondary-track selection and free choice) appears to be a factual description of the law's scope rather than a disputed empirical claim.

## 10. Prevalence estimates of segregation via special-needs misclassification are NGO-sourced and should be flagged as estimates, not audited statistics

**Claim (NGO estimate):** 
Advocacy sources state that in Hungary, children are massively and groundlessly declared mildly intellectually disabled, so they are not taught according to their actual abilities — one of the methods of segregating Roma children in the Hungarian school system
, and 
an NGO estimate holds that two-thirds of Roma children are taught separately in segregated schools, classes, or unjustifiably in special institutions.


**Status:** This is from Rosa Parks Foundation advocacy material — a plausible but **unaudited estimate**; it should not be treated as an official statistic, though it is consistent with the ECtHR case law pattern in §8.

## Direct answers to the two structuring questions

**How does the (unspecified) proposal interact with current Hungarian institutions?**
No specific policy proposal was given in the brief to test; however, the research indicates that *any* proposal touching SEN/BTMN identification would necessarily route through: (a) the single national expert-committee (pedagógiai szakszolgálat) diagnostic/placement gatekeeping structure (§5); (b) the legal definitions fixed in Act CXC of 2011 distinguishing SNI from BTMN (§3, §5); and (c) the specialist workforce pipeline currently under strain (§7). Historical precedent (the 2020 school-readiness rule tightening, §4) shows that policy changes in adjacent domains (school-entry age rules) can measurably affect SEN identification counts even without any intent to change SEN policy — a channel that should be anticipated in any impact assessment.

**Which existing selection, access, or governance mechanisms remain unchanged?**
Three governance mechanisms recur across sources as unchanged/untouched: (1) free parental school choice under the Nkt. voucher-type financing model (§6, §9); (2) the elite six/eight-grade gimnázium tracks operating alongside regular primary schools, explicitly excluded from the scope of the 2023 anti-segregation law per critics (§9); and (3) the sole-gatekeeper expert-committee diagnostic/placement system, which has not been restructured despite rising caseloads (§5, §7). These mechanisms are the ones through which parental responses to SEN/BTMN concentration (mobility between schools/classes) would operate, per the problem statement's own framing — but their unchanged status, and its consequences, are documented here only as structural facts, not as evaluated policy outcomes.
