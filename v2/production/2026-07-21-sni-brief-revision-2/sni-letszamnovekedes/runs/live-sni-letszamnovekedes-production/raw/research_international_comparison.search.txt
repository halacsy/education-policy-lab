# Research Notes: SEN Identification Trends — Comparative Perspective

**Scope note:** Notes address the two bounded questions (comparable mechanisms; transfer constraints) using verifiable, citable international evidence. This is not a policy recommendation. Findings are grouped: (A) verifying the Hungarian premise, (B) comparable mechanisms elsewhere, (C) contextual constraints on transfer.

---

## A. Testing the premise — is there actually a Hungarian rise, and of what kind?

**1. Some Hungarian data support a rise, but sourcing is thin and mixed with perception data.**
A 2023 survey of 121 Hungarian primary teachers found 
teachers who graduated in 2010 or later were more likely to have encountered students with SEN, and 83.6% of respondents reported an increase in the number of students requiring developmental
 support — this is a perception-based, small, non-representative sample, not an administrative count. Separately, a peer-reviewed article states 
in Hungary, between 2010 and 2020, the number of students with SEN (Special Educational Needs) in primary education
 changed (exact figures not visible in retrieved excerpt — magnitude needs verification from primary source before treating as established).

**2. Official European Agency country reporting flags a specific, large, uneven jump in one diagnostic sub-category.**
Hungary's own country profile for the European Agency for Special Needs and Inclusive Education states 
the number of learners with SEN due to other psychological developmental disorders increased fivefold over a five-year period and significant differences were found in the counties' statistics
, which prompted a policy response: 
the rules pertaining to diagnosis were amended and the population concerned was fully
 reviewed. This is a *fact* (official Hungarian source) and directly evidences that at least one component of SEN growth is category- and practice-driven rather than a simple demographic fact — county-level variance of this scale is difficult to explain by biological prevalence alone (*inference*).

**3. Hungary's own definitional history shows the SEN category itself has been unstable, which mechanically affects counts.**
The same source notes 
the previous definition of SEN was unstable and was based on organic causes... The dichotomy of organic versus non-organic does not accurately define the individual components of special education and rehabilitative services. It does not link the appropriate components with individual diagnostic categories nor establish the actual funding requirements
. A redefinition of what counts as SEN is a structural driver independent of "real" prevalence change (*fact* about definitional change; *inference* about its quantitative effect on trend lines).

## B. Where comparable mechanisms have been documented or attempted

**4. OECD-wide: rising identification is a general phenomenon, and diagnostic-practice change (not prevalence) is the leading explanation for at least one category (autism).**
Across OECD countries, 
autism diagnoses are rising rapidly across OECD countries, placing increasing pressure on health, education and social protection systems... the rate of ASD detection in children has risen sharply over the past decade
, with 
the number of children and youth diagnosed with autism spectrum disorder (ASD) has increased two to fourfold in just 15 years across OECD countries
. Critically, OECD's own assessment is that 
current evidence suggests that this is largely due to an increase in the detection and diagnosis of ASD, not in the underlying autism prevalence
. This is a direct international parallel to the Hungarian "growth in other psychological developmental disorders" finding (#2) — same open question (detection vs. prevalence) is being asked in many systems simultaneously, and OECD treats it as evidenced for autism specifically, not for SEN generally. General SEN growth beyond autism is documented separately (e.g., US special-education share 
14.1 percent of US public school students received Special Education services in 2018–2019, compared to 13.3 percent in 2000-2001, and 10.1 percent in 1980-1981
) but with less consensus on cause.

**5. England: a finance/incentive-linked mechanism producing a large, fast, well-documented rise — the closest comparator to the "incentives embedded in finance" mechanism named in the brief.**
England's Education, Health and Care Plan (EHCP) system shows 
the number of school pupils with EHCPs has risen by 180,000 or 71% between 2018 and 2024. As a result, nearly 5% of pupils now have EHCPs
, with pupils in mainstream schools holding EHCPs rising 
sharply from 126,000 in 2017 to 252,000 in 2024
. The Institute for Fiscal Studies (a non-partisan analytic body) explicitly frames causes as contested: 
the reasons behind this rise in EHCPs are complex, but potential explanations include increased severity of needs, expanded recognition and diagnosis of needs, and stronger incentives to seek statutory provision
. IFS further identifies specific funding-design mechanisms as plausible drivers: 
numbers and costs have been driven by financial incentives to seek EHCPs for less severe needs, the statutory obligations attached to EHCPs, poor practice, and seemingly artificial separations between high-needs and mainstream education budgets... the present system of ring-fenced funding blocks, a cash-terms freeze in the top-up funding threshold, and top-up funding being generally limited to specific pupils with EHCPs are likely to have played an important role in escalating EHCPs
. This is the clearest documented case internationally of the exact mechanism the Hungarian brief hypothesizes (finance/placement rules incentivizing identification) — but note causal attribution remains *contested*, not settled, even within England's own fiscal analysis.

**6. England: SEN identification and school segregation/composition are empirically linked — parallel to the Hungarian concern about parental mobility reshaping school composition.**
A 2026 study finds 
the percentage of children with SEND attending special schools is strongly associated with the percentage of children with the statutory protection of an EHCP in the LA, with an association between higher numbers of EHCPs and higher proportions of children in special schools
, and flags that 
this is concerning, since previous studies have strongly demonstrated that awarding EHCPs is not (only) underpinned by em
[pirical need] (sentence cut off in source, but signals a contested/critical literature on EHCP allocation drivers). Separately, regional funding variance appears linked to differential award rates: 
Lee et al.'s (2024) cohort study of children with genetic differences found significant regional differences in whether these children had EHCPs, potentially linked to funding levels; those regions with higher levels of funding awarded more
. This is a documented case of SEN classification correlating with subsequent segregation of school composition — directly analogous to the Hungarian brief's concern, though the causal direction (need drives funding vs. funding drives diagnosis) is *contested*.

**7. Hong Kong: a comparator case where officials attribute rising SEN identification primarily to improved detection/awareness rather than incentive structures — a useful contrast to the England case.**
Hong Kong's Legislative Council research notes 
the number of SEN students in public sector primary and secondary ordinary schools more than doubled between 2011-2012 and 2021-2022
, attributed by the official source to 
increased awareness of SEN amongst schools and parents and the continuous improvement in the identification mechanism
. This is offered as the government's own explanation (not independently verified against funding-incentive hypotheses in the retrieved source) — useful as a case where the "rising identification = improving detection, not gaming" narrative is the official position, contrasting with England's more contested framing.

**8. Wales: a rare documented case of a *category/system redesign* producing a measured *decline* in identification rates — directly relevant to testing whether redefinition (as Hungary did per finding #3) changes the trend.**
Under Wales' new Additional Learning Needs (ALN) framework (replacing the prior SEN categorical system), research notes 
this number has declined significantly since the introduction of the new ALN system, reaching 11.2% in January 2024
, compared with 
similar numbers of students receiv[ing] Special Educational Needs and Disability (SEND) support across England (13.6%)
, while 
higher rates are seen in Additional Support Needs (ASN) identification in Scotland (37%) and SEN identification in Northern Ireland (18.2%) due to each country taking different approaches to defining and supporting SEN
. This four-way UK/Ireland comparison (Wales 11.2%, England 13.6%, N. Ireland 18.2%, Scotland 37%) is strong evidence that identification rate is highly sensitive to definitional architecture, independent of any underlying population difference (*fact*, well-sourced; the inference that Hungary's rate could similarly respond to redefinition is *inference*, not tested).

**9. Finland: an alternative institutional mechanism — a graduated, non-diagnosis-gated tiered support model — explicitly designed to reduce reliance on categorical labeling as a gateway to support.**
Since reforms to the Basic Education Act, Finland runs 
a three-tiered support system... The three levels – general support, intensified support, and special support – create a comprehensive safety net
, where 
the Finnish education system views support needs on a continuum, rejecting rigid categorization of students
, and 
support does not determine placement. A child does not need to be in a special education class in order to get support
. This is the most direct comparator to a mechanism that decouples "formal SEN identification" from "receiving support" — potentially relevant to Hungary's finance/placement-incentive concern, since Finland's model is designed so that access to help does not require a labeling event that then triggers separate funding or placement consequences. Scale of usage: 
the number of Finnish students who need support for learning is high. In 2022, 31% of students in basic education received support for their studies at one tier or another
 — meaning Finland does not have low identification, it has decoupled identification from segregation/stigma (*fact*; whether it reduces false-positive labeling vs. simply relabeling need under softer terms is *contested/unclear* from retrieved sources).

**10. OECD comparative finding: whether entitlements are diagnosis-gated or needs-assessed varies by country and structures incentive intensity.**
Across an 11-country OECD review of autism-related supports, 
a key distinction across systems lies in how entitlements are allocated. In most OECD countries, needs-based allocation dominates. Therapy intensity, placement in mainstream versus special education and access to social services typically depend on a multi-disciplinary assessment rather than a diagnostic category
. Israel is the outlier: 
therapy hours in special education settings are standardised by diagnosis rather than individual need (e.g. a fixed number of weekly hours for all children with ASD)
. This provides a structural framework for classifying Hungary's own rules (need to establish empirically where Hungary sits on this needs-based vs. diagnosis-gated spectrum) and for understanding why diagnosis-gated systems are theorized to create stronger incentives toward over-identification (*inference*, plausible but not directly tested in the OECD excerpt retrieved).

## C. Contextual differences constraining transfer to Hungary

**11. Hungary's SEN/special-school placement history has a distinct, well-documented ethnic-segregation dimension largely absent (or differently structured) in England, Finland, Wales, and Hong Kong comparators.**
Historical and current research documents that in Hungary and the wider Central European region, SEN/special-school channels have functioned as a mechanism of Roma segregation: 
in the 1980s, almost every third Roma child in Hungary or in Czechoslovakia ended up being educated in a special school
, and more recent qualitative research found that 
in schools where the proportion of Roma pupils is between 50-75%, "children are more likely to be tested, to receive an SEN diagnosis and to be referred to segregated special education classes"
, while 
in segregated schools where the Romani students exceed 75%, the dilemma of choosing between normal and special education does not even arise, as there is no institutional or other interest in removing them from their segregated school... Romani children are often not sent for expert assessment as they are already ethnically segregated
. Historical Ministry documentation notes cases where 
there were some schools where the single written reason given in assessment requests suggesting the children are disabled, was their being 'of Romani parentage'
. This ethnic dimension is a Hungary/Central-Europe-specific institutional feature not present in the England, Finland, or Hong Kong comparator cases retrieved, meaning mechanisms designed abroad to address funding-driven or definitional over-identification (Wales ALN reform, Finnish tiering) were not designed to address, and may not automatically correct, an ethnically patterned diagnosis-and-placement dynamic. Academic literature places this as a regional pattern: 
systemic segregation, disablement, and overrepresentation of Romani children with special education needs (SEN) in Central Europe... institutional racism, poverty, and biased diagnostic practices disproportionately channel Romani students into segregated or SEN placements
.

**12. Formal EU/ECtHR legal pressure is a Hungary-specific (and CEE-regional) constraint/context not present for England, Finland, or Wales reforms.**
Hungary has been subject to European Commission infringement proceedings and ECtHR findings on this issue: 
such decisions have improved legislation and policy-making, but had far less impact on segregationist practices, despite the infringement proceedings by the European Commission against the Czech Republic, Slovakia, and Hungary
, and current estimates suggest 
one out of every ten Romani children on average is still placed in a special school or class that is mainly for Roma
. Any transferred mechanism (e.g., redefinition à la Wales, or tiered non-diagnosis support à la Finland) would operate in Hungary under an active EU legal-compliance constraint that does not apply to the Nordic or UK-devolved comparator systems in the same form (*fact* re: legal history; *inference* that this materially constrains transfer design, e.g., requiring desegregation safeguards built into any reform, not present in the source systems).

**13. Structural/workforce preconditions behind Finland's tiered model (teacher training depth, small classes, decoupled funding) differ from Hungary's reported classroom structure.**
Finland's model depends on 
highly trained ECE teachers
 and dedicated special education teachers (SETs) integrated into general classrooms at scale, with documented strain even within Finland itself: 
SETs don't have enough time. I've got used to coping alone, and I don't often ask for help
 (teacher testimony) and 
because of a lack of resources, the students have to survive in the general education classroom with me. It's not easy for them, nor for me. Differentiating is easy in theory, but succeeding with it in practice is another story
. By contrast, Hungarian mainstream classes are reported to run with 
each class typically consist[ing] of 24 children, with one teacher responsible for teaching all subjects... in most cases, one specialist monitors the progress and development of 24 children over a span of four years
 — i.e., without the dual-teacher/SET co-teaching infrastructure central to Finland's tier-2/tier-3 support. This is a *fact*-based structural gap (staffing model) that would need addressing before any Finnish-style de-diagnosis-gating reform could function as intended in Hungary (*inference* about transferability, grounded in documented resourcing differences).

**14. England's incentive-driven EHCP mechanism itself shows signs of not generalizing well even domestically (geographic unevenness, tribunal-driven distortion), which is a caution against assuming any single imported fix would be uniformly implementable.**
Even within England, the EHCP system shows strong regional variation and legal-process effects: 
greater parental awareness, advocacy, and increased rates of appeals to the SEND Tribunal—where families are successful in the vast majority of cases—have further increased the likelihood that children with complex needs secure statutory support that, in many areas, is more readily met in specialist settings
. This means the England "comparator" is itself an unstable, contested system undergoing active reform (white paper referenced in 
a sweeping reform of the Special Educational Needs and Disabilities (SEND) system
), not a stable model ready for transfer — it is better read as a cautionary case study of what happens when statutory rights and ring-fenced funding rules combine, rather than as a template to adopt.

## Summary of open/contested points (not resolved by this search)
- Whether Hungary's aggregate SEN/BTMN rise is large, continuous, and distinct from perception/anecdote is **not established** in the sources found here (only one small teacher-survey and one official five-fold sub-category figure were retrieved; a comprehensive KSH/Hungarian-ministry time series was not located and would be needed).
- Causal attribution of rising identification to funding incentives vs. genuine need vs. diagnostic-practice change is **actively contested** in every comparator system examined (England/IFS explicitly says "reasons... are complex"; OECD attributes autism rise mainly to detection).
- No source directly evaluates whether Hungary's own USEMI (Unified Special Educational, Conductive Educational Methodological Institutions) system functions more like Finland's tiered model or England's diagnosis-gated model — this remains a research gap for further investigation, not answered here.
