# Research Notes: SEN/BTMN Identification Trends in Hungary — Education Inequality Perspective

*Scope note: These are exploratory research notes, not a policy recommendation. Findings are separated into (a) reasonably well-documented facts from statistical/administrative sources, (b) contested interpretive claims, and (c) inferences requiring further testing.*

## 1. The rise in recorded SEN (SNI) numbers is documented but its size depends on which subgroup and years are compared

Multiple KSH (Central Statistical Office) data releases confirm a multi-year upward trend, though absolute figures vary by report scope (total vs. primary-school subset). 
Between the 2020/21 and 2021/22 school years, the number of children identified as having special educational needs rose from 92,800 to 97,300, and while the number of SEN-diagnosed children increases every year, the rise in that year was larger than in any of the preceding twelve years.
 A later report extends the series: 
by the 2024/2025 school year, 112,060 students were registered as SNI, an increase of nearly 28,000 over ten years.
 A 2024 industry report separately states 
that over the past 20 years the number of SNI children/students has risen by 40 percent, with 104,865 SNI children registered in the 2023–2024 school year.
 A 2026 KSH-based report gives a different, seemingly narrower figure — 
in the 2025/2026 school year the number of SNI students in Hungary exceeded 69,000, with most of these students in integrated education, learning together with peers in mainstream primary schools
 — suggesting this later figure may refer specifically to primary-school-level students rather than the full cross-level total (kindergarten through secondary). **Inference:** cross-report comparability is imperfect; a rigorous trend analysis needs to standardize by education level and definitional vintage rather than rely on secondary press summaries.

## 2. The fastest-growing subcategory is "severe learning/attention/behavioral disorder" (a proxy for BTMN-adjacent diagnoses), not sensory/physical/intellectual disability


KSH data show the largest proportional increase was among children with severe learning disorders: 45,300 in the 2021/2022 school year, 12 percent more than the previous year.
 A longer-run figure states 
the number of children with severe learning disorders grew roughly 50 percent over ten years.
 Within the currently integrated SNI population, 
the most frequent diagnosis is severe learning, attention, or behavior-regulation disorder, affecting 69 percent of integrated students.
 This concentration in the "soft" diagnostic category (as opposed to sensory/motor/intellectual disability, which have clearer biomedical markers) is the category most plausibly sensitive to changes in assessment practice, school-readiness rules, and teacher/parent referral behavior — a mechanism the sources below discuss directly.

## 3. Official and independent explanations for the rise are contested — diagnostic modernization vs. structural/legal change

KSH's own explanation is partial and non-committal: 
the statistical office attributes the growth "partly" to modernization of diagnostic assessment tools, without elaborating on other possible causes.
 Independent experts dispute that this alone can explain the magnitude. 
A clinical psychologist interviewed by Népszava stated that diagnostic tools have not changed enough to explain such a large increase in SNI numbers, particularly for developmental and learning-disorder diagnoses.
 Instead, 
she attributed much greater importance to mandatory school enrollment from age six and stricter school-readiness (iskolaérettség) assessments, which she said led many immature children into school without receiving developmental support — a situation COVID-era distance learning further worsened.
 A separate teachers' union source cites additional compounding factors, including a 2018 legal change (source truncated in results). A later (2025) report corroborates a possible link to policy timing: 
the pace of increase accelerated after 2020, possibly connected to government-introduced changes, including the tightening of school-readiness examinations and the mandatory school-start age of six, which led many children to enter school while still immature.
 **This is a genuinely contested empirical question** — administrative/legal-change explanations vs. diagnostic-improvement explanations vs. real-prevalence explanations have not been formally decomposed in the cited sources.

## 4. SEN status in Hungary is explicitly structured as a financing/service-eligibility category, not a strictly clinical diagnosis — creating potential incentive effects in both directions

A detailed overview states: 
SNI in Hungary is primarily not a professional but a service-provision and financing category; institutions educating SNI students received higher normative (per-capita) state funding until 2013.
 This matters for interpreting the trend: rising SNI counts could partly reflect institutional incentives to identify/register students for funding rather than (only) rising underlying need. However, a specialized pedagogical journal suggests the incentive may have reversed for milder categories: 
SNI status is increasingly a basis for disadvantage rather than benefit — while it once conferred advantages in admissions procedures, in many places it is now an exclusionary factor, partly because many institutions have removed the capacity to accept learning-disordered children from their founding documents as the financing system increasingly fails to support extra resources for them.
 **Distinct claims, both source-attributed but not independently verified against current normatíva regulation texts**: (a) historically SNI funding created inclusion incentives; (b) more recently, funding/administrative burden may create exclusion incentives for milder (BTMN-adjacent) cases specifically. This tension is central to "who gains/loses access."

## 5. Specialist workforce (gyógypedagógus) shortage is documented as concentrated and current, alongside rising training-program demand


The shortage is largest in Budapest, where 45 gyógypedagógus (special education teacher) positions are advertised, while it is smallest in Transdanubia — two positions each in Veszprém and Zala counties, three in Baranya.
 Simultaneously, interest in training has grown: 
in 2026 the special-education degree program became more popular than the previous year, with 3,768 first-choice state-scholarship applicants versus 3,270 the year before, a rise of more than 15 percent.
 However, 
many students in the correspondence (part-time) special-education programs are themselves parents of affected children seeking to help their own child, meaning they will likely never become practicing special education teachers
 — an inference-relevant caveat that rising enrollment does not map directly onto future practicing workforce supply. Current staffing levels reported: 
4,900 special-education/conductor teachers currently work in primary schools supporting SNI students' development, plus 949 developmental teachers and a further 1,404 specialists in the traveling special-education and conductor networks.


## 6. Most SEN students are now educated in mainstream ("integrated") settings, but resourcing has not scaled proportionally


Most SNI students are in integrated education, studying alongside peers in mainstream schools — nearly 51,000 students, or 73 percent of those affected, receive integrated instruction.
 Yet the same source frames this as a system under strain: 
a growing number of SNI students study in Hungarian schools while institutions and specialists face increasingly large challenges in providing integrated education.
 A teacher-survey study corroborates this from the classroom side: 
83.6% of surveyed teachers reported an increase in the number of students requiring developmental support,
 and the same study notes 
that while inclusive education has expanded in policy discourse, empirical research suggests diagnostic practices and school placement decisions remain strongly influenced by institutional structures and available provisions
 — an inference-supporting claim that integration outcomes are shaped as much by resource/institutional capacity as by student characteristics.

## 7. There is well-established, though partly older, research evidence that SEN/special-school assessment has functioned as an ethnic sorting channel for Roma students

Older but methodologically substantial research (Open Society Foundations monitoring; ERRC) documents that 
the separation of Roma children into segregated schools and classes rose over the 15 years preceding a 2007 report, with Roma overrepresented in schools and classes for children with intellectual disabilities.
 More granular qualitative research found a threshold effect: 
in schools where the Roma pupil proportion is between 50–75%, children are more likely to be tested, to receive an SEN diagnosis, and to be referred to segregated special-education classes,
 whereas 
in schools where Roma students exceed 75%, the dilemma of choosing between normal and special education does not arise, since children are often not sent for expert assessment because they are already ethnically segregated.
 A CEU working paper adds a mechanism explanation, noting that 
channeling of Roma pupils into special schools/classes is a traditional segregation channel widely applied across Central-Eastern Europe, though Hungary's SEN integration policy has been comparatively more successful than the region.
 **This evidence is largely historical/qualitative (2007–2020 sources) and geographically local; it should not be read as a claim about the current national-level scale of the phenomenon**, which the more recent 2023 Educatio-journal statistical study (search result 4) attempts to quantify using 2016 vs. 2021 Oktatási Hivatal data but whose specific findings were not retrievable in full from search snippets.

## 8. Free school choice is an independently documented driver of compositional sorting, separate from (but potentially interacting with) SEN classification

Research by economists Kertesi and Kézdi found that 
Hungary's free school-choice system allows students/parents to choose schools outside their residential district, subject to capacity limits,
 and that 
the strongest factor increasing school segregation in Hungarian towns is the proportion of Romani students in that town,
 with 
school switching occurring often enough to significantly change a school's ethnic composition, and diminishing the role of residential distribution because many students commute to their school of choice.
 A related CEU working paper frames parental behavior explicitly: 
non-Roma parents' pursuit of good schooling for their children is often counterbalanced by prejudice against Roma pupils, with the "pull" and "push" effects not fully separable, since parents judge school quality on incomplete information in which a high concentration of Roma pupils is often perceived as a negative signal.
 **Relevance to "relocation vs. reduction of sorting":** this literature indicates that even absent formal SEN mechanisms, school-choice-driven sorting exists independently — meaning SEN/BTMN classification is one channel among several (school choice, informal tracking, class-level segregation) through which sorting can occur or be displaced.

## 9. A minority-but-notable "over-diagnosis" critique exists within Hungarian special-education scholarship itself

A specialized pedagogical journal source states plainly: 
the domestic complex special-education diagnostic system faces frequent accusations of "over-diagnosing" — that the number of children with learning disorders is irrationally high in Hungary.
 The same source flags a methodological complication for any international benchmarking exercise: 
comparison with OECD countries is difficult because Hungary's diagnostic and service system differs structurally in several key respects, and the definition of children requiring special treatment rests on different principles than general OECD practice.
 This is a caution against naively citing Hungary's SEN prevalence rate against OECD averages as evidence of "excess" identification without correcting for definitional incompatibility.

## 10. Open/untested premises for further research (framing, not findings)

- **Whether rising identification changes future options** (tracking into vocational streams, secondary-school access, higher-ed pathways) is asserted contextually (e.g., 
SNI status shifting from advantage to frequent exclusion in admissions
) but not quantified longitudinally in the retrieved sources — this is a priority gap for "who gains/loses future options."
- **Whether any specific policy proposal reduces or merely relocates sorting** cannot be assessed from current evidence because no single proposal was in scope of this search; however, the co-existence of (a) financing-driven classification incentives, (b) free school choice, and (c) documented ethnic-composition thresholds affecting testing/referral rates (finding 7) together imply that interventions targeting only the classification/diagnostic layer risk leaving school-choice-driven and class-level sorting channels untouched, or displacing sorting into whichever channel remains least regulated. This is an inference requiring dedicated modeling/data work, not a settled finding.
