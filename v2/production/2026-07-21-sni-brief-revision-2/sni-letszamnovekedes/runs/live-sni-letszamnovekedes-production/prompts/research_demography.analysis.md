# Research Notes: Cohort Demography, Geography, and SEN Identification in Hungarian Education Policy

*Scope: Population/cohort-analysis perspective only. Findings are descriptive; contested causal claims are flagged. No policy recommendation is offered per task instructions.*

---

## 1. Birth-cohort collapse is accelerating and is a settled empirical fact, not inference
Hungary's annual birth numbers have fallen sharply and continuously since 2021. 
In 2025, just 72,000 children were born, 7.1% fewer than in 2024, a drop of more than 5,500 births in a single year
, and 
the total fertility rate dropped to 1.31 children per woman, down from 1.39 a year earlier — the lowest level in the past 14 years
. This continues a longer slide: 
2025 was heading for about 73,000 births, 22% down on the 2021 peak of 93,000
. **Inference**: any school-age cohort entering kindergarten/grade 1 from ~2028 onward will be materially smaller than cohorts a decade earlier — a mechanical, non-behavioral driver of falling enrollment independent of SEN policy.

## 2. Official EU projections confirm a further, near-term contraction of the exact 0–4 pipeline feeding schools

The population of children aged 0-4 changed significantly between 2013 and 2023 and is projected to change further by 2033; Hungary experienced an increase of 1% in the number of 0-4 year-olds, and is projected to see a decline of 5% between 2023 and 2033
. **Fact**: this is a near-term, already "locked-in" demographic pipeline (children already born), distinct from future fertility uncertainty — it directly bears on how many pupils, including SEN-identified pupils, schools will need to serve.

## 3. A measured rise in SEN identification is documented over 2010–2020, but the drivers are contested in the literature itself

In Hungary, between 2010 and 2020, the number of students with SEN in primary education increased from 6.7% to 7.7%, and in secondary education this rose from 3.9% to 6.2%
. The same source offers two non-exclusive explanations without adjudicating between them: partly 
greater awareness of developmental disorders in the media and education, resulting in both parents and teachers becoming increasingly aware and noticing signs earlier... an improvement in diagnostics, resulting in more timely and accurate diagnoses
; and partly social/structural stressors — 
social disparities, high divorce rates, and challenges compounded by lack of financial/moral recognition for teachers and shortage of qualified professionals
. **Contested**: the source does not separate "real prevalence change" from "diagnostic practice change" quantitatively — this is an unresolved attribution problem, consistent with the brief's premise.

## 4. Teacher survey data corroborate a perceived rise, but this is subjective/perception-based, not incidence data
A 2023 survey of 121 Hungarian primary teachers found 
teachers who graduated in 2010 or later were more likely to have encountered students with SEN, and 83.6% of respondents reported an increase in the number of students requiring developmental support
. **Caveat**: this is teacher-perceived prevalence (subject to awareness/labeling bias), not a population-based incidence measure, and should not be treated as independent confirmation of a "real" rise.

## 5. Cross-national OECD evidence on autism specifically attributes the bulk of rising diagnosis to detection, not underlying prevalence — relevant as an analogous, non-Hungary-specific benchmark

The number of children and youth diagnosed with autism spectrum disorder has increased two to fourfold in just 15 years across OECD countries; current evidence suggests that this is largely due to an increase in the detection and diagnosis of ASD, not in the underlying autism prevalence
. **Inference for Hungary**: no Hungary-specific incidence-vs-detection decomposition was found in this search; the OECD cross-national finding is suggestive but not direct evidence for the Hungarian case.

## 6. Specialist/teacher workforce is aging and geographically maldistributed — a capacity constraint independent of whether the SEN rise is "real"

Teacher shortages, linked to an ageing workforce, are a growing concern; the number of teachers in the younger age group (30–44 years) has declined, and half of all teachers in the 2024/2025 academic year were 50 or older, while only 7.2% were under 30
. Geographically, 
teacher shortages are greatest in rural areas, with the proportion of unqualified teachers in disadvantaged schools 2 to 3 times higher than in schools not disadvantaged
. A UN expert review specifically linked this capacity gap to SEN: 
the growing number of children with special education needs has not been matched by adequate teacher training or specialist support in Hungary; bigger classes in public schools pose significant challenges to accommodate SEN students and the shortage of specialists, such as doctors and sign language interpreters, further adds to the difficulties
.

## 7. Rural depopulation historically produced smaller classes, not school closures — a structural feature that shapes where "integration" actually happens
Unlike systems that consolidate shrinking rural schools, Hungary's local governments historically responded to enrollment decline by reducing class sections rather than closing schools: 
during previous decades the deterioration in efficiency caused by a decline in the number of pupils was typically mitigated by local self-governments by reducing the number of parallel classes, not by the closure or amalgamation of schools, leading to a large offer of small schools... This situation especially applies to the many small schools in small rural settlements that operate only one class per grade
. **Inference**: single-class-per-grade rural schools cannot separate SEN/BTMN pupils into parallel tracks the way larger urban schools can, meaning geography constrains *how* integration is operationally implemented, independent of national policy design.

## 8. Rural/small-school financial viability has been declining for two decades, pre-dating the current cohort collapse

Local authorities were suffering from sharply declining financial viability and — particularly in rural areas — problems in relation to service provision; the funding of public services was secured through co-financing, with central government covering 80–90% of primary-education costs while municipalities provided the rest
, but 
by the turn of the millennium the share of state support had dropped to 60–70% on average and 50% in small village schools
. **Fact**: this financing squeeze predates and is compounding the current cohort decline, meaning the two pressures (demographic and fiscal) are cumulative rather than newly-emerging.

## 9. Free school choice interacts with residential segregation to concentrate disadvantage — "mobility effect," geographically concentrated in the Northeast
Research by Kertesi and Kézdi (cited in secondary sources) found that 
due to free parental school choice, the mobility of pupils among school districts reduces the role played by residence... catchment areas are not mandatorily enforced, but in the case of over-application schools are obliged to award an advantage to pupils from within their designated catchment areas... the cost of pupil mobility is rather low in bigger settlements
. Geographically, 
ghetto schools are concentrated in North-East Hungary, and the territorial concentration of the Roma population is increasing the number of ghetto schools primarily in villages, not in cities
. **Contested/inference**: the source explicitly states 
the extent of segregation that is not explained by demographic reasons is caused by the outcome of social selection
 — i.e., researchers themselves distinguish a demographic-composition component from a behavioral "white flight"/mobility component, which the brief should keep analytically separate.

## 10. Regional divide in child population trends is uneven, not uniform — some regions face outright child-population contraction while others hold steady
A recent regional analysis found 
while median values remained relatively stable over the four-year period, clear regional disparities are evident: numerous rural settlements in Western Transdanubia, Southern Baranya, and some low-density regions of Northern Hungary recorded stagnation or decline, including areas where the child population contracted by 10–30% over the period... A clear North–South and East–West divide emerges
, with 
Northeastern counties (Borsod-Abaúj-Zemplén, Szabolcs-Szatmár-Bereg, Heves) and parts of Southern Transdanubia showing high disadvantage concentrations, some settlements exceeding 70–92% child disadvantage rates
. **Fact**: this means "cohort decline" is not a single national number — viability pressure is spatially concentrated in already-disadvantaged Northeastern/Southern regions where school-composition effects (segregation, teacher shortage) are also most acute, compounding rather than offsetting each other.

## Answers to the two framing questions (synthesis, not new claims)

**How do cohort decline and geography change viability?**
Nationally, shrinking birth cohorts (Finding 1) and a locked-in further 5% decline in the 0–4 population by 2033 (Finding 2) mechanically reduce the pupil base schools must serve. But this decline is geographically uneven (Finding 10) and interacts with a historical practice of shrinking class sizes rather than closing rural schools (Finding 7), under worsening local financing (Finding 8). The result is that "viability" is not simply a function of national pupil counts — it is co-determined by where cohorts are shrinking fastest, whether local schools can still field parallel classes, and whether the specialist/teacher workforce (itself aging and rurally scarce — Finding 6) is present to serve remaining pupils, including those SEN-identified.

**Which effects would occur without any policy change (baseline/counterfactual)?**
Based on current trends alone: (a) continued cohort shrinkage would further thin rural single-class-per-grade schools, intensifying the fiscal/viability pressure already documented since the 2000s (Finding 8); (b) the aging teacher/specialist workforce would continue narrowing (Finding 6), widening the gap the UN review already flagged between SEN identification and specialist capacity (Finding 6); (c) free school-choice-driven mobility would likely continue concentrating disadvantaged/SEN-relevant populations in specific village and Northeastern schools (Findings 9–10), independent of whether the underlying SEN prevalence is rising or diagnostic practice is changing (Findings 3–5). These are extrapolations from documented trends, not measured forecasts.
