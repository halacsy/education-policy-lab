# Research Notes: Political Economy of Hungary's SEN Identification Trend

*Scope: Coalition feasibility and reversal/resistance exposure for potential policy responses. Facts are reported as sourced; inferences are explicitly flagged as such.*

---

**1. The underlying prevalence trend is documented but its interpretation is contested in the literature itself.**
Peer-reviewed research confirms a measurable rise: 
between 2010 and 2020, the number of students with SEN in primary education increased from 6.7% to 7.7%, while in secondary education this rose from 3.9% to 6.2%, displaying a significant increase
. However, the same literature frames this as an open interpretive question rather than a settled fact about "true" prevalence, noting that 
the labelling process is influenced by current education policy and social and ideological trends, which, in turn, shape professional guidelines, diagnoses, and educational practices in relation to SEN
. *Inference: this ambiguity itself is politically consequential — it allows government, unions, and NGOs to each attribute the trend to a different cause (real need vs. system incentives vs. diagnostic drift) without a shared evidentiary baseline, which weakens the possibility of a single cross-coalition reform narrative.*

**2. Frontline teachers report the increase is real and outpaces their preparedness, but survey evidence is limited in scale.**
A 2023 survey found 
83.6% of respondents reported an increase in the number of students requiring developmental
 support, based on 
121 primary school teachers
 — a small, non-representative sample. This is a *perception* data point, not a national administrative count, and should be marked as suggestive rather than confirmatory.

**3. Diagnostic authority is legally concentrated in expert committees (szakértői bizottságok) that require parental initiation or consent, creating specific institutional veto/gatekeeper points relevant to any reform.**
Under current rules, 
this need is in every case determined or excluded exclusively by the expert committee, which in its expert opinion proposes the care, method, form and place of care appropriate to the special treatment need
. Investigation cannot generally proceed without parental agreement: 
parental consent is always required to initiate the investigation
. *Inference: because both diagnosis and mandatory periodic review are procedurally anchored to parent/school-initiated referrals reviewed by county- or district-level expert panels, reform coalitions seeking to change identification rates must work through this specific administrative layer — it is not solely a ministerial policy lever, and reforming it requires coordinating parents, schools, and committees simultaneously, raising the number of veto players.*

**4. The diagnostic/support infrastructure was centralized from municipalities to the state in 2012–13, meaning control over this domain sits with central government, not local authorities.**

Under Act CXC of 2011 on National Public Education, which came into force in September 2012, institutions involved in SEN diagnosis service provision were organised into regional networks (pedagogical assistance services). The state became the operator of these institutions and services, instead of municipalities. The service capacity was revised and restructured, aiming to operate a coherent system and provide more stable access to services.
 *Inference: this centralization means any coalition seeking to alter identification practice, funding formulas, or committee composition must engage central government structures directly, since local school boards and municipalities have no independent authority over the assessment network.*

**5. Education policy lacks a dedicated cabinet-level ministry, which unions and observers cite as a structural marker of low political priority for the sector, including specialist provision.**
A teachers' union representative stated that 
the lack of a separate education ministry is symbolic of the government's "criminal neglect" of the sector
, and reporting noted that 
the centralised management of schools also leaves school directors with limited autonomy to improve teaching quality... as part of sweeping reforms since Orban returned to power in 2010, locally-run schools were nationalised with a central authority controlling textbooks that teachers must strictly follow
. This is a contested framing (union perspective) but the institutional fact — education administered within the Interior Ministry portfolio rather than a standalone ministry — is corroborated by negotiation records identifying the government interlocutor as 
Zoltán Maruzsa, Secretary of State for Public Education at the Interior Ministry
.

**6. Specialist workforce capacity is strained: demand for special-education staff has grown, but supply constraints and geographic unevenness persist, and the overall teaching profession faces an acute shortage crisis that indirectly limits SEN service delivery.**
An EU country fiche notes 
more than 60% of participating schools stated that they need more special education teachers, psychologists, or pedagogical assistants... The number of special education teachers (and conductors), and psychologists employed in public education institutions has doubled from 2010
, while in at least one county 
the average is 39.6 students per special education teacher
 — indicating uneven capacity despite aggregate growth. Separately, the general teacher labor market was described as follows: 
Hungary is already in the grip of a chronic teacher shortage, with few young people joining the profession and 40 percent of teachers aged over 50
, and 
the education system is already short 16,000 teachers
 as of 2023. *Inference: any policy requiring expanded specialist staffing (e.g., more SEN/BTMN support personnel) competes directly with an already-acute, politically inflamed general staffing crisis — meaning workforce-dependent proposals are hostage to the broader teacher-labor dispute rather than solvable as a standalone SEN measure.*

**7. The 2022–2023 teacher mobilization and the subsequent 2023 "Status Law" illustrate that the government has both the capacity and demonstrated willingness to unilaterally restructure teacher working conditions over union objection, which is directly relevant to reversal risk for any workforce-dependent SEN reform.**
Following sustained protest, 
lawmakers in Hungary passed a controversial bill on 4 July 2023 affecting the country's teachers... voted without adequate consultation with the trade unions... considered a retaliation as it cements a regime in public education that has caused months of protests and strikes
. Provisions included 
a unified legal status law that contains extremely disadvantageous provisions for everyone covered by it, including those currently covered by the Labor Code
, and 
instead of trying to win over these teachers and improve their working conditions, the government wants to transfer teachers to other schools to cover the teacher shortage... employers such as school district centres can change an educator's place of work at any time
. A separate decree was described as making 
teachers' strike almost impossible and pointless
. *Inference: this establishes a template — the government can and has legislated over union/parent/student objection using its parliamentary majority and administrative decree powers, meaning any negotiated, union-endorsed SEN workforce reform is structurally exposed to unilateral reversal or reshaping by the executive at any point, independent of stakeholder consensus.*

**8. Roma over-identification/misdiagnosis as a driver of segregated placement is the subject of sustained, unresolved domestic and international litigation, giving rights-based NGOs and courts (rather than the education ministry) an alternative, semi-independent lever — but one the government has resisted implementing in substance.**
Historically, 
the EC criticized the unjustified qualification of large numbers of Roma children as disabled and the existence of segregated church schools
 as part of its infringement rationale. Court rulings continue: 
the European Court of Human Rights has issued a judgment against Hungary for the racial segregation of Romani children... the Court found that his right to education had been violated in conjunction with his right to non-discrimination... Hungary must desegregate the school and pay Imre Szolcsán €7000 in damages
 (March 2023). Yet compliance monitoring shows persistent non-implementation: 
the Committee of Ministers monitoring the implementation of the judgment has not been able to close the case, because the Hungarian government has not been able to convincingly demonstrate that it has been able to eliminate the discriminatory practices
, and as of 2024, 
this research conclusively demonstrates that the education system in Hungary continues to fail many of its Romani children
. The European Commission's 2016 infringement proceeding remains formally open with no public resolution: 
in 2016, the European Commission launched infringement proceedings against Hungary for the segregation of Romani children in schools. As yet there is no publicly available information on the status of the Commission's investigation
. Government rhetoric has been openly hostile to the desegregation framing, with the Prime Minister reportedly 
declar[ing] that the court's decision "violated the people's sense of justice"... "it's unacceptable for a majority to feel ill at ease in their own towns, or country, and it won't happen while I am premier"
 (contested/politically charged source — ERRC advocacy site — presented as reported claim, not verified neutral fact).

**9. Broader EU financial leverage over Hungary exists but has not been specifically or consistently deployed on the SEN/segregation dossier, and its use elsewhere has been criticized as politically transactional — indicating that EU conditionality is an uncertain, potentially reversible source of external pressure rather than a reliable lever for any particular education reform.**
At the aggregate rule-of-law level, 
the Commission suspended a total of €32 billion to Hungary, equalling roughly 16 per cent of its GDP
 across mechanisms, but 
the Commission released €10.2 billion in cohesion funds following judicial reforms in December 2023. The decision coincided with Budapest lifting its threat to veto funding for Ukraine and many, including the European Parliament, criticised it as untransparent and 'political'
. As of Q4 2025, Hungarian civil-society monitors conclude 
the Hungarian government has not taken adequate steps in order to fully address the rule of law and human rights concerns raised... there are areas where no progress has been made at all
. *Inference: since fund releases have historically tracked geopolitical bargaining (e.g., Ukraine aid) rather than sector-specific compliance, any expectation that EU conditionality will force change specifically in SEN/segregation practice should be treated as uncertain; the mechanism is real but its application to this dossier is neither dedicated nor predictable.*

**10. School-choice/catchment-area discretion — a mechanism directly relevant to the "parental mobility reshaping school composition" concern in the problem statement — is documented as asymmetrically applied, and church-run schools (a politically protected sector under the current governing coalition) have been separately flagged by the European Commission as part of the segregation pattern.**
Hungarian law grants schools discretion over out-of-catchment admission, and this discretion has been litigated: 
the boy's mother wanted him to study at a non-segregated school... the other school refused to enrol him, stating that he did not live in the school's catchment area. While that was true, schools in Hungary have the discretion to enrol a child from outside the catchment area
 — meaning the same discretionary tool can be used either to admit or to exclude, depending on the school's institutional incentives. Separately, the Commission's original infringement rationale explicitly named 
the existence of segregated church schools
 as a contributing factor. *Inference: because church-run and foundation schools are a politically aligned and expanding sector under the current government (a structural feature not itself contested here, but consistent with the sources' framing of protected providers), any reform touching admissions discretion or cross-sector composition effects would need to engage a provider coalition (state, church, private/foundation schools) with divergent incentives — the 2023 status law explicitly extended new unified rules to all provider types "regardless of the type of provider (state, church, private or foundation)," per 
the government's plans, from 1 June 2023, all employees in publicly accredited education regardless of the type of provider... would become subject to a unified legal status law
, indicating the government is willing to legislate across provider lines when it serves its own workforce-control objectives, but has not shown comparable willingness to legislate uniformly on segregation/placement practice.*

### Summary of coalition and reversal-risk implications (inference, not recommendation)

- **Who can enact:** Central government (via the Interior Ministry's education portfolio and parliamentary majority) holds near-exclusive formal authority over the diagnostic infrastructure, funding rules, and workforce conditions, having centralized these functions since 2012–13 and demonstrated willingness to legislate unilaterally (2023 status law) even against union and street-protest opposition.
- **Who can pressure but not enact directly:** Teacher unions (PSZ, PDSZ) have shown mobilizational capacity (mass 2022–2023 protests) but limited bargaining leverage, given restricted strike rights and a demonstrated government pattern of legislating around negotiations. Rights NGOs (ERRC, Chance for Children Foundation, TASZ) and courts (domestic and ECtHR) have secured individual rulings but face chronic non-implementation. The European Commission holds financial leverage in principle but has applied it inconsistently and, on this specific dossier, has left the 2016 infringement procedure unresolved for nearly a decade.
- **Reversal exposure:** Any reform dependent on negotiated union consent, sustained EU infringement pressure, or voluntary provider-sector (especially church-school) cooperation appears highly exposed to strategic delay, unilateral legislative override, or trade-off against unrelated geopolitical bargaining (as occurred with the December 2023 fund release tied to Ukraine-aid negotiations).
