# Research Notes: SEN Identification Trends and School-Network Delivery Capacity in Hungary

*Disciplinary lens: education infrastructure and school-network planning. Facts are separated from inference; contested claims are flagged.*

## 1. The scale and trajectory of recorded SEN identification is well-documented but definitional scope varies by source

National statistics confirm a rising trend, though absolute figures differ depending on whether counts cover general schools only or the full system (kindergarten–secondary/vocational). 
In the 2025/2026 school year, the number of children with special educational needs (SNI) in Hungary continued to rise, exceeding 69,000, according to a recent KSH (Central Statistical Office) Helyzetkép survey.
 A broader count covering all education levels gives a much higher figure: 
the number of SNI students rose to nearly 120,000 in the 2025/2026 school year across public education and vocational training.
 A narrower prior-year figure: 
preliminary data show 112,007 SNI children in kindergarten, primary and secondary education this year, up from 107,421 in the same period the previous year.
 **Fact, with a definitional caveat**: different reports use different denominators (general-school-only vs. full system), so "the number of SNI children" is not a single consistent metric across sources.

## 2. Longer-run trend and category-specific growth (autism) are large but partly attributable to diagnostic change


KSH data show a 40% increase in the number of SNI students in Hungary over the past 20 years.
 Autism identification has grown especially sharply: 
the number of children diagnosed with autism spectrum disorder (ASD) rose from just 30 registered students in 2001, to 3,319 by 2013, and to 9,737 by 2023
 — 
a threefold increase in autistic children in education over ten years.
 **Inference flagged in sources**: 
experts state the surge does not mean solely that more children "became" neurodivergent — the increase reflects complex causes, including improved diagnostic methods that can identify autism and other developmental disorders around age two, with diagnostic tools becoming more accurate and widespread.
 This is an interpretive claim, not a settled causal finding.

## 3. A specific policy change (school-readiness/deferral rules) is a plausible but contested confound for the 2021+ acceleration


The increase may also be linked to the government's January 2020 tightening of rules on school deferral and school-readiness assessment, which reportedly led many six-year-olds to enter school while still developmentally immature instead of receiving an extra kindergarten year.
 Deferral requests dropped sharply: 
the number of deferral applications fell to 16,400 by 2021, after a bureaucratic procedure requiring parents to obtain more documents and children to undergo assessments centralized the process through the Educational Authority (OH).
 
KSH data are cited as supporting a link between the stricter rules and SEN growth, showing SNI numbers rising markedly faster from the 2021/2022 school year onward.
 **This is a contested causal claim** attributed to advocacy group interpretation (Szülői Hang) and journalistic synthesis of KSH time series, not an official causal attribution by KSH itself.

## 4. Severe geographic disparity in recorded prevalence exists and is itself ambiguous evidence


The highest rate was measured in Békés county, where 15.6% of students are SNI, while the lowest rate is in Borsod-Abaúj-Zemplén county, at 6.2%.
 Crucially, researchers caution this gap is not straightforwardly interpretable: 
the difference might suggest Békés simply has far more affected children, but education researchers argue the situation is more complex — a lower rate does not necessarily mean fewer SNI children, but may instead reflect how well or poorly the diagnostic and support system is functioning.
 This directly bears on geographic delivery capacity: low recorded prevalence in some counties may indicate under-identification due to service-access gaps, not lower underlying need.

## 5. A documented supply-side cause of regional disparity: absence of specialist teacher training pipeline in the northeast


Northeast Hungary had no special-education-teacher (gyógypedagógus) training program until 2017, which may have worsened access in these counties.
 Additional sub-national research corroborates a weak northern service base: 
a 2009 study on the early-intervention system found substantial inequalities in how quickly children reach diagnosis and development services, with researchers noting the service system is particularly weak in Northern Hungary regions, where long waiting lists have formed in several locations.
 This is a structural, geography-linked workforce-pipeline constraint distinct from diagnostic-practice explanations.

## 6. National specialist workforce numbers are reported, but their adequacy relative to (rapidly rising) caseload is an open, contested question


Currently 4,900 special-education/conductor-role teachers work in general schools to support SNI students' development, plus 949 developmental teachers, and a further 1,404 specialists in the traveling special-education-teacher and conductor network.
 Practitioner-side sources describe strain rather than adequacy: 
a petition from staff at pedagogical service institutions warned that while paperwork increases, less help can be offered to children; well-trained support staff are leaving Budapest's Metropolitan Pedagogical Service institutions, and waiting lists and remaining staff workloads are growing in parallel.
 Whether the national specialist counts are sufficient relative to the ~69,000–120,000 identified children is not established in these sources; it is an open capacity question, not a settled fact.

## 7. Assessment-system capacity constraints (waiting lists, uneven committee practice) are documented as an access bottleneck, particularly for disadvantaged families


Expert committees (szakértői bizottságok) may operate unevenly across regions, and children of multiply disadvantaged parents reach these committees later.
 A related 2023 study finding: 
children born into disadvantaged families are more likely to have a developmental problem remain hidden, or if identified, not receive development services.
 Separately, 
nationally the share of SNI status is now 9.7% of daytime primary students, with data showing not only rising diagnosed numbers but large regional disparities.
 Advocacy-sector estimate: 
roughly 4,000 more SNI students in 2025/2026 than the previous year
, and separately 
official figures cover only students with a formal expert opinion; it is presumed additional children in the system may have SEN but have not yet undergone the required assessments or lack an official diagnosis.
 This "hidden/unassessed" population is an inference, not a measured figure, and represents unquantified latent demand on the assessment and delivery network.

## 8. A formal transport-support entitlement exists, but is assessment-and-voucher-dependent, implying delivery reach is gated by prior successful diagnostic access


SNI-status children, students, and up to two accompanying persons are entitled to free intercity travel between home and institution for early development, preparatory development, special-education or mainstream institutions, contingent on a travel voucher issued by the Pedagogical Service's Expert Committee.
 This means transport-access entitlements are activated only after a child clears the assessment bottleneck described in Finding 7 — geographic/transport support does not independently solve access if diagnostic throughput is constrained.

## 9. Institutional delivery model relies on itinerant/traveling specialist networks whose coverage varies widely by locality size and funding, creating capacity heterogeneity

Coverage examples from specific traveling-specialist networks illustrate wide variance in scale: one district network grew 
from serving only one or two kindergartens/schools and a few children to now covering more than thirty institutions and over two hundred children
; another regional network reports 
expanding from 3 kindergartens and 10 schools with 68 SNI children served by 10 special-education teachers to over fifty institutions and an average of 350 children, now served by nearly 60 traveling special-education teachers and conductors
. Some networks explicitly cannot cover certain disability types locally: 
a district network cannot take on care for sensory-disabled children due to lack of appropriately qualified specialists, and this specialist care is instead provided by other EGYMI institutions
 — indicating that even where a traveling network exists, category-specific specialist coverage (e.g., sensory disabilities) may require cross-institutional/cross-district referral, implying additional travel or capacity dependency for specific SEN subtypes.

## 10. Parental school-choice/mobility responses to rising SEN and disadvantaged-pupil concentration are documented as a segregation mechanism, though direct causal studies specifically on SEN-driven (not only Roma/socioeconomic) mobility are sparser

Broader segregation research (not always SEN-specific) documents selection dynamics relevant to the "mobility reshapes composition" question: 
Hungary shows extreme school segregation in small settlements, with some schools where the share of disadvantaged children is barely 20%, while other schools in the same area reach 80% disadvantaged pupils
, and 
this selection is often shaped by covert admissions practices or by church-run schools attracting higher-status families.
 One source frames a "white flight" dynamic explicitly including SEN status: 
advocates for ending free school choice argue it enables higher-status parents to remove their children from schools once the share of Roma, poor, or SNI children crosses a critical threshold (roughly 20–40%).
 **Caveat**: this is an advocacy/opinion-piece claim about a mechanism and threshold, not a cited empirical study result in the retrieved material — it should be treated as a contested hypothesis requiring separate verification, not an established finding.

### Summary of open/contested points for further verification
- Whether rising identification reflects true prevalence change, diagnostic-practice change, policy-induced artifacts (school-readiness rule change), or assessment-access differentials remains analytically unresolved in the sources; multiple explanations coexist and are not weighted against each other in available reporting.
- Regional prevalence gaps (Békés vs. Borsod-Abaúj-Zemplén) are explicitly flagged by researchers as possibly reflecting service-system quality/access rather than true need differences — this cautions against treating county-level SEN rates as a direct geographic-demand map for network planning.
- No source in this search provided a systematic, quantified national map of specialist-to-pupil ratios by district/county, nor a rigorous causal study isolating SEN-status-driven parental mobility from broader socioeconomic/ethnic segregation dynamics; these remain gaps for further targeted research.
