# Research Notes: Hungary SEN Identification Trend — Whole-System Reform Analysis

## 1. The headline trend is documented, but magnitude estimates vary by source and period

Between 2010 and 2020, the number of students with SEN in primary education increased from 6.7% to 7.7%, while in secondary education this rose from 3.9% to 6.2%, displaying a significant increase.
 Separately, 
according to data from the Hungarian Central Statistical Office, the number of Hungarian children and pupils with special educational needs has increased 1.6-fold over the past 20 years.
 **Fact, but not fully reconciled**: these are different metrics (share vs. absolute multiplier) from different reference periods; no single source in this search set gives a continuous, harmonized time series across the full 20+ years.

## 2. There is a documented historical precedent of definitional change driving apparent surges, independent of prevalence

A 2003 amendment of Act LXXIX of 1993 on Public Education extended the national categories of disability, including children with autism and children with learning difficulties due to other psychiatric developmental disorders.
 Following this, 
the number of learners with SEN due to other psychological developmental disorders increased fivefold over a five-year period, with significant differences found across counties' statistics
, which was severe enough that 
the rules pertaining to diagnosis were amended and the population concerned was fully reviewed in 2007.
 Separately, 
from the 2012–13 academic year, the Act on Public Education again revised the definition of special educational needs.
 **Fact**: category redefinition is a demonstrated, non-trivial driver of measured caseload change in Hungary, distinct from underlying prevalence.

## 3. Academic explanations for the current rise emphasize diagnostic/awareness improvement — this is authors' interpretation, not verified causal attribution

One account attributes the rise partly to greater awareness of developmental disorders in media and education, leading parents and teachers to notice signs earlier, alongside improved diagnostics resulting in more timely and accurate diagnoses.
 **Inference/contested**: this is a plausible interpretive claim from qualitative interview-based research (21 teacher interviews in one underdeveloped eastern region), not a population-level causal test distinguishing awareness/diagnostic-practice effects from true prevalence change or from incentive-driven over-identification (see #6-7 below).

## 4. A UN human-rights review frames the rise as outpacing system capacity — an external assessment, not an independent statistical study
Following a 2025 country visit, 
UN Special Rapporteur Farida Shaheed stated that the growing number of children with SEN has not been matched by "adequate teacher training or specialist support," citing bigger classes as a challenge to accommodation and shortages of specialists such as doctors and sign language interpreters.
 **Fact (as an official finding)**, though this is a rapporteur's assessment rather than a quantified capacity audit.

## 5. Independent labor-market evidence corroborates a general (not SEN-specific) teacher/specialist capacity constraint

A 2022 EU country report identifies teacher shortage in Hungary as driven partly by low pay — one of the lowest in Europe — combined with the highest student-teacher ratio and, in the absence of sufficient support staff, teachers having to perform non-teaching duties.
 In response, 
Hungary committed in its recovery and resilience plan and EFOP Plus programme to raise teachers' salary to at least 80% of the average tertiary-educated worker's salary by 2025, and to raise pay in disadvantaged settlements by 12.5% from 2023.
 By 2025, 
a government decree raised teacher salaries by an average of 21.2% from January 2025, fulfilling the prior commitment that average teacher salary reach 80% of average graduate salary.
 **Fact**: system-wide (non-SEN-specific) capacity investment is underway, but no source quantifies whether this addresses the specialist (as opposed to generalist classroom-teacher) shortage cited by the UN rapporteur.

## 6. Evidence that SEN/special-class assessment functions, in part, as a segregation channel correlated with school ethnic composition — a contested but documented mechanism
2024 field research found that 
in segregated schools where Romani students exceed 75%, there is no institutional interest in removing them from the segregated school, so Romani children are often not sent for expert assessment because they are already ethnically segregated from other children
, whereas 
in schools where the proportion of Roma pupils is between 50–75%, children are more likely to be tested, to receive an SEN diagnosis, and to be referred to segregated special education classes.
 Additionally, 
socially deprived and uneducated parents were often unaware of their procedural rights during expert assessments, with no support personnel to help them understand the process.
 **Fact (documented pattern)**, though the study's geographic scope and sampling are not fully specified in the retrieved excerpt; this indicates SEN classification rates cannot be read as a pure prevalence signal in ethnically mixed catchment areas.

## 7. Historical finance-incentive evidence links special/segregated classification to school funding streams
Earlier ombudsman findings noted a school 
unlawfully applying for and receiving "supplementary ethnic funding" — a government grant intended to facilitate the development of Romani students
 while operating segregated classes, and a Roma leader criticized 
the system of supplementary allocation of funds to public schools for "catch-up classes," noting that pupils in such classes fall so far behind that by sixth grade it is impossible to integrate them into normal classes.
 Academic literature summarizing case law describes segregated education as 
largely characterized as "subpar education" and notably "incentivized by state funding."
 **Fact (historical/documented in litigation and ombudsman findings)**, though these sources are largely pre-2015; whether the same finance mechanisms operate identically under current (post-2020) funding rules is not established in this search.

## 8. Parental exit to private/church schools is documented and plausibly reshapes residual public-school composition, but a direct causal link to SEN identification rates is not established in sources found
Research describes that 
the deterioration of the public school system has led to a "moral panic" amongst more affluent middle-class parents, who lost trust in state-run institutions and increasingly opt for private or denominational schools
, and that 
the number of church-run educational institutions has doubled since 2010.
 A 2025 UN assessment separately warned that 
as more schools go private, particularly in remote areas, public schools will be left with fewer resources and a higher concentration of disadvantaged students, leading to increased segregation.
 Comparative data (dated 2017) placed 
Hungary among countries with the highest social segregation in schools, alongside the UAE, Peru, Israel, Indonesia, Qatar and Chile — much higher than in Japan, Korea, Hong Kong, Taiwan, Finland, Poland and New Zealand.
 **Inference flagged**: these sources document parental sorting and rising compositional segregation as parallel trends to SEN identification growth, but none of the retrieved sources directly quantifies whether SEN/BTMN reclassification is a *cause* or *consequence* of this sorting, or merely co-occurring.

## 9. An existing "soft instrument" support infrastructure predates the current identification rise and was designed for integration rather than segregation
Since the early 2000s, 
Hungary transformed former special institutions into unified special educational/conductive-pedagogical methodological resource centres (EGYMI), which now provide both special educational and integrational services, aiming to help mainstream schools include pupils with special needs and to support mainstream teachers with inclusive attitudes, methodologies and techniques.
 This sits alongside a documented historical policy shift: 
until 2005, disadvantaged/Roma-inclusion policy operated mainly with "soft instruments" — development programs and financial incentives aimed at improving school capacity — rather than structural rules.
 **Fact**: a non-structural, capacity-building support architecture already exists; its adequacy relative to the current caseload increase is asserted as insufficient by the UN rapporteur (#4) but not independently quantified in these sources.

## 10. Official government strategy documents frame SEN and disadvantage jointly as a named 2021–2030 target area, but implementation evidence is limited

Comprehensive interventions to improve education outcomes and increase the participation of vulnerable student groups — including socially disadvantaged students and children/students with SEN — are part of the Public Education Strategy 2021–2030 introduced by the Hungarian government in 2020.
 **Fact of stated policy intent**; no retrieved source provides an interim (2021–2026) evaluation of outcomes against this strategy's SEN/disadvantage-integration targets, so its effectiveness remains an open empirical question.
