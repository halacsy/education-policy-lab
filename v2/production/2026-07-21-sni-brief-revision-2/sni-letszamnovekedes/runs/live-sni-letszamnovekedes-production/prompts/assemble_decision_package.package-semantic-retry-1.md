TRANSFORMATIONS:
TP-live-t1 — Passive baseline: no new SEN policy, observe demographic and system drift
Goal: Establish a rigorous counterfactual describing what happens to identification rates, capacity strain, and inequality if no policy changes are made to identification, gatekeeping, finance, or placement rules

TP-live-t2 — Build a reconciled national SEN measurement and monitoring architecture
Goal: Establish a single, harmonized, publicly auditable statistical series and monitoring framework that disentangles prevalence change from diagnostic-practice, access, and incentive effects across SNI, BTMN, and adjacent categories
Mechanisms: Reconciliation of divergent existing series (e.g., 92,800→97,300 vs. 112,060 vs. 69,000 figures using different bases) into one definitionally consistent longitudinal dataset, as the current fragmentation is documented across multiple independent findings; Decomposition analysis separating the contribution of school-readiness rule changes, category redefinition (e.g., BTMN grading-exemption reform), and diagnostic-practice shifts from true prevalence change, using natural policy variation such as the 2018 and 2020 rule changes as identification points
Evidence: strong
Risks: Without political commitment to act on findings, reconciled data alone may not change outcomes; Data reconciliation could reveal politically inconvenient patterns (e.g., incentive-driven over-identification) that face resistance to publication

TP-live-t3 — Transition toward a non-categorical, tiered support model with restructured gatekeeping
Goal: Redesign the SNI/BTMN category architecture and expert-committee gatekeeping process to reduce waiting-list bottlenecks, definitional volatility, and diagnosis-dependent access to support, informed by transferability-tested comparative models
Mechanisms: Adoption of a tiered support structure decoupling access to support from formal categorical labeling, modeled on international evidence that non-categorical or tiered frameworks materially change measured identification rates (e.g., Wales' ALN transition and Finland's three-tier model); Restructuring or augmenting the sole nationwide expert-committee gatekeeper to address documented multi-month waiting lists, informed by evidence that gatekeeping structure itself shapes measured prevalence independent of true need
Evidence: moderate
Risks: Comparative evidence shows non-categorical/tiered reforms produce large persistent regional variation even after redesign (Finland's 4%–13% municipal range), so redesign alone may not resolve Hungary's county-level disparities; Poland's experience shows rapid, non-consensual structural reform can produce severe implementation disruption and stakeholder backlash

TP-live-t4 — Shift SEN finance architecture from diagnosis-gated to needs/census-based allocation
Goal: Reduce identification-linked fiscal incentives by redesigning per-pupil funding multipliers and normative financing so that support resources are not contingent on formal diagnostic classification
Mechanisms: Replacement or supplementation of diagnosis-triggered per-pupil multipliers with census-based or population-level allocation formulas, following the evidenced pattern that census-based systems remove fiscal incentives to over-identify while non-census systems correlate with rising identification; Introduction of stricter accountability requirements tying supplementary SNI grants to documented local service delivery rather than headcount alone, addressing the finding that weak conditionality currently limits the grants' effectiveness
Evidence: moderate
Risks: No causal evidence currently links Hungary's specific financing mechanism to the documented identification rise, so the intervention rests on a plausible but untested incentive theory; Poland's contrasting experience shows disability-weighted per-pupil funding was expanded rather than removed, with subventions rising independent of prevalence, suggesting funding redesign choices are not universally convergent

TP-live-t5 — National specialist workforce and support-capacity expansion program
Goal: Close the gap between rising numbers of identified and assessed children and the specialist workforce and support infrastructure available to serve them, with priority targeting of underserved regions
Mechanisms: Regionally targeted recruitment and retention incentives for gyógypedagógus positions in underserved areas (e.g., northeastern counties with zero specialists in some districts), addressing the documented mismatch between rising enrollment and practicing workforce supply; Investment in co-teaching and traveling specialist-network infrastructure modeled on evidenced capacity gaps, alongside monitoring of whether assessed children actually receive legally prescribed habilitation and therapy hours
Evidence: strong
Risks: Rising enrollment in special-education training does not guarantee proportional increase in practicing workforce, given documented low completion/entry rates; Broader teacher salary reforms may not specifically address specialist shortages if not targeted, since no source quantifies whether general raises reach specialist supply

TP-live-t6 — Coordinated anti-segregation and adjacent-policy alignment package
Goal: Address how SEN/BTMN identification interacts with free school choice, institutional selectivity, and adjacent non-SEN policies (school-readiness rules, rural network structure, private/church growth) to reshape school composition and deepen inequality, while meeting outstanding legal obligations
Mechanisms: Extension of anti-segregation safeguards to cover free school choice and selective gimnázium tracks, directly responding to documented evidence that these channels remain structurally unaddressed and that school-switching behavior independently alters school ethnic composition; Establishment of a cross-domain coordination mechanism reviewing how school-readiness/entry-age rules, rural school-network consolidation decisions, and private/church school growth interact with SEN identification and segregation, using documented precedent that the 2020 readiness rule change measurably affected SNI growth as a model for anticipatory impact assessment
Evidence: strong
Risks: Historical precedent (Bridge classes, unresolved ECtHR compliance since 2013/2023) suggests strong institutional resistance to structural anti-segregation reform; Private/church school growth driven by middle-class exit from public schools may continue independent of policy coordination, as documented trust-erosion dynamics are not easily reversed by administrative review alone

Continued non-compliance carries reversal/escalation risk in the form of further legal and financial sanction, and the EU's demonstrated willingness to link fund releases to political bargains (F-live-political_feasibility-10) suggests non-action is not cost-free even if administratively convenient.

DILEMMAS:
- Diagnostic visibility versus classification-linked access: Building a reconciled measurement architecture (TP-live-t2) can reveal how much identification growth reflects diagnostic-practice, incentive, or definitional artifacts rather than true need, but by itself it leaves the sole gatekeeper and categorical entitlement structure untouched, meaning transparency does not translate into changed access unless paired with structural or finance reform.
- Structural gatekeeping redesign versus disruption risk: Transitioning to a non-categorical, tiered model (TP-live-t3) targets a documented bottleneck (sole nationwide gatekeeper, multi-month waiting lists) and could reduce diagnosis-dependent access barriers, but comparative evidence from Poland shows rapid, non-consensual structural reform can produce severe implementation crises and stakeholder backlash, while Finland's experience shows persistent regional variation survives even successful reform.
- Finance-incentive reform: theory-driven change versus unproven causal mechanism: Shifting from diagnosis-gated to census-based finance (TP-live-t4) rests on the plausible theory that fiscal incentives drive over-identification, but no causal evidence links Hungary's specific financing mechanism to its identification rise, and Poland's contrasting experience of expanding rather than removing disability-weighted funding while separately managing gatekeeping shows the theorized link is not universal.
- Workforce expansion targeting versus broad reform sufficiency: Targeted specialist workforce expansion (TP-live-t5) directly addresses documented regional shortages and is broadly politically uncontroversial, but rising training enrollment does not reliably convert into practicing specialists, and general salary reforms may not reach specialist supply, raising the question of whether targeted capacity investment alone can close access gaps without deeper organizational or entitlement change.
- Anti-segregation coordination versus institutional and maintainer autonomy: Extending anti-segregation safeguards to free school choice and selective gimnázium tracks (TP-live-t6) directly targets documented unaddressed sorting mechanisms and responds to unresolved ECtHR/EU obligations, but doing so requires new legal authority over admissions and provider autonomy (public, private, church), domains with a decade-plus record of institutional resistance and low likelihood of durable enactment absent external enforcement.
- Passive baseline: analytical counterfactual versus de facto policy of drift: A passive baseline (TP-live-t1) is proposed as a rigorous counterfactual to isolate the effects of demographic and system drift, but multiple lenses argue that inaction is not neutral because it allows compounding legal exposure, capacity strain, and sorting mechanisms to continue unaddressed, raising the question of whether deliberate non-intervention is a legitimate policy stance or an implicit acceptance of continued harm.
- Non-categorical reform transferability: mechanism transfer versus context-dependent infrastructure: TP-live-t3 draws on Wales' and Finland's non-categorical/tiered models as evidence that redesigning categories and gatekeeping can shift measured identification, but Finland's model depends on dual-teacher/SET co-teaching infrastructure that Hungary currently lacks, raising an open question about whether the mechanism is transferable without first building preconditions that TP-live-t5 targets.

RESEARCH AGENDA:
- What is the relative causal contribution of the 2020 school-readiness rule change, the 2017-18 BTMN/SNI legal reform, per-pupil funding incentives, diagnostic-practice evolution, and true prevalence increase to Hungary's documented SNI/BTMN identification growth over 20 years, including the sharper post-2021/22 acceleration and threefold autism rise?
- Does regional variation in Hungary's SNI/BTMN prevalence (5.4%-14% across counties, e.g., Békés vs Borsod-Abaúj-Zemplén) reflect genuine population differences, or differences in diagnostic/administrative capacity, expert-committee practice, and specialist availability?
- What is the actual specialist-teacher-to-SNI-pupil ratio by district and county, how large is the unmet/undiagnosed ('hidden') SEN population, and what magnitude of unmet prescribed habilitation/therapy hours exists by school type and region?
- To what extent does Hungary's per-capita SNI funding multiplier (2x-3x) create a measurable fiscal incentive for schools to classify marginal/borderline students as SNI, and does identification vary systematically with school financial capacity, enrollment decline, and size?
- If a Finland-style tiered support model were introduced in Hungary, would the new middle tier capture genuinely unmet need, or would it primarily relabel/reclassify students already served in mainstream or top-tier settings, and would county-level disparities persist under the new architecture?
- Are discriminatory misclassification patterns affecting Roma children statistically distinguishable from general over-identification trends, and what is the geographic/demographic overlap between high-SEN-identification zones and majority-Roma catchment areas?
- Will the January 2025 teacher salary reform and rising special-education training enrollments translate into increased practicing specialist supply, particularly in underserved regions, or does the training pipeline overstate future workforce availability?
- Does SNI/BTMN classification status itself operate as a sorting variable driving parental school mobility under Hungary's free-choice regime, amplifying segregation beyond baseline school-choice effects, and would extending anti-segregation law to free choice be legally and politically sustainable?
- Should Hungary prioritize structural reform (categorical/gatekeeping redesign) over incremental measures (measurement, workforce, finance) given documented implementation risks from Poland's disruptive 2016-2017 reform experience, or does phased sequencing reduce this risk sufficiently to justify structural change now?
- What is the current specialist capacity gap and would restructuring the 2013 KLIK-centralized service model reverse the documented shift of specialist time from therapy toward assessment, without requiring additional total staffing?
