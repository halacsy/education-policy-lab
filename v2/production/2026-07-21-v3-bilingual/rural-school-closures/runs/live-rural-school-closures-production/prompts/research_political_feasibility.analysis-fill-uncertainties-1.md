# Research Notes: Political Economy of Small Rural School Policy in Hungary

*Scope: coalition dynamics and reversal/resistance exposure. Facts and inferences are labeled; contested evidence is flagged.*

---

**1. Decision authority was centralized away from municipalities in 2013 (FACT).**

2013 was a major turning point in Hungarian public education: the maintenance of schools, previously under municipal control, was transferred to state hands
. 
From January 1, 2013, task-based financing replaced normative financing, and maintenance duties—except for kindergarten education—were assumed by the state through the Klebelsberg Institution Maintenance Center (KLIK)
. *Inference:* This means any pro-closure or pro-preservation coalition must now work primarily through central-government/tankerület (district) channels rather than local councils, which retain only limited "operator" (not maintainer) functions above a 3,000-population threshold.

**2. The scale of the underlying demographic problem is large and geographically concentrated (FACT).**

The population of every fifth locality in Hungary faces a slow decline into complete depopulation; if there is no significant change, 147 localities will eventually be completely depopulated
. 
645 localities have populations below 300, and 114 of those have under-18 populations below 10 percent; GKI considers it unlikely these villages will survive without significant development programs
. This scale suggests any "keep-schools-open" coalition faces a structurally shrinking pupil base, not a temporary dip.

**3. Church transfer is an established alternative pathway to closure, not neutral (FACT + contested normative dimension).**
A recurring institutional option is transferring a threatened municipal school to church maintenance rather than closing it. 
More than fifty municipal schools transferred to church authority in one academic year alone; the education state secretariat reported roughly 83 schools nationally taken over by churches
. 
Regional data compiled by the Teachers' Union showed the transfer wave hit Alföld and Northern Hungary counties hardest, while Transdanubia was barely affected
—i.e., concentrated in poorer, more depopulating regions. This creates a durable coalition (state + churches) offering an alternative to closure, but one contested by secular-neutrality advocates: 
questions arise about who can decide on the transfer, what special rules apply for those remaining in the school, and what options exist for those who insist on religiously and ideologically neutral education
. One historical case shows forced religious practice following such transfers, per contemporaneous reporting on Kecel.

**4. A parallel, independent constraint—teacher shortage—limits any policy option regardless of maintainer (FACT).**

Citing low pay, increasing workload, and burnout, thousands of Hungarian teachers are leaving the profession, and very few recent graduates are choosing to enter it
. 
An average of 6,200 teachers are projected to retire each year between 2023 and 2030, while student numbers will barely decrease, so there will be no one left to teach them
. *Inference:* Even a coalition that successfully blocks closures may be unable to sustain staffing in small rural schools, making "keep-open" victories potentially hollow/self-reversing through attrition rather than formal decree.

**5. Teacher-union bargaining power was weakened just before/during this period, reducing one channel for contesting closures (FACT).**

Lawmakers passed a controversial bill on 4 July 2023 affecting teachers, with education unions saying thousands would leave the profession; the bill was voted without adequate consultation with the trade unions
. 
From 1 January 2024, all teachers were shifted from public-servant/employee status to "school education employee" status, with new employment rules
. *Inference:* A union-based coalition to resist or negotiate around closures is structurally weaker post-2023 than in earlier periods (e.g., 2007 mobilization).

**6. Civil-society mobilization has historically been able to slow closures at specific sites, but is localized and resource-intensive (FACT).**

Following top-down pressure for rural school closures/mergers in 2007 that attracted media attention, the NGO "Human Chain" campaigned against closures under "Human Chain for Rural Schools," directing attention to small schools that survived
. 
The Erdőkürt school was studied specifically because of villagers' remarkably strong community engagement, raising the question of whether strong local community can save a school
. *Inference:* This suggests successful preservation coalitions require intense, site-specific civic capital—not a generalizable, scalable strategy—making country-wide "no closures" policy hard to sustain uniformly.

**7. Courts can override local political preference and force closure on desegregation (not demographic) grounds—creating a competing coalition dynamic (FACT).**

A 2016 court ruling was notable for not only finding illegality but ordering the phase-out closure of a segregated school branch—the first such concrete deseg­regation order in Hungary based on expert testimony
. Critically, local political actors actively resisted such orders: 
the municipal government led by mayor Szita Károly intensively obstructed desegregation despite the final court ruling, having secured support from local Roma representatives with good relations to power for keeping the segregated school
. *Inference:* This shows that even judicially mandated closures face durable local political resistance and strategic delay (the underlying case dates to 2010 with implementation still contested years later), indicating that "closure" and "keep-open" coalitions are not simply demographic-efficiency vs. community-identity, but can be cross-cut by ethnic/segregation politics with their own entrenched local patronage networks.

**8. International evidence on whether school closure causes further depopulation is contested (CONTESTED, not Hungary-specific).**

A Finnish study using DID regression on 2010–2018 data found that after school closure, the population of the surrounding community decreased more than before closure
. However, 
other studies—Barakat (2015) in Saxony and Amcoff (2012) in Sweden—find no significant connection between school closure and population decline
, and 
a Czech study found that while residents in villages with a school consider it difficult to dispense with, only 17% of residents in municipalities without an elementary school consider its absence a major shortcoming
. *Inference:* Because the causal evidence is genuinely mixed across countries, either side of the Hungarian debate can selectively cite supporting studies, weakening the ability of "hard evidence" alone to force policy consensus.

**9. Government rural-development spending appears to follow or substitute for school closure rather than reliably prevent it (FACT + inference).**
In one documented village (Garab, ~44 residents), 
the mayor's office now occupies the space where the village school once stood, since the school had to close because there were no more children
. Simultaneously, 
Hungary's Village Funding Scheme (Magyar Falu Program) finances municipalities to avoid depopulation and improve rural living circumstances, and has provided 600 localities with 10 billion forints to develop communal spaces and establish community life
. *Inference:* This suggests the dominant government-sponsored coalition response to depopulation is repurposing physical infrastructure and community-building funding after closure, not preventing closure itself—an important signal about which policy track (preservation vs. adaptive reuse) actually commands resources.

**10. There are institutional signals of a possible—but uncertain—partial decentralization/de-rigidification track that could alter future coalition possibilities (FACT + explicit uncertainty in source).**
A Hungarian pedagogical-policy journal notes: 
Hungary now has an extremely centralized public education system, similar to Romania's, but there are signs of intent to loosen the system's rigidity, as indicated by the creation of central district (tankerület) councils; whether this reform moves toward re-municipalization is an open question, though the framework for it exists in principle
. *Inference:* This indicates the current centralized architecture is not necessarily fixed, but any reversal toward municipal authority (which would reopen local closure/preservation battles at the community level) remains speculative and unconfirmed as of the source's writing.
