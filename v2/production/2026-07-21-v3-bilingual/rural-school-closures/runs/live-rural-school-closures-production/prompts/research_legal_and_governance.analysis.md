# Research Notes: Hungarian Primary Schools in Depopulating Villages — Legal Authority & Governance

## 1. Central nationalization of school maintenance (2013) removed municipal decision authority over school existence

In 2011, the Hungarian parliament passed Act CXC on National Public Education, which laid the groundwork for extensive centralization by mandating state oversight of public school operations previously managed by local municipalities. Effective from January 1, 2013, this reform transferred governance, funding, and maintenance responsibilities to a centralized agency, initially the Klebelsberg Institution Maintenance Centre (KLIK)
. 
All public educational institutions – with the exception of kindergartens - were taken over from the self-governments to be maintained by the newly established Klebelsberg School Maintaining Authority; state-owned schools were terminated as separate legal entities and merged into the organization of KLIK.
 **Fact.** This means the legal power to decide whether a given village school stays open now sits with a national/regional state body, not the local council — a foundational governance shift for any depopulation-driven closure question.

## 2. Partial re-decentralization (2017) into "school district centres" (tankerület) — but still state, not municipal

From 1 January 2017, the institution maintenance tasks of KLIK have been performed by the school district centres operating as independent budgetary units (initially 58, currently 60), facilitating the decentralisation of the system. The KLIK in its former form has ceased to exist and has been replaced by the Klebelsberg Centre (KK).
 **Fact.** This is deconcentration within the state administration, not a return of authority to elected municipal governments — an important distinction for "which legal authority" questions.

## 3. Split ownership structure: municipalities retain building ownership; state controls operation — creates accountability gaps

School buildings are still owned by the municipalities, not t
[he state]. A documented 2024 dispute (Tiszavasvári/Bűd, ERRC legal complaint) shows this split producing real accountability conflicts: 
the ERRC and the National Association of Disadvantaged Families took legal action to raise concerns about equal access to education and safety of Romani children forced to walk around seven kilometres to reach their local school after their bus service was stopped; the complaint was submitted against the Municipality of Tiszavasvári and the Educational District Centre of Nyíregyháza before the Deputy Commissioner for Fundamental Rights, Ombudsman for the Rights of National Minorities.
 
The mayor stated that organising a school bus service is the task of the school district, but the complaint argues that according to the relevant law this is not true.
 **Fact/contested legal interpretation** — the case illustrates unresolved statutory ambiguity over which authority (municipality vs. tankerület) bears responsibility when school access logistics (transport, catchment reassignment) change due to consolidation.

## 4. Church takeover ("churchification") is a parallel — and financially privileged — alternative maintainer track

The churchification of education is a policy direction embedded within a wider transformation of the state; an increasing number of public schools have been taken over by one of the main Christian denominations, enjoying larger autonomy and more advantageous state financing than state-run schools.
 
The number of church-run educational institutions has doubled since 2010, ranging from kindergartens to high schools.
 A 2026 civil-society source (contested/advocacy source, flagged) claims 
an amendment of the law on church reparations allows religious organizations to acquire any public building from a municipality simply by requesting it, provided they claim it serves a "community goal," described as a massive, permanent transfer of public wealth
, and further claims 
church-run schools receive state subsidies that are significantly higher—often four times higher per student—than those allocated to remaining public schools
. **Note: the funding-multiple figure is from an advocacy/NGO source and should be treated as contested pending independent verification**, but the underlying transfer mechanism and dual-track maintainer system (state tankerület vs. church) is corroborated by academic literature and is directly relevant to "which legal authority" a depopulating village's school might end up under.

## 5. UN Special Rapporteur (March 2025) flagged structural inequality and weak participation mechanisms — directly bearing on accountability

She warned of a "hollowing out" of the public education system, with growing inequalities between under-resourced state schools and better-funded church-run institutions.
 On governance/participation specifically: 
Hungary was commended for establishing mechanisms for consultation within the education system, but students, parents and teachers repeatedly stressed their felt need for ensuring their voices are heard and acted upon through meaningful channels of communication with the government; while official consultations occur a few times a year, stakeholders reported that there is no structured response from authorities and their recommendations.
 **Fact (from official UN visit statement, not yet the final report)** — this is a live, credible finding on the parent/community autonomy-accountability gap in decisions like school closure/merger. The Rapporteur's full report is due to the UN Human Rights Council in June 2026, per the same source: 
She will present her full report to the UN Human Rights Council in June 2026.


## 6. Real-world contentious example: parents petitioning against a tankerület's reorganization decision (2025)

Reported case: the school district center examined a school's capacity versus enrolled student numbers and classroom-standard compliance; the district found the school over capacity, with children studying in the library, cafeteria, basement, hallway and outdoor area, and stated that "efficient maintenance" decisions are made annually weighing both financial and professional considerations.
 This Budapest-area case (2025) involved a parents' petition against the head of the district center over planned school reorganizations — illustrating that reorganization/merger decisions are made administratively by the tankerület with parents having only informal/petition-based recourse, not a formal co-decision right. **Fact, single case, non-village but demonstrates the same authority structure (tankerület decides, parents petition after the fact) that would apply to rural consolidation decisions.**

## 7. Direct empirical case of depopulation-driven closure with no institutional remedy attempted

In Garab, one of Hungary's tiniest towns with a mere 44 residents, most over 50, the mayor's office now occupies the space where the village school once stood; the school had to close because there were no more children.
 **Fact (2023 report)** — an on-the-ground instance confirming that some village schools have already closed for demographic reasons rather than being kept open via subsidy or special legal status.

## 8. Legal right-to-education framework exists but is generally phrased at individual, not institutional-survival, level

Hungary's constitutional provisions guarantee the right to education, implemented through dissemination and general access to culture, free compulsory primary schooling, and secondary/higher education available to all on the basis of ability.
 
One of the primary manifestations of the freedom of education is the free choice of schools, a right set out by the Public Education Act.
 **Fact/legal-structural point** — the constitutional/statutory right attaches to individual access to *an* education, not to the continued existence of *a particular local* school; this is the legal hinge allowing closure/consolidation to proceed without violating the codified right, provided alternative access (e.g., transport to a hub school) is arranged — which is precisely where the Tiszavasvári-type disputes (Finding 3) arise.

## 9. Oversight/redress bodies exist but are advisory, not binding, on maintainer decisions

In Hungary, a specialized ombudsman system was established in 1999; any person involved in education—pupils, parents, teachers, students, researchers, lecturers and their associations—can turn to the Education Rights Commissioner if they believe their rights have been infringed or are at risk of being infringed.
 A parallel channel is the Deputy Ombudsman for the Rights of National Minorities, used in the Tiszavasvári/Bűd and Gyöngyöspata segregation cases (Findings 3, above, and separately documented ombudsman position papers on segregation remedies). **Fact** — these bodies can investigate and issue findings/recommendations but do not have direct power to reverse a tankerület's or church maintainer's closure/reorganization decision; enforcement depends on litigation (e.g., ECtHR Szolcsán v. Hungary 2023) or political response.

## 10. Historical governance pattern: centralization has repeatedly correlated with small rural-school attrition, and past attempts (interwar era) used mandated building programs, not maintenance guarantees, to sustain rural access

Given the regime's inherent aim to maximize growth and pressure on every actor to find additional resources for investment in a shortage economy, villages became massively exploited by regional urban centers, resulting in a historically unprecedented decline of small schools in Hungary during the 1960s and 1970s.
 This is offered as **background/inference context** (not directly about the current problem) showing that centralized resource-allocation regimes in Hungarian history have tended to disadvantage small rural schools, which is analytically relevant to assessing whether the current tankerület/church dual-maintainer system will behave similarly for depopulating villages — but this is a historical analogy, not a proven causal claim about present policy.
