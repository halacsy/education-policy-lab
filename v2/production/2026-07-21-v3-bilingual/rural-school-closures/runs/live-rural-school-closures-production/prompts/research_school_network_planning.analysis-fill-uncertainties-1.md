# Research Notes: Primary Schools in Depopulating Hungarian Villages
## Education Infrastructure Planning Perspective

---

### 1. Demographic decline is real and geographically documented, but school closure's causal role in depopulation is contested internationally

The comparative literature does not support a simple causal story. 
Barakat (2014) demonstrated that while school location may play a certain role for some individuals looking for housing areas, in general there is no evidence of a localised depopulating effect of primary school closures.
 However, this finding is scope-limited: 
the analysis focused on municipal-level data which does not show local changes in depopulation trends.
 Countering this, 
Finnish research using DID modelling (2010–2018) found that after school closure, the population of the surrounding community decreased more than before, meaning closures raise questions not just about the school but about the vitality and development of the whole municipality.
 **Contested**: Saxony/Sweden studies (Barakat 2015, Amcoff 2012) find no significant closure–depopulation link, while Finland-specific evidence finds an effect. No Hungary-specific causal (DID/matching) study was found in this search.

---

### 2. Hungary's school network is a historically centralizing, not locally adaptive, system since 2013


In 2011 the Hungarian parliament passed Act CXC on National Public Education, mandating state oversight of public school operations previously managed by local municipalities; effective January 1, 2013, this transferred governance, funding, and maintenance to a centralized agency (KLIK), later restructured into regional centers under the Ministry of Human Capacities.
 
KLIK was established with the stated aim of replacing uncertain local government funding to guarantee stable operation of schools.
 This is decision-relevant: local municipalities in depopulating villages generally no longer control school-continuation decisions directly — a national/regional authority does, per pre-set district (tankerület) boundaries. 
A special ministerial agency, KLIK, was set up to carry out these tasks in 198 school districts (tankerület).


---

### 3. Historical precedent: prior centralizing regimes explicitly used "uneconomical" settlement-size thresholds to justify rural school withdrawal


The Communist leadership considered settlements with fewer than 3,000 inhabitants — roughly half of the Hungarian settlement network — as "uneconomical" to maintain.
 
A 1949 government decree banned construction of permanent flats or public buildings, including schools, on scattered farms, while a 1951 official plan for the urban network urged a total ban on investment in these settlements.
 This is historical inference-relevant background, not current policy, but shows Hungary has precedent for population-threshold-based infrastructure withdrawal logic that recurs in planning debates. (Sourced from academic historical analysis; not current government policy.)

---

### 4. A counter-historical precedent shows large-scale rural school-building was feasible at similar settlement scales


In 1926, Klebelsberg's ministry launched a national program of building "people's schools," and by 1930 had established 5,000 new elementary schoolrooms and teacher flats, with two-thirds of new infrastructure—2,315 schoolrooms and 1,130 teacher flats—installed specifically in scattered farm and small-village regions.
 This is a historical fact demonstrating geographic feasibility of dense rural school provision under a different policy paradigm (early 20th century); it is not evidence of current capacity or funding availability.

---

### 5. Micro-level case evidence shows local social capital, not just enrollment numbers, determines whether "rescue" of a threatened village school is feasible

A documented Hungarian case study of Erdőkürt (Nógrád County) found that 
rural education has had to face demographic decline and the shrinking ability of municipalities to maintain schools, and the case study illustrates the cultural and spatial barriers impeding the creation of co-operation in the field of education.
 
Because the community concerned was remarkably vibrant with strong intra-community horizontal ties, the concept of social capital was used to explain how bonding and bridging networks, as well as "missing links," influence community
 capacity to keep a school open. Erdőkürt's own population trajectory is documented (611 in 2001, 537 in 2011 per KSH data cited on Wikipedia), illustrating the enrollment pressure such "rescue" cases face. This is a single qualitative case study — not generalizable proof that social capital reliably saves schools elsewhere.

---

### 6. Ethnic-Hungarian minority regions abroad show a scalable transport-based alternative to closure: dedicated minority-language school-bus networks

Outside Hungary's borders but directly relevant as a policy template, the Rákóczi Szövetség operates a cross-border school-bus program for dispersed ethnic-Hungarian children. 
In the 2025/2026 school year, 61 school buses assigned to 54 institutions abroad enable the daily commute of 861 mostly lower-grade students to the nearest Hungarian-language institution.
 
The organizers state the buses not only help families access Hungarian-language schooling but also decisively contribute to the survival of small-enrollment scattered institutions and thereby to community survival.
 This is an organizational claim from the program's own operator (potential promotional framing), not independently verified outcome data, but it demonstrates the transport-as-alternative-to-closure model is operationally implemented at meaningful scale (marginal case: minority-language motive differs from pure demographic decline motive within Hungary).

---

### 7. International comparative evidence on free school-bus programs shows mixed effects on the depopulation the busing is meant to offset

A Bulgarian municipal case (Blagoevgrad) found: 
students began to receive high-quality educational services without extra payment and their transportation was also free of charge; on the other hand, children had to travel different distances daily.
 The Blagoevgrad study explicitly investigated 
how this educational reform affected one of the most important and problematic processes in Bulgarian villages during the past decades – their depopulation.
 This is a foreign (non-Hungarian) case, offered as an analogous transport-substitution model; its depopulation findings are not summarized here beyond the framing quoted, and outcome data should not be assumed to transfer directly to Hungary.

---

### 8. Hungary faces a severe, aging, and geographically uneven teaching workforce — a capacity constraint independent of building/transport logistics


Teacher shortages, linked to an ageing workforce, are a growing concern; according to national KSH 2025 data on full-time employed primary teachers, the number of teachers in the younger age group (30–44) has declined, such that half of all teachers in the 2024/2025 academic year were 50 or older, while only 7.2% were under 30.
 Separately, 
40 per cent of Hungary's schools report a lack of appropriately qualified staff and 60 per cent of new teachers resign within the first three to four years after entering the profession because of poor pay and conditions
 (UN Special Rapporteur finding, March 2025). This is a capacity constraint that can make even a geographically "keep-open" decision practically unviable if qualified staff cannot be recruited/retained in remote postings — reported anecdotally as harder in rural, disadvantaged-student contexts. 
The mayor of a small village noted it is very difficult to attract young teachers to rural places, not only because of a lesser social life, but also because many local students come from disadvantaged families, which raises new problems.


---

### 9. National territorial data show measurable regional/peripheral contraction in child population and school counts post-2021, alongside centralization-driven closures


Spatial selectivity and sorting in the Hungarian education system is shaped by catchment policies, school closures in peripheral areas, and residential mobility toward more advantaged localities.
 At the national level, 
of institutions studied, 3,609 provided primary education (ISCED 1–2) in 2021, a number that declined to 3,521 by 2024
 — an aggregate net decline of roughly 88 primary-education-providing institutions in three years. Regionally, 
Baranya and some low-density regions of Northern Hungary recorded stagnation or decline, including areas where the child population contracted by 10–30% over the period, often coinciding with broader demographic decline and the closure or downgrading
 of institutions. This is descriptive/correlational territorial data (not a causal claim about which came first, decline or closure).

---

### 10. Governance structure limits the extent to which "the school network" can flexibly respond at the local level

Because school maintenance passed from municipalities to the state in 2013, 
prior to 2013 Hungarian municipalities maintained and operated public schools with significant autonomy in management, budgeting, and staffing — a decentralized model that allowed tailored local responses but contributed to regional disparities — whereas in 2013 the central government assumed responsibility through the Public Education Act to standardize operations and mitigate inequalities.
 Analysts characterize the resulting structure critically: 
the autonomy of schools has been drastically curtailed, with all competencies placed in the hands of one big national organization (KLIK)
 (SGI 2021 assessment — a normatively framed source, flagged as an evaluative/critical rather than neutral description). This matters for feasibility: any village-level "keep open" or "merge" decision is now filtered through centrally administered school-district (tankerület) boundaries and national staffing/funding allocation rather than local discretion.
