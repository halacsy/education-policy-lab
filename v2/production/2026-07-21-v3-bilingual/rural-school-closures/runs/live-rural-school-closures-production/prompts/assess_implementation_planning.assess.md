- F-live-implementation_planning-01 [strong]: Governance authority for school closure and consolidation in Hungary is split between municipalities (building ownership and maintenance) and the central state via the Klebelsberg Institution Maintenance Centre (employment of teachers and operational control), weakening local actor capacity to unilaterally design or deliver locally-driven school-network mechanisms.
- F-live-implementation_planning-02 [strong]: Sub-3,000-population settlements face a structurally more centralized administrative tier where the state district office (tankerület) acts as both maintainer and operator, whereas larger towns retain municipal operator status, concentrating decision authority further from local stakeholders in the villages most affected by depopulation.
- F-live-implementation_planning-03 [weak]: Church and NGO takeovers of small schools threatened by low enrollment represent an existing alternative pathway to state closure, as documented in a 2026 case where a religious maintainer (Kolping) absorbed two small-town schools into shared management, though the maintainer explicitly limited scope to school administration and did not commit to broader community social-care functions.
- F-live-implementation_planning-04 [moderate]: Hungarian education-research modeling determined that raising the minimum school size threshold to 100 students would directly affect more than five hundred village schools (2002 baseline), with many unable to survive even via consolidation with nearby small schools, demonstrating a fundamental trade-off between efficiency-based thresholds and school-network coverage in depopulated regions.
- F-live-implementation_planning-05 [strong]: Hungary had 1,036 settlements with under 500 residents (2008 count) capable of sustaining schools with approximately 20 pupils or fewer, mostly as branch institutions; these schools are structurally forced into multigrade (ungraded or partially graded) teaching because pupil numbers cannot support multiple parallel classes, requiring pedagogical capabilities not automatically present in a system built around single-grade classrooms.
- F-live-implementation_planning-06 [strong]: EU and OECD monitoring data show that Hungary has the largest urban–rural education-outcome gap of all OECD countries even before accounting for socioeconomic status, with teacher shortages most pronounced in rural areas where the share of unqualified teachers in disadvantaged schools is 2–3 times higher than in non-disadvantaged schools, and with half of all primary teachers aged 50 or older in 2024/2025 while only 7.2% were under 30, creating an ageing workforce and rural supply crisis that poses a binding constraint on any policy assuming staffing of retained rural schools.
- F-live-implementation_planning-07 [contested]: International empirical evidence on whether school closure causes community depopulation is contested: Finnish difference-in-differences analysis (2010–2018) found that post-closure population decreased more than pre-closure trends, while Saxon and Swedish studies (Barakat 2015, Amcoff 2012, Barakat 2014) found no significant connection between school closure and population decline; Hungarian data revealed ambiguous socioeconomic correlates where villages with schools showed better demographic performance but worse employment and welfare characteristics compared to villages without schools, indicating the core causal mechanism (school retention → community survival) lacks robust confirmation and cannot be assumed as a safe design premise.
- F-live-implementation_planning-08 [moderate]: Perceived symbolic value of the school among residents diverges sharply from revealed post-closure preferences: a Czech-context study found that while majorities in villages with schools consider it difficult to do without, only 17% of inhabitants of municipalities without schools considered its absence a major shortcoming; this feedback-loop distortion risk means that community consultation mechanisms relying on stated preference (surveys, hearings) may systematically overweight symbolic attachment relative to actual post-closure adjustment, distorting calibration of policy responsiveness.
- F-live-implementation_planning-09 [moderate]: Hungary operates a parallel, non-education-specific rural-revitalization funding channel (Hungarian Village Program) directed at financing municipalities to avoid depopulation and improve living circumstances, having provided 600 localities with combined 10 billion forints (EUR 28.3 million) for communal spaces and community-life development; however, a documented Romanian–Hungarian cross-border case showed that even after infrastructure investment (road paving, water/sewage systems) and pre-renovation demand forecasting, a renovated school closed five years later for lack of children, indicating that infrastructure-side interventions alone do not reliably reverse enrollment decline driving closure decisions, raising questions about coordination and integration between education-network planning and broader rural development funding.
- F-live-implementation_planning-10 [moderate]: No systematic, documented early-warning mechanism has been identified that links demographic microdata to proactive school-network planning at the district (tankerület) level; recent academic work (2025 territorial study using 2015 Hungarian Central Statistical Office data, 2024 'white spaces' mapping) demonstrates post-hoc identification of settlements without schools, but the existence of academic analysis rather than named institutional early-warning procedures suggests that real-time feedback connecting demographic decline to proactive (rather than reactive/crisis-driven) school-network decisions is not operationalized as a named safeguard.

TP-live-t1 — Baseline continuation: centralized case-by-case closure under current tankerület authority
Goal: Serve as the explicit no-new-policy counterfactual against which all other proposals are compared, documenting where current centralized, case-by-case decision-making leads absent any new safeguard, consolidation plan, or preservation measure.
Mechanisms: Closure/reorganization decisions remain centralized at ministerial level via the Klebelsberg Center's 60 tankerület districts, with no local veto except the specific parental veto on church transfer.; Institutional thinning continues at the observed pace: over 1,200 of 3,155 settlements already lack public education institutions by 2024, and 31% of municipalities lacked kindergarten in 2022, mostly where child populations fall below 8.; No systematic early-warning mechanism links demographic microdata to proactive network planning at tankerület level; academic post-hoc mapping exists but is not an operationalized institutional safeguard.
Risks: Ad hoc, reactive closures (Garab-type) will likely recur without any systematic demographic early-warning link, producing avoidable community disruption.; Uncoordinated policy drift if the 2026 diagnostic review produces recommendations that are not integrated with any of the other proposed directions.

TP-live-t2 — Managed structural consolidation with guaranteed transport access
Goal: Deliberately consolidate the smallest, least viable village primary schools into larger units while guaranteeing individual-level access to education through legally-owed transport, rather than relying on ad hoc case-by-case closure.
Mechanisms: Consolidation follows a size-and-distance framework similar to Norway's (target size, maximum bus travel time), which produced a 9% reduction in school count (2008-2018) alongside an increase in average school size from 196 to 222 pupils.; Transport to the mandatory-admission school is the legal responsibility of the maintainer; this proposal makes that obligation enforceable and monitored rather than dependent on parental complaint, addressing the documented lapse in practice.; Economies-of-scale evidence is partial: US data show per-pupil operating cost declines of 61.7% (300-pupil district mergers) and 49.6% (1,500-pupil mergers), concentrated in instruction/administration, but no economies (or diseconomies) for transportation costs, meaning the transport guarantee itself will not generate savings and must be separately funded.
Risks: Finnish quasi-experimental evidence (DID, 2010-2018) found that population decline accelerated after school closure, suggesting consolidation could itself deepen depopulation in affected villages, though this causal link is contested (Saxony, Sweden found no significant effect).; Danish evidence shows adverse short-run achievement effects for students in closing schools, concentrated among students from the smallest schools, though effects weaken over time.
Evidence: moderate

TP-live-t3 — Split-model retention: local lower grades, consolidated upper grades
Goal: Preserve local access for the youngest pupils via legally-sanctioned multi-grade classes while transporting older pupils to consolidated schools with specialized facilities, avoiding a binary open/close decision.
Mechanisms: Multi-grade (összevont) classes are already a legally sanctioned adaptation within the Public Education Act's class-size framework, requiring no additional authorization to merge small classes up to the legal maximum, making this an administratively low-friction implementation path.; The model is directly analogous to Poland's draft split-model legislation, which proposes retaining grades I-III locally with guaranteed enrollment while transporting grades IV-VIII to larger consolidated schools with specialized classrooms, and repurposing unused buildings as multipurpose community facilities.; This approach is functionally similar to Hungary's historical practice of reducing parallel classes rather than closing schools, which limited within-school segregation but is associated with between-school (between-settlement) segregation; the split model must be designed with this trade-off in mind.
Risks: Raising minimum size thresholds (as modeled in Hungarian research using a 100-student threshold) would directly affect more than five hundred village schools, many unable to survive even in split form, indicating the split model has a lower viability bound.; Between-settlement segregation risk persists or could intensify if upper-grade consolidation routes are not designed to avoid concentrating disadvantaged pupils in specific receiving schools.
Evidence: moderate

TP-live-t4 — Active preservation: procedural safeguards plus targeted rural-school funding
Goal: Establish explicit legal presumptions against closure of designated rural schools, paired with targeted funding and teacher supplements, to counteract the mechanical funding disadvantage facing small schools and preserve local provision where community and pedagogical conditions support it.
Mechanisms: Scotland's Section 11A requires closure proposals for designated rural schools to meet a 'strong, positive case' test and address three matters of 'special regard'; Wales establishes a presumption against closure for 218 designated rural schools with mandatory consideration of alternatives including federation.; Poland's rural teacher bonus mandate (minimum 10% salary supplement in municipalities under 5,000 inhabitants) provides a transferable model for offsetting the rural teacher-supply crisis, though it also creates sustained fiscal strain on small municipalities independent of consolidation decisions.; Hungary's own pre-2004 practice of targeted state support to small villages maintained a relatively intact rural school network despite grave municipal financial difficulties, providing a domestic precedent for this lever.
Risks: The evidence on whether school retention causally sustains village vitality is mixed: Hungarian village-level data show schools correlate with better demographic performance but worse employment/welfare outcomes, meaning preservation may not deliver the broader revitalization sometimes assumed.; Rural teacher-supply crisis (6,200 annual retirements projected 2023-2030, aging workforce, 60% of new teachers resigning within 3-4 years) could undermine a 'keep open' policy through attrition even with funding safeguards in place.
Evidence: moderate

TP-live-t5 — Alternative-maintainer pathway: church/NGO transfer with parental veto
Goal: Enable transfer of threatened small state schools to church or NGO maintainers, subject to the existing parental veto, as a way to keep the building in local educational use without requiring state-funded structural reform.
Mechanisms: Parents retain a formal decision-making veto over church-school transfer of a state primary school: transfer cannot proceed if more than half of parents do not support it, establishing a real constraint that channels this pathway through community consent rather than unilateral administrative decision.; When transfer occurs, the government office reassigns catchment-area boundaries to another state school, and affected parents retain a choice between the new mandatory-admission state school and the transferred church school, both obligated to admit the child, preserving a minimal choice architecture.; Documented precedent (2026 Kolping case) shows NGO/religious maintainers absorbing small-town schools into shared management, though the maintainer explicitly limited scope to school administration without committing to broader community social-care functions, indicating the pathway's scope is narrower than a full community-hub model.
Risks: In single-school villages, ownership-model change functions as a de facto reduction of educational choice even when the building remains open, since church school attendance already rose 60% (2001-2011) and 47% (2011-2014), raising concern about narrowing effective options over time.; Church-run schools' greater autonomy and structural differences from state schools could create inconsistency in curricular or pedagogical standards across the rural network.
Evidence: moderate

TP-live-t6 — Community-anchored resilience and equity-first restructuring combined
Goal: Support locally-driven, non-structural school-rescue through community mobilization and multi-service hub repurposing where viable, while explicitly subordinating any 'keep open' preference to desegregation obligations and addressing the rural teacher-supply crisis that constrains all retention-based options.
Mechanisms: Documented Hungarian case studies (e.g., Erdőkürt) show that local social capital—bonding and bridging community networks—influences local capacity to sustain a threatened school amid demographic decline, providing a template for technical-assistance targeting.; Cross-border ethnic-Hungarian minority school-bus networks (Rákóczi Szövetség, 61 buses to 54 institutions serving ~861 students in 2025/2026) demonstrate an operating model that enables dispersed students to access schools and contributes to small-institution survival, offering a transferable network-support mechanism.; The ECtHR ruled that a small local school's de facto role as the only option for Roma families can constitute racial segregation (Piliscsaba, Nyíregyháza), establishing that in Hungary's legal framework 'keep the school open' is not a neutral default and courts have treated closure/merger as a segregation remedy in specific settlement configurations.; The rural teacher-supply crisis—half of primary teachers aged 50+ in 2024/2025, only 7.2% under 30, 60% of new teachers resigning within 3-4 years, and 6,200 annual retirements projected 2023-2030—constrains any retention or community-hub approach that assumes stable local staffing.
Risks: Even where community mobilization succeeds in keeping a school open, the underlying teacher-supply crisis could undermine viability regardless of formal maintainer or closure status.; Judicially mandated closures on desegregation grounds face durable local political resistance and years-long implementation delays, as documented in a prior case where the municipal government actively obstructed a final court ruling.
Evidence: moderate
