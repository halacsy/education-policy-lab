# Research Notes: Primary Schools in Depopulating Hungarian Villages

## Demographic and Institutional Baseline

**1. Scale of the underlying demographic driver is large and accelerating.** Hungary's 
total births fell 9.1% in 2024 versus 2023, with the steepest drop (14%) recorded in summer months
, while 
the fertility rate dropped to 1.38 by 2024, with depopulation progressing at its fastest rate to date
. As of 2024, 
nearly 30% of Hungary's population lived in villages
, meaning the affected population base is substantial, not marginal. *(Fact)*

**2. Rural depopulation is geographically uneven, not uniform.** Statista data note that 
rural depopulation has slowed nationally and some people are moving to villages, but individual settlements like Lasztonya lost over 40% of population in five years
. This is corroborated by academic mapping showing 
stagnation or decline concentrated in Baranya and low-density parts of Northern Hungary, where child population contracted 10–30%, often coinciding with school closure or downgrading
. *(Fact — heterogeneity matters for any uniform national policy.)*

## Governance/Financing Structure and Incentive Effects

**3. Decision rights over school viability were removed from local municipalities in 2013.** Since the KLIK reform, 
in villages with population under 3,000, operational tasks of public educational institutions were also performed by the state
, and 
all primary and secondary institutions previously run by municipalities were taken over by the state, which became employer of all pedagogical staff
. **Inference:** this severs the historical link between local fiscal effort/political accountability and school survival — village governments can no longer independently choose to subsidize an under-enrolled school, shifting the closure/retention decision to a central bureaucracy with different (national, budgetary) incentives than local communities.

**4. The stated rationale for centralization was equalization, but it produced new funding pathologies.** The declared goal was 
to reduce quality differences between schools and equalise disparities from municipalities' different income-generating capacities
, and centralization was framed as 
replacing uncertain local government funding to guarantee stable school operation
. However, research finds 
the resource constraints of smaller municipal schools were addressed, but funding for previously better-off urban schools declined, and recurring KLIK solvency problems stemmed from systematic under-budgeting, forcing district leaders to cut spending to only mandatory minimums
. **Inference:** central financing may have redistributed resources toward small rural schools at the expense of urban ones, while overall system underfunding constrains the resources available for *either* keeping small schools open well-equipped or funding consolidation/transport alternatives properly — an opportunity-cost tension baked into the financing model itself.

**5. Hungary historically avoided formal closures by using an internal adjustment margin (reducing parallel classes), which had a segregation side-effect.** 
Local self-governments typically mitigated declining pupil numbers by reducing the number of parallel classes rather than closing or amalgamating schools, leading to a large offer of small schools but reducing scope for within-school separation of pupils by social/ethnic background — especially in small rural settlements running only one class per grade
. **Inference/contested:** this suggests school-network fragmentation (many small schools) and between-school ethnic segregation may be linked mechanisms — keeping small schools open is not "neutral" with respect to segregation dynamics; it can substitute between-school segregation for the within-school segregation that would occur in larger merged schools.

## Economic Evidence on Costs, Scale, and Transportation

**6. Cross-national evidence on consolidation shows real but partial and contested economies of scale.** A U.S. literature review notes 
annual operating spending per pupil declines by 61.7% when two 300-pupil districts merge and by 49.6% when two 1,500-pupil districts merge, with savings concentrated in instruction and administration
, but critically 
the study finds no economies — or diseconomies — of size for student transportation
, and 
consolidation also involves transition costs not associated with enrollment
. Other reviews are more skeptical: 
a U-shaped curve best characterizes the relationship, and older economy-of-scale studies may underestimate large-school costs because administrative/support costs get shifted to the district level
. **Contested:** net fiscal savings from closure are real for instructional/administrative costs but are NOT found for transportation — meaning the "efficiency gain" argument for closure is asymmetric across cost categories, and the travel-burden cost is a genuine (not merely perceptual) offsetting cost, not economies-of-scale territory.

**7. Multi-grade (combined) teaching is a documented middle-path resource strategy, not just a Hungarian idiosyncrasy.** Comparative research notes 
multi-grade teaching is implemented in order to prevent schools from closing, as it stabilizes learning-group sizes in rural areas with declining birth rates
. This is an incentive-compatible resource allocation: it lowers the marginal enrollment threshold needed to keep a school open, trading some pedagogical uniformity for continued local access, and is functionally similar to the Hungarian practice of reducing parallel classes described above (finding 5).

## Community and Distributional Effects

**8. There is direct (though single-country, contested) causal evidence that school closure itself accelerates community depopulation, not just the reverse.** A Finnish difference-in-differences study covering 
2010–2018 using DID regression combined with matching methods
 found that 
after school closure, the population of the surrounding community decreased more than before school closure
, concluding that 
school closures raise questions not just about the school itself but about the broader vitality and development of the municipality
. **Contested/limited external validity:** this is Finland-specific; Hungary-specific causal estimates were not found in this search, so this evidence should be treated as suggestive by analogy, not as a direct Hungarian finding. It does, however, imply a feedback loop/externality: closure decisions justified by low enrollment may cause further enrollment/population decline, undermining the premise that closure is a passive response to an exogenous trend.

**9. Poland's comparable experience separates the proximate/political cause from the underlying demographic cause.** Analysis of ~3,300 Polish school closures (2000–2016) found 
declining fertility plus migration combined to reduce primary-pupil numbers
, yet 
economic reasons were cited as the direct cause of school closures in the countryside
. **Inference:** decision-makers often frame closures as budget-driven even when the root cause is demographic — meaning the *stated* incentive (cost savings) and the *underlying* driver (fewer children) can be politically decoupled, which matters for how the policy is legitimated to affected communities.

**10. Hungary's own case literature on rescued/contested village schools shows partial, not binary, outcomes.** In the broader Hungarian rural school restructuring wave referenced in the Kovács (2012) case study, 
an estimated 10% of small schools were closed, while some 30–35% were amalgamated and continued under the administration of a central unit
. **Inference:** the realistic policy menu is not simply "close vs. keep open" — amalgamation-with-continued-local-operation is a common intermediate outcome, which changes the cost/benefit calculus (retains a building/local presence while pooling administrative overhead) but was not itself evaluated for effectiveness in the sources retrieved here.

## Distributional Note (Benefits vs. Costs)

Combining findings 3–5 and 9 above: the **fiscal/administrative benefits** of closure or centralization (instructional and administrative savings, per finding 6) accrue primarily to the central budget/school-maintenance authority, while the **costs** (travel burden, loss of community hub, potential acceleration of depopulation per finding 8) are concentrated on families and residents of the specific affected village — a spatial mismatch between who captures savings and who bears the burden. Separately, the segregation-related evidence (findings 5, and the broader Hungarian literature on Roma school segregation being among the highest in the EU per the Education and Training Monitor 2025, which found 
Hungary has the highest level of segregation of students from less and more advantaged socio-economic groups, and the highest segregation of most/least disadvantaged students in the 2022 PISA study among EU countries
) suggests that consolidation/closure decisions are not distributionally neutral with respect to ethnicity: whether a village school is closed, merged, or preserved can shift which children end up in majority-Roma versus majority-non-Roma settings, an effect distinct from and additional to the pure enrollment/cost calculus.
