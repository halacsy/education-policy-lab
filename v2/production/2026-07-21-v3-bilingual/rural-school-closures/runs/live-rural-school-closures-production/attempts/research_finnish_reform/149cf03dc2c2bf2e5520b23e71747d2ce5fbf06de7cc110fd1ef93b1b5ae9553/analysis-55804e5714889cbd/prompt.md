# Research Notes: Finnish Comprehensive-School Experience & Later Erosion/School-Choice Effects
*(Scope: Finland, 1970s–2026; sources below)*

---

**1. The 1972–77 comprehensive reform itself accelerated rural school closures, not just later "erosion."**
Fact: 
The comprehensive school reform was implemented between 1972 and 1977, beginning first in the northern and north-eastern areas of Finland and continuing towards the south, and while the state supported municipalities in constructing new schools, this resulted in the closure of some smaller rural schools
. Separately, 
due to reduced birth rate, changes in the rural economic structure, migration and improved road conditions, closures of small rural schools began increasing significantly in the late 1960s; the position of small rural primary schools improved only briefly at the end of the 1970s when the comprehensive system was founded
. Inference: the peruskoulu reform is often mythologized as purely protective of rural schools, but the record shows closures predated and continued alongside it — the reform slowed but did not reverse rural contraction.

**2. The reform was financed through heavy, non-selective state subsidy — a structural precondition often omitted from "Finnish model" narratives.**
Fact: 
financing for the new comprehensive system was the responsibility of the Finnish government, which increased education's share of its budget from 9.1% in 1960 to 16.9% in 1975, and municipal institutions received high state subsidies covering 81–90% of teachers' salaries, 84–93% of school transport and pupil accommodation, and 5–77% of other expenses
. Inference: the reform's capacity to preserve equity across sparsely populated regions rested on centrally guaranteed transport/salary subsidy, not merely pedagogical commitment — a fiscal-architecture point relevant to any transplant of the model.

**3. Small rural schools were historically documented as functioning as community hubs, not just instructional units — this is a research finding, not merely local sentiment.**
Fact: 
the historical function of small rural schools in Finland has traditionally been one of a community centre, serving as a meeting place and social/cultural mid-point for community activities, with the school-community interaction seen as an important social asset whose value has been emphasised particularly after closure has taken place
. Later interview-based research on closures explicitly 
uses social capital to understand the role of a school in the community, studying its role as a producer and maintainer of local social capital
.

**4. A distinct, later wave of closures — driven by 1990s recession fiscal retrenchment — is documented separately from the 1970s reform-era pattern.**
Fact: 
at the end of the 1980s and early 1990s, major economic depression forced municipalities to restart massive financial cuts on basic services; since then the position of small rural primary schools as part of the school network has been at risk
. Multiple sources independently state 
the network of small rural schools in Finland has been radically weakened since the global recession of the 1990s
. Inference: this suggests two analytically separable closure regimes — reform-era rationalization (1970s) and austerity-driven contraction (1990s–present) — with different political logics.

**5. Empirical (quasi-causal) evidence links school closure to *accelerated*, not merely coincident, local population decline — but this is a single study using DID/matching, so causal strength should be treated cautiously.**
Fact: 
using DID regression combined with matching methods over 2010–2018, the study found that in Finland, after school closure, the population of the surrounding community decreased more than before school closure
, and 
in rural communities the impacts of school closure were slightly more negative, suggesting a higher risk of self-reinforcing population decline
. Contested/flagged: this is correlational-with-controls evidence from one paper (Kortt/Dollery-type DID design), not a randomized or fully exogenous natural experiment; reverse causality (declining villages both lose population and lose schools for the same underlying reasons) cannot be fully excluded despite matching.

**6. A separate natural-experiment study on displaced students exists, but the direction/magnitude of achievement effects is not confirmed in the material retrieved here — noted as a gap, not a finding.**
Fact: 
the study examines 600 students at rural schools displaced in the last years of primary education due to closures in 1999–2000, where — unlike prior literature on closures driven by poor performance — these closures were due to cost savings alone
, and 
because of the rural setting, effects of displacement included longer journeys to school and increased school size
. Explicit gap: the retrieved sources describe the study's design (matching displaced vs. control students on grades and graduation rates) but do not state the direction of the estimated effect; this should not be treated as evidence of either harm or benefit until the primary result is verified.

**7. From the 1990s, Finland introduced school-choice policy, which is documented as increasing between-school variation/segregation — a distinct "erosion" mechanism operating mainly in urban/metropolitan areas.**
Fact: 
as school choice policies were introduced in Finland in the 1990s, the assumption was that increased mobility between schools would encourage the availability of specialised subjects, but research suggests Finnish school choice is associated with growing school segregation through middle-class choice patterns
. Further: 
the advent of school choice policy and the growing variation in between-school achievement results have diversified the previously homogenous education system
, with 
the rise of selective school classes and parental choice leading capable parents to select selective classes for their children, fragmenting the once equal and egalitarian comprehensive system in an urban setting
. Scope caveat: this literature concentrates on Helsinki/Espoo/urban lower-secondary schools, not small rural primary schools — the mechanism (choice-driven sorting) is conceptually distinct from rural depopulation-driven closure, though both erode the original comprehensive-school equality premise.

**8. PISA-measured equity has deteriorated concurrently with these structural changes — again correlational, and multi-causal (immigration, segregation, choice all implicated together).**
Fact: 
Finland long combined excellent overall PISA performance with high equality, but recent PISA studies point to deteriorating learning outcomes and increasing importance of social background, compounded by strongly increasing socio-economic and ethnic residential segregation in Finnish cities, where over one-third of Finnish schoolchildren live
. Contested: sources attribute this jointly to residential segregation, school-choice mechanisms, and migrant-background gaps 
(in PISA 2018 migrant pupils were nearly three years behind in literacy, and Finland has the OECD's largest gap between migrant/migrant-background and non-migrant students)
 — the relative causal weight of choice policy versus demographic/immigration change versus rural-vs-urban dynamics is not disentangled in a single source and should be treated as an open, debated attribution.

**9. Current official forecasting treats further rural closures as near-inevitable due to birth-rate collapse, independent of any policy choice about community value.**
Fact: a 2025 
Ministry of Education and Culture–commissioned report found the number of elementary schools in Finland could halve by the end of the next decade, based on an analysis of birth rate and demographic trends, forecasting almost 100,000 fewer elementary pupils by 2040
. It further states 
some areas will no longer be able to offer elementary education locally, meaning children will have to travel to neighbouring municipalities
. This is a projection, not a completed outcome, and depends on immigration assumptions 
since immigration is the main factor sustaining Finland's population growth, with non-native-background children under 15 projected to grow by about 86,000 by 2040
.

**10. Municipal fiscal architecture (non-earmarked state transfers) means closure decisions remain local political choices, not centrally mandated — illustrated by contemporaneous case examples showing both closure-driven and community-resistance dynamics.**
Fact: 
funding of basic services is shared roughly 75% municipality / 25% state, with state funding not earmarked, so municipalities decide how to allocate it
; remoteness and demographic factors affect the state transfer share 
via population density, age range, and morbidity of the population in each area
. Illustrative (single-case, not generalizable) evidence: one municipality, Asikkala, 
having lost a tenth of its residents since 1990, proposed closing three of its six schools as a cost-cutting measure to save €1.8 million
, while another village, Tiistenjoki, 
whose primary school of 40 pupils could see enrollment drop to 30 within five years risking closure
, mobilized residents to attract new families specifically to keep the school (and other services) open — showing that under identical national funding rules, local demographic and civic responses diverge markedly.
