DILEMMAS:
- Consolidation efficiency versus community and depopulation risk: Structural consolidation (TP-t2) promises documented per-pupil operating-cost savings and a legible, planned framework analogous to Norway's, but the causal question of whether closure itself accelerates village depopulation is genuinely contested in the evidence (Finnish DID finding an effect, Saxony and Sweden finding none). Even if consolidation is efficient and access is nominally guaranteed via transport, communities may experience it as an irreversible loss of a local anchor institution, and the transport guarantee that protects individual access carries its own unfunded fiscal burden since economies of scale do not extend to transportation.
- Split-model low-friction adaptation versus deferred, unresolved viability floor: The split-model (TP-t3) is administratively attractive because multi-grade classes are already legally sanctioned, offering an implementation path with minimal new legislative or bureaucratic cost. Yet the same evidence base shows a hard lower viability bound: at a 100-student threshold, over 500 village schools would be affected, many unable to survive even in split form. Choosing administrative ease over a fuller structural reckoning may simply defer, rather than resolve, closure for the most fragile settlements, while also risking reproducing between-settlement segregation at the point of upper-grade transition.
- Active preservation funding versus uncertain revitalization payoff and teacher-supply ceiling: Procedural safeguards and targeted rural funding (TP-t4) have domestic precedent (pre-2004 Hungary) and comparator support (Scotland, Wales, Poland) for keeping small schools legally and fiscally protected. However, Hungarian village-level data show school presence correlates with better demographic performance but worse employment/welfare outcomes, undercutting the broader revitalization narrative sometimes used to justify preservation spending. Separately, a severe rural teacher-supply crisis (6,200 annual retirements projected 2023-2030, only 7.2% of new teachers under 30, 60% early attrition) constrains retention regardless of funding, meaning public money could be committed to a goal that funding alone cannot secure.
- Church/NGO transfer as consent-based preservation versus long-run narrowing of effective choice: The parental-veto transfer pathway (TP-t5) channels maintainer change through documented community consent and preserves a nominal dual-option structure between the mandatory-admission state school and the transferred church school. But in single-school villages this is a formal rather than substantive protection: documented growth in church-school enrollment share (60% 2001-2011, 47% 2011-2014) suggests a structural, longer-run narrowing of effective alternatives that the moment-of-transfer veto cannot capture, since it addresses only the transfer decision, not subsequent erosion of choice. This is a values conflict between procedural consent as sufficient legitimacy and substantive pluralism as the actual goal.
- Desegregation-primacy versus local preservation preference: TP-t6 explicitly subordinates 'keep the school open' preferences to desegregation obligations, drawing on ECtHR rulings that a small local school's de facto monopoly role for Roma families can itself constitute racial segregation (Piliscsaba, Nyíregyháza). This directly conflicts with the community-anchored preservation logic underlying TP-t3, TP-t4, and TP-t5, which treat local retention as presumptively valuable. The conflict is not resolvable by evidence about outcomes; it is a genuine clash between local community self-determination/continuity and equal-treatment/anti-segregation obligations, and the proposal's own risk section acknowledges that legally mandated closures on these grounds face durable municipal political resistance, meaning the value conflict recurs at the implementation stage even after a legal resolution.
- Baseline inertia versus the political and administrative cost of systemic reform: Continuing the current centralized, case-by-case model (TP-t1) requires no new coalition-building and avoids triggering active opposition, making it politically the path of least resistance in the short term. Yet every lens assessment converges on the same diagnosis: the baseline perpetuates institutional thinning, offers no early-warning linkage between demographic data and planning, and inherits all documented equity and governance harms of the status quo. The tension is not primarily empirical (the consequences of inertia are well-documented) but a genuine trade-off between the political/administrative cost of change now versus the accumulating, largely irreversible costs of continued drift.
- Rural teacher-supply crisis as a cross-cutting constraint on every retention-based value choice: Multiple proposals (TP-t3, TP-t4, TP-t6) rest on an assumption of sustained local staffing, but the documented rural teacher-supply crisis (half of primary teachers aged 50+, only 7.2% under 30, 60% of new teachers resigning within 3-4 years, 6,200 annual retirements projected 2023-2030) is a structural constraint that funding, legal presumptions, or community mobilization cannot by themselves resolve. This raises a distinct empirical-open-question dimension (will any given retention mechanism remain staffed) layered under the values debate about whether preservation is worth pursuing at all: pursuing preservation-oriented policy without addressing supply risks investing political and fiscal capital in options that may become non-viable regardless of the chosen governance model.
