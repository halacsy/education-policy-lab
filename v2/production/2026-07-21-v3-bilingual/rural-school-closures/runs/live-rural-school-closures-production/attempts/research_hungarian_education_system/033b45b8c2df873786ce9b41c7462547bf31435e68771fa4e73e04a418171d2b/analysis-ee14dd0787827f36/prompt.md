## Research Notes: Hungarian Small-Village Primary Schools and Depopulation

### Governance/Institutional Architecture (facts)

1. **Centralized state maintainer structure remains the default framework.** Since 2013, the state-run schools are organized through the Klebelsberg Center (Klebelsberg Központ, formerly KLIK), which operates through territorial units. 
The Klebelsberg Center carries out its tasks related to maintaining state institutions through 60 tankerület (school district) centers, and these tankerület centers perform the maintenance tasks for basic and secondary state institutions.
 Any proposal to consolidate, close, or repurpose village schools would operate inside — not around — this existing hierarchical structure, since 
the tankerület center's jurisdiction includes making proposals to the minister responsible for education regarding the founding, reorganization, or termination of institutions and modification of their scope of activity
, meaning closure/reorganization decisions are formally centralized at ministerial level, not local.

2. **A parallel, non-state governance track (church, national-minority, private maintainers) is explicitly carved out of tankerület authority and stays untouched by any state-level reform of village schools.** 
The tankerület center has no jurisdiction over institutions operated under national-minority self-government maintenance, church, or private maintenance, or in the case of kindergartens, which continue to operate under municipal maintenance.
 This means kindergartens remain a separate (municipal) governance track even where primary schools are state-run — a bifurcation that persists regardless of any primary-school-specific policy.

3. **Very recent (2026) development: the government has ordered a review of the tankerület system itself**, though scope is diagnostic, not yet a concrete village-school policy. 
The government asked the education minister to assess whether the current operating form of the Klebelsberg Center, the intermediate body coordinating the tankerület centers, is justified; the review will include analysis of the key economic and personnel data of institutions maintained by the tankerület centers and evaluate the professionalism and legality of their operations, with the stated goal of reviewing the education system's functioning and making financing more transparent.
 This is a live, unresolved process as of publication (Index, dated May 26, 2026) — its outcome is not yet determined.

### Church-transfer mechanism (a major existing "release valve" for small schools)

4. **The dominant real-world mechanism for keeping a small village school open without state operation is transfer to church maintenance ("egyházi fenntartásba adás"), a pathway built into the Public Education Act (Nkt.) rather than a novel policy tool.** 
Under the Public Education Act, the state can fulfill its public-education duties not only by maintaining a state institution but also through a church or private school, in which case the state "outsources" its duties and imposes additional requirements on that church or private school not applicable to other church/private schools.
 
Such schools must, among other things, fulfill the duties of a mandatory-admission (catchment-area) school.


5. **Parents retain a formal veto over church transfer, but only via a majority mechanism, not individual consent.** 
In connection with transferring an educational institution to church maintenance, parents have not merely a consultative but a decision-making role — i.e., if more than half of parents do not support the transfer, it cannot proceed.
 This is a real, existing constraint on any "solution" that routes through church takeover of a depopulating village school.

6. **When a school exits state maintenance (e.g., becomes a church school), catchment-area (körzethatár) boundaries are administratively reassigned, and dual-obligation admission can result.** 
The government office modifies the catchment-area boundaries of primary schools: it attaches the catchment area of the school that has "dropped out" (i.e., become a church school) to another primary school's catchment area, and this "new" mandatory-admission state school is obliged to admit the children belonging to that catchment area, with the government office required to obtain the opinion of the affected municipality/municipalities and the relevant tankerület before deciding.
 
If the school was previously taken over by a church, the parent can choose between two institutions, both obliged to admit the child: the new school designated by the government office, and the transferred church school.
 This shows the catchment-area (mandatory-admission) mechanism is preserved and simply re-routed, not eliminated, when a village school changes maintainer.

### Catchment-area/admission mechanism (unchanged core access rule)

7. **The compulsory local catchment-area admission rule (kötelező felvételt biztosító iskola) is a stable, unchanged structural feature that any village-school proposal must operate within.** 
The law provides for a mandatory-admission institution for schools just as for kindergartens; under this provision, the primary school is obliged to admit/receive the compulsory-school-age student who actually resides within the primary school's catchment area.
 This obligation is annually re-published per tankerület (documented for 2022/2023, 2023/2024, and 2024/2025 school years across multiple tankerület centers), indicating it is a routine, unchanged administrative mechanism rather than something altered by depopulation responses.

8. **Legal responsibility for transporting children to their mandatory-admission school is assigned to the maintainer (fenntartó), a rule that predates and is unaffected by any closure decision, and is a live source of civic complaint activity.** 
Transportation to the mandatory-admission school is the responsibility of the maintainer.
 
If school attendance is not otherwise ensured, the Public Education Act makes creating the conditions for reaching the mandatory-admission (catchment) school the obligation of the school's maintainers, meaning maintainers are required to organize children's transportation to the catchment-area school.
 A civil-liberties group (TASZ) produced a complaint template in 2024 for parents to invoke this obligation where school-bus services had lapsed, indicating this legal duty is not automatically or reliably fulfilled in practice — a contested implementation gap rather than a settled fact.

9. **Class-size/merging rules allow administrative consolidation of small village classes without changing the underlying legal maximum-size framework.** 
The size limits for school classes are set out in an annex to the Public Education Act, which specifies average and maximum class sizes by grade-year group; the average size may be disregarded, but classes must be organized so that the number of admitted students does not exceed the maximum.
 
The maintainer may therefore merge small classes up to the maximum permitted size, and creating a merged (multi-grade) class within a given year is not unlawful at the start of the school year.
 This confirms that multi-grade ("összevont") classes — a common small-village-school adaptation — are an existing, legally sanctioned tool, not a new policy.

### Demographic scale and disparate impact (facts, largely uncontested)

10. **The scale of institutional thinning in small settlements is substantial and recently quantified.** 
By 2024, more than 1,200 of Hungary's 3,155 settlements had no public educational institutions, raising concerns about territorial equity.
 Separately, 
in 2022, 31% of municipalities had no kindergarten, typically in small villages where the number of kindergarten-aged children did not reach 8
 — indicating the pre-school pipeline into rural primary schools is itself already thinned in many localities.

11. **Depopulation is demographically documented and geographically concentrated, though the closure link is inferential/illustrative rather than a national census.** 
If there is no significant change in the growth rate of these populations, 147 localities will eventually be completely depopulated
, and 
645 localities have populations below 300, with 114 of those having under-18 populations below 10 percent.
 A journalistic case example (CGTN, 2023) describes a village of 44 residents where 
the mayor's office now occupies the space where the village school once stood, because the school had to close as there were no more children, in one of Hungary's tiniest towns.
 This is illustrative reporting, not a systematic closure count, and should be treated as anecdotal evidence rather than a generalizable statistic.

12. **Educational disadvantage is geographically and ethnically stratified in ways relevant to who is affected by rural school policy.** 
The early-school-leaving rate is higher in the least developed regions, in villages (község) and among Roma (58.7%; non-Roma: 9.3% in 2023).
 This indicates that village-school policy is entangled with pre-existing regional and ethnic disparities in educational outcomes, not a demographically neutral logistics question.
