- F-live-international_comparison-06 [strong]: In Finland, a rigorous quasi-experimental analysis (difference-in-differences with matching, 2010–2018 window) found that after school closure, the population of surrounding communities decreased more than before closure, indicating an accelerating depopulation effect linked to school closure policy.

- F-live-international_comparison-09 [moderate]: In Germany (Saxony), a cross-national study found that counties with the lowest propensities to close schools experience the largest negative effects on population, counterintuitively suggesting that policymakers may overlook important unintended consequences of school consolidation in rural communities, though a related meta-review cautions that consolidation effects are context-dependent and that positive outcomes (improved school performance, maintained hiring standards) occurred where school networks were very fragmented, requiring caution in generalizing across cases.

TP-live-t2 — Managed structural consolidation with guaranteed transport access
Goal: Deliberately consolidate the smallest, least viable village primary schools into larger units while guaranteeing individual-level access to education through legally-owed transport, rather than relying on ad hoc case-by-case closure.
Mechanisms: Consolidation follows a size-and-distance framework similar to Norway's (target size, maximum bus travel time), which produced a 9% reduction in school count (2008-2018) alongside an increase in average school size from 196 to 222 pupils.; Transport to the mandatory-admission school is the legal responsibility of the maintainer; this proposal makes that obligation enforceable and monitored rather than dependent on parental complaint, addressing the documented lapse in practice.; Economies-of-scale evidence is partial: US data show per-pupil operating cost declines of 61.7% (300-pupil district mergers) and 49.6% (1,500-pupil mergers), concentrated in instruction/administration, but no economies (or diseconomies) for transportation costs, meaning the transport guarantee itself will not generate savings and must be separately funded.
Risks: Finnish quasi-experimental evidence (DID, 2010-2018) found that population decline accelerated after school closure, suggesting consolidation could itself deepen depopulation in affected villages, though this causal link is contested (Saxony, Sweden found no significant effect).; Danish evidence shows adverse short-run achievement effects for students in closing schools, concentrated among students from the smallest schools, though effects weaken over time.
Evidence: moderate

TP-live-t3 — Split-model retention: local lower grades, consolidated upper grades
Goal: Preserve local access for the youngest pupils via legally-sanctioned multi-grade classes while transporting older pupils to consolidated schools with specialized facilities, avoiding a binary open/close decision.
Mechanisms: Multi-grade (összevont) classes are already a legally sanctioned adaptation within the Public Education Act's class-size framework, requiring no additional authorization to merge small classes up to the legal maximum, making this an administratively low-friction implementation path.; The model is directly analogous to Poland's draft split-model legislation, which proposes retaining grades I-III locally with guaranteed enrollment while transporting grades IV-VIII to larger consolidated schools with specialized classrooms, and repurposing unused buildings as multipurpose community facilities.; This approach is functionally similar to Hungary's historical practice of reducing parallel classes rather than closing schools, which limited within-school segregation but is associated with between-school (between-settlement) segregation; the split model must be designed with this trade-off in mind.
Risks: Raising minimum size thresholds (as modeled in Hungarian research using a 100-student threshold) would directly affect more than five hundred village schools, many unable to survive even in split form, indicating the split model has a lower viability bound.; Between-settlement segregation risk persists or could intensify if upper-grade consolidation routes are not designed to avoid concentrating disadvantaged pupils in specific receiving schools.
Evidence: moderate

TP-live-t4 — Active preservation: procedural safeguards plus targeted rural-school funding
Goal: Establish explicit legal presumptions against closure of designated rural schools, paired with targeted funding and teacher supplements, to counteract the mechanical funding disadvantage facing small schools and preserve local provision where community and pedagogical conditions support it.
Mechanisms: Scotland's Section 11A requires closure proposals for designated rural schools to meet a 'strong, positive case' test and address three matters of 'special regard'; Wales establishes a presumption against closure for 218 designated rural schools with mandatory consideration of alternatives including federation.; Poland's rural teacher bonus mandate (minimum 10% salary supplement in municipalities under 5,000 inhabitants) provides a transferable model for offsetting the rural teacher-supply crisis, though it also creates sustained fiscal strain on small municipalities independent of consolidation decisions.; Hungary's own pre-2004 practice of targeted state support to small villages maintained a relatively intact rural school network despite grave municipal financial difficulties, providing a domestic precedent for this lever.
Risks: The evidence on whether school retention causally sustains village vitality is mixed: Hungarian village-level data show schools correlate with better demographic performance but worse employment/welfare outcomes, meaning preservation may not deliver the broader revitalization sometimes assumed.; Rural teacher-supply crisis (6,200 annual retirements projected 2023-2030, aging workforce, 60% of new teachers resigning within 3-4 years) could undermine a 'keep open' policy through attrition even with funding safeguards in place.
Evidence: moderate

TP-live-t5 — Alternative-maintainer pathway: church/NGO transfer with parental veto
Goal: Enable transfer of threatened small state schools to church or NGO maintainers, subject to the existing parental veto, as a way to keep the building in local educational use without requiring state-funded structural reform.
Mechanisms: Parents retain a formal decision-making veto over church-school transfer of a state primary school: transfer cannot proceed if more than half of parents do not support it, establishing a real constraint that channels this pathway through community consent rather than unilateral administrative decision.; When transfer occurs, the government office reassigns catchment-area boundaries to another state school, and affected parents retain a choice between the new mandatory-admission state school and the transferred church school, both obligated to admit the child, preserving a minimal choice architecture.; Documented precedent (2026 Kolping case) shows NGO/religious maintainers absorbing small-town schools into shared management, though the maintainer explicitly limited scope to school administration without committing to broader community social-care functions, indicating the pathway's scope is narrower than a full community-hub model.
Risks: In single-school villages, ownership-model change functions as a de facto reduction of educational choice even when the building remains open, since church school attendance already rose 60% (2001-2011) and 47% (2011-2014), raising concern about narrowing effective options over time.; Church-run schools' greater autonomy and structural differences from state schools could create inconsistency in curricular or pedagogical standards across the rural network.
Evidence: moderate
