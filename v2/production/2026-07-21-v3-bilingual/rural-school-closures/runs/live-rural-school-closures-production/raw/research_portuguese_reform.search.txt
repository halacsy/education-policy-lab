# Research Notes: Hungarian Village School Depopulation Problem

## Scope of the Hungarian Problem (Facts)

**F1. Territorial coverage gaps are already severe and worsening.** 
By 2024, more than 1,200 of 3,155 settlements had no public educational institutions, raising concerns about territorial equity
. Adjacent early-years data show a similar pattern: 
in 2022, 31% of municipalities had no kindergarten, typically in small villages where the number of kindergarten-aged children did not reach 8
. (Source: EU Education and Training Monitor 2025, citing Varga 2024; researchgate.net paper on territorial patterns 2025.)

**F2. Rural school closures have already occurred at scale via forced amalgamation, not organic decline alone.** 
Pressed amalgamations/closures between 2004 and 2008 halved the numbers of independently run units
 in Hungary. This followed a longer period (until 2004) when 
closures or amalgamations occurred only rarely; the rural school network was kept relatively intact until as late as 2004 as a result of state support provided specifically to small villages to maintain schools
. This is a historical/administrative fact, distinct from demographic decline itself.

**F3. Financing structure disadvantages small rural schools mechanically.** 
Rural primary schools exhibit lower results in all performance measures than urban schools as enrollments are decreasing as a direct result of state support that is less than urban schools... because the amount of state subsidy is directly based on number of enrollments
. (Note: source is an older reference work; funding-formula details should be verified against current KRÉTA/centralized budget rules, but the enrollment-linked subsidy logic is a documented structural driver, not merely a symptom.)

## Governance History (Fact, contested interpretation)

**F4. The 2013 centralization (KLIK) was explicitly framed as an equity/rationalization tool but is widely assessed as dysfunctional.** 
In 2013, the central government assumed responsibility for public schools through the Public Education Act, transferring maintenance duties from local authorities to the state to standardize operations and mitigate inequalities... Schools were consolidated under the Klebelsberg Institution Maintenance Centre (KLIK), a highly centralized body that curtailed institutional autonomy by centralizing financial control, teacher employment, and curriculum oversight
. Critical assessments report 
instead of meeting its original goals – creating an effective and cheap way to run educational institutions and balancing out differences between schools of poorer and richer areas, the KLIK became the head of an inoperable, over-centralized and opaque system... in which teachers find it easier to buy chalk sticks or stacks of paper themselves
. **Contested**: this is a critical secondary-source assessment (4liberty.eu, a policy commentary site), not an official evaluation; it should be treated as one perspective on centralization's effects rather than a settled empirical finding.

**Historical precedent (inference-flagged fact):** Communist-era planners used near-identical rationalization logic — 
the regime judged that the best chance to improve the standard of education lay in diverting pupils from ill-equipped institutions in small villages to well equipped town schools, propagating these notions in a technocratic language as "rationalization"
 — suggesting the efficiency-vs-community tension is not new but recurring across regimes with different declared ends.

## Quality and Segregation Evidence (Contested)

**F5. Small-school quality evidence is mixed/contested.** One older source states rural schools 
exhibit lower results in all performance measures than urban schools
. However, a peer-reviewed study using 2013 national assessment data found the opposite pattern for "resilience" (outperforming SES predictions): 
urban schools were found to be significantly less likely to be resilient than village schools; regionally, the (rural or small) schools in the vicinity of Budapest seem to have a higher chance to become resilient
, with the explanation that 
effective and resilient schools are more likely to be found in smaller settlements because the effect of school choice on social segregation is greater in bigger settlements... resilient schools are more likely to be located in rural areas and in localities where school-choice options are more restricted
. **This is a direct contradiction between sources and must be flagged as contested**, likely reflecting different measures (raw outcomes vs. SES-adjusted "resilience").

**F6. Small one-class-per-grade schools structurally limit within-school segregation but do not prevent between-school (village-level) segregation.** 
The phenomenon that between-school segregation is much stronger in Hungary than within-school segregation is not independent of the fact that during previous decades the deterioration in efficiency caused by a decline in pupils was typically mitigated by local self-governments by reducing the number of parallel classes, not by closure or amalgamation of schools. This led to a large offer of small schools, but reduced the latitude for within-school separation of pupils with different social and/or ethnic backgrounds. This situation especially applies to the many small schools in small rural settlements that operate only one class per grade
. Related dynamic: 
a segregation mechanism operates via commuting to a smaller extent — due to the enrolment of non-Roma pupils in the schools of other settlements ("white flight")
.

**F7. Legal/rights dimension is active and binding.** In March 2023 
the European Court of Human Rights issued a judgment against Hungary for the racial segregation of Romani children in the Jókai Mór Primary School in Piliscsaba
, finding 
the school was almost exclusively attended by Roma pupils, despite Roma constituting less than 4% of residents in the catchment area
, and ruling that 
the Hungarian State had to adopt measures not only to end segregation at the Jókai Mór school but to ensure the development of a policy to put a stop to segregation in education
 nationally. This creates a binding external constraint on any village-school reform: closures/consolidations that inadvertently re-concentrate Roma pupils in particular buildings carry legal risk independent of enrollment economics.

## Community/Population Feedback Loops (Contested across countries)

**F8. Causal evidence on closure→depopulation is mixed and country-specific.** A Finnish DID study (2010–2018) found 
after school closure, the population of the surrounding community decreased more than before school closure
, concluding closures raise 
questions not just about the individual school... but more broadly about rural development
. By contrast, research on Sweden found 
limited empirical evidence for this standpoint
 (that closures cause further decline), and a Greek Islands case study frames it as contested: 
the closure of local schools is a negative factor for the local community, as it eliminates hope for growth; on the other hand, it is the wider lack of basic services and inaccessibility that pushes a community into oblivion
 — implying the school itself may be a symptom rather than sole cause. **This is genuinely contested cross-national evidence, not settled**.

**F9. Community mobilization and social capital can alter outcomes independent of top-down structural decisions.** A Hungary-specific case study found 
parental choices and community actions might resist state policies; inter-community and external networks and social capital generated by them determine the achievements of community action
 in "rescuing" a threatened small village school — evidence that non-structural, bottom-up organizing is a real (if not universally replicable) lever, separate from formal funding-formula reform.

## Non-Structural and Mitigating Measures Found Elsewhere (Comparative evidence, not Hungary policy)

**F10. Multigrade pedagogy and rural teacher networks are the leading non-closure quality interventions in the OECD literature.** OECD notes initiatives such as Chile's Puentes Educativos that 
exploit the potential of multigrade teaching in rural schools for developing students' competencies like creativity, collaboration and critical thinking through changes in rural teachers' educational practices, curriculum, and educational materials
, plus European multigrade-teacher networking: 
the Network Multigrade Education (NEMED) was a network of rural schools across nine European countries...A "Virtual Rural School" was set up within the programme, allowing e-learning and opportunities to share best practice and experiences and reduce teacher isolation
.

**F11. "Community school" (extended-function) models are studied as an explicit alternative to closure.** A quantitative/qualitative study (58 interviews) found 
rural schools and municipalities positively evaluate the approach of community schools with extended functions
 as an alternative to closure amid depopulation — though this is a single regional study (apparently Baltic/Eastern European context) and generalizability to Hungary is not established in these results.

**F12. Structural federation/clustering and a "presumption against closure" is the main coordinated-reform template used elsewhere (Wales).** Wales introduced rules including 
a presumption against the closure of rural schools... alternatives include linking up with other schools, known as federation... a new £2.5m rural and small schools grant... to boost the use of technology... also available to increase community use of school buildings
. This is a coordinated bundle (funding + federation/shared leadership + technology + community-use incentives), explicitly not a guarantee against closure but a proceduralized alternatives-first requirement — an illustrative comparator, not Hungarian policy.

**F13. Transportation cost/wellbeing trade-offs are a documented constraint on any consolidation strategy.** Cross-national evidence indicates 
rural school bus commutes are longer than urban counterparts and often exceed 30 minutes; students with longer travel times tend to skip breakfast and report lower grades, poorer fitness, fewer social activities, and worse study habits, disproportionately burdening lower-income households
, while 
bus operating costs per student are about 40% higher in rural districts, marginal cost of an additional student about 2.5 times higher, and an additional mile costs about 10 times more than in urban districts
. Hungary-specific mitigation is nascent: 
since 2021, the number of sustainable active-travel-to-school programs in Hungary has steadily increased
 (Pedibus/Bicibus pilots), but these are small-scale local pilots, not a systemic transport-burden solution for consolidated rural catchments.

---

### Answering the two framing questions (synthesis, not new sourced claims)
- **Non-structural measures alone**: Evidence is genuinely mixed. Community mobilization/social capital (F9) and community-school extended functions (F11) show documented cases of sustaining schools without closure, and multigrade-pedagogy/network interventions (F10) address quality without touching school structure. But these coexist with hard financing mechanics tied to enrollment (F3) and legal desegregation obligations (F7) that are structural in nature — suggesting non-structural measures can mitigate but the literature does not show them sufficient alone where funding formulas remain enrollment-linked.
- **Coordinated reforms referenced in the comparative literature** cluster around: funding-formula decoupling from raw enrollment, federation/shared-leadership models, a procedural presumption-against-closure with alternatives review (F12), transport-burden mitigation (F13), and desegregation-compliant catchment design (F7) — evidence for Hungary specifically remains fragmented and partly contested (F5, F8) rather than establishing one validated national model.
