# Research Notes: Early Academic Selection in Hungary (6- and 8-Year Gimnázium Tracks)

## Scope and structural facts

1. **Structure of selection.** 
Tracking can partially start at Grade 5 (age 10-11) or at Grade 7 (age 12-13), when students can apply to an 8-year or 6-year general secondary school, and early school choice/selection may hamper equity
, per the OECD's Hungary policy profile. 
8-year education is the standard mode; the 6- and 8-year gimnázium alternatives were introduced in the early 1990s
, i.e., they are a post-socialist re-introduction of pre-existing selective grammar-school traditions, not a permanent historical constant. This is a **fact** about system design, not a normative judgment.

2. **Admission mechanism.** Entry to these tracks depends on competitive tests and grades rather than residence. 
Admission to a secondary school depends on an admission test in reading and mathematics and the teacher-given school grades from the last two academic years of primary education
 — for the general (academic) track this admission screening is applied at ages 10-12 for the 6-/8-year gimnázium, years earlier than in most OECD systems where tracking occurs around 15-16. 
While most OECD countries will start tracking students aged 15 or 16, some countries, such as Germany, will start doing this as early as age 10 — though the tracking age varies considerably, even between industrialized countries
. Hungary's age-10 selection point places it among the earliest-tracking systems in Europe.

## Selectivity relative to international peers

3. **Hungary is unusually selective even compared to other early-tracking countries.** A CEU working paper using PISA 2015 science data found that 
selection in Hungary is even stronger than in three out of the four European countries (Austria, Germany, Lithuania and the Netherlands) that stream pupils into parallel school tracks as early as the age of 10 or 11
. The same source notes that 
the value of the socio-economic inclusion index for Hungary is the lowest among all developed countries, signaling higher comparative selectivity than the traditional indicator... suggests
. This is a specific, sourced empirical claim, not simply general commentary.

4. **Between-school segregation is the highest in the EU.** According to the EU's 2025 Education and Training Monitor country report, 
Hungary has the highest level of segregation of students from less and more advantaged socio-economic groups into different schools (Gutiérrez et al., 2020), and Hungary's segregation of the most and least disadvantaged students was the highest in the EU in the 2022 PISA study
. This measures overall school-level segregation (which includes but is not limited to the gimnázium tracks), so it is directly relevant but not a clean causal isolation of the 6-/8-year track effect specifically — an important caveat.

## Distributional consequences: who is affected

5. **Roma students are structurally concentrated outside the selective/academic pathway.** 
Segregation of Roma pupils is the second highest in the region, with 62% of Roma children aged 6-15 attending schools where all or most pupils are Roma
. Separately, 
about 14 percent of students in Hungarian primary schools are Roma, and Roma children are substantially more likely to have multiple social disadvantages than their non-Roma peers, which results in significantly lower average test scores by grade 8
 (Kertesi & Kézdi). Given that admission to 6-/8-year gimnázium tracks is grade- and test-based at age 10-12, and Roma students' test scores are systematically lower on average by that age, the track's selection criteria interact with, rather than remedy, prior inequality. This is an **inference** connecting two separately documented facts, not a single direct finding about the tracks themselves.

6. **Family socio-economic background strongly predicts achievement generally in Hungary, more so than the OECD average.** 
The gap in the reading comprehension scores of the 10 per cent most socio-economically advantaged students were 170 points higher than that of the 10 per cent most socio-economically disadvantaged pupils
, a differential 
equal to four years of formal education
. The OECD similarly found 
the impact of socio-economic background on mathematics performance of 15-year-olds was among the largest in OECD countries, with high variance between schools (school selection is made based on students' records)
. This is general system-level evidence of steep social gradient, not specific to the gimnázium tracks, but the tracks operate within and are argued by researchers to reinforce this gradient.

7. **Downstream effects of being routed into a lower-status/high-poverty school (the flip side of selective creaming).** Using large administrative datasets, Hermann & Kisfalusi find that 
pupils in segregated schools are less likely to complete upper secondary education, pass the general secondary school leaving examination and enter tertiary education
, and that 
in schools with poor learning environments (where at least 50% of students in a class are below basic performance levels), not only are students disadvantaged, but teachers are often less prepared
. Mechanistically, 
instructional quality might be lower in high-poverty schools due to unequal distribution of human and material resources, and more qualified teachers might self-select into schools with a higher share of high-status students where the circumstances of teaching are more attractive
. This describes the "residual" schools that lose their higher-achieving peers when the latter exit into 6-/8-year gimnázium tracks — a plausible mechanism (**inference**) though the cited studies examine high-poverty schools generally, not the specific counterfactual of gimnázium creaming.

8. **Existing desegregation rules had to be built precisely because selective tracks are hard to reverse locally.** 
Administrative desegregation rules were introduced, including rules guaranteeing the progression of pupils in schools with fewer than eight grades, because if an institution in a disadvantaged small town had fewer than eight classes, none of the larger institutions in a nearby town would take the difficult-to-handle, predominantly Roma pupils
. This indicates that early-selection structures (which fragment the 8-year comprehensive track into shorter feeder schools) can create downstream placement problems for remaining students, a structural side-effect of the 6-/8-year system's existence rather than a claim about its stated academic purpose.

## Does selection reduce or relocate sorting? (Comparative/causal evidence)

9. **Cross-national causal literature suggests early tracking (as a general institutional feature, not Hungary-specific) increases inequality without clearly raising average performance.** The seminal Hanushek & Woessmann difference-in-differences studies conclude: 
the results suggest that early tracking increases educational inequality, while less clear, there is also a tendency for early tracking to reduce mean performance
. A more recent replication states 
early tracking contributes to increasing overall variability and in particular the gap between children of different social backgrounds
, and a meta-analytic synthesis of 21 assessment cycles similarly 
found strong evidence that tracking increased social achievement gaps
. **Contested element:** this literature is about tracking systems in general (mostly Germany/Austria-type systems) and is not a Hungary-specific causal estimate of the 6-/8-year gimnázium policy; extrapolation to Hungary is an **inference**, albeit one explicitly supported by domain experts (see note 3 above placing Hungary among/above these same comparator countries).

10. **A counter-finding complicates the "tracking increases within-school homogeneity" assumption.** Newer research raises doubt about one mechanical assumption behind early-tracking equity concerns: 
the study by Suchań and Bergmüller (2010) showed surprisingly, within-school achievement variation was not significantly related to the first age of selection: there was nearly no change in within-school variation between age 10 and age 15 for countries that first tracked students into different schools at the age of 10 and those in which students remained untracked until the age of 15
. This is **contested/mixed evidence**: it does not overturn the between-school segregation and social-gap findings above, but it qualifies claims that early tracking straightforwardly "sorts" students into homogeneous ability groups within remaining schools — sorting effects may be more about *between-school* stratification (documented strongly for Hungary) than *within-school* homogenization.
