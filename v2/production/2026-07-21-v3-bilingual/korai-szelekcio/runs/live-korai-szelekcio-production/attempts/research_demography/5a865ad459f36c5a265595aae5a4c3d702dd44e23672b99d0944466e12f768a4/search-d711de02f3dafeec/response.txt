# Research Notes: Demographic/Cohort Perspective on Hungary's 6- and 8-Year Gimnázium Tracks

## 1. Rapid, accelerating birth-cohort decline shrinks the pool eligible for early selection

Hungary's population has fallen below 9.5 million for the first time in decades, as births declined sharply in 2025, with just 72,000 children born, 7.1% fewer than in 2024
. 
The total fertility rate dropped to 1.31 children per woman in 2025, down from 1.39 a year earlier — the lowest level in 14 years and far below the 2.1 needed for long-term replacement
. This means the age-10 and age-12 cohorts that feed 6- and 8-year gimnázium admissions are structurally shrinking year over year, independent of any policy change. **Fact.**

## 2. The 2011–2024 fertility "bump" has already reversed, meaning near-term cohort shrinkage is now compounding

In the previous decade, Hungary succeeded in increasing its fertility rate, which rose from 1.23 in 2011 to 1.61 in 2021, but since 2022 Hungarian birth rates have once again been falling, with the fertility rate dropping to 1.38 by 2024, and depopulation is now progressing at its fastest rate to date
. 
In 2024, only 77,500 children were born – the lowest figure since records began – representing a year-on-year decline of more than 7,000
. **Fact.** *Inference:* a system relying on early selection at ages 10–12 (5th/7th grade entry) will see its applicant pools thin fastest in the mid-2030s, when today's shrinking birth cohorts reach those ages.

## 3. Geographic child-population trends are strongly polarized, not uniform decline
A 2025 territorial study using settlement-level data 2021–2024 found 
five settlement types via k-means clustering — Overburdened Growth Areas, Declining but Stabilizing Areas, Disadvantaged Declining Areas, Moderately Declining Balanced Areas, and Balanced Growth Areas — revealing a deepening divide between dynamic urban/suburban zones and disadvantaged rural peripheries
. 
Rapidly growing urban/suburban settlements face rising child-teacher ratios (up to 24:1 in some areas), indicating capacity constraints, while disadvantaged children have become increasingly concentrated in rural areas (up to 94% in certain settlements)
. **Fact.** This means viability of selective tracks is not simply a function of the national cohort size but of where within the country children are born.

## 4. A large share of settlements now have no public educational institution at all

By 2024, more than 1,200 of 3,155 settlements had no public educational institutions, raising concerns about territorial equity
. **Fact.** *Inference:* for children in these settlements, 6/8-year gimnázium admission already requires commuting or boarding well before age 10–12; demographic decline plus institutional thinning compounds physical access barriers independent of academic merit.

## 5. 6/8-year gimnázium supply is heavily concentrated in Budapest and a handful of provincial cities, contested against rural need
Directory and ranking data show extended-track gimnáziums cluster in Budapest districts and a short list of larger cities. 
Of the ten best-ranked secondary schools nationally, only one rural gimnázium made the top ten — the Veszprém Lovassy László Gimnázium, which has held roughly the tenth position in the ranking for several years running
. This is a rankings-based, not enrollment-based, indicator, but it is suggestive: elite extended-track programs are disproportionately urban/Budapest-based (Wikipedia/pitagorasz district listing corroborates dense Budapest coverage vs. sparser rural distribution). **Fact, geographically skewed distribution; causal claim about "elite capture" is contested/inferential.**

## 6. Hungary already has among the highest levels of school-based socioeconomic segregation in the EU, and early tracking is implicated

Hungary has the highest level of segregation of students from less and more advantaged socio-economic groups into different schools, and Hungary's segregation of the most and least disadvantaged students was the highest in the EU in the 2022 PISA study
. A CEU research paper states 
Hungary operates one of the most selective education systems in Europe; the OECD PISA indicator of social selection (proportion of total variation in outcomes explained by between-school differences) shows that, per the 2015 PISA science data, selection in Hungary is even stronger than in three out of four other European countries that stream pupils into parallel tracks as early as age 10 or 11
. **Fact, but attribution of this segregation specifically to 6/8-year gimnázium tracks (versus free primary-school choice generally) is contested** — the same source notes segregation is driven more broadly by "free school choice" mechanisms, not only by the extended gimnázium tracks. 
Hungary adopted a free school-choice system for its elementary schools, with complete discretion allowed for schools to hand-pick their students
, meaning early gimnázium selection interacts with, rather than solely causes, broader sorting.

## 7. Academic research on early-tracking value-added: contested causal effects, favoring higher-status families
An academic study using panel data concludes: 
early selective tracks are more beneficial for higher status families; these tracks have a higher value-added in reading and mathematical literacy and in continuing studies in tertiary education
. Further, 
their student composition is of higher status and higher-skilled students, thus they lead to an increase in the inequality of opportunities; weaker tests show that while students of early selective tracks gain, those left in general tracks lose
. **This is a single strand of empirical literature (value-added/panel design) and represents a causal claim that should be marked as contested pending replication**; it is nonetheless a recurring finding across Hungarian sociology-of-education literature (Kertesi & Kézdi and related CEU/Budapest Working Papers cited in the same literature).

## 8. Roma segregation is measurably tied to the broader selective/school-choice system, an equity dimension distinct from cohort-size effects

The segregation of Roma pupils is the second highest in the region, with 62% of Roma children aged 6-15 attending schools where all or most pupils are Roma
, and 
pupils in segregated schools are less likely to complete upper secondary education, pass the general secondary school leaving examination and enter tertiary education
. **Fact**, though the causal chain from "early academic selection specifically" to Roma segregation (versus residential segregation, free school choice, or teacher-quality sorting) is not isolated in these sources and should be treated as associative rather than proven causal.

## 9. Without demographic-adjustment or reform ("counterfactual" / status-quo continuation): capacity mismatch is likely to widen
Combining findings 1–4: if 6/8-year gimnázium capacity (number of programme places) is held constant while (a) national birth cohorts continue shrinking (fact, per items 1–2) and (b) child population is redistributing toward a few growth nodes while emptying rural peripheries (fact, per item 3), then — as **inference, not direct source finding** — the selection ratio (applicants per seat) would likely fall in shrinking/rural areas (reducing selectivity, potentially undermining the "excellence" rationale) while remaining high or rising in Budapest/growth-node schools (intensifying competition and status-sorting there). No single source directly models this joint effect for gimnázium tracks specifically; this is a synthesis across the cited demographic and geographic studies, not a data-backed projection specific to 6/8-year gimnáziums.

## 10. Precedent: general rural-school contraction shows political and social costs of scale-driven consolidation, relevant analogically to gimnázium viability debates
Historical Hungarian evidence on ordinary (non-selective) rural primary schools shows that consolidation driven by demographic/fiscal logic is socially contested: 
the combination of legislative changes and fiscal arrangements of the central government brought the prospects of small rural schools to a turning point: there was a dramatic speed-up in the number of grassroots reorganisations and within four years the number of villages maintaining primary schools dropped by a half
, and 
this process fed into 2007 events when closure and merger of rural schools due to top-down pressure attracted considerable media attention, prompting an NGO campaign called "Human Chain for Rural Schools"
. **Fact about a related but distinct policy episode (ordinary primary schools, not gimnázium tracks)** — offered as analogical evidence on how Hungarian rural communities and politics have historically responded to consolidation pressures driven by demographic decline; not direct evidence about 6/8-year gimnázium viability specifically.

---

### Summary of contested vs. settled points
- **Settled/well-documented:** national birth-cohort decline and its acceleration since 2022 (items 1–2); geographic polarization of child population and rising "no-school" settlements (items 3–4); Hungary's top-EU ranking in socioeconomic school segregation (item 6); Roma school segregation levels (item 8).
- **Contested/inferential:** direct causal attribution of segregation/inequality specifically to the 6/8-year gimnázium mechanism as opposed to broader free-school-choice and residential sorting (items 6–8); any projection of future seat-competition dynamics under continued demographic decline (item 9), which is a synthesis, not a sourced finding; and the applicability of general rural-school consolidation history to the specific case of selective gimnázium tracks (item 10).

No policy recommendation is offered; these notes are descriptive and analytic inputs only.
