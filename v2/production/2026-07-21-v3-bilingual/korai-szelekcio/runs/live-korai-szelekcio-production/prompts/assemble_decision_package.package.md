- TP-live-t2 / L-live-equity_and_social_mobility / supports_with_conditions: Full abolition of age 10-12 selection targets the precise structural mechanism identified in the evidence base as driving Hungary's exceptionally early tracking and its associated segregation and achievement-gap outcomes. Cross-national tracking literature (not Hungary-specific but extrapolated to comparable systems) supports the expectation that removing early selection reduces inequality, though a finding on within-school variance suggests the mechanism operates mainly through between-school stratification, meaning abolition would need to be paired with attention to how schools sort post-abolition to fully realize gains. No isolated Hungarian causal evidence exists for the gymnázium track specifically, so the size of the equity gain in Hungary is inferred, not measured.
- TP-live-t3 / L-live-equity_and_social_mobility / supports_with_conditions: Delaying selection age targets the age-10 entry point specifically identified as most family-capital-dependent, with Polish precedent showing mean gains and reduced between-school variance from a comparable delay. However, retaining a six-year track even in delayed form preserves a residual sorting mechanism, and the evidence base for the causal link between age of selection and inequity (versus mean performance) is described as correlational rather than definitively causal, and not Hungary-specific.
- TP-live-t4 / L-live-equity_and_social_mobility / supports_with_conditions: This proposal keeps the structural sorting point intact but attempts to counteract its distributional consequences through admissions-side guardrails and a desegregation mandate. It directly addresses documented accountability gaps and threshold-tightening risks, and has institutional precedent from existing administrative desegregation rules. However, the evidence explicitly notes segregation arises through multiple channels beyond gimnázium admissions (residential sorting, free primary choice), meaning guardrails on one entry point cannot address the full distributional problem, and the underlying selection mechanism—identified as the earliest in Europe—remains unchanged.
- TP-live-t5 / L-live-equity_and_social_mobility / supports_with_conditions: This package proposal is the only option that explicitly targets the multiple channels through which segregation is documented to operate (teacher distribution, network capacity, equity-program integration) alongside a structural change, directly responding to evidence that structural change alone (Finland) was historically insufficient and that evidence dissemination alone (Poland) did not sustain reform. This is the most comprehensive fit to the lens's criteria of distributional impact, access, segregation, and mobility, but the coordination complexity across simultaneous workstreams and dependency on non-state maintainer cooperation introduce substantial implementation-linked equity risk not present in narrower proposals.
- TP-live-t1 / L-live-demography / cautions: From a demographic lens, the baseline proposal is best understood as a counterfactual-generating device rather than a policy: it holds admission capacity fixed while cohort size and spatial distribution shift under it. Given accelerating fertility decline (1.31 TFR in 2025, 72,000 births, 7.1% down year-on-year) and the reversal of the 2011-2021 fertility recovery, the age-10/12 cohorts feeding gimnázium admission will continue contracting structurally, not cyclically. Simultaneously, the geography of that contraction is uneven: growth nodes retain or increase demand pressure while rural/declining periphery settlements lose population share, and over 1,200 of 3,155 settlements already lack any educational institution. Holding capacity constant under this dual dynamic guarantees diverging selection ratios by geography, and the historical precedent of contentious 2000s rural school consolidation suggests unmanaged demographic drift towards under-enrolled rural seats and over-subscribed urban ones is not politically costless.
- TP-live-t2 / L-live-demography / neutral: Full abolition of early selection interacts with demographic contraction in a way that could reduce, rather than amplify, transition risk: as cohorts entering age-10/12 selection shrink structurally (F-live-demography-01, F-live-demography-02), the absolute number of pupils requiring reassignment under a comprehensive model is itself declining year over year, potentially easing the capacity-matching problem relative to a stable or growing cohort. However, this demographic tailwind interacts with severe spatial unevenness: because 6/8-year programmes are concentrated in Budapest and a few provincial cities (F-live-demography-05), a comprehensive restructuring must simultaneously resolve a geography problem (where do displaced-track schools/teachers relocate) and a segregation problem (Hungary's EU-highest socioeconomic and Roma segregation, F-live-demography-06, F-live-demography-08), which shrinking cohorts alone do not solve. The planning horizon required for full abolition is long relative to the pace of demographic change, raising the question of whether the reform target itself will have shifted by implementation.
- TP-live-t3 / L-live-demography / supports_with_conditions: A delayed-selection design intersects the demographic evidence in a mixed way. It retains the six-year track's geography-linked concentration problem — programmes remain clustered in Budapest and a handful of cities (F-live-demography-05) — so spatial access inequities persist even as the age of first selection shifts later. On cohort trajectory, delaying selection closer to age 14 shortens the multi-year window during which shrinking, geographically uneven cohorts must be tracked into scarce programme seats, which may somewhat reduce the compounding effect of early misallocation documented under proposal t9 (selection ratios diverging by geography). However, because the six-year track is preserved rather than eliminated, the underlying mechanism connecting family capital to early access (a demographic sorting channel operating independently of cohort size) is not removed, so contraction of the applicant pool does not by itself narrow the socioeconomic access gap.
- TP-live-t4 / L-live-demography / supports_with_conditions: Retaining tracks while adding equity guardrails directly engages the demographic finding that Hungary's between-school selection and Roma segregation are among the highest in the EU/region (F-live-demography-06, F-live-demography-08), since quotas and desegregation mandates target the sorting mechanism rather than cohort size per se. This is a plausible fit because segregation appears to be substantially a distributional/institutional phenomenon (documented independent of cohort trajectory), so demographic contraction alone would not resolve it and targeted guardrails could. However, the proposal does not address the spatial concentration of programmes in Budapest and a few cities (F-live-demography-05) or the settlement-level access gap (over 1,200 settlements without institutions, F-live-demography-04); guardrails on admissions cannot correct for the absence of a nearby track option. Under continued cohort shrinkage and urban-rural divergence, a counterfactual without this proposal would likely see selection ratios fall in shrinking/rural areas while remaining high in growth nodes (F-live-demography-09, weak evidence), intensifying status competition specifically where guardrails would be least able to reach absent complementary network measures.
- TP-live-t5 / L-live-demography / supports_with_conditions: The package approach is the only proposal that explicitly incorporates a network-planning bridge alongside structural and non-structural change, which is the most demographically literate design given the documented territorial polarization: five distinct settlement types ranging from overburdened growth areas to disadvantaged declining areas (F-live-demography-03), and over 1,200 settlements without any educational institution (F-live-demography-04). Combining structural selection reform with network rationalization directly addresses the counterfactual risk identified elsewhere (t1, weak evidence in F-live-demography-09) that constant capacity under diverging cohort trends would produce simultaneously falling rural selection ratios and intensifying urban status competition. The historical precedent of contentious rural school consolidation (F-live-demography-10) is directly relevant to the network-planning component and suggests this workstream carries its own distinct political-feasibility risk that is separate from, and additive to, the risks of the structural selection-age change itself. The multi-workstream design's complexity is therefore partly a demographic necessity — a single-lever reform cannot simultaneously address cohort contraction, spatial polarization, and segregation — but this also means the demographic case for its feasibility rests on the weakest-rated finding in this evidence set (F-live-demography-09).
- TP-live-t1 / L-live-school_network_planning / cautions: From a school-network-planning perspective, the baseline proposal is descriptively well-grounded in documented geographic mismatch but offers no mechanism to resolve it. The network already exhibits a stark spatial imbalance: nearly half of 6/8-year gimnázium places sit in small towns, roughly a quarter in provincial cities, over a fifth in Budapest, and almost none in villages, while flagship urban schools face oversubscription near 10x and several county-town schools carry unfilled seats. Holding capacity constant while cohorts shrink and urban demand concentrates will, on network-planning grounds alone, widen the gap between over-demanded and under-demanded nodes rather than close it, since nothing in the proposal redistributes places, adjusts catchments, or alters transport/access conditions.
- TP-live-t2 / L-live-school_network_planning / insufficient_evidence: Full structural abolition would remove the network-level driver of the current geographic mismatch by eliminating the age-10/12 selection point that produces both flagship-school oversubscription and peripheral under-enrollment, but the network-planning lens flags that converting roughly 25,962 students and over 190 differentiated institutions (100 eight-grade schools plus additional six-grade institutions) into a uniform comprehensive structure is a major capacity-reallocation exercise whose feasibility this lens cannot itself certify. The historical precedent of the 1995 amendment shows that policy signals alone, without full abolition, already triggered voluntary reversion by some municipalities, indicating both that the network is signal-sensitive and that abrupt structural change carries genuine transition risk at the institutional level, not just the political level emphasized in the proposal's own risk section.
- TP-live-t3 / L-live-school_network_planning / supports_with_conditions: Delaying selection toward age 14 while phasing out the age-10 eight-year track specifically targets the institutional segment with the most geographically concentrated, small-town-converted-vocational origin (38 of 50 documented small-town examples), which the network lens can assess concretely. However, retaining a six-year track means roughly half of the existing 121 small-town and additional urban institutions retain their function, so the geographic footprint of oversubscription/under-enrollment mismatch is only partially altered, not resolved, and the proposal does not specify how capacity at former eight-year sites would be reassigned within the network.
- TP-live-t4 / L-live-school_network_planning / neutral: Retaining the existing network footprint while re-engineering admissions is, from a network-planning standpoint, the least geographically disruptive of the proposals, since it makes no claim on physical capacity, catchment boundaries, or transport arrangements — it operates entirely within the current spatial distribution (roughly half in small towns, a quarter in provincial cities, over a fifth in Budapest, minimal village presence). This is a genuine strength for feasibility but also a genuine limitation: it does nothing to correct the underlying geographic mismatch between oversubscribed flagship urban schools and under-enrolled county-town programs, since quota and threshold changes affect who gets admitted, not where capacity physically exists.
- TP-live-t5 / L-live-school_network_planning / supports_with_conditions: This package proposal is the only one among the five that explicitly names network rationalization as a required workstream alongside structural and non-structural change, directly acknowledging the documented urban-oversubscription/rural-under-enrollment mismatch as an inheritable transition risk. This is a genuine network-planning strength, but the lens also notes that bundling network rationalization with legislative, teacher-distribution, and equity-program workstreams multiplies coordination points, and that maintainer dependency (church- and foundation-run schools) directly affects whether physical capacity adjustments in the network can actually be executed, since these maintainers control significant shares of the institutional base outside direct central authority.
- TP-live-t1 / L-live-education_finance / cautions: From a public-finance lens, the baseline proposal is fiscally passive: it holds nominal capacity constant while demographic change reallocates real per-pupil resources unevenly across geography. This creates a de facto subsidy pattern favoring oversubscribed urban flagship schools (implicitly rationing scarce, high-value seats) while rural capacity sits underused, wasting sunk infrastructure and staffing costs. No mechanism exists to redirect resources toward the disadvantaged periphery, so cost incidence continues to fall on low-SES and Roma students who lack access to the well-resourced tracks, while benefits concentrate upward. Direct fiscal cost data for comparing track types is absent from the evidence base, which limits precision but does not change the qualitative incidence pattern implied by segregation and staffing findings.
- TP-live-t2 / L-live-education_finance / supports_with_conditions: Full structural abolition represents the highest fiscal-magnitude intervention among the proposals, requiring system-wide reallocation of teaching staff, curricula, and facilities from the selective track structure into a unified comprehensive model. From a value-for-money standpoint, cross-national precedent (Poland's 1999 delay) suggests potential system-wide mean gains concentrated among currently disadvantaged students, which would represent a favorable equity-per-cost profile if realized, but no direct Hungarian fiscal cost estimates exist to quantify transition or steady-state spending. Cost incidence would shift: currently advantaged families lose exclusive early access to elite peer/resource concentration, while the resource base becomes more evenly distributed—an explicit redistribution of the incentive structure driving family investment in early test-prep and enrollment strategy.
- TP-live-t3 / L-live-education_finance / supports_with_conditions: Delaying selection age represents an intermediate fiscal-magnitude option: it requires reallocating admission points and adjusting one track's entry timing rather than dismantling the entire structure, likely implying lower transition cost than full abolition while still requiring curriculum and staffing adjustment at the affected entry grades. The incentive structure shifts partially—removing the age-10 entry point closes off the earliest and most family-capital-dependent access channel, but retaining a later selective entry preserves some upward resource concentration and does not fully equalize cost incidence between tracked and untracked students.
- TP-live-t4 / L-live-education_finance / supports_with_conditions: Retaining the track structure while adding admissions guardrails is a lower fiscal-magnitude intervention than structural abolition or delay, since it modifies allocation rules and adds an administrative desegregation mandate rather than reallocating the underlying school network or curriculum. However, its value for money is bounded by a documented limitation: segregation arises through multiple channels (residential sorting, free primary choice) beyond gimnázium admissions, so guardrails targeting only the admissions mechanism may capture only a partial share of the total segregation problem for the resources spent. Quota mechanisms also carry cost-incidence implications by explicitly reallocating access away from currently-favored applicants toward reserved-access groups, which is a direct, low-cost lever compared to physical capacity or staffing changes.
- TP-live-t5 / L-live-education_finance / supports_with_conditions: The package proposal has the highest fiscal magnitude and broadest cost incidence of all five options: it stacks structural change costs with parallel spending on curriculum reform, teacher-distribution correction, targeted equity programs, and network-planning bridge measures, plus a dedicated evidence-dissemination and consultation workstream. This magnitude is justified on value-for-money grounds by the finding that structural change alone (Finland's model) was historically insufficient without parallel non-structural measures, and that narrowly-funded equity overlays (IPR, Útravaló-Macika, Arany János, Tanoda) failed as marginalized, underfunded add-ons rather than integrated, sustained investments—implying that under-resourcing any one component would undermine the whole package's return. The teacher-distribution correction component directly targets a documented resource-incidence mechanism (less qualified teachers concentrated in disadvantaged schools), which if left unaddressed by any single-lever reform would preserve a key inequality-reproducing cost allocation.
- TP-live-t1 / L-live-legal_and_governance / cautions: From a legal and governance perspective, the baseline proposal requires no new legal authority, but it perpetuates a documented accountability gap: the central maintainer (Klebelsberg Center) has full legal capacity over admissions and operations since 2013 centralization, yet explicitly disclaimed any duty to monitor or address segregation in 2014 litigation. Retaining the status quo leaves this gap intact indefinitely, while Hungary's constitutional framework's tension between parental free-choice rights and principal-level gatekeeping remains unresolved. No enforceability mechanism is introduced or removed.
- TP-live-t2 / L-live-legal_and_governance / cautions: Full structural abolition would require primary legislative amendment to the Public Education Act and likely constitutional-level scrutiny given Article XVI (parental upbringing choice) and Article X (freedom of learning/teaching) provisions; it would remove the age-10/12 selection point that current law and centralized administration jointly sustain. This directly resolves the tension identified between formal choice rights and principal-level gatekeeping by eliminating the selective gatekeeping stage itself, but does so by curtailing an existing legally-recognized parental-choice pathway (the 6/8-year gimnázium), raising a rights-compatibility question: removing a currently lawful option is distinct from correcting an accountability gap. Maintainer autonomy is also directly implicated since many selective schools are church-run under formal state agreements; abolition would require renegotiating or overriding these agreements, a legally and politically complex governance change with no established enforcement mechanism given documented resistance to comparable reforms elsewhere.
- TP-live-t3 / L-live-legal_and_governance / cautions: A delay-based reform requires legislative amendment to shift entry ages but represents a more incremental legal change than full abolition, potentially reducing the maintainer-autonomy conflict since church-run 8-year tracks would be phased out (a stronger intervention) while the 6-year track is merely adjusted (a lighter one)—an asymmetric governance approach not fully justified in the proposal's own mechanisms. Enforceability remains uncertain given that a comparably partial Polish delay reform was itself reversed in 2017 despite favorable evidence, suggesting legal durability is not proportional to the reform's structural modesty.

Imposing a statutory desegregation obligation on the maintainer closes this accountability gap through existing administrative channels rather than new structural legislation, and existing administrative precedent for guardrail-style intervention (guaranteed progression rules) supports feasibility. However, quota or reserved-access mechanisms are likely to generate direct rights-compatibility conflict with the Fundamental Law's parental free-choice provisions as operationalized through the Public Education Act's principal-gatekeeping admission process—a tension the evidence base explicitly flags as unresolved and potentially litigable. Maintainer autonomy is also implicated for church-run and foundation schools not directly bound by a Klebelsberg Center-focused mandate, since the desegregation obligation as specified applies to the state maintainer rather than to all track-operating institutions.

It does not explicitly specify which legal authority governs the structural component, leaving ambiguous whether it inherits the same constitutional tensions as t2/t3. DILEMMAS:
- Structural abolition vs. incremental adjustment: how much disruption is justified by equity gains?: Full abolition of age 10/12 selection (TP-live-t2) targets the family-capital sorting mechanism most directly and has the strongest cross-national outcome evidence (Poland 1999, Hanushek & Woessmann), but represents the largest institutional discontinuity, carries documented transition-shock risk (Warsaw admission denials) and a proven reversal precedent even under favorable evidence. Delaying selection (TP-live-t3) or retaining tracks with guardrails (TP-live-t4) trade a smaller, more politically survivable change for a partial, structurally unresolved equity gain, since a residual selective track continues some family-capital sorting. This is not simply an empirical question because even with perfect knowledge of expected effect sizes, reasonable people can disagree about whether a larger but riskier equity gain is preferable to a smaller, more durable one, and about how much weight to give to preserving an existing lawful parental-choice pathway versus removing a sorting mechanism.
- Reform durability vs. speed: does packaging structural change with support workstreams justify the added coordination burden?: TP-live-t5 packages structural change with curriculum, teacher-distribution, equity-program, and network-planning workstreams specifically to avoid the fate of Poland's 2016-17 reversal, where strong outcome evidence alone did not prevent reversal (F-live-polish_reform-08). This design is comparatively best-supported for durability but multiplies veto points and depends on cooperation from church- and foundation-run maintainers that is 'not guaranteed' (F-live-implementation_planning-09, F-live-implementation_planning-08). A narrower, single-lever reform (TP-live-t2 or TP-live-t3) is faster and administratively simpler but inherits the same reversal vulnerability the evidence documents without a comparative solution. This is partly an empirical question about whether coordination capacity exists, but it also embeds a value trade-off between comprehensive, better-targeted reform and simpler, faster action whose political survivability is unresolved either way.
- Guardrails within existing tracks vs. removing the sorting mechanism itself: TP-live-t4 retains the tracking structure but adds admissions quotas, threshold guidance, and a statutory desegregation mandate on the Klebelsberg Center, directly closing a documented accountability gap (F-live-legal_and_governance-09) while requiring no legislative restructuring. However, evidence shows segregation operates through multiple channels beyond gimnázium admissions (residential sorting, free primary choice) (F-live-education_finance-06), and Hungary's 2021-2030 non-structural equity strategy already coexists with the EU's highest segregation ranking (F-live-portuguese_reform-02), suggesting guardrails alone have not shifted outcomes historically. This poses a genuine value conflict: is it preferable to work within existing lawful structures and accept a partial, less certain equity gain, or to accept the legal and political risk of removing the selection mechanism that guardrails cannot fully neutralize?
- Structural reform timing vs. demographic and network uncertainty: Full abolition (TP-live-t2) benefits from a shrinking cohort that eases the absolute capacity-matching burden (F-live-demography-01, F-live-demography-02), yet the planning horizon required for major structural reform is long relative to the pace of demographic change, and 6/8-year programmes are spatially concentrated (F-live-demography-05), meaning geography and segregation problems persist independent of cohort size. TP-live-t5's network-planning bridge addresses this directly, but rests on the weakest-rated demographic finding in the evidence set (F-live-demography-09) regarding whether constant capacity under diverging trends would worsen selection-ratio divergence. This raises an empirically resolvable question about magnitude and timing that is currently unresolved, distinct from the value question of how much structural change to pursue.
- Parental free-choice rights vs. desegregation obligations: Quota-based and reserved-access mechanisms proposed in TP-live-t4 (and implicit in structural alternatives) directly confront Hungary's constitutional protections for parental upbringing choice (Article XVI) and freedom of learning (Article X), which the evidence explicitly flags as an unresolved, potentially litigable tension with principal-level gatekeeping authority (F-live-legal_and_governance-03, F-live-legal_and_governance-06). This is a genuine value conflict between an individual family's right to select an academic pathway for their child and a collective/distributive interest in reducing segregation that a purely rights-based framework does not by itself adjudicate, since both claims have independent normative grounding rather than one simply outranking the other on evidentiary grounds.
- Evidence-based policy vs. political durability: does technical merit matter if reforms get reversed regardless?: Poland's 1999 delay reform produced measured mean gains and reduced variance yet was reversed in 2016-17 despite this evidence (F-live-polish_reform-07, F-live-polish_reform-08); Austria's comprehensive-school push failed for decades despite sustained advocacy (F-live-international_comparison-04). Hungary's own coalition already retreated from 'abolition' language to 'admissions review' within days of initial statements (F-live-political_feasibility-02), and opposition framing invokes ideological rather than technical arguments (F-live-political_feasibility-06). This raises a genuine tension about whether policy design should prioritize the strongest evidence-supported mechanism (favoring TP-live-t2) even if political durability is weak, or whether it should prioritize designs more likely to survive political change (favoring TP-live-t4 or a heavily consultation-oriented TP-live-t5) even at some cost to expected equity gains. This is not resolvable by more outcome evidence alone, since the documented pattern shows evidence does not determine political survival.

- What is the independent causal effect of the 6/8-year gimnázium track structure itself — net of free school choice, residential segregation, and church-school expansion — on socioeconomic and Roma school segregation in Hungary, and what share of total observed segregation would abolition or delay realistically remove versus merely displace into other sorting channels?
- What caused the 2026 divergence in track demand (6-year track +11%, 8-year track −14%), and does this pattern predict accelerating informal closures of peripheral 8-year programs before any formal policy decision is made?
- Does the Polish evidence of mean-achievement gains and reduced between-school variance from delaying tracking (age 12 to 15) generalize to Hungary, given Hungary's different baseline achievement, teacher preparation, and coexisting 4-/6-/8-year track diversity, and what would the expected Hungary-specific effect size be under full abolition (t2) versus partial delay (t3)?
- Under what conditions of stakeholder communication, evidence dissemination, and implementation sequencing could a Hungarian tracking reform (abolition, delay, or guardrail-based) avoid the political reversal Poland experienced in 2016–17, and does Hungary's political economy (elite institutional memory, teacher-union structure, coalition arithmetic) predict greater or lesser durability than Poland's?
- If the 6/8-year tracks were abolished or substantially delayed, into which alternative sorting mechanisms (within-school ability grouping, special-needs classification, private-school enrollment, residential sorting) would segregation migrate, and to what extent would each be legally and institutionally constrainable under current Hungarian law?
- Do the short-term test-score gains for students admitted to 6-year academic tracks persist into adult labor-market outcomes and earnings, particularly for borderline-qualified students near the admission margin, or does the null long-run finding (Gurzó & Horn 2015) indicate that initial gains are pure selection artifacts or are offset by segregation-induced costs to excluded peers?
- What is the magnitude of the peer-composition ('brain drain') effect on remaining students' academic outcomes in feeder schools when top achievers exit into 6/8-year gimnázium tracks, and how does this vary by grade, subject, and student socioeconomic background?
- What fraction of unfilled seats in peripheral 6- and 8-year gimnaziums is attributable to genuine geographic/transport barriers versus rational preference for higher-reputation urban schools, and what would be the cost and delivery timeline of transport/infrastructure investment to close the accessible-barrier component, relative to its equity payoff?
- Is the Klebelsberg Center's 2014 legal position disclaiming a proactive desegregation duty a binding statutory constraint or a discretionary policy interpretation reversible via administrative directive without amending the Public Education Act, and what current staffing, IT, and evaluation capacity exists to support a nationwide segregation-monitoring mandate if imposed?
- Whether the value assigned to preserving elite gymnázium tracks as a mechanism of family social-position protection and credential signaling — as opposed to their role as genuine pedagogical value-added — should outweigh documented equity costs is a question of competing social values (meritocratic reward vs. egalitarian access) that no measurement of achievement or segregation effects can resolve by itself.
