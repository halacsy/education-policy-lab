# Research Notes: 6- and 8-Year Gimnázium Tracks — Infrastructure & Access Perspective

## 1. Structural definition and selection points (FACT)
Hungary's basic education is delivered in 8-year single-structure primary schools (age 6–14), after which students choose among tracks; the 
upper secondary grammar school (gimnázium) provides general education, mostly for 4-years but there are also 6- and 8- grade high schools
. OECD confirms selection points precisely: 
tracking can partially start at Grade 5 (age 10-11) or at Grade 7 (age 12-13), when students can apply to an 8-year or 6-year general secondary school
.

## 2. Geographic distribution of supply is highly uneven (FACT)
A dedicated study of schools offering 6/8-grade programs found: 
nearly half (121) of schools offering 6/8-grade training are located in small towns, a quarter (63) in provincial large cities (county seats or cities with county rank), more than a fifth in the capital (59), and only 8 are in villages
. This confirms the track is structurally an urban/small-town phenomenon, with village-level access being a statistical outlier rather than a norm.

## 3. Supply origin shapes settlement pattern (FACT)

The "pure" six- and eight-grade gimnázium, and its combination with primary schooling, is most characteristic of the capital; the capital together with provincial large cities are the most important sites for the full (4-, 6-, and/or 8-grade) gimnázium offer
. Conversely, 
smaller towns prefer a combined gimnázium/vocational offer, of which there is hardly any example in the capital
. Separately: 
schools originally focused on vocational training typically switched to the new [6/8-grade] program in small towns (38 of 50), while "primary schools grown into gimnáziums" are large-city institutions (21 in Budapest, 15 in provincial large cities out of 50)
.

## 4. Scale of the system (FACT)

The eight-grade gimnázium is a defining training type in Hungarian public education; a total of 100 schools run 8-year programs, and about 7% of gimnázium students study in this school type today
. More recent aggregate data: 
25,962 students studied in 6- or 8-grade gimnázium in 2024/2025, roughly matching prior years' figures
 per KSH data.

## 5. Capacity has not expanded despite heavy demand — a supply-side rigidity (FACT, contested significance)

Despite enormous demand for "small-gimnázium" places, place numbers have not increased; in last year's admission process there was a top gimnázium where one stream saw roughly tenfold oversubscription
. Concrete examples: 
38 places at ELTE's practice primary/gimnázium 8-grade stream drew 369 applicants
, and 
32 places for the 6+1-grade program at the Radnóti school drew 300 applicants
. Inference (source's own framing, contested): 
place numbers in 6/8-grade programs have been essentially unchanged for years... recent education-policy decisions instead tend to steer students toward vocational training after 8th grade
 — i.e., capacity stagnation may reflect deliberate policy steering rather than a physical/geographic constraint.

## 6. Spatial mismatch between demand hotspots and unfilled capacity elsewhere (FACT, 2026 admissions cycle)
In the same year that flagship urban schools were massively oversubscribed, 
nationwide there were still available places in several 8- and 6-grade gimnáziums, with some institutions having more than 10 empty places left in their classes
, and 
6-grade gimnáziums have considerably more openings than 8-grade ones
. Named examples of extraordinary/second-round admissions with open seats include 
Miskolc SZC Mezőkövesd Szent László Gimnázium (10 empty places), Nyíregyháza University practice school (2), and Pécs Árpád Fejedelem Gimnázium (3)
, alongside 
a Debrecen institution with 14 empty places and Encs Váci Mihály Gimnázium with 6
. Inference: this pattern indicates that under-subscription is concentrated in mid-size/peripheral county towns while capital and top provincial-city schools remain highly oversubscribed — a distributional (not aggregate) shortage.

## 7. Selective commuting and school-choice geography complicate pure "network delivery" analysis (FACT/methodological note)
Segregation researchers explicitly excluded 6/8-grade gimnázium sites from primary-school segregation indices because their catchment logic differs from normal district-based primary enrollment: 
the analysis was restricted to primary-school sites, excluding six- and eight-grade gimnázium sites
, noting this 
certainly biases the calculated segregation indicators downward
. Cities are highlighted as a special case because 
within reasonable commuting distances a relatively varied school offer exists, which in principle would offer good chances for social mixing
, yet 
selective commuting also influences the social disparities manifested in schools' student composition
. Inference: geographic proximity does not guarantee access equity — self-selected commuting patterns interact with school choice to concentrate advantaged students regardless of nominal catchment-area design.

## 8. Socioeconomic selection effect linked to the track (FACT, research consensus but politically contested)
Academic literature: 
the early-selective academic tracks skim off the best students first at age 10, then at age 12, and finally at age 14 all students enter secondary level
, and 
higher socioeconomic status students are more likely to attend [these tracks]
. A Hungarian pedagogical-research journal notes 
children of university-educated fathers attend six- or eight-grade gimnáziums at an above-average rate, and attend four-grade gimnáziums at a below-average rate
. OECD's own country profile states plainly: 
early school choice, selection may hamper equity
, and separately that 
the impact of socio-economic background on mathematics performance of 15-year-olds was among the largest in OECD countries, with high variance between schools (school selection is made based on students' records)
.

## 9. Contested claim: selection benefits vs. does not raise top performance (CONTESTED)
A 2026 researcher interview (Nahalka István, education researcher, commenting amid a ministerial review) asserts: 
even though year after year there is enormous oversubscription for six- and eight-grade gimnáziums, PISA results also show that early selection does not increase the performance of the best students, but does strengthen social differences
, and that 
children arrive at the 6/8-grade gimnázium admission already showing social differences that coincide with knowledge differences, so it is effectively socially better-off children who get selected and admitted — a very hard, social-selection moment in the Hungarian education system with quite negative effects
. This is an interpretive/normative claim from one researcher, not a settled infrastructural fact, though it aligns with the broader academic literature cited above (finding #8). Countervailing operator-side rationale is also documented: 
a main argument for selection via admission tests is that it allows truly talented students to be singled out and taught at a high level without mixing with students who have fundamental problems
 (paraphrased position, attributed to proponents, presented by the same researcher as a claim he disputes).

## 10. Active 2026 policy review signals contested institutional status quo (FACT, current/live)
As of mid-2026, Hungary's education and child-affairs minister (Lannert Judit, under the "Tisza" government) has stated 
the admission system for secondary schools will need review, and the role of six- and eight-grade gimnáziums must also be debated, since in her view they strengthen social selection
. This indicates the arrangement is currently a live policy question, not settled infrastructure, as of this writing.

## 11. Historical precedent: structural reversals show path-dependency risk (FACT)
Legal history shows infrastructure/enrollment responses to policy signals: a 
1995 legal amendment made the future of "structure-changing" gimnáziums questionable because it favored the 8+4-year school structure; as a result several municipalities abolished their structure-changing schools and returned to traditional four-year gimnázium training
. Inference: this demonstrates that municipal-level school-network decisions around the 6/8-year track have previously proven reversible under central policy pressure, implying current capacity/geographic patterns are not fixed but path-dependent on national legal signals.
