LENS EVIDENCE:
- F-live-polish_reform-01 [strong]: Hungary's 6- and 8-year gimnázium tracks were introduced in the early 1990s as a minority pathway layered onto the dominant 8-year primary education system, creating parallel structural options for secondary selection at ages 10–12.
- F-live-polish_reform-02 [contested]: Academic research using Hungarian panel data finds that early selective 6- and 8-year tracks are more beneficial for higher-status families, show higher value-added in reading and mathematics, and correlate with continued tertiary education; however, weaker indirect measures suggest zero-sum effects where students remaining in general tracks experience relative disadvantage.
- F-live-polish_reform-03 [moderate]: The Hungarian 6/8-year gimnázium tracks persist partly due to path-dependent historical factors—elite academic school traditions (gimnáziums) and the decentralized Austro-Hungarian administrative legacy—rather than current outcome-based justification.
- F-live-polish_reform-04 [strong]: In a documented local case (Encs, Borsod-Abaúj-Zemplén county, 2010–2016), expansion of selective gimnázium tracks into grades 7–8 led to 'creaming' of higher-status pupils from primary schools into the selective secondary pathway, reshaping peer composition in remaining schools.
- F-live-polish_reform-05 [strong]: In PISA 2022, Hungary ranks 5th among 79 participating countries for the socioeconomic performance gap (mathematics achievement difference per ESCS unit), and 8th for the share of disadvantaged low performers in mathematics relative to non-disadvantaged peers; however, these figures describe system-wide stratification and are not decomposed to isolate the 6/8-year gimnázium tracks separately from other tracking mechanisms (vocational/general split at age 14, school choice, residential segregation).
- F-live-polish_reform-06 [strong]: Cross-national OECD research (2013, 2020) and subsequent multi-country studies show that the age of first selection in an education system is correlated with inequity in performance but not with mean achievement levels; early tracking (such as Hungary's age 10–12 selection) does not raise or lower average system performance but widens socioeconomic achievement gaps.
- F-live-polish_reform-07 [strong]: Poland's 1999 reform extended common general education by restructuring 8+4 to 6+3(gimnazjum)+3, delaying tracking from age 12 to age 15. Evaluation found system-wide mean gains (mathematics +0.25 SD, reading +0.28 SD, science +0.16 SD), concentrated among students likely bound for vocational tracks (gains ~1 SD); between-school variance decreased; later cohorts showed improved labor-market earnings and employment.
- F-live-polish_reform-08 [strong]: Poland reversed its 1999 reform in 2016–2017, abolishing gimnazja and restoring the 8+4 structure, citing administrative fragmentation reduction and strengthened foundational education. Despite strong PISA and labor-market evidence supporting the 1999 reform, the reversal proceeded; researchers noted that 'evidence from international and national studies was not sufficient to stop the reversal,' suggesting that structural school reforms are recurrently contested policy variables reversible despite positive outcome evidence.

PROPOSALS:
TP-live-t1 — Baseline: retain tracks unchanged, let demography and market forces play out
Goal: Establish an explicit no-new-policy counterfactual against which structural alternatives can be judged, tracing what happens to selection ratios, segregation, and geographic access if capacity is held roughly constant while cohorts shrink.
Mechanisms: Shrinking age-10 and age-12 cohorts feeding gimnázium admissions reduce absolute applicant numbers nationally even as urban demand concentration intensifies (F-live-demography-01, F-live-demography-02).; Geographic polarization (growth urban nodes vs. disadvantaged declining rural peripheries) produces simultaneous extreme oversubscription (9.7x, 9.4x at flagship Budapest schools) and unfilled rural seats (Debrecen, Miskolc, Encs, Pécs) (F-live-demography-03, F-live-school_network_planning-04, F-live-school_network_planning-05).; Stagnant capacity despite documented enormous demand suggests continued policy steering toward vocational education rather than supply expansion, holding segregation dynamics in place (F-live-school_network_planning-06).; Family socioeconomic status continues to predict track entry beyond prior test scores, and segregation (highest in EU, 2022 PISA) persists without corrective mechanism (F-live-hungarian_education_system-05, F-live-portuguese_reform-02).
Risks: Continued and likely worsening segregation given Hungary's already highest-in-EU segregation ranking, with no corrective mechanism in place (F-live-portuguese_reform-02, F-live-equity_and_social_mobility-03).; Growing mismatch between urban oversubscription and rural under-enrollment could produce inefficient use of public capacity and generate localized political friction reminiscent of prior rural school consolidation disputes (F-live-demography-10).
Evidence: strong

TP-live-t2 — Full structural abolition: comprehensive lower secondary to age 14
Goal: Eliminate age 10-12 academic selection entirely, converting to a uniform comprehensive structure until age 14, to remove the structural driver of early family-capital sorting while explicitly confronting documented transition-shock and reform-reversal risks.
Mechanisms: Removing the early selection point eliminates the mechanism by which higher-status families disproportionately access early tracks beyond what prior test scores predict, addressing the family-capital sorting effect (F-live-hungarian_education_system-05, F-live-international_comparison-07).; Cross-national evidence (Hanushek & Woessmann, 18-26 countries) finds early tracking increases inequality with a tendency to reduce mean performance and no equity-efficiency trade-off, supporting the expectation that abolition would reduce inequality without harming average outcomes (F-live-finnish_reform-04, F-live-polish_reform-06).; Poland's 1999 delay of tracking to age 15 produced system-wide mean gains and reduced between-school variance, concentrated among vocationally-bound students, providing a directly analogous mechanism for expected gains (F-live-polish_reform-07).; Documented reform-reversal risk (Poland 2016-17, Austria's decades-long failed reform) shows structural abolition faces entrenched political-institutional resistance regardless of empirical success, requiring explicit durability safeguards (F-live-polish_reform-08, F-live-international_comparison-04).
Risks: Reform reversal risk: Poland's 2016-17 reversal occurred despite strong PISA and labor-market evidence, showing that evidence-based success does not guarantee durability (F-live-polish_reform-08, F-live-international_comparison-06).; Transition-shock risk: Poland's 2017 implementation experienced capacity mismatches (3,173 Warsaw students denied secondary admission in one year), indicating a comparable Hungarian reform could face similar disruption (F-live-education_finance-07).; High-salience partisan framing (communist-era comparison by opposition leadership) risks entrenching resistance independent of the evidence base (F-live-political_feasibility-06, F-live-implementation_planning-07).
Evidence: moderate

TP-live-t3 — Delay the selection age: harmonize academic entry closer to age 14
Goal: Retain academic selection in principle but move the entry point later—phasing out the age-10 eight-year track while adjusting the six-year track toward a later single entry closer to age 14—to reduce the inequity associated with earliest selection while preserving some tracked pathway.
Mechanisms: OECD cross-national research (2013, 2020) finds age of first selection correlates with inequity but not mean performance, implying that delaying selection from age 10/12 toward 14-15 should reduce inequity with limited downside to average achievement (F-live-polish_reform-06).; Poland's 1999 delay from age 12 to 15 produced system-wide mean gains concentrated among vocationally-bound students and reduced between-school variance, providing a directly analogous precedent for a partial delay strategy (F-live-polish_reform-07, F-live-international_comparison-05).; Removing the age-10 entry point specifically targets the earliest and most family-capital-dependent selection stage documented in Hungarian research (disadvantaged elementary schools lack meaningful access to these tracks) (F-live-international_comparison-07).; Poland's subsequent 2017 reversal despite this evidence shows that a delay-only reform still carries reversal risk, requiring attention to political durability even in a partial-delay design (F-live-polish_reform-08).
Risks: Partial reforms may replicate Poland's post-2017 reversal risk since even a moderate delay-based reform lacked sufficient durability despite favorable evidence (F-live-polish_reform-08).; Retaining a 6-year track even in delayed form preserves some family-capital sorting mechanism, limiting the equity gain compared to full abolition (F-live-international_comparison-07).
Evidence: moderate

TP-live-t4 — Retain tracks, re-engineer admissions with equity guardrails and desegregation mandate
Goal: Keep the 6- and 8-year gimnázium track structure while reforming the admissions/sorting mechanism—via quota or reserved-access rules, revised threshold-score guidance, permeability/transfer mechanisms, and an explicit desegregation obligation on the central maintainer—to reduce socioeconomic and Roma segregation without abolishing the tracks.
Mechanisms: Reserved-access quotas and revised threshold-score guidance directly counter the documented pattern whereby 2025 threshold tightening (50/60/70 points) risked further narrowing access for disadvantaged applicants, by building explicit access guarantees into the admissions design (F-live-hungarian_education_system-06).; Placing a statutory desegregation obligation on the Klebelsberg Center directly closes the documented accountability gap where the maintainer explicitly disclaimed proactive integration duties in 2014 litigation (F-live-legal_and_governance-09).; Administrative desegregation rules already exist for related placement obstacles (guaranteed progression for schools with fewer than eight grades), demonstrating institutional precedent for guardrail-style intervention without full structural abolition (F-live-equity_and_social_mobility-07).; Permeability/transfer mechanisms (documented as partially effective in Germany's tracking system) could mitigate initial sorting inequality even though direct transferability of Germany's mechanisms to Hungary is not established (F-live-international_comparison-03).
Risks: Guardrails alone may be insufficient given that segregation arises through multiple channels (residential sorting, free primary choice) beyond gimnázium admissions, limiting the achievable equity gain (F-live-education_finance-06).; Quota-based mechanisms could face legal or political challenge given the constitutional tension between parental free-choice rights and institutional selection authority (F-live-legal_and_governance-03).
Evidence: moderate

TP-live-t5 — Package reform: structural change plus non-structural supports, with administrative and network-planning bridge
Goal: Combine a structural change to the selection point with parallel non-structural measures (curriculum, teacher-distribution correction, targeted equity programs, evidence dissemination) and an interim administrative/network-planning bridge, reflecting that successful detracking elsewhere was multi-measure and that evidence alone did not sustain comparable reforms, while also addressing demographic network mismatches.
Mechanisms: Finland's 1968-1980s reform combined structural detracking with parallel curriculum and assessment-practice change, demonstrating that structural change in isolation is historically insufficient for the kind of outcome sought here (F-live-portuguese_reform-08).; Poland's 2016-17 reversal despite strong evidence shows evidence dissemination alone was insufficient to sustain reform, motivating the explicit inclusion of a dedicated evidence-dissemination and stakeholder-consultation workstream rather than relying on outcome data alone (F-live-international_comparison-06).; Less qualified teachers are systematically concentrated in schools serving disadvantaged students, meaning a structural selection reform without teacher-distribution correction would leave a key inequality-reproducing mechanism intact (F-live-education_finance-04, F-live-legal_and_governance-05).; Documented network mismatch (extreme urban oversubscription alongside rural unfilled seats) means a structural reform implemented without network rationalization would inherit unresolved geographic capacity misallocation during transition (F-live-school_network_planning-04, F-live-school_network_planning-05).; Marginalized equity-focused programs (IPR, Útravaló-Macika, Arany János, Tanoda) became underfunded overlays rather than integrated supports, indicating that non-structural equity measures require integration and sustained funding, not just existence, to succeed alongside structural change (F-live-hungarian_education_system-09).
