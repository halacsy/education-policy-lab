**1. Structural facts about the Hungarian system**
Hungary's general secondary schools (gimnázium) exist in three parallel structural forms with different entry points. 
Secondary education usually lasts for 4 years; in gimnáziums it can also last for 5, 6 or 8 years depending on how many years the student spent in primary school.
 The standard 8-year primary track dominates numerically: 
8-year education is the most widespread; the other two options were introduced in the early 1990s.
 This means the 6/8-year tracks are a minority pathway layered onto a general system where 
the general phase of education lasts until the age of 16.
 Source: k12academics.com, undated Eurydice-derived summary.

**2. What the selective tracks appear to "solve" — but with a documented equity cost**
Academic research using Hungarian panel data finds a consistent pattern: 
early selective tracks are more beneficial for higher status families; these tracks have a higher value-added in reading and mathematical literacy and in continuing studies in tertiary education; their student composition is of higher status and higher skilled students.
 The same source concludes 
this leads to an increase in the inequality of opportunities; weaker tests show that while students of early selective tracks gain, those left in general tracks lose.
 *This is a specific empirical study's inferential conclusion (relative/zero-sum framing), not a universally agreed finding — flagged as contested since "losing" is measured via weaker/indirect tests per the same source.* Source: academia.edu (Segregation in the Primary School System in Hungary).

**3. Historical/institutional roots explain durability, not current justification**
The same research traces *why* the system persists rather than whether it should: 
three factors were important: (1) historical conditions (2) decentralization and (3) democracy — the two historical conditions being the memory of the elite 8-year-long academic schools, the gimnáziums, and the decentralized administrative structure of the Austro-Hungarian monarchy.
 *Inference*: this suggests the tracks' persistence is path-dependent/cultural rather than derived from contemporary outcome evidence — a distinct claim from whether the tracks currently produce good outcomes.

**4. Micro-level implementation mechanism: how expansion of selective tracks reshapes remaining schools**
A documented local case shows the mechanism by which extending selective tracks downward affects other schools. In Encs (Borsod-Abaúj-Zemplén county), 
a four-grade, already government-owned upper-secondary general school got permission to expand their educational offering to include grades 7 and 8; due to this, the school started to cream off the higher status pupils from the final two years of the two (already also "nationalized") primary schools.
 *Fact, single-locality case study (2010–2016); external validity to national scale not established in the excerpt.* Source: CEU Center for Policy Studies working paper, 2020.

**5. Aggregate outcome data (PISA/OECD) confirm a large socioeconomic gap, but do not isolate the 6/8-year tracks specifically**
National PISA data show 
the difference in mathematics performance associated with a one-unit increase in the PISA index of economic, social and cultural status (ESCS) is one of the largest among PISA-participating countries and economies
 (rank 5/79, 2022). Complementing this, 
compared to the share of non-disadvantaged low performers in maths, the share of disadvantaged low performers in maths is one of the highest among countries and economies participating in PISA
 (rank 8/79). *Caveat*: these figures describe the whole Hungarian system's socioeconomic stratification and are not decomposed by the search results into a share attributable specifically to the 6/8-year gimnázium tracks versus other tracking (vocational/general split at 14, school choice, residential segregation).

**6. Cross-national research: age-of-selection correlates with inequity, not mean performance — a generalizable but contested finding**
A broader comparative literature finds: 
the OECD (2013, p. 84; 2020a, p. 80) showed that the age of first selection in an education system is correlated to inequity in performance but not to the mean performance level.
 Newer multi-country studies reach a similar conclusion: 
newer studies including more countries, and more assessments came to similar results and conclude that early tracking increases achievement inequalities.
 However the same source notes nuance: 
an effect of selection age on mean achievement, however, was not evident.
 *This is the closest thing to a generalizable, discipline-level finding relevant to "which problems selection solves/creates": it does not raise or lower overall system performance on average, but is associated with wider gaps between socioeconomic groups.*

**7. Poland's 1999 reform: what raising the selection age implies — a natural comparator to the Hungarian case**
Poland's comparator reform delayed tracking by restructuring 8+4 into 6+3(gimnazjum)+3, extending common general education. Evaluation evidence found: 
on average, the reform was associated with significant improvements; Poland improved its score in mathematics by 0.25 of a standard deviation, in reading, by 0.28 of a standard deviation, and in science, by 0.16 of a standard deviation.
 The gains were concentrated among lower-track-bound students: 
the reform is associated with an improvement in likely vocational students' scores of about 100 points, or a whole standard deviation.
 Mechanistically, 
the fast increase in Polish students' PISA test scores was driven by potential vocational students, who after the 1999 reform were in the comprehensive lower secondary school instead of already having been tracked into the low-tier vocational schools... the increased resources of an additional year of better teachers, peers, and general curriculum have improved these students' test scores.
 Distributionally, 
the between-school variance of test scores decreased after the reform
, and later labor-market analysis found 
the reform had a significant positive effect on the earnings and employment chances of the post-reform cohorts... these results come from the comprehensive nature of the reform, i.e. general education was extended, and its quality was improved before students were tracked to upper secondary schools.
 Source: OECD working paper (2011); Journal for Labour Market Research (2022).

**8. Poland's 2016/2017 reversal: political durability of a "successful" reform was not guaranteed by evidence**
Despite the above evidence base, Poland's government reversed course. 
This overhaul abolished the junior secondary schools (gimnazja), which had been introduced in 1999, and restored a pre-1999 model of eight-year primary schools followed by four-year general secondary schools... The reform, led by Minister Anna Zalewska, sought to reduce administrative fragmentation, minimize early external examinations, and strengthen foundational education in core subjects like Polish language and history.
 Academic assessment of this episode is notable: 
the direction for the reform of the Polish education system that was set in 1999 is not continued by the current government, which in 2016 reversed the reform that extended comprehensive education, while it promotes vocational education and limits its support for preschool education.
 Critically, 
it also discusses how it was possible to find political support for the reversal of changes that seemed to be highly successful
, and 
the evidence from international and national studies was not sufficient to stop the reversal of the reforms.
 *This is a key "reversal history" finding: strong outcome evidence (PISA, labor-market data) did not prevent a politically motivated structural reversal — a caution against assuming evidence-based durability for any Hungarian reform in either direction.*

**9. Implementation history of the Polish reversal reveals concrete transition costs, independent of the underlying structural merits**
The 2017 changeover produced a documented "double cohort" crisis: 
In 2017 6th graders stayed in their primary schools to continue their education for another two years. This year both, those graduating from gimnazjums and those graduating from primary schools, applied to high schools which resulted in competition being so high that in Warsaw alone, 3173 students did not get into any school.
 Scale of institutional disruption: 
the changes led to the closure of approximately 7,000 gymnasia and the reorganization or closure of several hundred small primary schools by 2019, alongside curriculum updates... though they triggered widespread protests from teachers and parents over rushed implementation and job insecurity.
 Parents mounted legal challenges: 
the culmination of an overhaul of the school system that began in 2017 breaches children's constitutional right to equal access to education, to say nothing of the pressure it subjects them to.
 *Fact-level finding: any structural resequencing (in either direction — adding or removing early selection) carries transition-cohort risk (double intake years, school closures, legal disputes) distinct from the merits of the end-state structure itself.*

**10. Poland's oscillation shows the same structural question has been reversed twice within living memory, suggesting no stable consensus exists even where evidence is strong**
The historical sequence is documented as: 
the 8+4 system existed in Poland from 1961 until 2000... one of the authors of the 1999 educational reform through which gimnazjum was reintroduced (after its abolishment in 1948), described the recent reform as a move behind which stands the idea of 'progress through regress.'
 The system has thus moved 8+4 → 6+3+3 (with gimnazjum) → 8+4 across less than two decades, illustrating that structural school-architecture choices are recurrently contested and reversible policy variables rather than settled technical parameters, even when comparative PISA evidence favored one configuration.
