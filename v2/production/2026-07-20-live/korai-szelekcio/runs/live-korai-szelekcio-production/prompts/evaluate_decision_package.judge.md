PACKAGE SUMMARY:
The question of whether Hungary should keep its 6- and 8-year gimnázium tracks admits five distinct transformation paths, each acting on a different lever, and none is unambiguously superior across all lenses of assessment. What can change ranges from narrow procedural adjustment to full structural reorganization to no active policy change at all, and the choice among them turns partly on empirical uncertainty and partly on unresolved value conflicts.

TP-t1 keeps the 6/8-year structure fully intact and instead modifies admissions mechanics: coaching-resistant exam redesign, SES-weighted proxies layered onto the existing centralized deferred-acceptance algorithm, capped interview weight, and outreach mandates for disadvantaged-school participation. Its leading mechanism is procedural fairness within an unchanged gate; its central constraint is that it operates on the least consequential lever according to cross-national tracking evidence, which attributes inequality primarily to the existence and age of early selection rather than to entry-test fairness. Evidence is moderate, and the key risk is elite adaptive response—coaching pivots to new criteria—plus potential legal challenge to SES proxies absent explicit legislative basis.

TP-t2 targets capacity and rigor thresholds rather than admission rules, using binding minimum scores and capacity caps to shrink the state-sector selective footprint, aided by demographic decline as political cover and existing ministerial discretionary approval power already used to deny expansion. This has strong institutional precedent and directly manipulates the network-capacity variable most lenses agree is tractable. Its binding constraint is jurisdictional: church, foundation, and private schools sit outside Klebelsberg Centre authority and could absorb displaced demand, potentially converting state-sector contraction into segregation relocation rather than reduction—a dynamic with no international comparator to validate against.

TP-t3 is the most structurally ambitious option, delaying first tracking to age 14+ through legislative amendment, modeled on Finland's 1972-1977 reform and Poland's 1999 delay, bundled with anti-sorting catchment rules and phased multi-cohort transition to avoid Poland's 2017 reversal shock. This is the only path directly engaging the mechanism international and equity evidence identifies as the primary inequality driver, and it has the strongest comparative outcome evidence supporting its direction. But it faces the highest legal-authority bar (primary legislation), a foreseeable clash with the constitutionally protected free-choice guarantee, the strongest coalition of veto players (gimnázium directors, ideologically invested governing coalition), and a severe teacher-workforce capacity constraint (16,000-teacher shortfall, aging workforce) that threatens the feasibility of its own phasing safeguard. Even Finland's own experience shows structural delay yields real but partial and non-permanent gains, with later PISA decline and informal sorting re-emergence.

TP-t4 leaves selection age and track structure untouched entirely, pursuing compensatory funding, teacher-quality incentives, curriculum clarity, and instructional-time increases. This is the lowest-friction option politically and legally, requiring no confrontation with free-choice rights or director veto power, and it directly targets a documented capitation-funding incidence problem. Its constraint is evidentiary: this reform class is explicitly unevaluated for equity impact, and because it does not alter the structural driver of loss (departure of high-performing peers and their funding), it can only partially offset rather than close the underlying gap that persistent within-school sorting may further absorb.

TP-t5 serves as the explicit no-new-policy counterfactual: shrinking cohorts and unenforceable statutory class-size minimums mechanically force network consolidation and reduce the selective track's absolute size, without any active equity mechanism. This is the best-evidenced baseline by construction, requiring zero legislative risk, but every lens converges on the same caution: it is likely to be geographically uneven, preserving urban flagship prestige while eroding only marginal rural provision, potentially worsening rather than improving equity, and leaving SES sorting, coaching investment, and Roma segregation entirely unaddressed.

The research most likely to change this choice concerns the causal contribution of the track structure itself net of primary-school choice and residential sorting, the magnitude of likely displacement into non-state channels under contraction or delay, and whether compensatory funding alone measurably narrows the SES/performance gap absent any change to selection age or track architecture.
