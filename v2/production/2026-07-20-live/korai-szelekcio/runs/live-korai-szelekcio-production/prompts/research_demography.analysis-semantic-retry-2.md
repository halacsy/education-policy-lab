# Research Notes: Cohort & Demographic Perspective on Hungary's 6- and 8-Year Gimnázium Tracks

**Scope:** Population/cohort-analytic angle only; no policy recommendation offered. Facts vs. inference labeled; contested findings flagged.

---

**1. Structural baseline (fact)**
Hungary tracks academically at two early entry points: 
tracking starts early in Hungary, partially at Grade 5 (age 10-11) or later at Grade 7 (age 12-13), when students can apply to an 8-year or 6-year general secondary school, compared to the OECD average of age 15, and around 85% of Hungarian students are in schools which can select students according to academic performance.
 The dense network of 880 secondary schools is designed so 
every student can find a secondary general school close to their home, with the aim that the school be accessible by public transport in under an hour
 — a network-density goal that interacts directly with shrinking local cohorts (see #4-5).

**2. Cohort decline is steep and accelerating (fact)**
Absolute birth numbers — the pool from which future 10-12-year-old selection cohorts are drawn — have fallen sharply and consecutively: 
In 2024, only 77,500 children were born – the lowest figure since records began – representing a year-on-year decline of more than 7,000
, and 
Hungary's population has fallen below 9.5 million for the first time in decades, as births declined sharply in 2025... just 72,000 children were born, 7.1% fewer than in 2024
. The total fertility rate has fallen in tandem: 
the total fertility rate dropped to 1.31 children per woman, down from 1.39 a year earlier: the lowest level in the past 14 years and far below the 2.1 needed for long-term replacement.
 This means the age-10 and age-12 selection cohorts entering 6/8-year gimnáziums in the early 2030s will be materially smaller than those of the 2020 school-leaving cohort — a mechanical, near-certain demographic effect independent of any policy choice (inference from #2 data).

**3. Fertility decline is compounded by a shrinking pool of potential mothers, not just behavior change (fact/inference mix)**
Analysts note 
the TFR will only be down perhaps 18% on the 2021 number, reflecting the fact that the number of potential mothers is dropping each year... potential mothers are having 18% less children than they did 4 years ago.
 This dual mechanism (fewer women of reproductive age plus lower fertility per woman) implies that cohort decline affecting future selective-track intake is structurally embedded for at least another 20+ years (inference), not merely a short-term fluctuation.

**4. Geography of selection is already highly uneven, independent of future cohort shrinkage (contested/fact)**
Research led by education sociologist Berényi Eszter found 
according to national competence-test data, in 40% of Hungarian primary schools not a single child attempts the entrance exam in grade 4 or 6 (in 60% of village schools this is the case), while in other institutions — mainly better schools in county seats and the capital — class size drops by 20-30% by grade 7 as so many students leave for gimnázium.
 This indicates the selective track already functions as an urban/regional-center phenomenon with minimal rural uptake in a large share of settlements — a baseline geographic asymmetry that a shrinking national cohort would tend to sharpen in absolute numbers (inference).

**5. Recent settlement-level research documents diverging demographic trajectories across regions (fact)**
A 2025 territorial study found 
numerous rural settlements in Western Transdanubia, Southern Baranya, and some low-density regions of Northern Hungary recorded stagnation or decline, including areas where the child population contracted by 10–30% over the period, often coinciding with a broader demographic decline and the closure or downgrading
 of local institutions. Some small settlements show child populations of only "50–70 children" combined with high disadvantage rates, per this source — meaning viability of maintaining a full 5-12 grade selective pipeline in such areas is already marginal on cohort-size grounds alone (fact, with viability implication as inference).

**6. Statutory class-size floors interact mechanically with shrinking cohorts (fact)**
Hungarian law sets 
a minimum number of students per class of 26, with an average of 28 recommended, and a maximum of 34, with deviation only permitted in rare legally specified cases.
 As local age cohorts fall below these thresholds in more settlements, forming even one viable class (let alone parallel 4-year, 6-year, and 8-year gimnázium intake classes in the same locality) becomes numerically harder — a structural, not political, constraint (inference from combining #5 and #6).

**7. Equity/selection effects: contested evidence**
- Hungarian-specific research (Horn, TARKI) found that in micro-regions with an available 8-grade "kisgimnázium," 
while at grade 6 there was no difference in inequality of opportunity between the two groups of micro-regions, by grade 8 in micro-regions with an accessible 8-year kisgimnázium, socioeconomic status correlates significantly more strongly with competency scores
 — suggesting the early track itself widens SES-linked gaps within the same locality over time.
- By contrast, a separate international comparative study using PISA data reached a different conclusion: 
PISA data clearly demonstrated that after the reform, there was no significant decrease in correlations between socioeconomic status of students and their results in three PISA domains... PISA data did not confirm that anything changed in this respect after the reform
 regarding social selection into upper secondary schools — i.e., this study found the reform did not measurably worsen (or improve) SES-outcome links relative to the counterfactual. These two strands of evidence are in tension and should be treated as contested rather than settled.
- At system level, OECD notes 
a higher share of low performers (28.1% of students below proficiency Level 2, compared to the OECD average of 23.0%)... The impact of socio-economic background on student performance was among the largest in OECD countries (23.1%, compared to the OECD average of 14.8%)
, and 
between-school variance in Hungary is the third highest among all OECD countries, while within-school variance is below the OECD average
 — consistent with (but not proof of) a tracked, sorted system architecture.

**8. Political-economy of demand for the track (fact, sociological, relevant to "who benefits")**
Sociological interview research describes convergent constituencies sustaining demand for the 6/8-year track: 
the interests of often conservative, provincial, high-status families, whose children typically attend church schools, and Budapest's liberal intelligentsia, whose children attend elite state or alternative gimnáziums, have found common ground — one group hopes such schools ease entry into medical or law faculties, the other wants to send children abroad for university, but both groups are interested in preserving this school type.
 This is inference-adjacent qualitative evidence about political sustainability of the track independent of cohort size, but demographically relevant because it identifies which subpopulations (by geography and class) most value continuation.

**9. Rural/periphery institutional strain is compounding independently of the selection-track debate (fact)**
Beyond gimnázium-specific dynamics, general rural school viability is under multi-factor pressure: 
teacher shortages, linked to an ageing workforce, are a growing concern... half of all teachers in the 2024/2025 academic year were 50 or older, while only 7.2% of teachers were under 30
, and 
teacher shortages are greatest in rural areas, with the proportion of unqualified teachers in disadvantaged schools 2 to 3 times higher than in schools not disadvantaged.
 A shrinking, aging teaching workforce combined with shrinking pupil cohorts is a compounding — not merely additive — pressure on any multi-track (4/6/8-year) provision model in peripheral areas (inference).

**10. "No policy change" counterfactual (inference, clearly labeled as such)**
Extrapolating current trends without any structural reform to the 6/8-year track: (a) absolute intake cohorts for age-10 and age-12 selection points will continue to shrink nationally, per current KSH-reported trajectories (fact-based extrapolation, not certainty); (b) the existing urban-concentration pattern of selective-track access (documented in #4) would likely persist or intensify in relative terms as rural micro-regions lose the critical mass needed to sustain feeder classes, though the exact magnitude is not directly evidenced in available sources and should be treated as inference; (c) the SES-outcome divergence documented by Horn/TARKI (fact, contested against the Polish-PISA comparative finding in #7) would, on Hungarian-specific evidence, plausibly continue absent intervention, while system-level OECD indicators of high between-school variance and SES impact provide corroborating (but not dispositive) system-level context.
