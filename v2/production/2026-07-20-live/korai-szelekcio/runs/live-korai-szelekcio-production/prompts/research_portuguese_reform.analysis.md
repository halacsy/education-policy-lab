# Research Notes: Early Academic Selection in Hungary's 6- and 8-Year Gimnázium Tracks

*Prepared as descriptive research notes — no policy recommendation offered. Facts are marked [F]; inferential/interpretive claims from cited studies are marked [I]. Contested evidence is flagged explicitly.*

## Background facts on the structure

[F] 
Compared to the OECD average (age 15), tracking starts early in Hungary: partially at Grade 5 (age 10-11) or later at Grade 7 (age 12-13), when students can apply to an 8-year or 6-year general secondary school, and around 85% of Hungarian students are in schools which can select students according to academic performance or recommendations.
 This is far above 
the OECD average of 43% of students in such schools.
 [F] 
Since 1991, there have also been six- and eight-year secondary schools; pupils can enrol from the fifth grade (8-year) and seventh grade (6-year).
 [F] 
The 4-year gimnázium/8-year track split dates to the early 1990s, with 8-year education remaining the most widespread of the three options.


## Finding 1 — Equity metrics associated with the current selective structure

[F] Hungary's PISA profile shows a pattern consistent with (though not proven solely caused by) early tracking: 
Hungary has a lower share of top performers (9.3% at Level 5+, vs. OECD average 12.6%) and a higher share of low performers (28.1% below Level 2, vs. 23.0% OECD average); the impact of socio-economic background on performance was among the largest in OECD countries (23.1% vs. 14.8% average); and between-school variance in Hungary is the third highest among all OECD countries, while within-school variance is below the OECD average.
 [I] The OECD's own interpretive summary states 
"Evidence shows that the Hungarian system of school choice can lead to socio-economic drawbacks and disparities in student performance."


## Finding 2 — Cross-national segregation comparison

[F] More recent data confirm and sharpen this picture: 
on average in the EU there is a 33-point PISA gap between children of a graduate and non-graduate parent, but 53 points in Hungary; Hungary has the highest level of segregation of students from less and more advantaged socio-economic groups into different schools, and Hungary's segregation of the most and least disadvantaged students was the highest in the EU in the 2022 PISA study.
 [F] An earlier comparison specific to early-tracking countries found 
selection in Hungary is even stronger than in three out of four European countries (Austria, Germany, Lithuania, the Netherlands) that stream pupils into parallel tracks as early as age 10 or 11,
 and 
a PISA socio-economic inclusion index for Hungary was the lowest among all developed countries, signaling higher comparative selectivity than traditional indicators suggest.


## Finding 3 — Short-term academic "value-added" for selected students, at measurable cost to peers

[I] Contested but influential domestic research (Horn 2013, cited in a 2016 academic chapter) found: 
early-selective academic tracks increase students' 10th-grade test scores compared to similar students in other tracks; students who apply to but are not admitted to selective tracks perform worse than similar admitted peers; so early tracking and vocational tracking both appear to have harmful short-term effects on educational equality.
 A doctoral thesis summarizing this line of work states plainly: 
tracking schools have higher value-added than general schools, while they have a deteriorating effect on the test scores of students left in normal schools — tracking is able to improve the short-term school achievement of the selected students at the expense of other children's achievement.


## Finding 4 — Long-term labor-market/attainment effects are null (contested, single natural-experiment study)

[I] This is the most policy-relevant and least settled finding. The same research program, using a quasi-natural policy experiment, reports: 
no comparable findings are available on long-term effects, but Gurzó and Horn (2015) show there are no visible effects of early tracking on average labour market outcomes — introduction of early tracking during the post-Communist transition has not [durably improved outcomes].
 The underlying thesis elaborates: 
tracking is able to improve the short-term school achievement of the selected students at the expense of other children's achievement, but its impact disappears in the long run; the zero tertiary education effect suggests the Hungarian policy could not fulfil its expectations and prepare the selected children better for higher education.
 *This is a single strand of research (one working paper/thesis); it should be treated as suggestive rather than definitive, though it is the only long-run causal estimate identified.*

## Finding 5 — Segregation is driven by school-choice mechanics, not only by income inequality, and begins before the formal tracking age

[I] A Central European University policy paper argues the structural driver is broader than tracking per se: 
even setting aside countries that stream at age 10-11, Hungary's educational selectivity is related more to the characteristics of schools than to their social environments, and the pressure of societal inequalities cannot be reduced to income inequality alone.
 Separately, cross-country segregation research finds: 
Hungary adopted a free school-choice system for elementary schools with complete discretion for schools to hand-pick students, and high-segregation countries include Austria, Belgium, Germany and Hungary,
 while 
cross-country differences in segregation are associated with the prevalence of selective choice of pupils by schools.
 [I] This implies segregation dynamics operate at the level of ordinary primary schools (via catchment/choice rules) as well as through the 6/8-year gimnázium selection specifically — a point relevant to whether abolishing only the gimnázium tracks would resolve the equity problem.

## Finding 6 — Comparative evidence: delaying tracking has produced positive equity/attainment effects elsewhere, but always as part of a bundled reform

[F] Finland's 1970s comprehensive school reform is the most-cited comparator: 
the school tracking age was increased from 11 to 16, all students up to 16 provided the same curriculum, and this was one of the main factors behind Finland's PISA success in terms of both ranking and equality of opportunity.
 Quantified effects: 
a move towards a more comprehensive system reduced Finland's intergenerational income elasticity from 0.30 to 0.23, a substantial reduction, with a large part driven by improved cognitive skills among disadvantaged pupils.
 A related French estimate: 
a delay in tracking from age 11 to 13 in France led to improvements in completed education and a 6% wage increase across affected cohorts, concentrated among disadvantaged backgrounds.
 Poland's 1999 reform: 
comprehensive general education was extended to nine years and vocational tracking postponed by one year;
 
the reform substantially increased test scores and improved labor market outcomes.
 [I] Reviews of these reforms emphasize they were never isolated structural moves: 
post-WWII European comprehensive reforms typically combined increased compulsory schooling years, national curricula, and delayed/abolished tracking together
 — i.e., structural delay was coordinated with curricular and compulsory-schooling reforms, not deployed alone.

## Finding 7 — A cautionary counter-case: partial reversal of de-tracking correlates with performance decline (confounded by COVID)

[F] Poland's reform was later partly reversed: 
for Poland, PISA 2022 showed the impact of Covid-19 and the reversal of the successful 1999 reform, which had delayed vocational education.
 *This is confounded — the same PISA cycle captured both pandemic learning loss and the structural reversal, so the decline cannot be cleanly attributed to re-tracking alone; the source itself frames it as a joint effect.*

## Finding 8 — Non-structural (quality/access) levers already being pursued in Hungary in parallel to the tracking debate

[F] Hungary has ongoing non-structural reforms addressing teacher supply/quality: 
the Act on the New Careers of Teachers (Act LII of 2023) entered into force on 1 January 2023, replacing teachers' previous public-servant status with a new "school education employee" status and changing employment rules.
 [F] Targeted subject-teacher incentive funding also exists: 
a grant scheme offers a monthly stipend to teachers of core subjects (Hungarian, history, maths, physics, biology, chemistry, geography, IT, music) with at least 3 years' experience who are involved in talent development, with 957 of 1,048 applications funded in the latest round.
 [I] These measures are non-structural (they do not touch the age-10/12 selection point) and coexist with, rather than substitute for, the tracking debate; no located source directly evaluates whether such measures alone (without structural change) closed the equity gaps documented in Findings 1–2.

## Finding 9 — Political/discursive use of equity evidence has not translated into structural change

[F] Hungarian governments have selectively invoked international evidence: 
the Hungarian government utilized PISA findings to legitimize educational reforms, especially in addressing equity issues, though PISA's reception in Hungary revealed a mix of acceptance and skepticism, significantly shaping national education discourse.
 [I] However, implementation has lagged: 
the study reveals that PISA's impact is limited by local cultural contexts and disorganized institutional structures, resulting in a failure to align educational governance with OECD expectations.
 Domestic policy discourse historically emphasized broad redistributive framing over targeted mainstreaming: 
the 'social equity' PISA findings were predominantly translated into "one size fits all" approaches by governmental actors, and until recently the policy discourse was not inclusive or prone to mainstreaming approaches promoting "fairness and justice."


## Finding 10 — Roma access/ethnic-equity dimension remains distinct and unresolved

[F] The OECD flagged this as a separate lever from tracking structure per se: 
"Hungary can further improve access for Roma, to provide better equity opportunities in education."
 [F] Current outcome gaps remain large: 
early school leaving decreased in 2024 to 10.3% (against an EU average of 9.3% and EU target below 9%), but the rate is higher in the least developed regions, in villages, and among Roma (58.7% vs. non-Roma 9.3% in 2023).
 [I] This suggests any structural reform to the gimnázium tracks would interact with, but not by itself resolve, ethnic/regional equity gaps that predate and extend beyond age-10 selection.

## Synthesis relevant to the two framing questions (descriptive, not prescriptive)

**Can non-structural quality/equity measures alone achieve the stated goals?** The evidence base located does not contain a study isolating non-structural measures (teacher policy, funding, targeted access programs) from structural tracking changes within Hungary specifically. Comparative literature (Finland, Poland, France) on delayed tracking shows structural change *co-occurring* with curricular unification and extended compulsory comprehensive schooling (Finding 6), which is the closest available evidence to an answer — implying, inferentially, that structural and non-structural levers were combined rather than substituted for one another in the cases where positive equity effects were documented. Hungary's own segregation research (Finding 5) suggests school-choice mechanics operate below the formal tracking age, meaning structural changes to the 6/8-year gimnázium alone might not address segregation that arises through ordinary primary-school choice.

**Which coordinated reforms were necessary in comparator cases?** In the documented reform episodes, delay of tracking was paired with: (a) unified national curricula extending general education years (Finland, Poland, per Finding 6); (b) extension of compulsory comprehensive schooling duration; and (c) in Poland's case, a subsequent partial reversal correlated with (but not cleanly isolated from COVID-19 effects on) declining performance (Finding 7), which is a contested data point given the confound. No source located specifies a comprehensive Hungary-specific reform package (e.g., admissions-lottery rules, teacher allocation formulas, school funding equalization) that was modeled or piloted alongside a hypothetical delay of gimnázium selection.
