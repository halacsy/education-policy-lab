LENS: Equity and social mobility

LENS EVIDENCE:
- F-live-equity_and_social_mobility-01 [strong]: Hungary selects students into academic tracks at age 10–12, earlier than almost any comparable OECD system, and cross-country panel evidence shows early tracking is significantly associated with higher educational inequality and a tendency toward reduced mean performance.
- F-live-equity_and_social_mobility-02 [strong]: Students from higher socioeconomic backgrounds are significantly more likely to access the selective early-academic tracks in Hungary even after controlling for prior ability; these tracks show higher value-added in literacy and tertiary progression, thereby increasing inequality of opportunity.
- F-live-equity_and_social_mobility-03 [moderate]: Entry to selective grammar-school tracks functions as a capital-intensive competition requiring families to invest financial, social, and cultural resources before formal testing occurs; elementary schools serving disadvantaged populations report not offering genuine participation opportunities to pupils.
- F-live-equity_and_social_mobility-04 [moderate]: Students remaining in ordinary primary tracks after selection show deteriorating test scores and reduced performance relative to peers in selective tracks, indicating selection relocates rather than concentrates advantage.
- F-live-equity_and_social_mobility-05 [moderate]: Short-term test-score gains in selective tracks do not translate to reliable long-run tertiary-education or labor-market outcome improvements; the value-added advantage attenuates by adulthood.
- F-live-equity_and_social_mobility-06 [strong]: Ethnic Roma segregation in Hungarian schools has significantly increased since 1989 and is distinct from socioeconomic segregation; Roma students are concentrated in weaker tracks system-wide and less likely to attend academic tracks or continue secondary schooling.
- F-live-equity_and_social_mobility-07 [moderate]: Segregation and selection in Hungary are amplified by privatization and church-school growth rather than by parental school choice per se; the share of church-run primary schools rose from 8.6% to 16.6% and secondary schools from 10% to 26.1% between 2010 and 2021.
- F-live-equity_and_social_mobility-08 [moderate]: Geographic and rural-urban disparities in access to extended-track gimnáziums layer on socioeconomic selection; distance to nearest 6- or 8-year track school is used as a causal instrument because proximity is not randomly distributed and clusters in urban areas.
- F-live-equity_and_social_mobility-09 [strong]: Hungary has one of the strongest socioeconomic-status/performance gradients among OECD countries in PISA mathematics, reading, and science; however, most students are in their first year of secondary education at age 15 PISA testing, so performance differences cannot be fully attributed to track type.
- F-live-equity_and_social_mobility-10 [moderate]: Even absent formal early-selection tracks, Hungarian schools engage in substantial within-school classroom sorting by socioeconomic status, which has significant effects on test-score inequality; sorting would likely relocate rather than be eliminated if the 6-/8-year gimnázium structure were abolished.

PROPOSALS:
TP-live-t1 — Coaching-resistant, SES-weighted admissions reform within the existing track structure
Goal: Reduce family-capital-driven sorting at the point of gimnázium entry without altering the 6/8-year track structure itself
Mechanisms: National uniform exam design changes to reduce coaching-return (F-live-hungarian_education_system-02, F-live-hungarian_education_system-05); SES-aware quota or bonus-point proxies layered onto the existing centralized deferred-acceptance algorithm (F-live-legal_and_governance-03); Capping interview weight already regulated at 25% and further limiting subjective/coachable components (F-live-legal_and_governance-03); Address disadvantaged-school non-participation in the entry competition by mandating outreach/registration support (F-live-equity_and_social_mobility-03, F-live-education_finance-02)
Risks: Elite families adapt coaching strategies to new exam format, eroding gains over time; Gimnázium directors, an organized interest group with direct political access, may resist changes perceived as diluting selectivity (F-live-political_feasibility-07); SES proxies could be legally contested as discriminatory absent explicit legislative basis (F-live-legal_and_governance-02); Does not address segregation originating in primary-school choice, a documented independent mechanism (F-live-portuguese_reform-06, F-live-polish_reform-07)
Evidence: moderate

TP-live-t2 — Score-threshold and capacity-cap contraction of the selective state-sector track
Goal: Shrink the state-sector footprint of 6/8-year gimnázium places using score thresholds and capacity caps rather than admission-rule redesign
Mechanisms: Binding national minimum score thresholds (building on 2024/25 recommended 70-point threshold) applied uniformly to reduce places awarded below a rigor bar (F-live-school_network_planning-04); Capacity caps on state-sector 6/8-year gimnázium enrollment, holding or reducing the current ~25,962 stagnant capacity in the face of falling cohorts (F-live-school_network_planning-03); Use of demographic decline as political cover to justify contraction rather than framing as ideological reversal (F-live-hungarian_education_system-10); Ministerial discretionary approval power already used to deny track expansion requests on segregation grounds, extended systematically (F-live-hungarian_education_system-09, F-live-political_feasibility-08)
Risks: Church and foundation schools, outside Klebelsberg jurisdiction, could absorb displaced demand and expand their own selective tracks, undermining net contraction (F-live-legal_and_governance-07, F-live-implementation_planning-05); Contraction without addressing admission-mechanism bias could simply concentrate remaining highly limited places even more among already-advantaged families; Risk of political reversal given documented director lobbying access to top leadership (F-live-political_feasibility-07)
Evidence: moderate

TP-live-t3 — Structural delay of first tracking to age 14+ with unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase
Goal: Eliminate age-10/12 academic tracking by converting the 6/8-year entry points into a single comprehensive lower-secondary structure with first selection deferred to age 14 or later
Mechanisms: Raise statutory tracking age following the Finnish precedent of comprehensive schooling to 16 and Poland's 1999 delay to 16, both associated with reduced inequality when bundled with curriculum reform (F-live-international_comparison-07, F-live-international_comparison-08, F-live-portuguese_reform-05); Bundle structural delay with catchment/anti-sorting rules addressing the independent segregation mechanism of free primary-school choice (F-live-portuguese_reform-06, F-live-polish_reform-07); Phase transition over multiple cohort years to avoid Poland's 2017 single-year 8+4 reversal shock (double-cohort crisis, 3,173 unplaced Warsaw students) (F-live-polish_reform-05, F-live-international_comparison-07); Address the four-selection-point structure (early-childhood, primary choice, age-10/12, post-16) recognizing that tracking-age reform alone has limited systemic effect without addressing other junctures (F-live-legal_and_governance-08)
Risks: Poland's 2017 reversal demonstrates that abrupt, non-phased structural change produces severe capacity shortfalls and job-loss disruption; a comparable Hungarian misstep is plausible given similar administrative capacity constraints (F-live-polish_reform-05, F-live-international_comparison-07); Constitutional free-choice guarantee may be invoked to challenge catchment/anti-sorting rules bundled with the reform (F-live-legal_and_governance-02); Finland's own recent PISA decline (64-point fall 2003-2022) and emergence of informal 'emphasized classes' sorting shows structural delay alone does not guarantee durable equity gains absent sustained systemic commitment (F-live-finnish_reform-07, F-live-finnish_reform-08, F-live-finnish_reform-09); Church/foundation/private schools outside Klebelsberg jurisdiction may resist or be exempted, preserving a segregated selective channel outside state control (F-live-legal_and_governance-07); Gimnázium directors as veto players could mobilize to block or reverse legislation (F-live-political_feasibility-07)
Evidence: moderate

TP-live-t4 — Non-structural compensatory equity package (Portugal-style, selection age unchanged)
Goal: Improve outcomes and narrow equity gaps for disadvantaged students without altering the 10-12 selection age or track structure, via targeted funding, curriculum clarity, and school-quality levelling
Mechanisms: Targeted (TEIP-style) supplementary per-pupil or per-school funding for schools serving disadvantaged populations, compensating for capitation losses when high performers depart (F-live-education_finance-05, F-live-education_finance-06); Address teacher-quality gap in disadvantaged/feeder schools where unqualified-teacher proportion is 2-3x higher, via incentive funding and placement policy (F-live-demography-09, F-live-portuguese_reform-07); Curriculum clarity and workload/memorization reduction reforms already proposed by the ministry, extended systematically to comprehensive-track schools (F-live-polish_reform-10); Increase overall instructional time allocation, given Hungary ranks 32/34 OECD in lower-secondary instruction hours, as a lever independent of track structure (F-live-education_finance-06)
Risks: Evidence record explicitly notes this class of reform has not been evaluated for impact on the equity gaps it targets, meaning outcomes are unproven (F-live-portuguese_reform-07); Without altering the selection mechanism or track structure, high-performing peer and funding outflows continue unabated, so compensatory funding may only partially offset — not eliminate — the underlying structural loss (F-live-education_finance-05, F-live-school_network_planning-07); Government has selectively invoked equity findings for legitimation while implementation has lagged in the past, raising credibility and follow-through risk (F-live-portuguese_reform-09); Persistent within-school classroom sorting by SES may absorb some of the intended benefit, since sorting tends to relocate rather than disappear (F-live-equity_and_social_mobility-10)
Evidence: weak

TP-live-t5 — Demographically-driven passive contraction (no-new-policy baseline)
Goal: Serve as the explicit counterfactual: allow shrinking birth cohorts and statutory class-size minimums to force network consolidation and a natural decline in the selective track's absolute size and share, absent any new active equity or structural policy
Mechanisms: Structural, multi-decade fertility and cohort decline reducing absolute demand for all track types including 6/8-year gimnázium (F-live-demography-01, F-live-demography-02); Statutory minimum class-size thresholds (26 recommended/28/34 maximum) becoming mechanically unenforceable in shrinking rural and low-density regions, forcing de facto closure or consolidation of viable intake classes independent of policy preference (F-live-demography-05); Regional child-population contraction (10-30% in Western Transdanubia, Southern Baranya, Northern Hungary) creating structural viability challenges for multi-track pipelines even absent any equity-motivated reform (F-live-demography-04); Existing geographic concentration of tracks in urban/Budapest areas means rural contraction disproportionately affects already-marginal rural provision, while urban flagship oversubscription may persist longer (F-live-school_network_planning-05, F-live-demography-03)
Risks: Passive contraction is geographically uneven: urban flagship schools with path-dependent prestige may retain oversubscription and selectivity intensity even as rural provision withers, potentially worsening geographic equity even as national aggregate share falls (F-live-school_network_planning-05, F-live-demography-04); Without any composition-focused rule, the SES/family-capital sorting mechanism identified across the evidence record continues unabated among whatever reduced capacity remains; Church/private schools outside demographic-driven state consolidation pressure (or with different capacity dynamics) could see relatively increased share, altering sector composition beyond public-sector planning intent (F-live-legal_and_governance-07, F-live-implementation_planning-05)
Evidence: strong
