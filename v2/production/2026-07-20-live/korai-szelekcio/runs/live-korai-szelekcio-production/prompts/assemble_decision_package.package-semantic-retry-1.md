DILEMMAS:
- Structural depth versus institutional friction and legal risk: Deeper structural reform (delaying tracking to age 14+) most directly targets the documented driver of inequality — early selection age — but requires primary legislative change, confronts the constitutional free-choice guarantee, and faces the strongest coalition of veto players (gimnázium directors, ideological governing coalition). Shallower reforms (admissions tweaks, capacity caps) are more politically and legally deliverable but leave the structural mechanism intact.
- Free parental choice versus anti-sorting catchment constraints: Hungary's constitutionally protected free choice of educational institution is treated by the sitting minister as non-negotiable, yet the evidence identifies primary-school choice as an independent segregation mechanism that undermines equity gains from any tracking-age reform unless bundled with catchment/anti-sorting rules that necessarily constrain that same choice.
- Contraction location: state-sector control versus displacement to ungoverned channels: Any active contraction of the selective state track (via caps, thresholds, or structural reform) is bounded by jurisdiction: church, foundation, and private schools sit outside Klebelsberg Centre authority and could absorb displaced demand, meaning state-sector equity gains could be offset by expansion of an unregulated, less transparent, possibly more segregated channel.
- Compensatory equity versus structural equity: symptom relief or root-cause change: Non-structural compensatory funding (teacher incentives, curriculum, instructional time) can be enacted with minimal political and legal friction and targets a documented resourcing gap, but the evidence base is weak/unevaluated and structural mechanisms (early tracking, SES-skewed access) continue operating unabated, meaning compensatory spending may only partially offset losses rather than closing the underlying gap.
- Passive demographic drift versus active policy intervention as the default: Relying on demographic decline and statutory class-size mechanics to shrink the selective track requires no legislative risk and has strong empirical grounding, but leaves every documented sorting mechanism (SES access, coaching, Roma segregation, urban/rural imbalance) untouched and may worsen geographic equity by preserving urban flagship prestige while eroding only marginal rural provision.
- Implementation sequencing risk: phased structural transition versus capacity to execute it: The structural reform proposal explicitly designs multi-cohort phasing to avoid Poland's abrupt 2017 reversal shock, but this sequencing strategy depends on stable-enough demographic projections and adequate teacher-workforce capacity, both of which are themselves in flux (accelerating fertility decline, 16,000-teacher shortfall, aging workforce), creating a compounding uncertainty that phasing alone may not resolve.
- Durability of equity gains versus the limits of structural design alone: Even the best-supported comparator case (Finland) shows structural delay of tracking produced real but partial and non-permanent equity gains, with later PISA decline and informal 'emphasized classes' sorting re-emerging; this raises the question of whether any single-lever reform (structural or otherwise) can achieve durable equity without sustained multi-decade systemic commitment that may exceed what any single policy proposal can guarantee.

RESEARCH AGENDA:
- What is the causal contribution of the 6/8-year gimnázium track structure itself—net of free school choice, residential sorting, church-school expansion, and selective commuting—to Hungary's EU-leading segregation and SES/performance gradient?
- If selective-track capacity or entry points are contracted or delayed, how much of the current segregation would be displaced into church/foundation-school admissions, private tutoring markets, within-school classroom sorting, or residential sorting rather than genuinely reduced?
- What would be the transition costs (capacity shortfalls, unplaced students, teacher redeployment strain) of phasing in a delayed tracking age given Hungary's current teacher workforce shortage and aging demographic pyramid, and can phased sequencing avoid Poland's 2017 reversal shock?
- At what local cohort size and under which demographic scenarios do peripheral Hungarian settlements become unable to sustain even one viable selective-track or comprehensive-school intake class, and how many settlements cross this threshold under current fertility trends?
- Does compensatory funding, teacher-quality incentives, and instructional-time equalization (without altering track structure) produce measurable reductions in the documented SES/performance gap, or does the gap persist because selection and peer-sorting dynamics continue unabated?
- Would SES-weighted or coaching-resistant admissions redesign (T1) meaningfully change enrollment composition, or would elite families adapt through alternative investment channels (e.g., targeted coaching for new criteria, private tutoring pivot) to fully offset intended gains?
