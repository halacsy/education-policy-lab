DP-live-production.summary	Hungary's 6- and 8-year gimnázium tracks sit inside a system already ranked highest in the EU for socioeconomic school segregation [F-live-hungarian_education_system-06], produced by early tracking [F-live-equity_and_social_mobility-01], its interaction with free school choice [F-live-international_comparison-09], and a coaching market concentrated among university-educated parents [F-live-hungarian_education_system-05]. Six directions define the option space, distinguished by how much of the existing legal-institutional architecture they leave untouched versus restructure.

T1 (coaching-resistant SES-weighted admission redesign) and T2 (state-sector capacity contraction via thresholds) both work entirely inside the existing exam and ministerial-discretion infrastructure [F-live-hungarian_education_system-02, F-live-legal_and_governance-03, F-live-hungarian_education_system-09], requiring no new legislation. Their shared constraint is jurisdictional and mechanistic partiality: neither reaches non-applicant pools in disadvantaged feeder schools [F-live-education_finance-02], neither touches free choice or primary-school sorting that independently drives segregation [F-live-portuguese_reform-06, F-live-polish_reform-07], and T2's contraction is legally confined to the Klebelsberg Centre, excluding church/private schools that are the fastest-growing selective segment [F-live-legal_and_governance-07, F-live-equity_and_social_mobility-07]. T6 closes that maintainer gap by extending precedented discretionary gatekeeping (already used in the Tiszavasvári refusal [F-live-hungarian_education_system-09] and demonstrated causally in the Encs creaming case [F-live-hungarian_education_system-07]) across all providers, but remains preventive only, unable to remediate already-existing segregated tracks.

T3 (statutory delay of tracking to 14+ with a unified comprehensive phase) has the strongest comparative rationale: cross-national evidence links early tracking to higher inequality and flat-to-lower mean performance [F-live-international_comparison-04], and Finland's 1972-77 reform delayed tracking from 11 to 16 [F-live-international_comparison-08]. But that evidence also establishes a value-relevant condition, not just an empirical one: delay worked only bundled with ability-grouping abolition and curriculum/teacher-training reform [F-live-finnish_reform-06, F-live-portuguese_reform-05]. Empirically, Poland's 1999 delay correlated with equity gains for former vocational-track students, while its unphased 2017 reversal caused 3,173 unplaced students in Warsaw [F-live-polish_reform-03, F-live-polish_reform-05]—a direct transition-design warning. T3's material constraints are legal (primary legislation plus a constitutionally protected free-choice right [F-live-legal_and_governance-01, F-live-legal_and_governance-02]) and capacity-based (a 16,000-teacher shortfall and aging workforce [F-live-implementation_planning-07]).

T4 (compensatory funding, unchanged selection age) targets a concrete resource asymmetry—capitation funding strips feeder schools of both revenue and peer effects with no offset [F-live-education_finance-05]—and Hungary's low disadvantaged-skill attainment (12.1% vs 16.3% EU average) leaves room for such investment [F-live-education_finance-07]. Its constraint is that comparative cases show non-structural measures worked only as a complement to, not substitute for, structural delay [F-live-portuguese_reform-05], and within-school SES sorting would likely persist regardless [F-live-equity_and_social_mobility-10].

T5 (passive demographic-contraction baseline) is not a policy but a counterfactual: birth cohorts are collapsing structurally for 20+ years [F-live-demography-01, F-live-demography-02], statutory class-size minimums (26-34) make some rural intake classes numerically untenable [F-live-demography-05], and passive shifts are already visible in application data [F-live-school_network_planning-02]. Its risk, empirically grounded, is geographic unevenness—urban capacity may persist while rural provision collapses [F-live-demography-03, F-live-demography-04]—leaving Roma segregation and church-sector growth wholly unaddressed [F-live-equity_and_social_mobility-06, F-live-equity_and_social_mobility-07].

The empirical uncertainties concentrate on displacement: whether coaching advantage migrates to redesigned instruments (as Finland's 'emphasized classes' suggest [F-live-finnish_reform-08]), whether contracted state capacity is absorbed by exempt maintainers, and whether Hungary's specific entanglement of tracking with ethnic segregation and free choice [F-live-international_comparison-06, F-live-international_comparison-09] limits transferability of Finnish/Polish comparators. The value choices are separable: whether to accept a lower, more certain equity ceiling for legal/political feasibility (T1, T2, T4, T6) versus pursue larger, contested structural change (T3) against an ideologically committed governing coalition and an organized director lobby with direct political access [F-live-political_feasibility-05, F-live-political_feasibility-07]; and whether free choice should be treated as a fixed constraint or a legitimate reform target. Research that would most change the choice: the causal contribution of track structure itself net of choice and church expansion; where displaced demand and advantage would relocate under each lever; and whether a phased, resourced delay is feasible given the teacher shortage [F-live-implementation_planning-07].
