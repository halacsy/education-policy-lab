# Research Notes: Political Economy of Village Primary School Policy in Hungary

## 1. Centralization removed the traditional veto player (municipalities)
Since 2013, municipalities lost formal authority over school maintenance to a central agency. 
Two years before 2015, all primary and secondary-level educational institutions that had been run by municipalities were taken over by the state, and a special ministerial agency, the Klebelsberg Institution Maintenance Centre (KLIK), was set up to carry out these tasks in 198 school districts.
 
Act CXC on National Public Education (2011) laid the groundwork for extensive centralization, mandating state oversight of public school operations previously managed by local municipalities, effective January 1, 2013.

**Inference:** This shifts the locus of closure/keep-open decisions from locally accountable mayors to a national bureaucracy nominally insulated from village-level electoral pressure — but see Finding 6 on reversal.

## 2. Church takeover is an established "alternative operator" coalition for keeping village schools open
Rather than closing schools, a recurring solution has been transferring the same building/staff/students to a church operator. 
A new expansion was facilitated by an amendment that either nationalized schools previously run by municipalities or allowed them to be handed over to other non-state organizations; the expansion of the denominational sector involved not only new schools but also the takeover of old ones, becoming denominational without disruption from one day to the next.
 This is fiscally attractive to local government: 
a church takeover means the local government can save a lot of money, while the overall costs for national government are greater because of supplementary grants for church-run facilities.
 It also serves the ruling coalition's ideological goals: 
the movement tailors well with the goals of government education policy, namely to promote ethical and religious education, and this goal is apparently worth the money for the ministry.

**Contested/distributional caveat:** 
As many smaller villages have only one school and the closest neighbouring village is too far away, such a takeover can represent a violation of the right to unbiased, neutral education
 — i.e., "keeping the school open" via church transfer removes school choice in monopoly-supply villages, and expansion has been geographically skewed: 
according to TÁRKI Societal Report data, increasingly only church-run schools are found in small towns and villages; attendance grew 60% between 2001-2011 and a further 47% between 2011-2014.
 France24/sociologist commentary flags a further contested equity effect: 
some religious schools actively seek out students from wealthy families at the expense of poorer children and those from the Roma minority.


## 3. Community "rescue" coalitions exist but are not generalizable
Peer-reviewed case-study evidence from a Hungarian village shows local coalitions can save a school, but success is conditional on rare social capital. 
The case study illustrates the cultural and spatial barriers impeding cooperation in education, and — given a remarkably vibrant community with strong intra-community horizontal ties — uses the concept of social capital to explain how bonding and bridging networks, as well as "missing links," influence a school-rescue operation.

**Inference:** This implies rescue coalitions are village-specific (contingent on local leadership/network structure), not a scalable policy mechanism — a "coalition of the willing" model rather than a systemic solution.

## 4. Government has an independent, parallel political incentive to keep rural infrastructure alive (not necessarily schools specifically)
Village-level depopulation is a salient policy target with its own funding vehicle, the "Magyar Falu Program" (Hungarian Village Program), which is not specifically a school policy but shapes the political environment schools sit in. 
Hungary's Village Funding Scheme is directed specifically at financing municipalities to avoid depopulation and improve rural living circumstances, and has provided 600 localities with a total of 10 billion forints (EUR 28.3 million) to develop communal spaces and community life.
 Orbán has personally framed rural revival as a governing narrative: 
Prime Minister Viktor Orbán has stated that Hungary is "now close to being able to say that life in a Hungarian village can be of similar quality to life in the capital."

**Fact vs. inference:** The scale of depopulation is independently documented — 
if there is no significant change in growth rate, 147 localities will eventually be completely depopulated; 645 localities have populations below 300, and 114 of those have under-18 populations below 10 percent, a situation analysts (GKI) consider unlikely to survive without significant development programs.
 Whether the Village Program's community-space investments translate into school-specific protection is not established in the sources — it is inference, not documented fact.

## 5. Fiscal/administrative dysfunction in the centralized model undermines the "efficiency" rationale for closures
Centralization was justified partly on cost-equalization grounds, but has produced documented budget failures. 
Placing institutions under direct state maintenance seemed to address problems from differentiated municipal finances; resource constraints of smaller municipal schools were addressed, but the funding situation of previously better-off urban schools declined substantially, and recurring KLIK solvency problems required financial consolidation due to systematic under-budgeting.
 Independent commentary corroborates persistent insolvency: 
KLIK is in the red ever since its first year, despite annually receiving billions of forints.

**Inference:** A chronically underfunded central maintainer has weaker capacity to sustain a costly network of small rural schools long-term, creating latent pressure toward consolidation regardless of stated policy — but this same dysfunction (e.g., delayed repairs, bureaucratic paralysis) also undercuts government claims that centralization improves service delivery, a point opposition and unions can use rhetorically.

## 6. Centrally-driven closures/mergers remain empirically reversible under local mobilization
A 2025 Budapest case (not rural, but structurally identical governance mechanism — tankerület authority) shows that district-level merger/reorganization decisions can be reversed after local pushback. 
A district school authority (Külső-Pesti Tankerületi Központ) planned to merge a school with another citing low child numbers; the plan, which the local municipal education committee did not support, provoked parent and student dissatisfaction, a petition gathering 4,000 signatures, and two demonstrations.
 The authority then withdrew: 
the district centre "withdraws its reorganization proposal" for merging the two named schools.

**Inference:** This demonstrates that even after formal decision-making authority was centralized away from municipalities, closure/merger proposals remain politically exposed to reversal when they generate visible, organized local opposition (petitions, demonstrations, negative media coverage) — a dynamic plausibly transferable to rural cases, though this specific example is urban and enrollment-decline-driven rather than depopulation-driven.

## 7. Teacher shortage and 2022–2023 labor unrest constrain both closure and non-closure paths
A nationwide teacher mobilization created a union/parent/student coalition opposing government education policy generally, which affects the political cost of any school reorganization. 
Hungary's teachers were not resisting alone — thousands of pupils and parents protested in solidarity, with the largest demonstration in October 2022 drawing crowds of over 50,000.
 Structural staffing pressure is independently documented: 
teachers launched an "I want to teach" campaign calling for civil disobedience to demand higher wages, a solution to a deepening shortage of teachers, and the right to strike.
 Government response included legal restriction of strike rights: 
denied the legal right to strike, teachers resorted to "civil disobedience," which was treated as unlawful refusal to work and grounds for dismissal.

**Inference:** A national teacher-shortage crisis makes staffing small, isolated village schools harder (pushing toward consolidation for staffing-efficiency reasons) while simultaneously giving unions (PDSZ) and allied parent/student networks a mobilization infrastructure that could be redirected against specific village closures, raising the political cost of consolidation.

## 8. Historical pattern: rural school network size has swung repeatedly with regime/political-ideology shifts, not settled by technocratic logic alone
Long-run Hungarian evidence shows the rural school network has expanded and contracted in step with political centralization cycles across regimes. 
Under 1950s communist-era centralization, small rural villages proved incompatible with the regime's notion of centralization and large-scale industrialization; public services and infrastructure rapidly declined in many small villages during the 1970s, and the total number of primary schools nationwide dropped from above 6000 to nearly 4200 in just 10 years.
 Earlier, church-run rural primary education had already been drastically reduced under a different centralizing regime: 
only ten parochial secondary grammar schools were maintained from the churches' more than 4000 primary and roughly 100 secondary schools
 after nationalization.
**Inference:** This suggests the current coalition (state-centralization + church-outsourcing hybrid) is one point in a long historical oscillation between decentralized/pluralist and centralized rural-school governance, implying no institutional settlement is likely to be permanent — future political turnover (e.g., a change of government) carries historically grounded precedent for reversing the current arrangement.

## 9. Post-2004 Hungarian precedent: prior wave of amalgamation/closure already occurred and reshaped supply
Earlier reforms already substantially reduced the number of independently operating village schools, meaning the "stock" of villages with a school to fight over has already shrunk once. 
Small scale rural municipalities in Hungary faced grave financial difficulties, and pressed amalgamations/closures between 2004 and 2008 halved the numbers of independently run units.

**Inference:** Any future coalition debate operates on a residual set of villages that already survived one closure wave (either through political protection, catchment advantages, or local mobilization), suggesting remaining cases may be disproportionately those with either strong local coalitions or particular political salience (e.g., Fidesz-aligned rural strongholds) — this is inference, not directly evidenced in the sources retrieved.
