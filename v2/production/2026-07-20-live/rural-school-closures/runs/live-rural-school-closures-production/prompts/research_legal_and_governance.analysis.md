# Research Notes: Hungarian Primary Schools in Depopulating Villages — Legal Authority and Governance

## 1. Baseline legal framework and the 2013 centralization
Until 2013, Hungarian public schools were maintained by local municipalities. 
Until 2013, public schools in Hungary were maintained by local governments. In that year, the government decided that the state would take over this duty and for that purpose a state agency has been created.
 This was implemented through Act CXC of 2011 on National Public Education (Nkt.): 
Public schools have been nationalized as of 1 January 2013, with a new centralized body, the Klebelsberg School Maintainer Centre, becoming the sole maintainer of all primary and secondary schools handed over by municipalities.
 A key structural effect was loss of institutional legal personality: 
this move was widely referred to as the "nationalization of schools"; state-owned schools were terminated as separate legal entities and merged into the organization of KLIK.
 Municipalities retained only kindergarten maintenance as a rule: 
while kindergarten education is partly funded by municipalities, the funding of primary and secondary education used to be partially a municipality task, though this changed with the Public Education Act.
 Note a size-based split on operational (not maintenance) tasks: 
in villages with a population of less than 3,000 people, the operational tasks of public educational institutions were also performed by the state, while municipalities of towns over 3,000 were, as a rule, operators within their own administration.
 This is directly relevant to small depopulating villages, most of which fall under 3,000 inhabitants and thus have no operational role even for the physical school building.

## 2. Reorganization into deconcentrated "school district centers" (2016–2017)
Maintainer authority was reorganized under Government Decree 134/2016: 
until 1 January 2017 maintainer rights over most schools were exercised by the Klebelsberg Institution Maintenance Centre directly, but decree 134/2016 restructured the system so that KLIK's territorial units merged into the districts, creating 59 tankerületi központok (school district centers).
 These centers are subordinate to the Klebelsberg Center and operate with statutorily limited local input: 
a "tankerületi tanács" (district council) may invite ad hoc participants such as affected local municipalities, but the district center's economic director attends council meetings only in an advisory capacity.
 Practitioner accounts describe near-total loss of site-level and municipal autonomy: 
schools have been directly micro-managed by the 58 deconcentrated administrative departments of KLIK, called school district directorates; all school directors are appointed by the minister responsible for education, and school district directors have taken over all employment-related decision-making competencies from school directors, with all teachers employed by KLIK, not by their schools.


## 3. Ministerial responsibility shifted to the Ministry of Interior (2022)
Since the 2022 government reshuffle, oversight of public education (including the Klebelsberg Center and tankerület system) sits with the Interior Ministry rather than an education ministry: 
under Government Decree 182/2022 (V.24.), the Minister of Interior became the Government member responsible for public education, so sectoral direction of public education moved to the Ministry of Interior in the government formed in 2022
, and 
the statute annex shows the Klebelsberg Center and the Educational Authority placed among the central state administration bodies directed or supervised by the Minister of Interior.
 This is a governance-structure fact with direct bearing on which authority approves village-school maintainer changes or closures.

## 4. Documented closure practice and its legal justification
Concrete 2022 cases from the Sopron district illustrate how "temporary closure" of small village schools is enacted and justified administratively rather than through a dedicated closure statute: 
the Sopron District Center told a newspaper that "the district centers continuously examine low-enrollment, uneconomical educational sites," explaining why several small lower-grades-only village schools were temporarily closed.
 Affected villages sometimes reject the decision and self-organize replacement provision: 
residents of Babót, Osli and Agyagosszergény did not want to accept the district's decision and decided that if the state would not perform its task, they would themselves ensure that local education continued rather than sending children to another settlement's school.
 Municipalities have taken on facility costs to keep local classes running: 
Agyagosszergény's municipality undertook to continue student instruction in a suitable municipal building, while Babót's municipality committed to heating the school's usable teaching space with a mixed-fuel boiler instead of gas.
 A comparable 2024 case in Debrecen shows the same "suspension of educational activity" (rather than formal legal closure) mechanism used for a small merged classroom: 
the school district center reported that it had not closed any of its institutions, but that "educational work is suspended this school year" at a small school with 19 students in one combined class.


## 5. Unresolved allocation of pupil-transport duty between state and municipality (contested)
Legal responsibility for getting children to a consolidated school is disputed and appears to have a genuine gap. The Interior Ministry's position: 
the Ministry of Interior's Communications Department stated that under current legislation, organizing local transport is not a task of the state but of the municipality.
 However, an ombudsman inquiry found the rules do not clearly assign this duty: 
the deputy ombudsman established that the legal environment did not clearly determine whose obligation it is—the municipality's or the school district's—to provide public transport (or a school bus) for children to attend school
, while also noting 
nothing would have prevented any body, alone or jointly, from voluntarily taking on this task.
 This gap was documented in a specific case (Tiszavasvár/Bűd) involving Roma children walking long distances, described in press reporting as occurring "in leszakadó régiók" (disadvantaged regions) — this framing is journalistic characterization, not the ombudsman's own wording, and should be treated as a contested/interpretive claim.

## 6. Procedural rule for maintainer transfer to church actors, including consent threshold
When a church actor seeks to take over maintainer rights of a state-run school, a specific parental-consent gate applies, per the ministry's own statement: 
any specific maintainer change of a public education institution is decided by the state secretary for public education following a procedure involving all stakeholders; this is not a matter of intent or planning but a statutory obligation the state secretariat has fulfilled in every prior procedure
, and 
if a church initiates the takeover of an institution, more than half of the parents must support it, failing which the institution cannot be transferred.
 This is a factual procedural safeguard, though its application to village primary schools threatened by depopulation-driven closure (versus consolidation into a state district) has not been evidenced in the sources reviewed here.

## 7. Alternative legal maintainer route for minority (including Roma) communities
A distinct legal channel exists for national minority self-governments to become school maintainers, relevant where depopulating villages have high Roma population shares: 
Act CLXXIX of 2011 on the rights of minorities gives grounds for the takeover of primary schools by national minority self-governments in cases where 75% of the students of the respective school already receive minority education.
 Uptake has been limited: 
as of September 2012, three schools had been transferred to the National Roma Self-Government.
 This mechanism sits alongside, and can substitute for, ordinary state/district maintenance, creating a parallel governance track with its own accountability structure (self-government bodies rather than municipal councils or the district center).

## 8. 2025–2026: reversals via political intervention and a proposed structural rollback (current, in flux)
Recent cases show closure/merger decisions by school district centers being reversed not through a formal legal appeal but through political-administrative intervention after public protest, indicating an informal, non-codified accountability channel: 
parents of children attending one Budapest school began collecting signatures and organized a protest after learning the district center would close and merge the institution with another; parents gathered over 4,000 signatures, and the county government commissioner announced he had persuaded district leaders to abandon their plan.
 Separately, the district's own municipal education committee had opposed the plan without binding effect: 
the district center's plan — which was not even supported by the municipality's Education, Culture, Sport, Youth and Nationalities Committee — provoked dissatisfaction among parents and students.
 As of a report from May 2026, the government is reportedly planning a structural (not abolitionist) overhaul: 
the government wants not to abolish but to radically transform the Klebelsberg Center and the school-district system; under the plans, school directors would gain greater autonomy, and municipalities and local communities would again have a say in how schools operate.
 A government spokesperson's stated rationale: 
"central control has proven unworkable in daily practice," citing cases where a school director cannot independently decide on a substitute teacher even in urgent situations, or needs district permission for equipment purchases or even replacing a burnt-out lightbulb.
 Separately, at least one district (Budapest District XVIII) has formally petitioned to reclaim maintainer rights over multiple schools nationalized in 2013: 
the XVIII district municipality initiated the reclaiming of maintainer rights over a total of 24 schools, with its council largely supporting a request to negotiate with the Ministry of Education and Child Affairs over the return of institutions nationalized 14 years ago.
 These are current, unresolved policy developments, not settled law.

## 9. International human-rights oversight flags rural/church-vs-state funding disparity (contested characterization)
The UN Special Rapporteur on the right to education visited Hungary in March 2025, including village sites: 
Shaheed visited Budapest, Miskolc, Dunakeszi, Eger, and several villages in northeastern Hungary.
 Her assessment specifically links church-school growth to resourcing gaps that could affect rural/remote schools: 
Shaheed warned of a "hollowing out" of the public education system, with growing inequalities between under-resourced state schools and better-funded church-run institutions, which now account for about 20% of schools (over 33% of secondary schools); she warned that as more schools go private, particularly in remote areas, public schools will be left with fewer resources and a higher concentration of disadvantaged students, leading to increased segregation.
 This is the Special Rapporteur's independent assessment (a human-rights monitoring viewpoint, not Hungarian domestic law), and her full report is not yet finalized: 
she will present her full report to the UN Human Rights Council in June 2026.


## 10. Distinguishing fact from inference
- **Fact**: Maintainer authority for nearly all Hungarian primary schools (kindergartens excepted) shifted from municipalities to a central state body in 2013, then to a district-center structure in 2017, then under Interior Ministry oversight in 2022 — each a discrete, documented legal/administrative change 
(municipal maintenance until 2013; district-center reorganization in 2017; Interior Ministry sectoral responsibility from 2022)
.
- **Fact**: Closure/merger of small village schools is currently executed as administrative "temporary closure" or "suspension" decisions by district centers citing low enrollment/cost, without evidence in these sources of a distinct statutory "school closure" procedure specific to demographic decline.
- **Inference/contested**: Whether pupil-transport obligations legally fall on municipalities or the state remains an acknowledged regulatory gap per ombudsman findings, and this gap plausibly increases practical hardship in consolidations, though no source quantifies this systematically nationwide.
- **Contested characterization**: Framing of school-district decisions as driven purely by "efficiency" (district centers' own stated rationale) versus civil-society/UN characterizations emphasizing segregation and disinvestment effects are differing interpretive frames from different stakeholders, not adjudicated legal findings.
