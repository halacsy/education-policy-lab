## A. What the Finnish comprehensive-school (peruskoulu) experience implies

**1. The original 1970s reform was explicitly built to protect equal provision regardless of settlement type, and was piloted first in the rural periphery.**

The comprehensive school reform, following the principle of equality, started in the remote rural areas of northern and eastern Finland in 1972 and was completed in southern Finland in 1977.
 
This reform and its implementation was a success because it was realized by experiments step-by-step from North Finland to South Finland after getting experience in setting it into smaller rural area schools.
 The design logic treated rural areas as the proving ground for equality, not as an afterthought — inference: this sequencing suggests rural viability was treated as a precondition of system legitimacy, not a residual problem to be solved later.

**2. Equalization was achieved through uniform teacher qualifications and curriculum, not through guaranteed school survival.**

Every teacher, all the way down to primary school, was required to earn a fifth-year master's degree, paid for by the state, and from then on teachers were effectively granted equal status with doctors and lawyers.
 
The result is that a Finnish child has a good shot at getting the same quality education no matter whether he or she lives in a rural village or a university town.
 This is a distinct mechanism from *keeping every village school open*: equality was pursued via teacher quality and national curriculum standardization, a lever independent of school-network size.

**3. Even so, the era of strongest rural-school protection coincided with a policy of politically-set low pupil-minimums, not market logic — and this was economically contingent, not permanent.**

Economic growth in the 1980s allowed the Finnish state to maintain the network of small schools for a considerable period by adjusting exceptionally low pupil minimums according to rapid population decline, mainly due to migration losses.
 Inference: protection of small rural schools was a policy choice enabled by fiscal conditions, not an automatic feature of the comprehensive model — meaning the "Finnish success story" narrative that is often invoked already contained an active, reversible political decision to subsidize small schools.

**4. The core equality mandate persisted in law even as delivery method changed — a formally-stated national commitment to regional equity.**

Education shall promote civilization and equality in society and pupils' prerequisites for participating in education, and the aim of education shall further be to secure adequate equity in education throughout the country" (Basic Education Act 628/1998).
 This statutory language remained even as the school network itself was later restructured — implying a distinction between "equity of the system" as a legal/curricular guarantee versus "equity of access to a specific local building," which diverged over time (see below).

## B. Later erosion / decentralization and its effects on rural schools specifically

**5. From the 1990s recession onward, Finland shifted from centralized equalization to municipal autonomy, and this is precisely when rural school closures accelerated.**

Municipal decentralization in the 1990s and PISA results in the early 2000s were contingent events that supported continuation of the comprehensive institution
, but decentralization also transferred school-network decisions to local government. 
The network of small rural schools in Finland has been radically weakened since the global recession of the 1990s, and this article focuses on the social role of rural schools and the phenomenon of school closures.
 
During 1995–2003 in particular the number of small rural primary schools (with less than 50 pupils) decreased dramatically.
 This is a documented empirical trend, not contested.

**6. Closures were driven by municipal cost-saving logic, and evidence on whether this cost-saving actually materializes is thin/contested.**

In Finland municipalities are in charge of planning their school network, and closures often hinge on one thing — saving money; it's definitely cheaper to have a bigger school, as the cost per pupil can be twice as high in smaller schools.
 However, 
few follow-up studies have been conducted to determine whether municipalities actually saved money by closing small schools
 — flagging an evidentiary gap between the stated rationale and verified fiscal outcome.

**7. Rigorous causal research finds school closures accelerate, rather than merely follow, community population decline — a self-reinforcing feedback loop.**

In Finland after school closure, the population of the surrounding community decreased more than before school closure; in rural communities the impacts of school closure have been slightly more negative, suggesting a higher risk of self-reinforcing population decline.
 This finding used 
DID regression modelling combined with matching methods, focusing on the years 2010–2018
 — a robust quasi-experimental design, making this one of the stronger causal claims in the literature (contrasted with the more anecdotal/qualitative literature on community-hub value).

**8. Independent quasi-experimental evidence also shows measurable short-term achievement disruption from school consolidation itself (displacement effect), separate from any long-run efficiency gain.**

A study of 600 students at rural Finnish schools displaced due to closures in 1999–2000 examined closures that, unlike prior literature on closures driven by poor performance, were due to cost savings alone.
 This isolates the "pure consolidation shock" (as opposed to closing genuinely failing schools), which is analytically important for Hungary since Hungarian rural closures/consolidations are also typically demography/cost-driven rather than performance-driven.

**9. School choice/decentralization (parallel 1990s reform) produced segregation effects that were *not* originally intended and are broadly convergent across multiple Finnish/Nordic studies — though magnitude and causal weighting (choice vs. residential sorting) remain debated.**

International research points to school choice increasing school segregation, and long-term research on the Helsinki metropolitan area demonstrates this in the Finnish context as well; Finnish school choice is associated with growing school segregation through middle-class choice patterns.
 A contested point: 
In Sweden, school choice has been found to have a larger effect than residential segregation on the growing differences between schools with regard to secondary school performance
 — but other Finnish sources attribute much of the segregation to residential sorting 
since residential patterns are a central factor in school segregation, and micro-level processes may lead to growing segregation even when initial differences are small as parents compare and navigate the network of schools close to their residential location.
 This is directly relevant to the "erosion" question: the mechanism of decline is disputed even within Finnish scholarship (choice-driven vs. geography-driven segregation).

**10. Contemporary Finland is now facing a second, more severe wave of closures driven purely by demographic collapse (fertility decline), independent of the earlier choice/marketization debate — suggesting closures are a recurring, structurally-forced feature rather than a one-off historical episode.**

A dramatic predicted drop in the number of students will make it impractical for some rural schools to remain open, with Finland's fertility rate hitting a historic low of 1.25 in 2024.
 
In 2018, Finland had 100 schools with over 700 students, up from 13 schools of that size in 2000, while average school size increased from 145 to 236 pupils
 — a long-run consolidation trend that predates and outlasts the specific school-choice reform debate, indicating demographic pressure operates as an independent driver alongside marketization.

## C. Hungary-specific contrasts relevant to interpreting the Finnish comparison

**11. Hungary historically responded to declining rural enrollment differently than Finland — by shrinking class sections within schools rather than closing/merging schools — which produced a distinctive segregation pattern.**

The deterioration in efficiency caused by a decline in pupil numbers was typically mitigated by local self-governments by reducing the number of parallel classes, not by the closure or amalgamation of schools; this led to a large offer of small schools but reduced the latitude for within-school separation of pupils with different backgrounds — a situation especially applying to many small schools in small rural settlements operating only one class per grade.
 Analytically, this means Hungary's rural small-school retention (unlike Finland's closure-driven consolidation) shifted segregation dynamics to the *between-school* rather than *within-school* level.

**12. Since 2010, Hungarian rural/small-settlement schools have increasingly passed to church/faith-based operators, which several peer-reviewed sources link to selective enrollment and ethnic segregation — a contested but well-documented "erosion of universal public provision" pathway distinct from the Finnish trajectory.**

FBOs (Hungarian Baptist Aid and the Charity Service of the Order of Malta), the Greek Catholic Church, and small neo-Protestant churches have taken over or reopened small-size, segregated schools in depressed rural and urban peripheries, and the structure of segregated schools has changed significantly as a result.
 
Compared to state schools, church-run educational institutions have larger freedom to select their students, enjoy greater autonomy from central administration and are often better supported financially, whereas state-run primary schools have to take every student from their catchment area.
 Independent corroboration: 
the Fidesz government introduced financial measures that allowed religious schools to prosper, and in many villages and rural towns religious schools became the only option
 — convergent across an academic source (Springer/T&F) and an advocacy/journalistic source, though interpretive framing (deliberate segregation vs. incidental effect) differs.

**13. Hungary's 2013 re-centralization (KLIK) of school governance did not resolve — and by some measures entrenched — achievement inequality, cautioning against assuming centralization alone (the reverse of Finland's decentralization move) fixes rural/disadvantaged-school disparities.**

Until 2013, Hungary had a decentralized school system in which local governments were responsible for provision, and although a 2013 reform transferred responsibility for school governance to a central agency, the reform did not decrease inequalities in student achievement.
 This is a directly relevant counter-lesson: Hungary already tried the "opposite" institutional lever from Finland's decentralization trend, and outcome inequality did not improve — suggesting governance-level structure (centralized vs. decentralized) is not, by itself, the decisive variable; provider-type (state vs. church) and settlement-level poverty may matter more in the Hungarian case.
