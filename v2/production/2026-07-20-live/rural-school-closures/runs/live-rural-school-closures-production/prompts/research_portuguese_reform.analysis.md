# Research Notes: Small-School Viability in Depopulating Hungarian Villages

## Scope note
Search covered Hungarian-specific governance/demographic data and comparative/international evidence on rural school size, closure effects, and non-structural quality/equity interventions (multigrade pedagogy, funding formulas). Findings are grouped as facts (with citations) and flagged where evidence is contested or inferential.

---

### 1. Hungary already centralized school governance nationally in 2013, removing local control as a lever

Prior to 2013, Hungarian municipalities maintained and operated public primary and secondary schools, affording local governments significant autonomy, but this decentralized model contributed to disparities in resource allocation and educational quality across regions; in 2013 the central government assumed responsibility for public schools through the Public Education Act to standardize operations and mitigate inequalities.
 
Schools were consolidated under the Klebelsberg Institution Maintenance Centre (KLIK), which curtailed institutional autonomy by centralizing financial control, teacher employment, and curriculum oversight, with principals losing authority over hiring and budgets.
 This means village-level decisions about school viability are no longer purely local — any reform interacts with a centralized administrative structure.

### 2. Funding/maintenance architecture was itself twice restructured (2013, 2017), directly affecting small villages

In villages with a population of less than 3,000 people, the operational tasks of public educational institutions were performed by the state, while local municipalities of towns over 3,000 were, as a rule, operators of public education institutions.
 
From 1 January 2017 maintenance was transferred to 58 (later 60) school district centres replacing KLIK, which eliminated the prior duality of funding (municipal operation budget vs. state maintenance budget) that had caused many conflicts.
 *Inference:* small villages have already been structurally separated from ordinary municipal funding logic, meaning "closure vs. keep-open" decisions are made administratively at district level, not by village councils.

### 3. Scale of the current attrition: primary "school sites" (not necessarily whole schools) fell between 2021 and 2024

Nationally, there were 9,046 school sites (feladatellátási hely) in 2021, decreasing to 8,590 by 2024; of these, 3,609 provided primary education (ISCED 1–2) in 2021, declining to 3,521 by 2024.
 A related figure states 
by 2024, more than 1,200 of 3,155 settlements had no public educational institutions
, though this figure covers all institution types (kindergarten through secondary), not primary schools specifically — flagged as a scope ambiguity in the source.

### 4. Rural areas show growing concentration of disadvantaged children and diverging child-teacher ratios (a possible equity red flag distinct from mere enrollment size)

A 2021–2024 settlement-level clustering study identified five settlement types — Overburdened Growth Areas, Declining but Stabilizing Areas, Disadvantaged Declining Areas, Moderately Declining Balanced Areas, and Balanced Growth Areas — revealing a deepening divide between dynamic urban/suburban zones and disadvantaged rural peripheries, with disadvantaged children increasingly concentrated in rural areas (up to 94% in certain settlements).
 
The same research links this to spatial selectivity and sorting shaped by catchment policies, school closures in peripheral areas, and residential mobility toward more advantaged localities, arguing these disparities are products of systemic processes, not just demographic reflections of poverty.
 This is an *interpretive claim by the study's authors*, not a directly observed causal mechanism.

### 5. A national teacher shortage and aging workforce constrains any small-school-preservation strategy regardless of closure decisions

The situation in Hungary's education system is described as dire, with a severe teacher shortage and an aging teaching workforce.
 
Actual salaries of primary teachers in Hungary are 34% lower than those of tertiary-educated, full-time, full-year workers, compared to an average of 17% lower across the OECD.
 
In countries where the share of older teachers was already high – such as Estonia and Hungary – it has remained elevated.
 A partial policy response: 
a significant average salary increase of 32% for teachers occurred in early 2024.
 *Inference:* even non-structural measures (better pedagogy, mentoring, multigrade training) presuppose a stable teacher supply, which is currently constrained nationally — a coordinating precondition independent of the closure decision itself.

### 6. Comparative causal evidence (Finland) suggests school closures are followed by accelerated local depopulation — but this is correlational/DID from a different country, not proven for Hungary

Over the last two decades, many Finnish municipalities attempted to cut costs by closing small schools, particularly rural schools with low enrolment, on the argument that out-migration and depopulation require adjustments to public infrastructure.
 
A difference-in-differences study combined with matching methods, focusing on 2010–2018, found that after school closure, the population of the surrounding community decreased more than before the school closure.
 This is **contested/context-specific evidence**: it establishes a Finland-specific association between closure and subsequent depopulation, but does not by itself demonstrate that closure *causes* depopulation versus both being driven by a common prior trend, and it has not been replicated for Hungary in the search results found.

### 7. International evidence on rural-urban achievement gaps suggests socio-economic composition, not school size/location per se, drives most of the gap — supporting the plausibility of non-structural equity measures

Some of the perceived challenges of providing rural education, such as inadequate infrastructure or a lack of quality teachers, are far from universal, and rural-urban gaps in academic performance generally disappear after accounting for [socio-economic status].
 
Rural schools are usually smaller and have lower student-teacher ratios than urban schools, but they are also more likely to have a less socio-economically advantaged student body, experience staff shortages and have a lower proportion of qualified teachers.
 *Inference:* this OECD (PISA/TALIS-based) finding implies that if Hungarian rural underperformance is primarily an SES/staffing composition effect rather than a school-size effect, then equity-targeted, non-structural levers (staffing incentives, targeted resources) could in principle address much of the quality gap without closing schools — though this is a general OECD pattern, not a Hungary-specific causal test.

### 8. A specific non-structural pedagogical lever — multigrade/mixed-age teaching — has documented models but requires deliberate teacher training/networking, not just default coping

Initiatives such as Chile's Puentes Educativos exploit the potential of multigrade teaching in rural schools for developing student competencies through changes in rural teachers' educational practices, curriculum, and materials, alongside "urban" alternative models based on small schools, multigrade teaching, moderate student-teacher ratios and close home-school relationships.
 Separately, Norwegian research on "Schools with an age-mixed organisation of teaching" found that 
cross-network collaboration among such schools strengthens teacher collaboration and mutual professional learning, and that professional learning communities in age-mixed, small-school contexts are reinforced when schools join cross-school networks enabling shared responsibility and deeper trust.
 *Inference:* multigrade teaching is not inherently a "coping" default (as sometimes assumed) but functions as a genuine quality strategy only when supported by specific teacher training, materials, and inter-school networks — an infrastructure Hungary would need to build deliberately rather than simply choosing "not to close."

### 9. Hungary's parallel church-school expansion complicates any small-school equity strategy, because it changes who bears catchment obligations

The number of church-run educational institutions in Hungary has doubled since 2010, and compared to state schools, church-run institutions have greater freedom to select their students, enjoy greater autonomy from central administration, and are often better financially supported, whereas state-run primary schools have to take every student from their catchment area.
 *Inference:* if better-off rural families increasingly opt into selective church schools, remaining state village schools may retain a residual, more disadvantaged population — meaning "keep the village school open" and "achieve equity" are not automatically aligned goals; the selection dynamic could concentrate disadvantage in the schools most at risk of low enrollment.

### 10. Roma segregation litigation shows that catchment-area/school-network policy is independently binding under human-rights law, constraining any village-school solution

In Szolcsán v. Hungary (2023), the European Court of Human Rights ruled that education of Roma children in a school almost exclusively attended by Roma children breached the prohibition of discrimination, holding that the State had a duty not to discriminate and that Hungary must develop a policy to end segregation in education.
 
In 2016, the European Commission had already launched infringement proceedings against Hungary for the segregation of Romani children in schools
, and as of a 2024 study, 
this research conclusively demonstrates that the education system in Hungary continues to fail many of its Romani children.
 *Fact, contested in effect:* this is a binding legal finding on discrimination, not a specific ruling on rural depopulation policy, but it establishes that catchment-based decisions about which village schools to keep/close/merge are subject to ongoing legal scrutiny for their segregation effects — an external constraint on any purely enrollment/efficiency-based closure criterion.

### 11. A documented Hungarian case (Erdőkürt) shows community social capital, not central policy, has been the mechanism sustaining at least one at-risk village school

Despite demographic and financial pressures, closures or amalgamations occurred only rarely in Hungary; the rural school network was kept relatively intact until as late as 2004 as a result of state support provided specifically to small villages to maintain schools.
 
A case study of one village illustrates the cultural and spatial barriers impeding cooperation in education, using the concept of social capital to explain how bonding and bridging networks, as well as "missing links," influence community actions to save a school.
 *Inference:* this single case cannot be generalized as a scalable model — it is presented in the literature as illustrating how uneven local social capital (not policy design) determines which villages succeed in retaining schools, implying that a purely non-structural, community-reliant strategy would produce uneven outcomes across villages rather than a uniform solution.

## Summary of fact/inference separation

**Established facts:** Hungary's school governance is fully centralized since 2013 (state/district-level maintenance); primary school site numbers declined 2021–2024; disadvantaged children are increasingly concentrated in rural settlements; Hungary has a documented national teacher shortage and below-OECD-average relative teacher pay; the ECtHR has ruled Hungary in violation of non-discrimination obligations for school segregation; church-run schools have expanded and can select students while state schools cannot.

**Contested/non-Hungary-specific evidence:** the Finland DID finding that closures precede accelerated depopulation is Finland-specific and correlational; the OECD finding that rural-urban achievement gaps "generally disappear" after SES controls is a general OECD pattern, not verified for Hungary specifically; multigrade-teaching quality models (Chile, Norway) are drawn from non-Hungarian systems and presuppose training/networking infrastructure not confirmed to exist in Hungary.

**No source found** in this search directly quantifies whether non-structural Hungarian equity interventions alone (without any consolidation) have measurably closed rural attainment or retention gaps in Hungary specifically; this remains an open evidentiary gap in the available search results.
