# Research Notes: School Network Planning for Depopulating Villages in Hungary

## Scope note
These notes address the disciplinary question — network geography, access/transport, and capacity — not policy recommendations. Evidence draws on Hungarian case studies, Central Statistical Office (KSH) data, comparative Central/Northern European research, and administrative/legal history.

---

**1. National demographic baseline constrains any "keep schools open" scenario.**

Hungary's 2022 census recorded a population decline of 334,000 individuals (3.4%) over the preceding decade, the largest inter-census drop on record
. 
In 2024 births fell faster than deaths, with only 77,500 children born (a 9.1% drop from 2023) and the total fertility rate falling to an estimated 1.38
. This directly shrinks the school-age cohort feeding rural primary schools nationwide, independent of any single village's circumstances.

**2. Rural settlements are losing population share and net migration gains faster than urban areas.**

As of 1 January 2024, nearly 18% of Hungary's population lived in the capital, almost 53% in towns, and 30% in villages
. 
During the pandemic migration shifted from larger to smaller settlements, but from 2023 there was a migration gain in the capital again while gains in villages declined significantly
. This reverses a short-lived counter-urbanization trend that had temporarily supported some village school rolls (2020–2022).

**3. Hungary's school network has undergone repeated large-scale contraction driven by rationalization policy, not only demographics.**

Historically, the total number of primary schools nationwide dropped from above 6,000 to nearly 4,200 in a period of only 10 years
 under Communist-era "rationalization" programs (1960s–70s), showing that network consolidation in Hungary has precedent as a top-down infrastructure policy independent of pure demand shifts. 
In recent years, due to changes in financing, the number of rural schools declined also notably in Hungary
 (secondary citation, Kovács 2012).

**4. Since 2013 school maintenance has been centralized, removing local municipal control over closure/merger decisions — a structural planning constraint.**

In 2013 the central government assumed responsibility for public schools through the Public Education Act, transferring maintenance duties from local authorities to the state; schools were consolidated under the Klebelsberg Institution Maintenance Centre (KLIK), a centralized body controlling finances, teacher employment, and curriculum, with principals losing authority over hiring and budgets
. 
Nearly all primary and secondary institutions previously run by municipalities were taken over by the state and administered through 198 school districts (tankerület)
. This means village-level advocacy for "keeping our school" operates within a national administrative and funding structure rather than local budget authority — a key geographic-governance constraint on any bottom-up solution.

**5. A documented 2007 wave of closures/mergers shows the network response pattern and its contestation.**

In 2007 the closure and merger of rural schools as a result of top-down pressure on local governments attracted considerable media attention, prompting the NGO "Human Chain" to campaign against closures under the "Human Chain for Rural Schools" banner
. Case evidence from this period (Erdőkürt, Nógrád county) is instructive for capacity thresholds: 
the village school operated with eight grades and 61 students in 2008/2009 using mixed-year classes, down from about 65 pupils in the early 1990s to 35 by 2003 due to a sharp birth-rate drop
. 
A neighboring village (Mátraszőlős) had already lost its school in 2007, with students transferred to the town of Pásztó, while nearby Tar (5.5 km from Pásztó) still maintained its own school unaided at the time but faced likely future "association pressure" (társulás) due to falling child numbers
 — illustrating a cascading, distance-graded closure pattern typical of network contraction (nearest surviving hub absorbs displaced pupils).

**6. Comparative international evidence on the "closure → further depopulation" causal link is genuinely contested, which matters for any "vital community hub" argument specific to Hungary.**
Findings diverge by country and methodology:
- 
A Finnish DID/matching study focusing on 2010–2018 found that after school closure, the population of the surrounding community decreased more than before the closure
.
- 
By contrast, studies by Barakat (2015) in Saxony and Amcoff (2012) in Sweden find no significant connection between school closure and population decline
, and 
Barakat (2014) demonstrated that while school location may play a role for some individual housing decisions, there is in general no evidence of a localized depopulating effect of primary school closures
.
- 
A 2022 study (Bernard, Joukl, Vítková) found a paradox: in villages with a school, most residents consider it difficult to do without, but only 17% of residents in municipalities *without* an elementary school consider its absence a major shortcoming
 — suggesting perceived versus revealed importance of school presence diverges.
This is a genuinely unsettled empirical question across the comparative literature; Hungary-specific quantitative causal studies were not found in this search, so the "recipe for depopulation" claim for Hungarian villages specifically remains inferential, extrapolated from other CEE/Nordic contexts.

**7. Transport/access infrastructure for rural pupils in Hungary is geographically uneven, creating capacity and equity constraints on any centralization-with-busing model.**

Almost every Hungarian settlement has access to some form of public transport, but in rural areas and small settlements — characteristic of the hilly North-Eastern, South-Western, and Western parts of the country — the frequency and scheduling of public transport often make it an impractical mobility option, with a significant gap between Budapest, other urban centers, and the rest of the country
. 
Development of barrier-free infrastructure for transport, while ongoing in places, is lagging behind other service provider developments
. This means a "bus children to the nearest surviving school" solution is not uniformly deliverable across Hungary's regions — terrain and existing route density vary sharply, and consolidation is not geographically neutral.

**8. Dedicated minority-language school transport schemes show a working (though externally funded, ethnic-Hungarian-specific) model for extending access across dispersed settlements, illustrating what organized route-based transport can look like.**

A school bus program collects Hungarian-minority students from several, sometimes dozens of, scattered villages in Transylvanian counties and transports them to Hungarian-language schools in towns and village centers
, 
transporting more than 500 students across 13 counties in 2024–2025 using vehicles seating 8 to 21 people
. This is a cross-border (Romanian) case, not a domestic Hungarian rural-consolidation transport scheme, but it demonstrates the vehicle-scale and multi-village routing logistics relevant to any Hungarian domestic hub-and-spoke design; direct applicability to intra-Hungary village school consolidation is inferred, not confirmed by this source.

**9. Community/social-capital dynamics complicate "geographic rationality" as the sole planning criterion — evidenced through segregation and status effects, not just distance.**

In one Hungarian micro-region, several settlements had Roma population shares well above county and national averages, with this disproportionately reflected in schools' Roma pupil shares
, and 
neighboring settlements perceived a successful nearby school's "pull" of pupils as a threat to their own enrollment numbers, leading to informal attacks against it
. This shows that school-network capacity planning in depopulating micro-regions interacts with ethnic segregation dynamics and inter-municipal competition for pupils (and thus per-capita funding), not solely with raw travel distance — a factor that can distort simple catchment-area/geographic-efficiency models.

**10. Broader European comparative literature confirms the tension is structural, not uniquely Hungarian, and centers on the same three planning variables (enrollment threshold, cost, community function).**

Across multiple countries (e.g., Finland), municipalities attempting to cut costs have targeted rural schools with low enrollment for closure, arguing that out-migration and depopulation require adjustments to public infrastructure — a continuing and controversial tendency
. 
Parallel Hungarian-focused research states that school closures in rural Hungary have not only reduced physical access to education but have also triggered long-term community disintegration, with several authors noting that closures in marginalized areas often intensify social isolation and ethnic segregation
 — though (per finding 6 above) the causal population-decline link itself remains contested in the wider literature even as the access/social-fragmentation effects are more consistently reported.
