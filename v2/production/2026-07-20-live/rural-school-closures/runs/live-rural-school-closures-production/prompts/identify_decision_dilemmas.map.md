PROPOSALS:
TP-live-t1 — Status-quo continuation with informal ad hoc negotiation (passive-change counterfactual)
Goal: Describe the baseline trajectory if no new statutory framework is adopted: village schools persist only as long as enrollment and district-center discretion allow, with survival determined by informal local mobilization rather than rule-based criteria.
Mechanisms: Closure decisions continue to be made administratively by school district centers citing enrollment/cost criteria without a dedicated statutory procedure for demographic decline (F-live-legal_and_governance-04).; Closure/merger decisions are occasionally reversed via informal political-administrative intervention after public protest, not through codified appeal (F-live-legal_and_governance-08, F-live-political_feasibility-07).; Local social capital (bonding/bridging networks) determines survival unevenly across villages with similar demographics (F-live-portuguese_reform-10, F-live-political_feasibility-03).; Structural fiscal pressure from national class-size/per-pupil funding formulas persists regardless of governance form (F-live-hungarian_education_system-04).
Evidence: strong
Risks: Uneven outcomes across villages with similar demographics purely due to differential social capital (F-live-portuguese_reform-10); Continued territorial equity gaps as 38% of settlements already lack any public educational institution (F-live-hungarian_education_system-06); Persistent contested segregation dynamics via church-sector growth continuing unchecked (F-live-finnish_reform-09, F-live-demography-08)

TP-live-t2 — Structural grade-splitting: retain lower grades locally, centralize upper grades (Polish-pattern reorganization)
Goal: Preserve early-years community access to schooling in the village while consolidating upper primary grades into a larger, better-resourced center school, reducing per-pupil overhead for the highest grades where economies of scale matter most.
Mechanisms: Splitting cycles mirrors Poland's 1999 catchment-area consolidation for older pupils while preserving primary buildings, distinct from outright closure (F-live-polish_reform-03).; Retaining only lower grades locally reduces the commute burden for younger children, who are most vulnerable to the documented negative effects of long bus rides (F-live-equity_and_social_mobility-10).; Consolidating upper grades allows access to specialist teachers, addressing the shortage/aging-workforce problem more efficiently than spreading thin subject specialists across many small schools (F-live-portuguese_reform-04, F-live-equity_and_social_mobility-09).; National funding formula minimum class-size thresholds (14-27 pupils) are more feasible to meet at a consolidated upper-grade site than dispersed across villages (F-live-hungarian_education_system-04).
Evidence: moderate
Risks: Poland's 2017 experience shows structural reorganization reversed for political rather than evidence reasons can create acute disruption (~7,000 school closures, 10,000 displaced teachers) if implementation is rushed (F-live-polish_reform-04, F-live-polish_reform-05); Upper-grade consolidation site selection could recreate inter-municipal competition dynamics documented in Hungarian micro-regions (F-live-school_network_planning-09); Church-sector schools may selectively absorb upper-grade pupils, intensifying segregation given their greater selection freedom (F-live-finnish_reform-09, F-live-portuguese_reform-08)

TP-live-t3 — Networked retention: multi-village pedagogical clusters and multi-service community hubs
Goal: Keep village primary schools open by pooling resources across multiple villages through inter-municipal pedagogical networks and multi-service hub models, avoiding closure while addressing the diseconomies of very small isolated schools.
Mechanisms: French conventions ruralité demonstrate a reciprocal three-year State-municipality contract model where the State maintains teaching posts while municipalities reorganize school networks, with co-financing grants (F-live-international_comparison-03).; Poland's 2025 draft legislation proposes repurposing small-school infrastructure as social/cultural centers rather than binary closure, a template for hub conversion (F-live-polish_reform-06).; Kindergarten coverage gaps mirror primary-school gaps in the same villages and are governed by similar enrollment thresholds, so combining kindergarten and primary provision in a hub addresses both problems jointly (F-live-hungarian_education_system-08).; Historically, Hungary limited within-school segregation by avoiding closures via reduced parallel classes; networked retention could preserve this benefit while addressing enrollment viability (F-live-education_finance-09, F-live-hungarian_education_system-05).; Multigrade/mixed-age teaching models show quality potential when supported by deliberate teacher training and cross-school professional networks, as demonstrated in Chile and Norway, informing the pedagogical design of clusters (F-live-portuguese_reform-07).
Evidence: moderate
Risks: Success may be contingent on rare local social capital and bridging networks, as in the single documented Erdőkürt case, limiting scalability (F-live-portuguese_reform-10); Inter-municipal competition for pupils (documented informal competition dynamics) could undermine cooperative network design (F-live-school_network_planning-09); No dedicated statutory closure/network procedure currently exists, creating implementation ambiguity absent new legislation (F-live-legal_and_governance-04)

TP-live-t4 — Consolidation with statutory mitigation: closure as managed default plus transport, equity and consultation guarantees
Goal: Where village schools are demonstrably no longer viable (including zero-enrollment cliff cases), close them through a formal, transparent procedure with binding transport guarantees, targeted disadvantage compensation, and consultation rights, rather than through the current ad hoc administrative closure practice.
Mechanisms: Scotland's presumption-against-closure model requires formal documented assessment of alternatives, educational benefits, community effects and travel arrangements before any closure — a template for procedural rigor even where Hungary does not adopt an outright presumption (F-live-international_comparison-02).; The legal ambiguity over transport-provision responsibility (municipality vs. school district) documented by the ombudsman must be resolved by statute to prevent transport gaps that disproportionately burden lower-income households (F-live-legal_and_governance-05, F-live-equity_and_social_mobility-10).; Free public transport for children under 14 introduced in 2023 provides a partial foundation but rural frequency gaps remain unaddressed and must be closed via route guarantees (F-live-implementation_planning-09, F-live-school_network_planning-07).; Given ECtHR (Szolcsán v. Hungary 2023) and ongoing EU infringement proceedings on segregation, any consolidated destination school must be subject to non-discrimination catchment review to prevent reproducing segregation via closure (F-live-portuguese_reform-09, F-live-implementation_planning-07).; Zero-enrollment cliff cases (e.g., Garab) demonstrate that in some settlements closure is not a policy choice but a demographic fact, requiring dignified/managed closure procedures analogous to Austria's 'gestures of reconciliation' model rather than contested viability debate (F-live-demography-05, F-live-international_comparison-08).
Evidence: moderate
Risks: Consolidation can accelerate local depopulation according to Finnish and Danish quasi-experimental evidence, an effect not yet tested for Hungary (F-live-international_comparison-01, F-live-demography-07, F-live-portuguese_reform-05); Closure decisions have historically provoked political costs and reversals (Swedish evidence on trust decline; Hungarian precedent for protest-driven reversal), risking policy instability if mitigation is perceived as insufficient (F-live-international_comparison-06, F-live-political_feasibility-07); Consolidated destination schools may face 'association pressure' cascades affecting neighboring villages not originally targeted (F-live-school_network_planning-05)

TP-live-t5 — National transparency and evidence infrastructure: viability thresholds, cost-visibility, and a closure/outcome registry
Goal: Establish a national, cross-cutting evidence and transparency framework — publicly visible minimum-viability thresholds, standardized cost accounting, and a closure/outcome registry — that informs but does not mandate any specific local model (status quo, grade-split, network, or consolidation), improving the quality and accountability of decisions made under any of T1-T4.
Mechanisms: Historical evidence shows repeated top-down policy swings without institutionalized feedback mechanisms (interwar expansion, Stalinist demolition, 1970s rationalization, 2013 centralization, 2017 re-decentralization), indicating the absence of a standing evidence infrastructure as a root cause of policy instability (F-live-implementation_planning-02, F-live-education_finance-10).; Finnish municipalities cited cost-savings as the primary closure rationale, but few rigorous follow-up studies verified whether savings were realized — indicating a need for mandatory post-decision cost verification (F-live-finnish_reform-04, F-live-education_finance-02).; Contested and mixed cross-national evidence on closure-depopulation linkage (Finland vs. Saxony vs. Sweden) demonstrates the need for a Hungary-specific evidence base rather than reliance on non-replicated foreign findings (F-live-school_network_planning-06, F-live-education_finance-04, F-live-polish_reform-07).; The 2022 finding that only 17% of residents in schoolless municipalities consider the absence a major shortcoming, versus over 81% anticipatory attachment in villages with schools, shows a measurement gap between anticipated and revealed preferences that a registry could track over time (F-live-education_finance-05).; Transport-cost offsets are rarely fully counted in closure proposals, indicating a need for standardized cost-accounting rules covering transportation as well as instructional costs (F-live-education_finance-03).; Legal ambiguity over transport-obligation assignment (municipality vs. district) similarly indicates the need for standardized, transparent procedural rules independent of which operational model is used (F-live-legal_and_governance-05).
Evidence: moderate
Risks: Purely informational infrastructure may be circumvented or ignored absent binding decision rules, as seen in the historical pattern of top-down political decisions overriding evidence (F-live-implementation_planning-02, F-live-polish_reform-04); Given post-2010 curtailment of stakeholder consultation mechanisms, a new registry could be under-resourced or politically deprioritized (F-live-implementation_planning-10); Data collection itself could be gamed or inconsistently reported by tankerület centers absent independent audit
