PUBLIC QUESTION: What should happen to primary schools in depopulating villages?

TRANSFORMATIONS:
TP-live-t1 — Legally codified presumption-against-closure with mandatory alternatives assessment
Goal: Prevent avoidable closures of viable village primary schools by requiring formal, documented consideration of alternatives before any closure decision, replicating the Scotland/Wales procedural safeguard within Hungary's centralized governance structure.
Mechanisms: Codifying a presumption-against-closure duty analogous to Scotland's model forces documented consideration of alternatives, educational benefits, community effects, and travel arrangements before closure (F-live-international_comparison-02).; Currently closures are decided via administrative 'temporary closure'/'suspension' actions without a dedicated statutory procedure specific to demographic decline (F-live-legal_and_governance-04).; Existing reversals of closure decisions happen only through informal political-administrative intervention after protest, not codified appeal (F-live-legal_and_governance-08, F-live-political_feasibility-07).; The 2026 governance reform already plans to restore some principal/municipal input while retaining centralized authority, providing a legislative vehicle for adding procedural safeguards (F-live-hungarian_education_system-07, F-live-legal_and_governance-09).
Evidence: moderate
Risks: Procedural safeguard may become a formality if not paired with genuine authority to halt closure, given centralized ownership of the maintenance function (F-live-international_comparison-04); Risk of inconsistent application in absence of independent oversight body; Political resistance from central authorities protecting administrative discretion

TP-live-t2 — Reciprocal rural-school contracts (convention-style) between state and municipalities
Goal: Re-introduce municipal co-responsibility and bargaining power over village-school viability through time-bound reciprocal contracts, replicating the French convention ruralité logic within Hungary's centralized maintenance framework.
Mechanisms: France's conventions ruralité establish reciprocal three-year contracts between the State and local elected officials where the State maintains posts while municipalities commit to network reorganization, with co-financing via equipment grants (F-live-international_comparison-03).; Hungary's 2013 centralization removed municipal bargaining power that such reciprocal arrangements presume, meaning any Hungarian version must be constructed as a state-initiated contractual instrument rather than a municipal right (F-live-international_comparison-04, F-live-portuguese_reform-01).; National class-size and per-pupil funding formulas create structural fiscal pressure independent of governance form, meaning the contract must include a funding-formula exception or guarantee to be effective (F-live-hungarian_education_system-04).
Evidence: moderate
Risks: Municipalities lack the fiscal capacity and legal standing that French communes have, so contracts may be asymmetric or purely symbolic; Contract enforcement is unclear given no precedent of binding state commitments at this scale (F-live-international_comparison-04); Political sensitivity value if perceived as re-introducing municipal financial burden without matching devolved authority

TP-live-t3 — Structural grade split: village-based lower grades with centrally sited upper-grade schools
Goal: Preserve early-years village schooling (grades 1-4) while consolidating upper primary grades into larger, better-resourced town schools, following the structural logic of the Polish 1999 gimnazjum-style reorganization adapted to Hungarian primary-grade structure.
Mechanisms: Poland's 1999 gimnazjum reform functioned as a structural reorganization lever distinct from simple closure, consolidating pupil access across neighbouring villages while formally preserving some primary buildings (F-live-polish_reform-03).; Poland's 2017 reversal of this reform was explicitly political rather than evidence-based, disregarding positive PISA and labour-market evidence, and caused acute disruption including displacement of 10,000 teachers and unplaced student cohorts, indicating high transition risk if reorganization is later reversed abruptly (F-live-polish_reform-04, F-live-polish_reform-05).; Younger children (grades 1-4) benefit most from proximity given documented harms of long rural bus commutes (breakfast-skipping, lower grades, reduced social activity), while older students can more feasibly travel to a consolidated school (F-live-equity_and_social_mobility-10).; Multigrade/mixed-age teaching quality depends on deliberate teacher training and cross-school professional networks, as shown in non-Hungarian systems (Chile, Norway), which would be required to support the retained lower-grade village schools (F-live-portuguese_reform-07).
Evidence: moderate
Risks: Reversal risk if structural reform is later abandoned for political reasons, as in Poland 2017, with severe disruption to displaced teachers and students (F-live-polish_reform-04, F-live-polish_reform-05); Older students' commute burden shifts rather than disappears, and current rural transport infrastructure is documented as impractical in several regions (F-live-school_network_planning-07); Risk that even lower-grade retention remains fiscally exposed to national class-size minimums (F-live-hungarian_education_system-04)

TP-live-t4 — Networked retention: inter-municipal school clusters and multi-service community hubs
Goal: Retain village primary schools as viable units by formally networking them into inter-municipal pedagogical clusters and/or converting them into multi-service hubs, drawing on French RPI-style clustering and Polish community-run and repurposing models.
Mechanisms: Poland's 2025 draft legislation proposes repurposing small-school infrastructure for crèches and community services rather than binary closure, framing rural schools as social/cultural centres (F-live-polish_reform-06).; France's conventions ruralité and clustering logic enable co-financing and staffing allowances across cooperating municipalities, providing a template for shared-resource networks (F-live-international_comparison-03).; Local social capital and bonding/bridging networks were the determining factor in the one documented Hungarian case (Erdőkürt) of a saved school, indicating that formal networked structures could substitute for reliance on rare organic social capital (F-live-portuguese_reform-10, F-live-political_feasibility-03).; Kindergarten coverage gaps mirror primary-school access problems under the same enrollment-threshold framework, suggesting hub co-location with kindergarten services could jointly stabilize both under shared thresholds (F-live-hungarian_education_system-08).
Evidence: moderate
Risks: Success may remain uneven and dependent on local social capital, replicating the Erdőkürt pattern rather than producing uniform outcomes (F-live-portuguese_reform-10); Informal inter-municipal competition over pupil 'pull' has been documented and could undermine voluntary clustering cooperation (F-live-school_network_planning-09); Regulatory guidance alone (without funding guarantee) may be insufficient absent dedicated capital investment

TP-live-t5 — Consolidation-with-mitigation: default closure paired with statutory transport, funding transparency, and targeted equity support
Goal: Where village schools become genuinely unviable under transparent minimum-enrollment criteria, close them by default while guaranteeing statutory transport, cost transparency, and targeted compensatory support to offset travel burden and segregation risk for affected pupils.
Mechanisms: Legal responsibility for pupil transport to consolidated schools is currently disputed between the Interior Ministry and municipalities, with an ombudsman inquiry finding no clear statutory assignment—this gap must be closed to make consolidation defensible (F-live-legal_and_governance-05).; Rural school bus commutes averaging over 30 minutes are associated with skipped breakfast, lower grades, poorer fitness, and fewer social activities, disproportionately burdening lower-income households, indicating mitigation must go beyond simple transport provision to address these downstream harms (F-live-equity_and_social_mobility-10).; A quasi-experimental Finnish study isolated a measurable short-term achievement disruption from consolidation itself (independent of prior school quality) affecting displaced students, indicating need for transition support distinct from general disadvantage funding (F-live-finnish_reform-06).; Consolidation/closure removes local options most accessible to poorer and Roma families with lower mobility, and Roma children constitute 45% of students in segregated schools/classrooms nationally, requiring explicit anti-segregation safeguards tied to any closure (F-live-education_finance-06).; Free public transport for under-14s introduced in 2023 provides a partial existing infrastructure base, but rural transport frequency remains limited in the same regions facing highest depopulation (F-live-implementation_planning-09).
Evidence: moderate
Risks: Even with mitigation, Danish and Finnish evidence suggests closures may accelerate local depopulation via social capital and housing-market lock-in mechanisms, an effect mitigation measures may not offset (F-live-demography-07, F-live-international_comparison-01); Contested evidence on consolidation cost savings (Texas study found none; NY study found substantial savings) means fiscal rationale for the underlying threshold-triggered closures is uncertain (F-live-education_finance-02); Community hub loss and its second-order effects on rural viability are not addressed by transport/funding mitigation alone

TP-live-t6 — National evidence and transparency infrastructure: viability registry, cost-visibility rules, and no-new-policy baseline
Goal: Establish a national, non-mandating framework of transparent minimum-viability thresholds, standardized cost-visibility rules, teacher-training standards, and a closure/outcome registry to inform local and central decisions across whichever structural model (T1-T5) is applied, while explicitly documenting the counterfactual of continuing current ad hoc practice.
Mechanisms: Hungary has experienced repeated historical cycles of rural school expansion and contraction without establishing a durable institutionalized feedback mechanism, meaning past sequences were driven by top-down political decisions rather than standing demographic-trigger rules (F-live-implementation_planning-02).; Whether school consolidation produces actual cost savings is contested internationally (NY studies found savings, Texas and multi-state studies found none), and Finnish municipalities cited cost-saving as rationale without rigorous follow-up verification, indicating a registry tracking realized (not projected) savings is needed before further consolidation decisions rely on cost claims (F-live-education_finance-02, F-live-finnish_reform-04).; Transportation cost offsets systematically reduce net fiscal savings from consolidation but are rarely fully counted in closure proposals, meaning cost-visibility rules must explicitly require their inclusion (F-live-education_finance-03).; Evidence on whether closure causes further population decline is genuinely mixed across contexts (Saxony/Sweden find no link, Finland and Denmark find a link), meaning any national framework should track Hungary-specific outcomes rather than assume international findings transfer directly (F-live-education_finance-04, F-live-demography-07).; A no-new-policy counterfactual (continuing current ad hoc administrative practice) is documented as producing 38% of settlements without any public educational institution and unresolved disputed transport-responsibility assignment, establishing the baseline against which a transparency framework's marginal value must be assessed (F-live-hungarian_education_system-06, F-live-legal_and_governance-05).
Evidence: moderate
Risks: An information-only framework may be ignored in practice given the historical pattern of top-down political decisions overriding technical evidence (F-live-implementation_planning-02, F-live-polish_reform-04); Risk of registry capture or selective publication reducing its independence and credibility; Delay risk: establishing evidence infrastructure takes time during which the underlying demographic cliff (F-live-demography-05) continues

DILEMMAS:
- Procedural safeguard versus genuine authority transfer: Adding a presumption-against-closure duty (T1) or reciprocal contracts (T2) can force documented consideration of alternatives, but both leave the core closure decision with the same centralized tankerületi/state authority that currently applies enrollment thresholds, risking a purely symbolic safeguard unless paired with real veto power or restored municipal standing.
- Protecting youngest children locally versus resolving contraction structurally: Grade-splitting (T3) protects the youngest, most commute-vulnerable children by preserving lower-grade village schools while consolidating upper grades, but this relocates rather than eliminates burden onto older students, leaves lower-grade units still fiscally exposed to national class-size minimums, and carries high reversal risk (as in Poland 2017) that could produce severe, asymmetric disruption if later abandoned.
- Default closure with mitigation versus community/network preservation: T5 accepts closure as the default where enrollment thresholds are unmet, compensating via transport, funding transparency, and equity support, while T4 attempts to avoid closure altogether through networked clustering and hub conversion. The tension is whether individual-level mitigation (transport, funding) can substitute for the community-level and social-capital losses (documented in Polish and Danish/Finnish evidence) that closure produces, especially given contested evidence on whether closure itself accelerates depopulation.
- Acting now under demographic pressure versus building evidence infrastructure first: T6 proposes a national viability registry and cost-visibility framework to inform decisions across all structural models, but building this evidence infrastructure takes time during which the demographic cliff (cohort collapse, zero-enrollment cliffs) continues, and Hungary's history shows top-down political decisions have repeatedly overridden technical evidence (as in Poland 2017), raising the question of whether investing in transparency now is worth delaying structural action.
- Fiscal cost transparency versus symbolic versus substantive municipal contracting: T2's reciprocal contracts aim to re-couple municipal commitment with state financing, but Hungary's centralization stripped municipalities of the fiscal capacity and legal standing that make such contracts meaningful in France, so any Hungarian version risks becoming a state-initiated formality that imposes co-responsibility without matching devolved authority or funding-formula relief.
- Coordinated structural reform versus isolated single-lever fixes: The Portuguese-reform lens repeatedly cautions that no isolated procedural, contractual, or structural fix (T1, T2, T3) can substitute for coordinated reform addressing funding formulas, teacher shortages, and the church/state selection asymmetry; yet pursuing a fully coordinated bundle is politically and administratively more demanding than adopting any single proposal, raising the question of whether partial reforms should proceed despite acknowledged insufficiency.

- What is the causal effect of village school closure on subsequent out-migration and depopulation in Hungary specifically, net of pre-existing socioeconomic and demographic trends?
- What is the net fiscal balance (savings minus transport, capital, transition, and opportunity costs) of closing versus retaining a village primary school, conditional on school size, district, and region?
- Does the 2026 tankerület restructuring preserve, modify, or abolish the per-pupil funding penalty structure that creates fiscal pressure on small classes, and does it grant municipalities or principals any formal standing in closure decisions?
- What proportion of recent Hungarian village school closures were mandated by central tankerület policy versus initiated at municipal/principal discretion citing unsustainability, and what proportion of closures/suspensions have been reversed, and under what conditions?
- What is the net segregation effect of consolidating village schools into hub/urban schools—does it reduce isolated single-class segregation or increase it via non-Roma family flight to church or alternative schools—and does this vary by consolidation model (full closure, grade-split, networked hub)?
- What is the enrollment/cohort-size threshold below which a village primary school (or grade-band unit) becomes operationally unsustainable, and how does this threshold interact with teacher-labor availability and settlement geography?
