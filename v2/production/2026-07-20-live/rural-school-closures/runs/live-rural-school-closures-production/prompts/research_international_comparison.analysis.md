## Research Notes: Comparative Policy Mechanisms for Depopulating-Village Primary Schools

### 1. Baseline pattern: school closure and depopulation are mutually reinforcing across multiple European systems (contested causal direction)
Empirical studies from several countries find a correlation, and in some cases causal evidence, that primary school closures are followed by *accelerated* local depopulation rather than simply resulting from it. 
Over the last two decades, many municipalities in Finland have attempted to cut costs by closing small schools, in particular rural schools with low enrolment, with the main argument being that out-migration and depopulation require adjustments to public infrastructure.
 
Using DID regression modelling for 2010–2018, the study found that after school closure, the population of the surrounding community decreased more than before school closure, meaning closures raise questions about the vitality of the whole municipality, not just the school.
 A broader systematic review notes 
evidence that depopulation rates increase significantly after the closure of primary and secondary schools in small municipalities (Lehtonen, 2021, Sørensen et al., 2021).
 This is **inferential/correlational-to-causal evidence from specific national contexts (Finland, Saxony)**, not a universal law; the same review cautions that 
the patterns in countries with intensely depopulated rural areas, like Spain or the Nordic countries, contrast with the reality of population losses in regions with vibrant rural areas like the Netherlands or Germany.


### 2. Legal "presumption against closure" mechanism — Scotland
Scotland enacted a specific legal safeguard rather than a discretionary consultation process. 
The Children and Young People (Scotland) Act 2014 inserted a new section 11A ("Presumption against rural school closure") into the Schools (Consultation) (Scotland) Act 2010, which applies specifically to closure proposals for rural schools.
 Under the mechanism, 
proposals to close rural schools should require a strong, positive case for closure following revised guidance on the 2010 Act.
 Authorities must formally assess alternatives: 
for the proposal and any alternatives identified, the education authority must assess likely educational benefits, effect on the local community and effect of different travelling arrangements, and reasonable alternatives may include steps that would avoid closure entirely.

- **Implementation problem (documented, not contested):** the law produced conflict rather than clarity. 
Cosla, representing Scotland's 32 councils, rejected a government call for a moratorium on closures pending legislative review, with one council (Argyll and Bute) proposing to shut 11 primary schools despite the request.
 The government's own review found 
the number of schools classified as rural and with a presumption against closure is very high, including not just those schools in remote or fragile communities but the majority
 of rural schools generally — diluting the safeguard's targeting.
- **Related outcome:** in at least one documented case a "mothballed" (paused, not permanently closed) school was reopened later. 
Three years after mothballing, the community approached the local authority to reassess the school's status due to increased school-age population; Highland Council reopened it with a roll of three primary pupils and two nursery pupils, and it remains open.


### 3. Consultation/federation model — England and Wales (contrast within UK)
England applies a similar but administratively separate rural presumption: 
decision-makers should adopt a presumption against the closure of rural schools, meaning a rural school will not never close, but the case for closure should be strong and clearly in the best interests of educational provision.
 Wales took a different, non-presumptive route: 
there is no presumption in favour of or against the closure of rural schools in Wales; guidance instead requires consideration of alternatives such as the federation model.
 Northern Ireland/Republic of Ireland used yet another instrument: 
a value for money review of small primary schools was underway in the Republic of Ireland in light of recommendations regarding those with fewer [pupils]
 pupils, rather than a closure presumption.

### 4. Negotiated, subsidized retention contracts — France ("conventions ruralité")
France's mechanism is contractual rather than judicial/legislative. 
Conventions ruralité are established on a shared territorial diagnosis of demographic forecasts and enact reciprocal commitments: the State maintains teaching posts and grants specific staffing allowances, while local elected officials commit to reorganizing the school network; the State can co-finance projects via the rural territorial equipment grant (DETR).
 
The instrument is officially defined as a reciprocal-engagement contract for rural/mountain territories whose school network no longer matches local school demographics, aiming to safeguard children's educational future in these territories, and is generally signed for three years between the rector, the prefect, mayors' association and departmental council.

- **Effectiveness evidence is genuinely contested.** An inspection-general study found: 
conventions ruralité only partially succeeded in restructuring first-degree school networks between 2010 and 2019, with results uneven across the territory.
 Government inspectors themselves concluded, per a Senate report, that 
the contractual arrangement does not by itself produce decisive transformations at this stage, with most conventions remaining focused on short-term organizational and staffing issues rather than an integrated territorial and pedagogical strategy.
 Success was strongly conditional on local buy-in: 
conventions ruralité show that they are dependent on stakeholder buy-in to enable significant progress.


### 5. Community mobilization / bottom-up rescue as a de facto mechanism — Hungary and Poland
Within Hungary itself, non-state actors have contested closures through mobilization rather than formal legal protections. 
The NGO "Human Chain" campaigned against closures under the banner "Human Chain for Rural Schools," directing attention to small schools that survived; the village of Erdőkürt was selected as a case precisely because of villagers' strong community engagement in saving their school.
 This occurred against a backdrop of severe fiscal pressure: 
small-scale rural municipalities in Hungary faced grave financial difficulties, and pressed amalgamations/closures between 2004 and 2008 halved the number of independently run school units, though parental choices and community actions might resist state policies.
 A comparable pattern of localized resistance appears in Poland: 
a multiple-case study of four Polish villages that in 2000 organized protests against school closures found two schools were saved and two were closed.


### 6. "Reconciliation" / soft-landing approaches to unavoidable closure — Austria
Rather than preventing closure, some systems focus on managing its social aftermath. Literature identifies 
"Gestures of reconciliation – the case of small primary school closures in rural Austria"
 as a distinct policy/community response type, i.e., accepting closure while structuring the process (e.g., building repurposing, ceremony, negotiated timing) to reduce community rupture — a different logic from Scotland/France's closure-prevention framing.

### 7. Political-accountability evidence on closures — Sweden
Sweden offers systematic quantitative evidence on the *political* cost of pursuing closures, relevant to why governments might design protective mechanisms. 
Using a novel geocoded dataset on all rural school closures in Sweden between 2013 and 2020, the study found that closures decrease trust in municipal politicians and negatively affect support for the incumbent, an effect most pronounced for left-leaning parties.
 This is offered as one explanation for why closures, though economically rational, are politically costly and often resisted or delayed by local officials — a dynamic 
directed towards struggling rural communities, already experiencing substantial depopulation
 in times of austerity.

### 8. US large-N historical consolidation — different institutional logic (economies of scale)
The US case is the largest-scale, longest-running precedent, but operates on a different premise (aggregate system efficiency rather than village survival). 
In the United States, school closures have been a common policy response to declining student populations in sparsely populated rural communities, with the rationale that economies of scale would enable higher quality education to be delivered efficiently.
 
A trend of consolidating small schools during much of the 20th century reduced the total number of public schools from 248,117 in 1929–30 to 98,271 in 2013–14.
 This is a fundamentally different governance environment (locally elected school boards with taxing power) than Hungary's centralized system, discussed below.

### 9. Contextual constraint: Hungary's post-2013 centralization removes the local-actor lever that most comparator mechanisms rely on
Nearly every comparator mechanism above (Scotland's consultation/presumption law, France's convention ruralité, Wales's federation model, Hungary's own pre-2013 community mobilization) presumes a municipal or quasi-autonomous local authority with some budgetary or decision-making standing to negotiate, resist, or co-design outcomes. Hungary's structure changed fundamentally in 2013: 
prior to 2013, Hungarian municipalities maintained and operated public primary and secondary schools with significant autonomy in management, budgeting and staffing, but in 2013 the central government assumed responsibility for public schools via the Public Education Act, consolidating them under the Klebelsberg Institution Maintenance Centre (KLIK), a highly centralized body that curtailed institutional autonomy over financial control, teacher employment and curriculum, with principals losing authority over hiring and budgets.
 Contemporary reporting corroborates the scale of this shift: 
the government centralised the running of schools in 2013, taking control away from local authorities, and the Klebelsberg Institution Maintenance Centre now employs all of Hungary's teachers with direct power to hire and fire them.
 This is a **structural transfer constraint**: mechanisms like Scotland's local-authority-driven consultation process or France's department-level contracting between State and elected local officials assume a bargaining partner and budget lever that Hungarian municipalities largely no longer possess post-2013.

### 10. Contested/qualitative caveat on transferability of "keep-the-school" evidence
The systematic review of rural depopulation policy explicitly flags a scope limitation relevant to any transfer judgment: 
the fact that almost all the literature covers only European regions limits findings' scalability, and while all reviewed policies target rural depopulation reduction as a quantitative variable, no study addresses the qualitative aspects of depopulation reduction.
 This means even within Europe, the closure→depopulation causal literature (Finland, Saxony) has not been validated against Hungary's specific settlement structure (many pre-existing very small, often ethnically Roma-concentrated villages) or its post-2013 centralized financing model, so direct transfer of the "keep school open to prevent depopulation" logic remains an *inference*, not a demonstrated result, for the Hungarian case specifically. Separately, Hungarian-specific research documents that closures have coincided with 
catchment policies, school closures in peripheral areas, and residential mobility toward more advantaged localities
, indicating the Hungarian pattern is entangled with segregation/poverty dynamics not equally salient in, e.g., Scottish Highlands or French Alpine cases — a further contextual difference constraining direct transfer of mechanisms designed primarily around demographic sparsity rather than socioeconomic marginalization.
