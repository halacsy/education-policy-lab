# Research Notes: Primary School Provision in Depopulating Hungarian Villages

*Disciplinary lens: education inequality / stratification research. Notes separate empirical findings (F) from inference/interpretation (I). Contested points are flagged.*

## 1. Scale and demographic context
Village depopulation is a broad, ongoing structural trend rather than an isolated local issue. 
The population of every fifth locality in Hungary faces a slow decline into complete depopulation
, and 
the communist era caused domestic migration, growing elderly populations, and job shortages in many rural areas
 that persist into the present. National statistics confirm this is reflected in school enrollment: 
compared to the previous school year, the number of children in kindergartens and students in secondary and tertiary education has increased, while that of primary school students has decreased
 (2024/2025 school year). At the national level, 
primary enrollments are falling by about 4 percent per annum, and the decline in enrollments is exacerbated in rural areas as a result of migration into the cities as young people pursue employment opportunities
 (F, though this specific figure is from an older source and should be treated as indicative of a longstanding trend rather than a current precise rate).

## 2. Governance structure shapes who decides school fate
Since 2013, Hungary has had a highly centralized system, which changes who bears closure/consolidation decisions. 
Public schools have been nationalized as of 1 January 2013, with a new centralized body, the Klebelsberg School Maintainer Centre, becoming the sole maintainer of all primary and secondary schools handed over by municipalities
. This centralization means 
schools have been directly micro-managed by the 58 deconcentrated administrative departments of KLIK, called school district directorates, with all school directors appointed by the minister responsible for education himself
. (I: This concentrates closure/maintenance authority away from local communities, altering local "voice" in the debate compared to pre-2013 municipal control.)

## 3. Church takeover as an alternative to closure — with contested segregation implications
A documented pattern is that rather than being closed, some struggling/segregated village schools have been transferred to church maintainers. A campaigner quoted in contemporary reporting stated: 
"There has been a trend in the last few years of churches taking over segregated schools,"
 with the suggestion that 
the government was trying to wash its hands of the segregation problem by giving control to the church
 (I/contested: this is an advocacy interpretation, not a settled empirical finding). Separately, comparative data show a modest but measurable gap: 
the average family-background status index was minus 0.05 in public schools and 0.18 in church schools in grade 6
 in 2017, indicating church schools serve a somewhat more advantaged intake on average (F).

## 4. Rural school closure and segregation have a documented, non-obvious relationship
Research by Kertesi and colleagues finds that keeping many small village schools open (rather than merging them) historically limited *within*-school segregation but did not prevent *between*-settlement segregation: 
the deterioration in efficiency caused by a decline in pupil numbers was typically mitigated by local self-governments by reducing the number of parallel classes, not by the closure or amalgamation of schools, which led to a large offer of small schools but reduced the latitude for within-school separation of pupils with different social/ethnic backgrounds
. However, 
the territorial concentration of the Roma population is increasing the number of "ghetto schools" primarily in villages, not in cities, in part because in villages — due to the higher costs of commuting — segregation occurs less through between-school choice, though this "white flight" mechanism does occur
. Separately, 
about half of all ghetto schools are located in North-East Hungary
. This suggests that **whether closure "reduces or relocates sorting" is regionally and mechanism-dependent**: closing small rural schools and busing children to regional hubs could either reduce village-level ethnic/class concentration or, if better-off families exercise school choice at the hub school, relocate sorting to the between-school level within the larger settlement (I, drawing on this evidence but not directly tested in the source for the closure scenario itself).

## 5. Presence of a village school does not straightforwardly track community advantage — evidence is ambiguous, not settled
A Hungarian Central Statistical Office-based analysis of village-level data found a non-obvious pattern: 
the better socioeconomic situation of villages with a school was not clear — villages with schools had better demographic performance and partly better living/infrastructural conditions, but employment and welfare characteristics were worse, compared to those without schools
. A parallel Hungary–Romania comparative study similarly concluded: 
rural communities with schools have a higher level of welfare, and even though in both countries school closures had a negative impact on the community, it is not implied that in those communities the worst living conditions were recorded
. (F, but explicitly flagged by the authors as "ambiguous"/contested — presence of a school correlates with some but not all measures of community wellbeing, and causal direction is unclear.)

## 6. Comparative (non-Hungarian) evidence suggests closures can accelerate — not just follow — depopulation, but causality is contested
Cross-national rural-education research states: 
the main argument for closures has been that out-migration and depopulation require adjustments to public infrastructure; this continuing tendency has raised concerns about the effect of school closures on rural development, and results indicate that in Finland after school closure, the population of the surrounding community decreased more than before school closure
. Similarly, 
among local policymakers and inhabitants of rural villages, a persistent image exists that villages without a primary school are not an attractive place of residence for families with children, and that school closures therefore are a "recipe for depopulation"
 — but this is described as a perception/thesis, not a uniformly proven causal effect, and the same source notes a paradox: 
in villages with a school, the majority of residents consider it difficult to dispense with, but only 17% of inhabitants of municipalities without an elementary school consider its absence a major shortcoming
 (contested — self-reported importance of schools may not match observed demographic behavior). Note: the Finland finding is foreign evidence being used analogically to the Hungarian case in the literature, not a direct Hungarian causal study.

## 7. Hungary has an unusually large rural–urban achievement gap, and disadvantage is geographically concentrated
Independent of the closure question, existing rural schools already underperform: 
Hungary has the largest urban/rural gap in education outcomes, before accounting for socio-economic status, of all OECD countries
 (2019 EU Monitor, citing OECD 2019). Territorial concentration of disadvantage is also documented in 2020s data: 
some regions have settlements where over 70% of children are disadvantaged
, with certain low-density regions showing demographic and institutional decline where 
rural settlements in Western Transdanubia, Southern Baranya, and parts of Northern Hungary recorded stagnation or decline
, sometimes 
coinciding with a broader demographic decline and the closure or downgrading
 of local institutions.

## 8. Early school leaving and dropout risk are sharply higher for rural/Roma students already, meaning "future options" are unevenly distributed before any policy change
Current EU Monitor data show: 
the early school leaving rate is higher in the least developed regions, in villages (község), and among Roma (58.7%; non-Roma: 9.3% in 2023)
. This baseline inequality is the context into which any closure/retention decision is made (F). Kindergarten access — a known predictor of later school readiness — is also geographically uneven: 
in 2022, 31% of municipalities had no kindergarten, typically in small villages where the number of kindergarten-aged children did not reach 8
, suggesting primary schools may face a similar small-numbers threshold problem.

## 9. Teacher-quality distribution compounds any access effects of consolidation
Even where rural schools remain open, staffing quality is unequal: 
teacher shortages are greatest in rural areas, with the proportion of unqualified teachers in disadvantaged schools 2 to 3 times higher than in schools not disadvantaged
, and nationally 
half of all teachers in the 2024/2025 academic year were 50 or older, while only 7.2% of teachers were under 30
. (I: this implies that "keeping the school open" does not by itself guarantee equal educational quality relative to urban/hub schools — a relevant caveat when weighing access against quality.)

## 10. Prior consolidation waves in Hungary have already been substantial, and rural bus/commute burdens are a documented general risk (though Hungary-specific transport studies are scarce in the results found)
A Journal of Rural Studies case study on Hungary notes: 
small-scale rural municipalities in Hungary are facing grave financial difficulties, and pressed amalgamations/closures between 2004 and 2008 halved the numbers of independently run units
. This indicates the current depopulation-driven pressure is a continuation of an already large historical consolidation, not a new phenomenon (F). On travel burden — general (non-Hungary-specific) rural-schooling literature found in this search — 
rural school bus commutes are longer than urban counterparts and often exceed 30 minutes; students with longer travel times tend to skip breakfast and report lower grades, poorer fitness, fewer social activities, and worse study habits, with longer commutes disproportionately burdening lower-income households
. (I: This is drawn from non-Hungarian rural-education research and is being applied analogically; no Hungary-specific quantitative study of commute-length effects on rural pupils was found in this search.)

### Summary framing against the two guiding questions (synthesis, not recommendation)

- **Who gains/loses access, quality, future options:** Evidence is genuinely mixed rather than one-sided. Village schools are already lower-performing on average (Finding 7) and often understaffed with less-qualified/older teachers (Finding 9), meaning "access" to a nearby school does not equal equal "quality." At the same time, closure introduces commuting burdens documented elsewhere to fall hardest on lower-income children (Finding 10), and community-level survey/statistical evidence on whether school presence tracks broader village welfare is explicitly ambiguous in the Hungarian data (Finding 5).
- **Does the proposal reduce or relocate sorting:** The Kertesi/Kézdi evidence (Finding 4) indicates ethnic/social sorting in Hungary operates differently in villages (mostly between-settlement, "white flight" to nearby towns) versus cities (mostly between-school within a settlement). This means closing a village school and merging pupils into a larger hub school could relocate rather than reduce sorting if better-off families exercise choice at the hub, though this specific counterfactual is not directly tested in the sources found — it is an inference from adjacent findings, not a settled result.
