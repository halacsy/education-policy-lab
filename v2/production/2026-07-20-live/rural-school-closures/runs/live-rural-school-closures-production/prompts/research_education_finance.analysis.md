# Research Notes: Primary Schools in Depopulating Hungarian Villages
## Public Finance & Education Economics Perspective

---

### 1. Scale of the demographic problem in Hungary

The population of every fifth locality in Hungary faces a slow decline into complete depopulation, and if there is no significant change in the growth rate, 147 localities will eventually be completely depopulated.
 
645 localities have populations below 300, and 114 of those have under-18 populations below 10 percent; a research institute (GKI) considers it unlikely that these villages will survive without significant development programs.
 **Scope note:** this is a stock/trend estimate from 2021 reporting on GKI research; precise timelines are inherently uncertain (inference from projection, not observed fact).

### 2. Direct fiscal/governance history: municipal era → centralization (KLIK)
Until 2013, 
public education was operated, governed and financed by elected municipal and county bodies with central government support
. This produced 
significant inequalities in funding and performance across municipalities of different wealth levels
. The 2013 reform 
mandated state oversight of public school operations previously managed by municipalities, transferring governance, funding, and maintenance responsibilities to a centralized agency (KLIK)
. **Contested evidence:** Proponents framed this as equalizing resources; critics found 
the resource constraints of smaller municipal schools were addressed, but the funding situation of previously better-off urban and metropolitan schools declined substantially
, and 
the centralization failed its goal of creating an effective and cheap system, instead producing an over-centralized and opaque bureaucracy
, with 
systematic under-budgeting forcing school district leaders to cut spending to only minimum mandatory tasks
.

### 3. Opportunity cost/resource allocation trend: reduced classes vs. school closure
A key historically distinct Hungarian pattern (unlike many Western systems) was that 
the efficiency decline caused by falling pupil numbers was typically mitigated by local self-governments by reducing the number of parallel classes rather than closing or amalgamating schools, leading to a large offer of small schools
. This is a direct policy trade-off: preserving village schools as institutions came at the cost of reduced within-school heterogeneity (fewer classes to sort students into), which had downstream segregation effects (see #7).

### 4. International evidence on cost savings from consolidation is contested, not settled
This is the single most consequential empirical uncertainty for a public-finance evaluation, and evidence is genuinely mixed:
- **Pro-savings evidence:** 
Studies using rural New York districts find economies of size in operating spending: doubling enrollment cuts operating costs per pupil by 61.7% for a 300-pupil district and 49.6% for a 1,500-pupil district
, though 
consolidation also involves large adjustment costs, particularly for capital spending, which lower net savings to 31.5% and 14.4% respectively
.
- **Skeptical evidence:** 
Multi-state U.S. research (Jewell 1989, 50 states) found per-pupil cost and enrollment were not statistically related, suggesting no economies of scale
, and 
a Texas study of 20 consolidated districts found no financial saving on a per-pupil basis compared with non-consolidated schools post-consolidation
. A review summary concludes: 
"there is no evidence to suggest that consolidation of small school districts into larger ones will necessarily reduce expenditures per student, increase standardized test scores, or reduce dropout rates."

- Small/sparse districts systematically 
spend more on student transportation and federal funds per pupil
 — i.e., part of any "savings" from closing a village school is offset by new transportation cost, an opportunity cost rarely fully counted in closure proposals.

### 5. Contested causal claim: does school closure *cause* further depopulation?
This is the central empirical fault line in the literature and is explicitly contested:
- **Finding of no strong link:** 
Studies in Saxony (Barakat 2015) and Sweden (Amcoff 2012) find there is no significant connection between school closure and population decline
; and 
Barakat (2014) demonstrated that while school location may play a certain role for some individuals looking for housing, in general there is no evidence of a localised depopulating effect of primary school closures
 — though this analysis reportedly focused on specific municipality types (partial scope limitation noted in source).
- **Finding of a link:** By contrast, a Finnish difference-in-differences study covering 2010–2018 
found that after school closure, the population of the surrounding community decreased more than before the closure, meaning closures raise questions about the broader vitality of the municipality
.
- **Perception vs. behavior gap:** 
Survey research (Bernard, Joukl, Vítková 2022) found a paradox: in villages with a school, most residents consider it difficult to do without, but only 17% of residents in municipalities *without* an elementary school consider its absence a major shortcoming
 — suggesting the community/identity value attributed to school presence may be partly anticipatory/symbolic rather than revealed through actual dissatisfaction once the school is gone. The narrative that closures are a 
"recipe for depopulation" persists among local policymakers and rural inhabitants
 even where quantitative evidence for the localized causal effect is weak — a case where politically salient framing may diverge from measured effects.

### 6. Distributional incidence: costs are not symmetric across social groups within villages
Hungary-specific evidence indicates that closure/non-closure decisions interact strongly with ethnic and socioeconomic stratification, making this a distributional (not just efficiency) question:
- 
In villages, segregation occurs to a smaller extent than in cities due to higher commuting costs, but still manifests through enrolment of non-Roma pupils in schools of other settlements — a phenomenon referred to as "white flight."

- 
A ghetto school can be created through "white flight" of non-Roma children even where the proportion of Roma in the community is low.

- Roma-majority schooling has grown over time: 
the number of trade schools with a Roma-majority student body more than doubled over a decade under the recentralized system, with no signs of improved school conditions in KLIK-maintained schools
, and 
45% of Roma children in Hungarian education are placed in segregated schools or classrooms
 (figure from Humanium citing ECtHR case documentation). **Note:** these figures concern segregation broadly, not specifically rural closure decisions, but they establish that consolidation/closure choices in mixed-ethnicity villages carry a segregation dimension since families with resources/mobility (non-Roma, wealthier) can more easily choose alternative schools when the local one closes or is perceived as declining, while poorer/Roma families are more dependent on whichever local option remains.
- Teacher labor-market incentives compound this: 
faculty and administration of segregated (often small, rural, poor) schools often fight against closure because they don't want to lose their jobs, and teachers who have taught in such schools find it difficult to get jobs afterward in other schools
 — an incentive misalignment where staff self-interest can conflict with student welfare arguments on either side of the closure debate.

### 7. Household/family cost side: attractiveness of villages to families with children

Among local policymakers and rural inhabitants, a persistent belief exists that villages without a primary school are unattractive to families with children.
 Separately, 
when schools are closed, the families and children living in the community may consider themselves disadvantaged.
 These are largely perception-based/qualitative findings rather than robust causal estimates (compare with #5's contested causal evidence), so should be read as documenting stakeholder beliefs and self-reported disadvantage, not proven population or welfare effects.

### 8. Hungary's per-pupil spending baseline is low by international standards, constraining fiscal slack

Expenditure per student for core educational services on primary, secondary and post-secondary non-tertiary education in Hungary is comparatively low, at roughly $7,115 USD equivalent (rank 25 of 29 OECD/partner countries, 2022 data).
 This matters directly for the small-schools question: a system with less overall fiscal slack has less room to subsidize the higher per-pupil overhead costs that small rural schools structurally entail, sharpening the trade-off between equity/access goals and budget constraints compared with higher-spending OECD peers.

### 9. Governance instability itself is a resource/incentive cost
The Hungarian system has oscillated between decentralization and centralization multiple times in roughly a decade: municipal control (pre-2013) → full central KLIK control (2013) → partial re-decentralization to 58–60 school district centres (from 2017), described as 
bringing maintenance and operational tasks back into the same hands, eliminating a duality of funding that had caused many conflicts (operation from municipality budget, maintenance from state budget)
. Frequent structural reorganization itself generates transition/adjustment costs (staff uncertainty, administrative relearning, capital-project disruption) distinct from the underlying demographic problem — an often-overlooked category of cost in this debate.

### 10. Broader U.S./international literature on optimal scale suggests no consensus "efficient size," undermining simple efficiency arguments for closure

Research (Duncombe and Yinger, 2003 and 2005) concluded that per-pupil expenditures reach cost-to-benefit maximums when district size exceeds 1,500 students, and per-pupil spending begins to increase again when district size reaches 6,000 students
 — implying a U-shaped cost curve rather than "bigger is always cheaper." Similarly, 
research suggests educational and social benefits plateaued or declined as schools exceeded certain sizes — perhaps 300–600 students for elementary schools
. This means any generic "closure saves money" claim needs school/district-size-specific evidence rather than an assumption that consolidation monotonically improves efficiency — most Hungarian village schools are far below any threshold where these studies find savings materialize cleanly, but the size of realizable savings (net of transport and adjustment costs) is not established for the Hungarian context specifically in the sources found here (inference/extrapolation from non-Hungarian data, flagged as such).
