# Research Notes: Primary Schools in Depopulating Hungarian Villages

## 1. Governance is already centralized at national level — closure/merger authority sits with the state, not municipalities
Since 2013, the maintenance of most public schools has been transferred from local governments to a national body and its regional arms. 
The Klebelsberg Intézményfenntartó Központ and the tankerületek (school district centers) were created in 2013 by the Fidesz government after it took over school maintenance from municipalities; this was reorganized in 2016, and the number of tankerületek was reduced.
 Concretely, in most small settlements, 
a municipality above 3,000 inhabitants keeps day-to-day operation while the Klebelsberg Center handles maintainer duties, but for settlements below 3,000 people, the tankerület itself is both maintainer and operator of the school.
 This means village-level actors have no formal authority to decide whether "their" school stays open — the decision-relevant institution is the tankerület, not the parish council.

## 2. The compulsory catchment-area ("körzet") admission mechanism remains untouched regardless of what happens to a school
Hungary's admission architecture is fixed in national law and does not change based on closure/merger/consolidation decisions. 
The Public Education Act guarantees parents free choice of kindergarten/school according to the child's abilities and the family's religious/ideological or national conviction, while also guaranteeing that no child is left without a place due to lack of capacity — the instrument for this is the regulation of the "compulsory admission" (kötelező felvételt biztosító) institution tied to place of residence.
 Tankerületi centers publish these catchment boundaries annually (e.g., 
under Section 50(8) of Act CXC of 2011 on National Public Education, the Sárvár tankerület publishes the admission catchment areas of the general schools within its jurisdiction for the coming school year
). Whatever happens to a village school (closure, merger, church transfer), this catchment mechanism is the tool used to redirect children — it is not itself up for reform in the current debate.

## 3. Transferring a school to church maintenance is the dominant "soft alternative" to closure, but it does not eliminate the state's admission obligation
When a municipal/state school is transferred to a church maintainer instead of being closed, a parallel structure is created rather than a substitution. 
The county government office redraws catchment boundaries, attaching the catchment of the now-church school to another school; this "new" state school with compulsory admission is obliged to accept children from the catchment, and the government office must obtain the opinion of the affected municipality/municipalities and the relevant tankerület before deciding.
 
If the school was previously taken over by a church, parents can choose between two institutions, both obliged to admit the child: the new state school designated by the government office, and the church school that took over the original.
 This is a well-established administrative pathway (documented consistently in TASZ legal guidance) and is likely to remain unchanged under current governance reform proposals.

## 4. Historical precedent: 2004–2008 saw closures/mergers halve the number of villages independently running a school — a reversible political choice, not a demographic inevitability
Village school closure in Hungary has previously been driven by legislative/fiscal policy shifts more than by raw demography. 
Closures or amalgamations occurred only rarely and the rural school network was kept relatively intact until 2004, sustained by state support specifically for small villages and a 1997 act promoting voluntary local-government associations; but a 2004 Act on Multi-Functional Micro-Regional Local Government Associations rechanneled state incentives, and within four years the number of villages maintaining primary schools dropped by half.
 
This top-down pressure on local governments to close/merge schools attracted national media attention in 2007 and prompted an NGO campaign ("Human Chain for Rural Schools").
 This is a factual historical episode; it is *inference* (not directly evidenced here) that the same fiscal-incentive lever remains available to today's tankerület system, though the funding-formula finding below (#7) suggests structural continuity.

## 5. Recent tankerület practice continues to close small "uneconomical" sites, provoking local self-help responses
Contemporary cases (2022) show tankerület centers still using an efficiency/enrollment rationale to shutter small lower-grade-only schools. 
The Sopron Tankerület Center told journalists that "tankerület centers continuously examine low-enrollment, uneconomically operable educational sites," explaining its decision to temporarily close several small schools with only lower grades in the region.
 
Residents of affected villages (Babót, Osli, Agyagosszergény near Sopron) refused to accept the decision and organized to keep local education running themselves — in Agyagosszergény, students were relocated into the changing room of the local third-division football club.
 This illustrates that (a) closure decisions are still framed administratively as capacity/economy questions, and (b) communities can and do mount ad hoc resistance, but without a formal veto power.

## 6. The class-size and per-pupil funding formula creates built-in fiscal pressure on small village schools, independent of who governs them
Legally set minimum/maximum class sizes interact with a funding mechanism that penalizes small classes. 
Under Annex 4 of Act CXC of 2011, kindergarten groups must have a minimum of 13 and maximum of 25 children, and general-school classes must have a minimum of 14 and maximum of 27 children.
 Historically, financing rules have linked funded teaching hours to how far a class's enrollment falls below the legal maximum: 
a June discussion among tankerület leaders floated that when a class has fewer than 27 students (the legal maximum), the number of funded hours per student would decrease, with concrete impact on small village schools where "the enrollment is so low that under this idea only 34 hours could be funded, when the mandatory teaching load is 30 hours and, due to the mandatory 4pm supervision requirement, the minimum required is 40 hours."
 This is a documented policy mechanism (2017) whose contemporary exact parameters were not verified in this search, but it demonstrates that funding formulas — not merely enrollment counts — are a structural lever bearing on village school viability, and this lever is set at the national/legal level, largely outside local control.

## 7. School closure/consolidation interacts with, and can worsen, Roma school segregation — a contested but well-documented dynamic
Research identifies a mechanism whereby the survival of many small, single-class-per-grade rural schools has actually *limited* within-school segregation compared to what happens in larger, consolidated schools. 
Between-school segregation is much stronger in Hungary than within-school segregation partly because efficiency declines from falling pupil numbers were historically mitigated by self-governments through reducing the number of parallel classes rather than closing/merging schools — leading to many small schools but limiting the scope for within-school separation by social/ethnic background, especially in small rural settlements with only one class per grade.
 Separately, closure/merger of a school can trigger "white flight," where 
a strong correlation between residential and school segregation is evident in village ghettos but is not straightforward in mixed communities, where a ghetto school can form through "white flight" of non-Roma children even where the proportion of Roma in the community is low.
 A concrete case study: 
in Encs, a small town of seven thousand, two municipally-owned primary schools in 2010 had almost identical shares of Roma/disadvantaged pupils, but by 2016 this had changed following institutional reorganization.
 This is a *contested/complex* causal area — closure/merger can either reduce or increase segregation depending on local configuration; the literature treats it as context-dependent rather than a uniform effect.

## 8. Scale of the demographic problem is large and growing, not marginal

By 2024, more than 1,200 of Hungary's 3,155 settlements had no public educational institution at all, raising concerns about territorial equity.
 This sits within a broader national demographic context: 
according to the 2022 census, Hungary's population stood at 9.6 million, a 3.4% decrease (334,000 individuals) over the preceding decade — the largest decline ever recorded between two census cycles.
 Village population share is also shrinking relatively: 
migration gains in villages declined significantly from 2023 onward, while the capital city recorded renewed migration gains.
 Regionally, disadvantage is concentrating spatially: 
the data reveal a deepening divide between dynamic urban/suburban zones and disadvantaged rural peripheries, with disadvantaged children increasingly concentrated in rural areas (up to 94% in certain settlements).


## 9. A live governance reform is underway (2026) that would partially — not fully — reverse centralization, but retains the tankerület layer
As of mid-2026, the incoming government is reviewing but not abolishing the centralized structure. 
The government wants not to eliminate but to radically restructure the Klebelsberg Center and the tankerület system; under the plans, school principals would gain greater autonomy, and municipalities and local communities would again have a say in how schools operate.
 
The government spokesperson stated that central control "has proven unworkable in daily practice," citing examples where a principal cannot independently decide on urgent teacher substitution, or where even procurement or replacing a burnt-out light bulb requires tankerület permission.
 However, 
it was specifically emphasized that abolishing the Klebelsberg Center "is not the goal," though its operation would be radically transformed.
 A related party's education program is more explicit that tankerületek would persist in reduced form: 
the tankerületek would apparently remain, even if with fewer powers, since the program states "we will free the tankerületek from political influence."
 *Inference:* this suggests that whichever entity ultimately holds closure authority over village schools, the multi-tier tankerület/central-authority structure — and by extension its role in enrollment-based closure decisions — is likely to persist in some form rather than being replaced by direct municipal control.

## 10. Kindergarten coverage gaps mirror and compound the primary-school problem in the same small villages
The upstream institution feeding village primary schools is itself patchy. 
Regional coverage of kindergartens remains uneven: in 2022, 31% of municipalities had no kindergarten, typically in small villages where the number of kindergarten-aged children did not reach eight.
 This is a directly comparable, currently-unresolved access mechanism (an 8-child threshold functioning similarly to enrollment thresholds for primary schools) operating in the same demographic context, and it is governed by the same national early-childhood-education framework rather than by local discretion.
