# rejected_framings.md

## S1 — Admission reform within the current structure

- CHOSEN: Reduce the SES skew of 6/8-year gimnazium intake by standardising and de-coaching the admission process, while leaving the track structure itself in place.

- REJECTED: "Eliminate gymnasium gatekeeping bias through centrally managed admissions" — reason: overstates causal effect; the mechanism is SES-skew reduction, not elimination; gatekeeping itself (the track structure) remains untouched, so "elimination" misrepresents scope.

- REJECTED: "Admission lottery replaces merit assessment" — reason: contradicts the actual mechanism (lottery weight applied ABOVE a threshold); presents the design as binary when it is conditional, and risks legal and public-legitimacy challenge on meritocratic grounds.

- REJECTED: "Open access to selective tracks via random selection" — reason: misleading; a qualifying threshold exists; the lottery only applies to above-threshold candidates, so the framing obscures the gating function and invites both false equity claims and false meritocratic challenges.

## S2 — Gradual phase-down of 6/8-year entry places

- CHOSEN: Shrink the selective-entry share of each cohort over a decade via declining annual caps, redirecting freed capacity toward strengthening general-school lower-secondary programmes — with an explicit implementation step securing the political capital the freed capacity requires.

- REJECTED: "End early tracking by 2035" — reason: falsely implies a completed structural transformation; S2 achieves a reduction in *early* sorting, not removal of tracks, and depends on a decade of political continuity that is explicitly uncertain; the timeline framing invites overconfidence.

- REJECTED: "Replace selective intake with universal advanced programmes" — reason: confuses mechanism with outcome; S2 does not replace selectivity, it redistributes it over time via caps; the advanced programmes are a retention device for ambitious families, not a replacement structure, and may not materialise if political capital is not actively secured.

- REJECTED: "Protect current students while phasing out selectivity" — reason: accurate but passive; obscures the active political work required (step 1, multi-party pact) to prevent reversal; a framing that emphasises protection without cost-raising invites the reversal risk that the mechanism depends on mitigating.

## S3 — Comprehensive school to age 14 (structural reform)

- CHOSEN: Delay between-school selection to age 14 by converting lower secondary to mixed-ability comprehensive provision, bundled with teacher and support investment.

- REJECTED: "Mixed-ability comprehensive schooling raises peer effects and equity" — reason: the asymmetric peer-effect assumption (load-bearing claim) is contested; this framing presents it as settled mechanism rather than an uncertain assumption on which S3's entire equity case depends; fails to signal the contested evidence status.

- REJECTED: "Eliminate early selection and its SES gradient" — reason: overstates durability; the mechanism depends on concurrent choice/catchment reform and teacher investment; without those, high-SES families re-sort through non-state channels, and selection merely shifts, not eliminates; framing does not acknowledge the conditional nature of the equity gain or the middle-class-exit threshold.

- REJECTED: "Comprehensive lower secondary with no differentiation" — reason: does not match the mechanism; S3 includes mixed-ability grouping but explicitly depends on concurrent differentiation retraining and support; a no-differentiation framing either misrepresents the design or invites false claims about homogeneity, both of which weaken credibility.

- REJECTED: "Teacher investment closes the equity gap in comprehensive schools" — reason: reverses causal priority; teacher investment is a necessary condition, not the sufficient mechanism; selection delay is the mechanism; framing teacher investment as primary risks misdirecting implementation effort or claiming equity gains that depend on both mechanisms together.

## S4 — Keep the structure, compensate general schools (Portuguese-style package)

- CHOSEN: Leave the 6/8-year structure in place and neutralise its equity deficit through non-structural investment (TEIP-style targeted funding, curriculum standards, school clustering) in general schools. NOTE: the premise that early selection is 'politically fixed' is itself a political judgment, not evidence; treating it as fixed forecloses options and is flagged as such.

- REJECTED: "Targeted support closes the equity gap without structural change" — reason: the framing asserts outcome ("closes gap") when the mechanism is conditional and unverified; the load-bearing assumption (compensation offsets, not masks, segregation) is unverifiable ex ante at Hungary's selection age; framing does not expose the offset-vs-mask uncertainty that determines whether S4 is equity-transformative or equity-preserving.

- REJECTED: "Portugal's TEIP model transferred to Hungary solves selective-track inequality" — reason: conflates analogue transfer with demonstrated causation; Portugal's TEIP addressed *within-track* inequality in schools that already existed post-selection; its outcome at Hungary's *early selection age* (10/12) is unverified; framing risks importing success narrative without testing applicability.

- REJECTED: "Maintain track structure while raising general-school floor" — reason: technically accurate but passive; obscures that the floor-raising depends on sustained political capital and budget protection, both of which are explicitly at risk (−30% sensitivity); framing does not signal the fiscal fragility on which S4's equity case depends.

- REJECTED: "Structure is fixed; invest in what we can control" — reason: presents a political judgment as fact; the assumption that structure is politically fixed is contested (received feedback names this as a political judgment that forecloses options); framing it as constraint rather than assumption risks self-fulfilling prophecy and precludes consideration of lower-cost structural windows.

## Cross-scenario framing discipline notes

**Rejection pattern:** Across all four scenarios, rejected framings fall into three categories:
1. **Overstated causation** — presenting conditional mechanisms as unconditional outcomes (S1 "elimination," S3 "raises peer effects," S4 "closes gap")
2. **Obscured assumptions** — treating assumptions or contested claims as settled evidence (S3 asymmetric peer effects, S4 offset-vs-mask, S4 political fixedness)
3. **Passive mechanism representation** — framings that hide active work required (S2 political capital, S4 budget protection, S3 choice/catchment concurrent reform)

**Chosen framings preserve:**
- Conditional language where assumptions are load-bearing
- Explicit scope bounds (e.g., "reduce" rather than "eliminate")
- Active implementation language (e.g., "securing political capital" in S2)
- Contested status signalling (e.g., S3 asymmetric peer effects, S4 offset-vs-mask)
- Foregrounding of unverified transfer claims (S4 TEIP, S2 family retention)
