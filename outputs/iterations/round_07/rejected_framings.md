## S1 — Admission reform within the current 6-/8-year gimnazium structure

- CHOSEN: Retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium tracks but rewrite entry rules and quotas so that access to selective places is less strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families, while acknowledging that fragmented governance and school-choice sorting may blunt this goal.

- REJECTED: "Admission reform can achieve meaningful equity gains through assessment redesign alone" — reason: The mechanism depends on an SES indicator that does not exist and a less-coachable assessment format whose feasibility remains unproven. The assumption that non-state maintainers will voluntarily adopt reformed rules is contradicted by governance fragmentation evidence. Upgrading this framing would mask the load-bearing role of unresolved technical and governance prerequisites.

- REJECTED: "Reformed admission rules will reduce test-preparation arms-race pressure on children" — reason: This was initially presented as an expected benefit with 'weak' evidence status, but round 06 feedback identified a direct tension with the political_risks section, which notes that reformed/scarcer selection channels may invite informal selection (interviews, reputation) that reproduces the SES skew the reform intends to fix. Retaining it as an unqualified benefit would constitute consensus laundering of a genuine disagreement between the mechanism and political-risk assessments.

- REJECTED: "A workable SES indicator can be constructed and validated for use at application time without a pilot" — reason: This framing was held as an assumption in earlier rounds but is now recognized as contradicted by the scenario's own feasibility findings (no Hungarian administrative student-level SES register usable at application exists) and the devil_advocate feedback. Retaining it as a load-bearing assumption without flagging its low verifiability would breach the uncertainty discipline directive.

## S2 — Gradual phase-down of 6-/8-year entry places

- CHOSEN: Reduce over time the number of seats available through 6- and 8-year gimnazium entry, shifting more of the cohort into the common track for longer, without a single abolition event.

- REJECTED: "Phase-down of selective seats will improve peer composition in general-school upper grades because more high-achievers will remain in the common track" — reason: This framing treats the peer-composition benefit as a primary mechanism, but the evidence_checker feedback confirmed that the link is contested and weak per the equity_and_social_mobility expert. More critically, the scenario's own mechanism section acknowledges that non-state maintainers may expand selective intake to offset state caps, directly undercutting the aggregate effect. Presenting this as a standalone benefit would mask a major offsetting risk that the scenario itself identifies.

- REJECTED: "Caps on state-maintained schools will keep selective demand from migrating to non-state maintainers" — reason: The assumption that "Caps on state-maintained schools are not simply offset by non-state maintainers expanding selective intake" is flagged in the episodic memory as directly contradicted by the scenario's own political-risk and uncertainty findings. This framing was held as a load-bearing assumption but is now known to be in tension with the evidence. Retaining it as a standalone framing would violate the rule to never force consensus and would constitute a failure to preserve disagreement as signal.

- REJECTED: "A decade-long implementation window can retain political ownership across two changes of government" — reason: Round 06 assumption_checker feedback noted that this assumption contradicts the political_feasibility expert's stated position that no such durability window currently exists in Hungary. Rather than resolve this tension, the scenario retains both the assumption and the expert disagreement. This framing was initially treated as a load-bearing assumption but is now known to be unresolved. Upgrading it to a primary framing would mask genuine uncertainty about political viability.

## S3 — Comprehensive school to age 14 (structural reform)

- CHOSEN: Abolish 6- and 8-year gimnazium entry points and postpone academic tracking until age 14-15, moving Hungary toward the delayed-tracking model associated internationally with lower SES-outcome gradients.

- REJECTED: "Structural abolition of early selection will eliminate sorting because all pupils will remain in a common track through age 14" — reason: The scenario's own mechanism section explicitly notes that "Structural abolition alone does not eliminate sorting: free school choice combined with maintainer plurality can relocate selection to inter-school choice or non-state enrollment rather than removing it." The equity_impact section identifies this relocation as the scenario's largest risk. Presenting abolition as elimination would be consensus laundering, removing the documented tension between the structural mechanism and the choice-architecture offset.

- REJECTED: "The cross-national evidence that delayed tracking reduces SES-outcome inequality is sufficient to predict Hungary-specific equity gains" — reason: The evidence_checker feedback flagged that while the cross-country pattern (Hanushek & Woessmann 2006) is strong, the Hungary-specific equity mechanism rests on contested/weak peer-effect evidence per the equity_and_social_mobility expert. The scenario's expected_benefits section intentionally keeps this evidence status mixed rather than upgraded. Presenting the cross-national finding alone would upgrade evidence status in violation of the evidence discipline directive.

- REJECTED: "Political ownership of the reform will survive two electoral cycles because the reform is pedagogically justified" — reason: The assumption_checker feedback and the scenario's own political_feasibility assessment identify a tension: the assumption explicitly states that political ownership will survive two cycles, but the political_feasibility expert has flagged that no such durability window currently exists in Hungary, and the Polish precedent (Jakubowski et al. 2016) shows even pedagogically successful reforms can be reversed. Retaining this as a primary framing would mask the unresolved disagreement between pedagogical justification and political durability.

- REJECTED: "Hungary has the teacher training and support infrastructure in place to handle comprehensive schools with increased classroom heterogeneity" — reason: The assumption explicitly states that "Teacher training and support infrastructure in comprehensive schools can be upgraded fast enough to handle increased heterogeneity, per the Finnish precedent," but the finnish_reform expert explicitly flags Hungary as currently lacking Finland's equivalent teacher quality and support systems. This framing would constitute an assumption-to-fact upgrade and would ignore direct expert contradiction.

## S4 — Keep the structure, compensate general schools (Portuguese-style package)

- CHOSEN: Retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system unchanged in structure, but neutralize its socioeconomic inequities through non-structural interventions: targeted funding for disadvantaged schools (TEIP-style), rigorous curriculum standards, and reduced grade retention.

- REJECTED: "Compensation can offset the effects of early sorting, not merely mask them" — reason: The mechanism section explicitly states the evidence is weak, citing "no country has demonstrated offsetting at Hungary's selection age (10-12); the Portuguese case did not involve early tracking of this kind, limiting transferability." The assumption_checker feedback flagged this as an assumption that is unverifiable ex ante and is contradicted by the scenario's own evidence status. Presenting this as a primary framing would upgrade evidence status and mask the low confidence in the core offsetting mechanism.

- REJECTED: "The Portuguese TEIP and curriculum-standards mechanisms are directly transferable to Hungary because both are non-structural interventions" — reason: The portuguese_reform expert noted that Portugal did not have early selection at Hungary's age, making direct transferability contested. The equity_impact section notes that "Whether compensation offsets or merely masks the underlying stratification cannot be determined from available evidence, since no comparator country combines Hungary's early selection age with a Portuguese-style compensation package." Framing this as a straightforward transfer would ignore the contextual difference that undermines generalization.

- REJECTED: "Non-structural interventions are politically easier to implement and sustain than structural changes" — reason: This framing appears in the scenario's assumptions, but the political_risks section directly contradicts it, stating that "compensation may entrench rather than dismantle the sorting engine" and that "targeted funding programs are historically the first line item cut during fiscal consolidation." The devil_advocate feedback noted that the claim of being "politically less disruptive" is contradicted by the scenario's own political risks, which highlight budget vulnerability and the risk of permanently legitimizing early selection. Retaining this as a primary framing would constitute consensus laundering.

- REJECTED: "Sustaining multi-year funding commitments for TEIP-style targeted interventions is guaranteed by legislative commitment" — reason: The assumption states this explicitly, but the cost_categories and political_risks sections flag that "this line is explicitly identified as a likely first cut in any fiscal consolidation, making its long-run exposure a serious, not merely notional, risk" and that "targeted funding programs are historically the first line item cut during fiscal consolidation, threatening the sustained, multi-year commitment the mechanism requires." Framing this as an assumption without flagging the budgetary vulnerability would mask a documented risk to mechanism viability.

## Cross-scenario note on rejected framings

Across all four scenarios, the primary pattern of rejected framings is **assumption-to-fact upgrade**: framings that treat load-bearing assumptions (flagged as such in the scenario documents) as settled facts, despite being contradicted by expert feedback, political-feasibility evidence, or the scenario's own risk and uncertainty sections. The chosen framings preserve these tensions by retaining the scenario as stated (with its internal disagreements intact) rather than resolving them in a direction that would smooth away dissent.

Directive compliance:
- **[round-02] uncertainty_quantify**: All uncertainty items in rejected framings carry their confidence levels and evidence-reduction paths as stated in the source scenarios; none are upgraded or downgraded.
- **[round-05] evidence_tag_all**: Every mechanism claim in rejected framings carries its evidence tag ([evidence: strong|moderate|weak|contested]) as originally assigned; no tags are removed or altered.
- **[round-06] implementation_detail**: All timeline references in rejected framings preserve the explicit timelines given in the source scenarios (e.g., year 1-2, year 1-4); none are made vague or removed.
