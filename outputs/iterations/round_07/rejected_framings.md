# rejected_framings.md

## S1 — Admission reform within the current structure

- CHOSEN: Retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium tracks but rewrite entry rules and quotas so that access to selective places is less strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families — *acknowledging* that this goal is in tension with the scenario's own uncertainty about whether a workable SES indicator can be built at application time.

- REJECTED: "Admission reform within the current structure can substantially reduce test-preparation pressure on age-10 children" — reason: The mechanism claim that a redesigned assessment "weakens the advantage that paid test-prep confers" is tagged weak (model knowledge only; Kertesi & Kezdi 2013 and Horn 2013 show selection effects, not coaching resistance). The political_risks section contradicts the expected benefit by noting that reformed channels may invite informal selection (interviews, reputation) that reproduces SES sorting. This framing suppresses the contradiction.

- REJECTED: "SES-indicator construction is a solvable implementation problem" — reason: The scenario documents that Hungary has no administrative student-level SES register usable at application time, and any proxy would be gameable. The assumption is flagged [assumption] and directly contradicted by feasibility_checker. Framing this as a solvable problem masks the low confidence (confidence: low).

- REJECTED: "Quota reform is equity-complete for low-SES pupils" — reason: The equity_impact field explicitly states that "gains accrue only to high-ability low-SES pupils who already apply" and that "the bulk of low-SES pupils who never apply are untouched." Framing quotas as equity-complete suppresses the selection-on-observables problem.

## S2 — Gradual phase-down of 6/8-year entry places

- CHOSEN: Reduce the number of age-10 and age-12 selective entry places over successive cohorts, shifting more pupils into the common track without abrupt structural closure — *while acknowledging* that non-state maintainers can offset state caps by expanding selective intake.

- REJECTED: "Phased caps reduce early sorting across the system" — reason: The mechanism assumes that "caps on state-maintained schools are not simply offset by non-state (church/foundation) maintainers expanding selective intake," but this assumption is directly contradicted by the scenario's own political_risks and uncertainties sections and by school_network_planning evidence (independent maintainers can absorb selective tracks). Framing caps as system-wide sorting reduction suppresses the offset risk.

- REJECTED: "Peer-composition gains are a reliable expected benefit" — reason: The expected-benefits section cites the peer-composition mechanism as "contested — the asymmetric peer-effect mechanism is tagged weak/contested." The evidence tag is [evidence: contested], not moderate or strong. Framing this as a reliable benefit upgrades the evidence status and suppresses uncertainty.

- REJECTED: "Parallel-regime administration is a transitional cost" — reason: The cost_categories field specifies "medium and sustained across the decade, not merely transitional." The resolution from round 06 changed this from "transitional" to "sustained." Framing it as transitional understates the recurring fiscal burden.

## S3 — Comprehensive school to age 14 (structural reform)

- CHOSEN: Abolish age-10 and age-12 selective entry and defer academic tracking to age 14, moving toward comprehensive schooling with within-school differentiation — *while acknowledging* that middle-class and church/foundation exit to non-state schools can relocate sorting rather than remove it, and that no demonstrated political window exists beyond one electoral cycle.

- REJECTED: "Comprehensive schooling eliminates the SES-outcome gradient" — reason: The expected-benefits section states the benefit is "weaker SES-outcome gradient within a decade," not elimination. The evidence status on the peer-effect channel carrying this benefit is "contested — weak/contested," not strong. Framing this as elimination upgrades the evidence status and oversimplifies the mechanism.

- REJECTED: "Sorting will not migrate to school choice and non-state maintainers" — reason: The scenario's own equity_impact field states that "middle-class and church/foundation exit to non-state or private schools can relocate sorting rather than remove it" and calls this "the largest risk." The assumptions section asserts this will not happen, creating an unresolved internal contradiction. Framing the assumption as assured suppresses this flagged tension.

- REJECTED: "Political ownership is assured across two electoral cycles" — reason: The assumptions section states this, but political_feasibility explicitly contradicts it: "no such durable window is demonstrated." Poland reversed a comparable reform (Jakubowski et al. 2016). The confidence level is "medium," and the mechanism would be reduced by "stakeholder interviews with Polish reformers on reversal switching costs." Framing ownership as assured suppresses the contradiction and overstates confidence.

- REJECTED: "Teacher retraining is the binding implementation lever" — reason: While teacher retraining is flagged as high-cost with a supply constraint, the scenario assumes "comprehensive schools can be equipped with the teacher quality and diagnostic support that made the Finnish transition work." Hungary currently lacks this (per finnish_reform). Framing retraining alone as sufficient suppresses the deeper institutional gap.

## S4 — Keep the structure, compensate general schools (Portuguese-style package)

- CHOSEN: Retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium structure but neutralise its equity effects downstream via rigorous curriculum standards, school clustering, and TEIP-style targeted funding for disadvantaged schools — *acknowledging* that compensation is unverifiable ex ante and that no country has demonstrated offsetting at Hungary's selection age.

- REJECTED: "Compensation can offset the effects of early sorting" — reason: The mechanism claim is tagged [evidence: weak — no country has demonstrated offsetting at Hungary's selection age; the Portuguese case did not alter selection age but also did not select as early as Hungary]. The assumption explicitly states "Compensation can offset, not just mask, the effects of early sorting [assumption — unverifiable ex ante]." The confidence level is "low." Framing compensation as capable of offsetting upgrades the evidence status from weak to moderate and suppresses the ex-ante unverifiability.

- REJECTED: "S4 is politically more durable than structural reform" — reason: While the assumptions section asserts "non-structural interventions are politically easier to sustain," the political_risks section identifies that "targeted funding is the first line cut in fiscal consolidation, undermining durability" [evidence: weak]. The equity_impact section notes that compensation "may entrench rather than dismantle the sorting engine." Framing durability as assured suppresses the budget-vulnerability risk and the entrenchment risk.

- REJECTED: "The root cause of SES sorting is not addressed because addressing it is infeasible" — reason: The equity_impact section states clearly: "Because the early partition persists, the root cause — selection at age 10-12 concentrating low-SES pupils in general schools — is not addressed, only mitigated." This is not a framing choice but a documented structural limitation. Framing S4 as addressing root causes (rather than mitigating symptoms) contradicts the scenario's own equity_impact assessment.

- REJECTED: "Portuguese TEIP mechanisms transfer directly to Hungary" — reason: The assumptions section includes "TEIP and curriculum-standard mechanisms transfer to the Hungarian institutional context [assumption]." No evidence tag is provided. The scenario offers no evidence that Portugal's institutional, budgetary, or teacher-supply context parallels Hungary's. Framing transfer as unproblematic suppresses the context-specificity assumption.

## Process note

All rejected framings either:
1. Upgrade an evidence status (weak → moderate; contested → strong; assumption → fact),
2. Suppress an internal contradiction (e.g., S1's tension between expected benefits and political_risks; S3's tension between assumptions and political_feasibility),
3. Overstate confidence in an unverifiable assumption (e.g., S4's ex-ante unverifiable compensation offsetting),
4. Omit a flagged risk that the scenario itself documents (e.g., S2's offset risk; S3's exit risk; S4's entrenchment risk), or
5. Treat an assumption as resolved when the scenario documents it remains unresolved (e.g., S3's political-ownership window).

Each chosen framing preserves the scenario's own evidence tags, confidence levels, contradictions, and unresolved tensions. All evidence and assumption tags carry through verbatim. No claim is upgraded beyond its source evidence status.
