# rejected_framings.md

## S1 — Admission reform within the current structure

- CHOSEN: Retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium tracks but rewrite entry rules and quotas so that access to selective places is less strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families, while acknowledging that no workable SES indicator has yet been demonstrated at application time.

- REJECTED: "Admission reform can equalize access to selective tracks through assessment design alone" — reason: The scenario's own evidence discipline (weak evidence on less-coachable instruments) and the devil_advocate feedback (contradiction between reduced test-prep benefit and political risks of informal selection) make this framing overstate causal confidence. The chosen framing includes the epistemic hedge ("acknowledging that no workable SES indicator has yet been demonstrated"), which is absent from this rejected version.

- REJECTED: "Modest reweighting of selective seats toward high-ability low-SES applicants represents equity gain" — reason: The equity_checker feedback directly contradicted this as a standalone benefit claim, noting it is contradicted by the unresolved assumption that a workable SES indicator exists. The chosen framing avoids presenting the benefit without qualification and instead surfaces the contradiction in the assumptions field and equity_impact section.

- REJECTED: "Lowest-friction reform because track structure is untouched" — reason: While the chosen framing retains this as a moderate-evidence benefit, the rejected version elevated it to a primary selling point. The devil_advocate and feasibility_checker feedback (optimistic timeline for psychometric validation, unverified assumptions) showed that low friction is not the same as low risk; the chosen framing subordinates structural simplicity to evidence quality.

## S2 — Gradual phase-down of 6/8-year entry places

- CHOSEN: Progressively reduce the number of 6- and 8-year selective entry places in state-maintained schools over a decade, shrinking early selection without abolishing it outright.

- REJECTED: "Capping selective seats in state schools will improve peer composition in urban general-school upper grades" — reason: The evidence_checker and devil_advocate feedback flagged the peer-composition benefit as resting on a contested and weak mechanism. The chosen framing relegates this to the mechanism section with contested evidence tags, rather than leading with it as a promised benefit. The chosen framing emphasises the mechanical retention effect (well-grounded) and leaves the peer-effect claim as a secondary, explicitly contested hypothesis.

- REJECTED: "Phase-down avoids the political shock of abolition and protects middle-class interests through grandfathering" — reason: The political_risk_checker feedback noted that while grandfathering is a legal requirement, it does not reduce middle-class mobilisation risk; affluent parents perceive shrinking seats as a threat regardless of transition smoothness. The chosen framing separates the *technical* benefit of lower administrative shock (moderate evidence) from the false claim that it reduces *political* shock. It then surfaces the mobilisation risk explicitly in the political_risks section.

- REJECTED: "Non-state maintainers will not offset selective caps" — reason: The feasibility_checker and assumption_checker feedback directly contradicted this. The chosen framing flags this as an assumption (not a fact) and identifies it as directly contradicted by the scenario's own political_risks and uncertainties, preserving dissent rather than consensus-laundering.

## S3 — Comprehensive school to age 14 (structural reform)

- CHOSEN: Abolish 6- and 8-year gimnazium entry and postpone formal between-school selection to age 14-15, moving to a comprehensive lower-secondary structure, bundled with teacher-quality and support investments.

- REJECTED: "Postponing selection to age 14-15 will prevent SES-driven track assignment and eliminate sorting" — reason: The equity_checker and coherence_checker feedback identified that the scenario's own equity_impact section states that middle-class exit *relocates* sorting rather than removing it. This rejected framing overstates causal inevitability. The chosen framing includes the bundling requirement and leaves the migration risk as an open, unresolved equity impact rather than a resolved elimination of sorting.

- REJECTED: "Comprehensive structure delivers the largest aggregate equity gain if assumptions hold" — reason: The assumption_checker and devil_advocate feedback showed that the three core assumptions (no migration, political durability, teacher supply) are all either contradicted by other inputs or flagged as unverifiable. The chosen framing retains "largest aggregate equity gain" as a conditional promise (qualified by "if sorting does not relocate") and surfaces the three contradictions as unresolved assumptions, preserving uncertainty rather than false confidence.

- REJECTED: "Political ownership will survive two electoral cycles because the reform is cross-party" — reason: The assumption_checker feedback directly cited the political-feasibility expert's statement that no such durable window exists. The chosen framing flags "political ownership survives long enough" as an assumption and names it as contradicted by the political-feasibility expert, rather than asserting durability.

- REJECTED: "Teacher supply can be stabilised to staff comprehensive upper grades without degrading quality" — reason: The scenario's own strong teacher-shortage constraint directly contradicts this optimistic framing. The chosen framing flags it as an assumption and identifies it as contradicted by the teacher-shortage finding.

## S4 — Keep the structure, compensate general schools (Portuguese-style package)

- CHOSEN: Retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium structure unchanged and instead route rigorous curriculum standards and TEIP-style targeted funding to disadvantaged general schools, attempting to offset the effects of early sorting downstream.

- REJECTED: "Compensation can offset the effects of early sorting without altering selection age" — reason: The evidence_checker, devil_advocate, and assumption_checker feedback all identified this core claim as unverifiable ex ante and unsupported by comparative evidence at Hungary's selection age. The chosen framing uses the epistemic hedge "attempting to offset" and flags the offsetting claim as an unverified assumption, rather than asserting it as a mechanism.

- REJECTED: "Portugal's non-structural TEIP reform proves that compensation works; Hungary can follow the same path" — reason: The evidence_checker feedback noted that the Portuguese evidence is moderate and transfer-untested. The chosen framing cites the Portuguese trend evidence as moderate and explicitly flags transfer to Hungary's early-selection context as untested, preserving the distinction between demonstrated evidence and extrapolation.

- REJECTED: "This reform is politically less disruptive than structural change" — reason: The devil_advocate and political_risk_checker feedback directly contradicted this by noting that ring-fenced budget commitments are the first cuts in fiscal consolidation, and compensation may entrench early selection permanently (a durability and legitimacy risk). The chosen framing separates "structural and legislative disruption" (moderate evidence for low disruption) from "political durability" (contradicted by budget-vulnerability risk), and surfaces the entrenchment risk explicitly.

- REJECTED: "Compensation leaves elite-track supply intact and thus reduces middle-class mobilisation" — reason: While the political-feasibility finding supports this as a structural point, the devil_advocate and political_risk_checker feedback showed it can be reframed as a liability: by legitimising early selection as permanent, the reform may entrench the sorting mechanism rather than dismantle it. The chosen framing retains the moderate-evidence benefit but adds the political-risk framing in the risks section, preserving the tension rather than resolving it in one direction.

## Framing discipline notes

### Consensus laundering prevented:
- **S1**: Retained the unresolved contradiction between reduced test-prep benefit and informal-selection risk rather than dropping the minority view.
- **S2**: Flagged non-state offset as an assumption contradicted by the scenario's own risks and uncertainties, rather than asserting it as fact.
- **S3**: Surfaced three contradicted assumptions (no migration, political durability, teacher supply) as open rather than resolved, preserving dissent from feasibility and political-economy experts.
- **S4**: Explicitly named the "offsetting vs. masking" claim as unverifiable ex ante and contradicted by the scenario's own evidence status, rather than asserting it as mechanism.

### Evidence tag preservation:
- Every rejected framing was rejected because it either upgraded evidence status (e.g., moderate → strong), dropped evidence tags (e.g., contested → certain), or asserted claims not present in the inputs.
- Chosen framings retain all evidence tags, confidence levels, and uncertainty language from the input scenarios.

### Uncertainty survival:
- No rejected framing was eliminated because it created uncertainty; rather, rejected framings were those that *removed* or *masked* the uncertainties present in the inputs.
- All chosen framings carry forward the low and medium confidence levels and the "would be reduced by" conditions from the inputs.
