# Policy scenarios

## S1 — Admission reform within the current structure
**Goal**
Retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium tracks but rewrite entry rules and quotas so that access to selective places is less strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families — noting that this goal is in tension with the scenario's own uncertainty about whether a workable SES indicator can be built at application time.

**Mechanism**
- Replacing the central written admission exam with a less preparation-sensitive assessment is intended to weaken the advantage that paid test-prep confers on high-SES applicants [evidence: weak — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013 and Horn 2013 show the selective-track advantage is mostly a selection effect, but do not demonstrate that a redesigned assessment resists coaching].
- Reserving a share of selective seats for low-SES or under-applying settlements mechanically raises their representation in selective tracks [evidence: moderate — mechanical quota effect, contingent on a usable SES indicator].
- Documented outreach to low-SES and Roma families in under-applying settlements can raise application rates [evidence: weak — asserted by political_feasibility expert; conditional on barriers being informational rather than structural].

**Evidence status**
weak-to-moderate — the quota mechanism is mechanically sound but depends on an SES indicator that does not yet exist; the coaching-resistance and outreach mechanisms are weak.

**Assumptions**
- A workable SES indicator can be built at application time [assumption — contradicted by the scenario's own uncertainties and by feasibility_checker: Hungary has no administrative student-level SES register usable at application, and any proxy would be gameable].
- Selection rate (8-12%) reflects capacity constraint rather than pure policy choice [assumption].
- Low-SES underapplication is remediable by outreach rather than deep structural barriers (transport, information asymmetry, prior family exposure) [assumption — weak, per political_feasibility].

**Expected benefits**
- Higher representation of low-SES and rural pupils among selective-track entrants where quota seats are reserved [evidence: moderate — mechanical, conditional on a usable SES indicator].
- Reduced test-preparation arms-race pressure on age-10 children insofar as the new assessment resists coaching [evidence: weak — model knowledge; note contradiction with political_risks, where reformed/scarcer channels may invite informal selection that reproduces SES sorting].
- Least legal and procedural disruption of the four scenarios, since the Nkt. structure is unchanged [evidence: moderate — Nkt. analysis].

**Equity impact**
Gains, if any, accrue to high-ability low-SES pupils who already apply and are reweighted into selective seats [evidence: moderate — mechanical], but this is contradicted by the assumption that a workable SES indicator exists at application, and the bulk of low-SES pupils who never apply are untouched. Informal selection (interviews, reputation) may reproduce SES sorting in reformed channels.

**Cost categories**
- Assessment redesign, psychometric validation and piloting — high, because it requires multi-year psychometric development with no demonstrated coaching-resistant instrument [evidence: weak — expert estimates].
- SES-indicator construction and audit — cost not estimable; no administrative register exists [evidence: weak].
- Outreach and information campaigns in under-applying settlements — medium, recurring [evidence: weak].

**Implementation steps**
- Oktatási Hivatal — design and pilot a less-coachable assessment with psychometric validation (timeline: year 1-3); note this timeline is in tension with the scenario's own assumption that no coaching-resistant instrument has been demonstrated.
- Ministry / KSH — attempt to construct and audit a usable application-time SES indicator (timeline: year 1-2).
- Maintainers (state, church, foundation) — apply reserved-seat quotas across selective intake (timeline: year 2-4).
- Esélyegyenlőségi civil society — run and measure outreach campaigns by application rate (timeline: year 1 onward).

**Political risks**
- Reformed or scarcer selection channels may invite informal selection (interviews, reputation) that reproduces SES sorting [evidence: weak — asserted here, not independently evidenced].
- Middle-class parents may perceive quota reform as dilution of a mobility asset and mobilize [evidence: moderate — domestic press and stakeholder analyses].
- Pedagogus unions treat any assessment redesign as performance-audit creep absent explicit decoupling [evidence: strong — round 06 episodic memory].

**Uncertainties**
- Can a non-gameable SES proxy be built at application time? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: SES-proxy piloting showing non-gameability, per political_feasibility].
- Does low-SES underapplication stem from information, transport, family confidence, or school-level gatekeeping? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: qualitative research in 5-10 under-applying settlements].
- Can a genuinely coaching-resistant assessment be designed and validated? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: psychometric development and pilot validation of the new instrument].

## S2 — Gradual phase-down of 6/8-year entry places
**Goal**
Reduce the number of age-10 and age-12 selective entry places over successive cohorts, shifting more pupils into the common track without abrupt structural closure.

**Mechanism**
- Capping and shrinking selective seats over time reduces the share of each cohort sorted early [evidence: moderate — mechanical, given enforceable caps on state-maintained schools].
- Fewer selective seats keep more high-achievers in the common track, which can improve peer composition in urban general-school upper grades [evidence: contested — the asymmetric peer-effect mechanism is tagged weak/contested in equity_and_social_mobility].
- Phased caps respect Nkt. transitional guarantees and multi-year notice periods [evidence: strong — Nkt. analysis].

**Evidence status**
contested — the seat-reduction step is mechanical, but the peer-composition benefit rests on a weak/contested mechanism and can be offset by non-state maintainers.

**Assumptions**
- Caps on state-maintained schools are not simply offset by non-state (church/foundation) maintainers expanding selective intake [assumption — directly contradicted by the scenario's own political_risks and uncertainties, and by school_network_planning: independent maintainers can absorb selective tracks and bypass consolidation].
- Grandfathering enrolled cohorts is politically and legally required [assumption].
- Demographic cohort shrinkage continues, making parallel selective programs increasingly inefficient [assumption].

**Expected benefits**
- Gradual reduction of early sorting without abrupt closure, lowering legal and political shock [evidence: moderate — Nkt. analysis].
- Stronger peer composition in urban general-school upper grades as high-achievers are retained [evidence: contested — asymmetric peer-effect link is weak/contested per equity_and_social_mobility].
- Alignment with demographic contraction, reducing per-pupil inefficiency of parallel selective programs [evidence: moderate — KSH demographic yearbooks; OECD school network studies].

**Equity impact**
As caps tighten, residual selective places become even more SES-skewed absent an S1-type admission-rule change — this residual-skew effect is mechanical and near-certain (fewer seats, same applicant pool) [evidence: moderate], and directly cuts against the equity goal unless paired with admission reform. Non-state maintainers absorbing displaced selective demand can relocate rather than remove sorting.

**Cost categories**
- Parallel-regime administration while grandfathered cohorts run under old rules alongside capped intake — medium and sustained across the decade, not merely transitional [evidence: weak — expert estimates].
- Capacity expansion in general-school upper grades to absorb retained high-achievers — medium [evidence: weak].
- Monitoring and enforcement of caps across maintainer types — medium, recurring [evidence: weak].

**Implementation steps**
- Parliament — amend Nkt. to authorise phased seat caps with transitional guarantees (timeline: year 1-2).
- Ministry / tankerület — set and enforce declining seat quotas on state-maintained selective intake (timeline: year 2 onward, declining across the decade).
- Ministry — negotiate parallel caps with church and foundation maintainers to prevent offset (timeline: year 1-3).
- General schools — expand upper-grade capacity for retained high-achievers (timeline: year 3-6).

**Political risks**
- Middle-class parents perceive shrinking seats as a threat to a mobility/insurance asset and mobilize [evidence: moderate — domestic press and stakeholder analyses].
- Non-state maintainers expand selective intake to absorb displaced demand, defeating the cap [evidence: moderate — school_network_planning; maintainer statistics].
- Prior curtailment attempts (1990s-2010s) triggered sustained organised opposition [evidence: moderate — domestic press and stakeholder analyses].

**Uncertainties**
- Will non-state maintainers offset state caps by expanding selective intake? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: registry data on selective seats by maintainer and settlement type].
- Does retaining high-achievers in the common track actually improve outcomes for non-selected pupils? [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: longitudinal peer-effect studies in Hungarian primary schools].
- Is the parallel-regime transition cost genuinely transitional or sustained across the decade? [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: Ministry of Finance transition budget forecasts].

## S3 — Comprehensive school to age 14 (structural reform)
**Goal**
Abolish age-10 and age-12 selective entry and defer academic tracking to age 14, moving toward comprehensive schooling with within-school differentiation.

**Mechanism**
- Deferring selection to age 14 removes the early partition that locks in SES differences before academic stabilisation [evidence: moderate — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006; corroborated by Pekkarinen et al. 2009 and Jakubowski et al. 2016].
- Delayed tracking widens access to high-achieving peers for pupils who would otherwise be sorted out early [evidence: contested — the asymmetric peer-effect mechanism is tagged weak/contested in equity_and_social_mobility].
- A weaker SES-outcome gradient can emerge within a decade via broader access to high-achieving peers [evidence: contested — the mechanism is weak/contested even though the cross-country delayed-selection association is moderate].

**Evidence status**
moderate on the delayed-selection/equity association, contested on the peer-effect channel — international evidence is consistent but the specific Hungarian peer-effect mechanism is weak/contested.

**Assumptions**
- Sorting does not simply migrate to school choice between general schools and to non-state maintainers [assumption — this is asserted here yet the equity_impact field calls exactly this migration the largest risk; the tension is unresolved].
- Comprehensive schools can be equipped with the teacher quality and diagnostic support that made the Finnish transition work [assumption — Hungary currently lacks this, per finnish_reform].
- Political ownership survives two electoral cycles [assumption — contradicted by political_feasibility, which states no such durable window is demonstrated; Poland reversed a comparable reform].

**Expected benefits**
- Largest expected reduction in early SES sorting of the four scenarios [evidence: moderate — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006; Pekkarinen et al. 2009; Jakubowski et al. 2016].
- No demonstrated loss in mean achievement from delayed selection [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006].
- Weaker SES-outcome gradient within a decade [evidence: contested — the peer-effect channel carrying this benefit is weak/contested].

**Equity impact**
Largest expected aggregate equity gain of the four scenarios, but also the largest risk: middle-class and church/foundation exit to non-state or private schools can relocate sorting rather than remove it. That exit does not harm the exiting pupils but can re-concentrate disadvantage in general schools, partially offsetting the equity gain [evidence: moderate on the gain, moderate on the exit risk — Pekkarinen et al. 2009; finnish_reform; school_network_planning].

**Cost categories**
- Nationwide teacher retraining in differentiated instruction — high; no per-cohort/per-district unit cost available, and teacher supply is a binding constraint [evidence: weak — expert estimates; strong on the supply constraint per implementation_planning].
- Facility and curriculum redesign for comprehensive upper grades — high, front-loaded transition cost [evidence: weak — education_finance].
- Diagnostic and remedial support systems (Finnish-style) — high, recurring [evidence: moderate — Pekkarinen et al. 2009].

**Implementation steps**
- Parliament — legislate abolition of 6/8-year entry with multi-year notice and grandfathering (timeline: year 1-2).
- Ministry / Klebelsberg Centre — plan and fund teacher retraining in differentiated instruction, contingent on teacher-supply stabilisation (timeline: year 1-5).
- Ministry — build universal diagnostic and remedial support in general schools before rollout (timeline: year 2-5).
- Ministry — negotiate binding intake rules with church/foundation maintainers to limit sorting migration (timeline: year 1-3).

**Political risks**
- Middle-class and church/foundation flight relocates sorting rather than removing it [evidence: moderate — school_network_planning; finnish_reform].
- Reversal after an electoral cycle, as in Poland 2016-2019, despite measured gains [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD].
- Teacher shortage makes redistribution across comprehensive schools infeasible in the short term, degrading academic quality [evidence: strong — implementation_planning; OECD].

**Uncertainties**
- How much current urban sorting would simply migrate to school choice and non-state maintainers? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: quasi-experimental evidence on residential-choice elasticity to track structure].
- What is the counterfactual effect size of moving selection to age 14 in Hungary? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: synthetic-control comparison with Poland/Czech Republic].
- Will political ownership survive two electoral cycles? [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: stakeholder interviews with Polish reformers on reversal switching costs].

## S4 — Keep the structure, compensate general schools (Portuguese-style package)
**Goal**
Retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium structure but neutralise its equity effects downstream via rigorous curriculum standards, school clustering, and TEIP-style targeted funding for disadvantaged schools.

**Mechanism**
- Targeted funding and standards raise attainment in disadvantaged general schools without structural change [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020 on Portugal].
- Compensation is intended to offset, not merely mask, the effects of early sorting [evidence: weak — no country has demonstrated offsetting at Hungary's selection age; the Portuguese case did not alter selection age but also did not select as early as Hungary].
- Reduced grade retention and school clustering improve system-wide outcomes [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020].

**Evidence status**
moderate on the Portuguese non-structural package, weak on offsetting at Hungary's early selection age — no comparator has demonstrated compensation offsetting selection as early as age 10.

**Assumptions**
- Compensation can offset, not just mask, the effects of early sorting [assumption — unverifiable ex ante; no country evidence exists at Hungary's selection age, per assumption_checker].
- Non-structural interventions are politically easier to sustain than structural change [assumption].
- TEIP and curriculum-standard mechanisms transfer to the Hungarian institutional context [assumption].

**Expected benefits**
- Attainment gains in disadvantaged general schools without structural disruption [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020].
- Avoids the legal and transitional friction of structural reform [evidence: moderate — Nkt. analysis; education_finance].
- Compatible with maintainer plurality since no closure is mandated [evidence: moderate — maintainer statistics].

**Equity impact**
Narrows the SES gradient at best partially, since it leaves the sorting mechanism itself intact and only compensates downstream [evidence: weak on offsetting]. Because the early partition persists, the root cause — selection at age 10-12 concentrating low-SES pupils in general schools — is not addressed, only mitigated.

**Cost categories**
- TEIP-style targeted funding for disadvantaged schools — high; no numeric range available, and this line is typically the first cut in fiscal consolidation, so exposure is serious [evidence: weak — expert estimates].
- Curriculum-standards development and rollout — medium [evidence: weak].
- School-clustering administration — medium, recurring [evidence: weak].

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry — design and legislate rigorous national curriculum standards as guidance, not audit triggers (timeline: year 1-2).
- Ministry / Klebelsberg Centre — route TEIP-style targeted funding to disadvantaged general schools (timeline: year 1 onward, recurring).
- Tankerület — implement school clustering and administrative consolidation (timeline: year 2-4).
- Ministry — establish comparison-group evaluation of high-achiever retention in funded general schools (timeline: year 2-5).

**Political risks**
- Budget vulnerability: targeted funding is the first line cut in fiscal consolidation, undermining durability [evidence: weak — expert estimates; contested on magnitude].
- Legitimising early selection permanently: compensation may entrench rather than dismantle the sorting engine [evidence: weak — asserted here, not independently evidenced].
- Teacher shortage undermines curriculum-standard and support delivery in disadvantaged schools [evidence: strong — implementation_planning; OECD].

**Uncertainties**
- Can compensation genuinely offset, not merely mask, early sorting at Hungary's selection age? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: comparison-group evaluation of funded general schools with high-achiever retention tracking].
- Will Hungarian budgetary commitment to targeted funding survive without reversal? [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: analysis of budgetary and legislative continuity in education policy].
- Do Hungarian teacher shortages permit Portuguese-style standards and support delivery? [confidence: high — would be reduced by: comparative studies on teacher supply in Hungarian disadvantaged schools versus historical Portuguese levels].
