## Overview

Ten expert analyses converge on a single empirical anchor — Hungary's 6- and 8-year gimnazium tracks select ~8–12% of a cohort at ages 10/12, are strongly skewed toward high-SES urban families, and Hungary's SES-outcome gradient (~19–25% PISA variance) is an OECD outlier — but diverge sharply on what follows from this: whether abolition is the right lever, what it must be bundled with, and whether it is administratively, legally, or politically survivable. Demographic contraction (births down from ~125,000/yr to ~85,000–95,000) is treated by some as an independent forcing function for network consolidation, while finance and implementation experts treat structural reform as costly friction rather than savings. Political and legal analyses converge on entrenchment (maintainer plurality, urban middle-class mobilization, Nkt. legislative anchoring) as the dominant constraint on any reform pathway, structural or not.

## Disagreement map

- **equity_and_social_mobility, international_comparison, finnish_reform, polish_reform, demography, school_network_planning** (majority position, though with differing bundling conditions): Hungary should phase out or curtail the 6-/8-year gimnazium tracks to delay selection (typically to age 14–15/16). Why: cross-national evidence (Hanushek & Woessmann 2006; Polish and Finnish reform trajectories) links earlier tracking to higher inequality without mean-performance gains, and Hungary-specific value-added studies (Horn 2013; Kertesi & Kezdi) suggest the tracks' advantage is mostly selection effect, not causal gain.

- **portuguese_reform** (minority): Hungary should retain the 6-/8-year gimnazium system and instead deploy non-structural interventions (TEIP-style targeted funding, curriculum standards, reduced grade retention) to neutralize inequities. Why: Portugal raised PISA outcomes substantially (2006–2015) without altering selection age, suggesting structural reorganization may be a "distraction" from more tractable classroom- and resource-level levers.

- **education_finance, implementation_planning** (minority): Hungary should retain the current system, at least in the medium term. Why: per-pupil operating costs are flat across tracks, so structural consolidation yields no steady-state savings, only front-loaded transition costs (retraining, facility reallocation); implementation_planning adds that acute teacher shortages (especially STEM) make simultaneous restructuring and staff redistribution "functionally impossible" without degrading delivery in disadvantaged regions.

- **legal_and_governance** (distinct minority procedural position): Hungary should not attempt legislated closure at all, but first commission an independent equity audit and use it to co-design a graduated reform pathway. Why: no recent Hungarian outcome study empirically grounds the equity claims driving reform, and legislative/maintainer entrenchment (Nkt., church/foundation agreements) makes swift uniform closure legally and procedurally unrealistic regardless of the substantive merits.

- **school_network_planning vs. hungarian_education_system / devil_advocate (round 2–3 unresolved)**: school_network_planning assumes "church and private maintainers will continue to prioritize selective academic tracks to attract students" and that state-mandated closure would let these maintainers "absorb selective tracks and bypass national consolidation plans" — implying non-state actors will *keep* selecting. hungarian_education_system's disagreement note (carried from round 2–3 episodic memory) directly contradicts the premise that non-state maintainers can be *restricted* from absorbing phased-out selective capacity, since governance fragmentation is exactly what constrains uniform structural intervention. This tension is unresolved: one camp assumes non-state actors are a controllable variable, the other treats them as a structural escape valve no policy instrument currently closes.

- **hungarian_education_system vs. international_comparison/equity_and_social_mobility/polish_reform** on causal certainty: hungarian_education_system holds that "whether tracks amplify or merely reflect underlying inequality cannot be determined from available evidence" and rates the causal-direction question as low confidence (minority, cautious position), while the equity- and comparison-oriented experts treat the amplification story as sufficiently supported by cross-national DiD patterns and Hungarian value-added studies to justify a policy position, despite acknowledging (in their own uncertainty sections) that no Hungary-specific causal (DiD/RDD) estimate exists.

- **finnish_reform vs. polish_reform** on bundling necessity: finnish_reform holds abolition should proceed *only if* bundled with systemic teacher-training and diagnostic-support investment, treating this as a hard precondition. polish_reform treats the primary precondition as *political* (multi-party pacts, compensatory funding) rather than pedagogical-infrastructural, reflecting a different diagnosis of what caused Poland's reversal (political backlash, not implementation failure).

- **political_feasibility vs. hungarian_education_system/school_network_planning stakeholder assumptions (unresolved from round 3 episodic memory)**: political_feasibility's stakeholder analysis assumes urban middle-class opposition can be defused via credible enrichment-within-general-schools substitution, whereas the earlier unresolved criticism (political_risk_checker, round 3) noted that scenario-level risk framing (elite-school resistance, middle-class flight) had ignored contradictory assumptions about maintainer compliance — this tension about whether middle-class/maintainer resistance is a manageable political variable or a structural constant persists across the record and is not resolved here.

## What the experts agree on

- The 6-/8-year gimnazium tracks select ~8–12% of a cohort at ages 10 or 12, via centralized examination, with strong skew toward high-SES, urban families [evidence: strong, multiply corroborated].
- Hungary's PISA SES-outcome gradient is an OECD outlier on the high-inequality end [evidence: strong — OECD PISA country notes, cited independently by international_comparison and equity_and_social_mobility].
- Hungary's system is fragmented across state (tankerulet), church, and foundation maintainers, and this fragmentation constrains the feasibility of any uniform top-down structural mandate [evidence: strong, corroborated by hungarian_education_system, school_network_planning, legal_and_governance].
- Cross-nationally, earlier tracking is associated with higher inequality without a demonstrated aggregate-performance cost — the tradeoff is distributional, not about mean achievement [evidence: strong/moderate — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006, corroborated directionally by Finnish and Polish cases].
- No Hungary-specific causal (quasi-experimental) estimate of the selection effect on inequality currently exists; all causal claims about Hungary rely on cross-national generalization or value-added studies with acknowledged limits [evidence: absence, flagged independently by hungarian_education_system, international_comparison, legal_and_governance].
- Any structural reform faces material transition costs and multi-year implementation timelines, regardless of the reform's ultimate merit — flagged by finance, implementation, legal, and political experts independently.
- Demographic cohort shrinkage (births down from ~125,000/yr to ~85,000–95,000) is a real, independent pressure on school-network sustainability, operating on a timescale (2020s–2030s) that will force some consolidation decisions irrespective of selection policy [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks].

## Minority positions

- **portuguese_reform**: Retain the 6-/8-year gimnazium structure entirely; pursue equity via TEIP-style targeted funding and curriculum standardization instead. Rationale: Portugal achieved substantial PISA gains (2006–2015) through non-structural reform, suggesting the tracking-age debate may misallocate reform attention away from more tractable, less politically disruptive levers. Held by a single expert against a majority favoring some form of track curtailment.

- **education_finance**: Retain the current system because structural consolidation produces no steady-state fiscal savings — per-pupil costs are flat across tracks, and the entire fiscal impact is front-loaded transition cost (retraining, facility repurposing). Rationale: national teacher wage grids and centralized (KK) funding mean local restructuring cannot generate the savings often assumed to justify reform.

- **implementation_planning**: Retain the system in the medium term specifically because of acute, systemic teacher shortages (math/science, marginalized regions); simultaneous restructuring and staff redistribution would degrade delivery in disadvantaged areas before any equity gain materializes. Rationale: administrative capacity is treated as a binding constraint independent of the reform's substantive merit.

- **legal_and_governance**: Oppose legislated closure as a first move; require an independent equity audit before any structural reform is designed. Rationale: the absence of a recent Hungary-specific empirical study on the tracks' actual effect on mobility/equity means reform advocates (including the majority above) are extrapolating from cross-national evidence that this expert holds insufficiently grounded for Hungary specifically; also treats constitutional equality-of-opportunity exposure as a two-sided risk (both selection and its abrupt removal could trigger scrutiny given transitional guarantee obligations).

- **hungarian_education_system** (partial minority, on causal certainty specifically, not on policy conclusion): Even while other experts converge on phase-out, this expert maintains that the causal direction of the SES/track relationship — amplification vs. reflection of pre-existing inequality — "cannot be determined from available evidence," a lower-confidence stance than the causal language used in equity_and_social_mobility, international_comparison, and polish_reform's positions.

- **school_network_planning** (minority premise, flagged as internally contested): Assumes non-state (church/foundation) maintainers will keep prioritizing selective tracks and can act as an escape valve that undermines state consolidation plans — a premise in direct tension with hungarian_education_system's finding that maintainer governance fragmentation constrains rather than enables predictable behavior, meaning it is unclear whether non-state maintainers are a controllable policy lever or a structural constant. This tension is carried forward unresolved from round 2–3 episodic memory and remains unaddressed by any input in this round.
