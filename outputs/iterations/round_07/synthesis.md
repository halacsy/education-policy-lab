## Overview

The expert record addresses whether Hungary should phase out early academic selection via the 6- and 8-year gimnázium tracks (entry at ages 10–12). Experts converge on the empirical diagnosis — early selection sorts by socio-economic status (SES) rather than latent ability, and Hungary's SES–achievement gradient is among the steepest in the OECD — but diverge sharply on whether to reform the structure, and how. Positions cluster into three camps: (a) phase out early tracking (equity_and_social_mobility, international_comparison, demography, school_network_planning, and — conditionally — finnish_reform, polish_reform, political_feasibility); (b) retain the structure and pursue non-structural or timing-deferred alternatives (portuguese_reform, education_finance, implementation_planning); (c) condition any reform on prior empirical audit and legal-procedural design (legal_and_governance). Cross-cutting constraints — teacher shortage, maintainer fragmentation, political durability, transition cost, demographic contraction — are invoked by different experts to support opposite conclusions.

## Disagreement map

- **equity_and_social_mobility, international_comparison, demography, school_network_planning**: Hungary should phase out the 6/8-year tracks to delay sorting to age 14–15. Why: early selection amplifies SES segregation without raising mean achievement [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006; OECD PISA], produces only modest value-added for selected pupils [evidence: moderate — Horn 2013], and concentrates demographic vulnerability on rural primary networks [evidence: strong — KSH].
- **portuguese_reform, education_finance, implementation_planning (minority)**: Hungary should retain the 6/8-year structure and instead pursue non-structural reforms (TEIP-style targeted funding, curriculum standards) or defer structural change. Why: Portugal raised PISA outcomes without changing selection age [evidence: moderate — Crato 2020]; structural consolidation yields no steady-state savings and is dominated by front-loaded transition costs [evidence: weak — budget chapters; strong — centralized KK funding]; and the teacher shortage makes redistribution across comprehensive schools functionally impossible in the medium term [evidence: strong — OECD; PSZ/PDSZ].
- **finnish_reform (conditional)**: Phase out early selection, but only if bundled with standardized teacher training and universal diagnostic/remedial support. Why: Finnish equity gains cannot be isolated from these accompanying reforms [evidence: moderate — Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009]; unbundled abolition risks within-classroom variance and middle-class flight.
- **polish_reform, political_feasibility (conditional)**: Phase out early tracking, but anchor it in multi-party pacts / co-authored implementation roadmaps and compensatory funding. Why: Poland's delayed-tracking reform raised outcomes yet was politically reversed in 2016–2019 [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD]; evidence of success did not prevent reversal, so durability depends on sustained stakeholder buy-in, not retrospective results.
- **legal_and_governance (minority)**: Do not attempt legislated closure first; commission an independent equity audit and co-design a graduated pathway respecting legal timelines and maintainer plurality. Why: no recent Hungary-specific impact study exists [evidence: weak — policy documents], and legislative entrenchment plus maintainer diversity impose high procedural cost and multi-year windows [evidence: strong — Nkt. analysis].
- **hungarian_education_system (minority scope caveat)**: Even full track abolition would only address one layer of segmentation. Why: free school choice and catchment-based sorting produce strong between-school segregation independent of formal tracks [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kézdi 2013], so abolition alone is insufficient and would apply unevenly across maintainers.
- **school_network_planning vs. hungarian_education_system (on maintainer response)**: school_network_planning assumes non-state maintainers can be restricted / will be absorbed into consolidation; hungarian_education_system holds that church and foundation schools can absorb phased-out selective capacity and bypass national plans. Why: unresolved — maintainer autonomy under the post-2013 fragmented landscape means a state mandate need not bind non-state operators [evidence: strong — maintainer statistics]. This directly bears on the persisting critique that any "restriction" assumption is contradicted by the maintainer-fragmentation finding.

## What the experts agree on

- Entry to the 6/8-year tracks is skewed toward high-SES, urban families [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal].
- Hungary's SES–achievement gradient (19–25% of PISA variance) is well above the OECD average of 12–14% [evidence: strong — OECD PISA].
- Since 2013 the maintainer landscape is fragmented between state (tankerület/KK) and a growing church/foundation share, complicating any uniform mandate [evidence: strong — public education act amendments; maintainer statistics].
- Demographic cohort contraction (births down from ~125,000 to ~85,000–95,000) forces school-network consolidation regardless of selection policy [evidence: strong — KSH].
- Structural-reform costs are front-loaded transition costs (retraining, facilities, administration), not steady-state operational savings [evidence: weak — budget chapters].
- The value-added of selective tracks for admitted pupils is modest; the raw advantage is largely a selection effect [evidence: moderate — Horn 2013; Kertesi & Kézdi].
- Where experts endorse structural reform, none claims abolition alone is sufficient: complementary measures (choice/catchment reform, teacher quality, support systems, political insulation) are treated as necessary.

## Minority positions

- **portuguese_reform, education_finance, implementation_planning**: Retain the 6/8-year structure. Non-structural reforms can address equity (Portugal precedent) [evidence: moderate — Crato 2020]; transition costs outweigh flat steady-state savings [evidence: weak — budget chapters; strong — centralized funding]; and the teacher shortage makes structural redistribution unviable in the medium term [evidence: strong — OECD; PSZ/PDSZ]. Not resolved: the majority does not rebut the teacher-supply constraint, treating it instead as a design condition.
- **legal_and_governance**: Reject legislated closure as a first move; require an independent equity audit and graduated, maintainer-respecting pathway first. Why: no recent Hungary-specific equity impact study exists [evidence: weak], and procedural/legal entrenchment imposes multi-year windows [evidence: strong — Nkt.].
- **hungarian_education_system**: Track abolition addresses only one layer of segmentation; choice- and catchment-based sorting would persist [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kézdi 2013]. This caveat qualifies every phase-out position without endorsing retention.
- **finnish_reform (conditional dissent from unbundled reform)**: Phase-out without simultaneous teacher-training and diagnostic-support investment is rejected as counterproductive [evidence: moderate — Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009].
- **Unresolved contradiction (school_network_planning ⟷ hungarian_education_system)**: whether non-state maintainers can be restricted from absorbing phased-out selective capacity. hungarian_education_system's evidence indicates they cannot be assumed to comply; school_network_planning's consolidation logic and political_feasibility's roadmap both rely on containment that this finding contradicts. Carried forward unresolved.
