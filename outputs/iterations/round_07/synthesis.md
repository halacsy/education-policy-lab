## Overview

Thirteen expert analyses converge on a shared factual base — Hungary selects roughly 8–12% of each cohort into 6- and 8-year gimnázium tracks at ages 10–12 [evidence: strong], entrants skew strongly high-SES and urban [evidence: strong], and Hungary's SES-linked score variance (19–25%) is well above the OECD average of 12–14% [evidence: strong]. From this common base the experts diverge sharply on what to do: whether to phase out early selection at all, and if so, under what conditions, at what pace, and with what accompanying measures. The disagreement is not about whether the system stratifies, but about causal weight, feasibility, political durability, and whether structural change is the right lever. Demographic contraction (births down from ~125,000 to ~85,000–95,000 [evidence: strong]) enters as a cross-cutting pressure that several experts treat as decisive independent of selection policy.

## Disagreement map

- **international_comparison, equity_and_social_mobility, hungarian_education_system, demography, school_network_planning**: Hungary should phase out early selection (delay to age 14–16 / comprehensive schooling). Why: early tracking amplifies existing SES stratification without raising mean performance [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006], value-added for selected pupils is modest [evidence: moderate — Horn 2013], and demographic shrinkage makes parallel selective tracks unsustainable while hollowing rural primary networks [evidence: strong — KSH].

- **portuguese_reform, education_finance, implementation_planning**: Hungary should retain the 6/8-year system and pursue non-structural or delayed alternatives. Why: Portugal improved PISA outcomes without structural change via TEIP targeting and curriculum standards [evidence: moderate — Crato 2020]; structural consolidation yields no steady-state savings and is dominated by transition costs [evidence: weak/strong — education_finance]; and the teacher shortage [evidence: strong — OECD/PSZ/PDSZ] makes dismantling functionally impossible without degrading delivery in disadvantaged regions.

- **hungarian_education_system (minority)**: Track abolition must be coupled with binding intake rules (randomized/balanced admission) AND maintainer-governance consolidation. Why: abolition alone will not reduce inequality because free choice and residential catchment already fragment urban schools [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013]; without eliminating denominational/foundation choice-compatible selectivity, sorting persists.

- **finnish_reform (minority)**: Phase-out is endorsed only if bundled with standardized teacher training and universal diagnostic support. Why: Finnish equity gains cannot be isolated from teacher-quality and early special-education bundling [evidence: moderate — Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009]; unbundled abolition risks within-classroom variance and middle-class flight.

- **polish_reform, political_feasibility (minority on durability)**: Even a well-designed phase-out will be reversed unless anchored in multi-party pacts / irreversible decoupling and compensatory funding. Why: Poland's 1999 reform produced measurable PISA gains yet was fully reversed in 2016–2019 under political pressure [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD]; organized urban middle-class opposition has blocked prior Hungarian attempts [evidence: moderate].

- **legal_and_governance (minority)**: No legislated closure should be attempted before an independent equity audit; reform should be a graduated, audit-justified pathway. Why: no recent Hungary-specific impact study exists [evidence: weak], and early selection already carries constitutional equality-of-opportunity exposure (Art. 66/a) and EU equal-treatment risk [evidence: moderate]; legislative entrenchment plus maintainer plurality impose multi-year windows.

- **school_network_planning vs. hungarian_education_system (contradiction on maintainer response)**: school_network_planning holds that non-state (church/foundation) maintainers *can and will absorb* phased-out selective capacity and bypass consolidation [assumption]; hungarian_education_system's position depends on maintainer governance being *consolidatable* to prevent exactly this. Why: unresolved — both cite the same strong maintainer-fragmentation finding but draw opposite feasibility conclusions. This contradiction remains open from rounds 02–03 and is not resolved here.

## What the experts agree on

- Hungary selects ~8–12% of each cohort at ages 10–12 via centralized examination, with entrants strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families. [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal]
- Hungary's SES-linked variance in PISA scores (19–25%) substantially exceeds the OECD average (12–14%). [evidence: strong — OECD PISA]
- Age 10–12 selection is early by European standards; comparators defer tracking to 14–16 or use within-school differentiation. [evidence: moderate — OECD Education at a Glance]
- Maintainer plurality (state tankerület, church, foundation) fragments any uniform structural mandate. [evidence: strong — Nkt. amendments; maintainer statistics]
- Structural reform costs are front-loaded transition costs (retraining, facilities, administration), not steady-state operational costs. [evidence: weak/strong — education_finance; implementation_planning]
- Teacher shortage — especially maths/science and in marginalized regions — is a binding constraint on any restructuring. [evidence: strong — OECD; PSZ/PDSZ]
- Demographic cohort contraction forces school-network consolidation in the 2020s–2030s regardless of selection policy. [evidence: strong — KSH]
- Structural change alone is insufficient: choice, residential sorting, teacher quality, and support systems must be addressed for equity gains to materialize. [evidence: moderate — multiple]

## Minority positions

- **hungarian_education_system (minority)**: Abolition without binding intake rules (randomized/balanced admission) and maintainer-governance consolidation will fail to reduce inequality. Rationale: free choice and residential catchment already produce between-school stratification at primary stage; denominational/foundation schools function as choice-compatible selectivity [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013]. Not resolved away — this position also carries an unresolved internal tension flagged since round 02: its assumption that non-state maintainers can be restricted is contradicted by school_network_planning's finding that they can absorb phased-out capacity.

- **finnish_reform (minority)**: Phase-out endorsed only conditional on bundled teacher-training and universal diagnostic support investment. Rationale: Finnish equity outcomes are inseparable from teacher-quality and remedial-support bundling [evidence: moderate — Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009]; unbundled abolition risks increased classroom variance and middle-class flight.

- **polish_reform + political_feasibility (minority on durability)**: A phase-out is not durable absent multi-party entrenchment / irreversible decoupling of standards-assessment-funding from teacher accountability and compensatory local funding. Rationale: Poland's evidence-backed reform was reversed in 2016–2019 despite PISA gains [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD]; Hungarian middle-class opposition is organized and has blocked prior attempts [evidence: moderate]. Unresolved from round 01: the pro-phase-out scenarios still lack explicit entrenchment/cross-party consensus design to counter this named reversal risk.

- **legal_and_governance (minority)**: Reject legislated closure without a prior independent equity audit; pursue a graduated, audit-justified pathway respecting legal timelines. Rationale: no recent Hungary-specific impact study exists [evidence: weak]; constitutional (Art. 66/a) and EU equal-treatment exposure plus legislative entrenchment demand empirical justification and multi-year windows.

- **portuguese_reform + education_finance + implementation_planning (minority against structural change)**: Retain the 6/8-year system; achieve equity via non-structural means (TEIP-style targeted funding, curriculum standards) or delay until teacher supply stabilizes. Rationale: Portugal improved outcomes without structural reorganization [evidence: moderate — Crato 2020]; transition costs dominate with no steady-state savings [evidence: weak/strong]; teacher shortage makes dismantling infeasible without degrading disadvantaged-region delivery [evidence: strong].
