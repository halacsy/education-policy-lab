## Overview

The expert record examines whether Hungary should phase out, retain, or reform its 6- and 8-year gimnazium tracks, which select roughly 8–12% of a cohort at ages 10–12 via central written examination. Experts converge on the diagnostic evidence (early selection skews toward high-SES/urban families; Hungary's SES–achievement gradient is among the OECD's steepest) but diverge sharply on the prescription. Positions range from full phase-out (equity, international-comparison, demography, school-network, Finnish- and Polish-reform experts, with conditions) to retention (education-finance, implementation-planning, Portuguese-reform experts) to a middle path of audit-first graduated reform (legal/governance). Political-feasibility warns that durability, not desirability, is the binding constraint. All positions carry substantial live uncertainties; the demographic driver and maintainer fragmentation cut across the entire debate.

## Disagreement map

- **equity_and_social_mobility, international_comparison, demography, school_network_planning**: Hungary should phase out the 6/8-year tracks to delay sorting (to age 14/15), reduce SES segregation, and stop demographic contraction from hollowing rural primary networks. Why: early selection amplifies inequality without raising mean achievement, and its value-added for admitted pupils is mostly a selection effect [evidence: moderate — Horn 2013; Kertesi & Kezdi]; concentrating top pupils in urban selective tracks accelerates rural-school collapse under cohort shrinkage.

- **finnish_reform, polish_reform**: Phase out early tracking, but only if bundled with structural supports — standardized teacher training and universal diagnostic/remedial systems (Finnish), and long-term multi-party pacts plus compensatory local-school funding (Polish). Why: Finnish equity gains cannot be isolated from teacher-quality and early-support bundling [evidence: moderate — Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009]; Poland's technically successful 1999 reform was politically reversed 2016–2019 [evidence: moderate — OECD].

- **portuguese_reform (minority)**: Retain the tracks and neutralize inequities through non-structural reforms — TEIP-style targeted funding for disadvantaged schools and rigorous national curriculum standards. Why: Portugal raised PISA results 2006–2015 without altering selection age, suggesting structural reorganization may be a distraction from higher-impact classroom-level interventions [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trends; Crato 2020].

- **education_finance (minority)**: Retain the system because transition costs (teacher retraining, facility re-adaptation) outweigh marginal steady-state savings. Why: per-pupil funding is roughly flat across tracks and wage grids are national, so consolidation yields no operational savings while front-loading transition costs [evidence: strong for flat costs — model knowledge; weak for cost magnitudes — budget chapters].

- **implementation_planning (minority)**: Retain in the medium term, delaying integration until teacher supply stabilizes. Why: Hungary's systemic teacher shortage (especially maths/science and marginalized regions) makes staff redistribution across comprehensive schools functionally impossible without degrading delivery in disadvantaged regions [evidence: strong — OECD; PSZ/PDSZ surveys].

- **legal_and_governance (minority)**: Neither phase out nor retain by default — commission an independent equity audit first, then co-design a graduated pathway respecting legal timelines and maintainer plurality. Why: reform is legislatively entrenched (Nkt.) with multi-year notice and transitional guarantees, and no recent Hungary-specific equity impact study exists to justify legislated closure [evidence: strong for entrenchment — Nkt. analysis; weak for equity-tension claim — no recent impact study].

- **hungarian_education_system**: Do not abolish tracks without first addressing primary-stage preparation inequality, catchment-based sorting, and maintainer fragmentation. Why: abolition alone will displace stratification rather than reduce it, because sorting continues through catchment geography and parental choice independently of track abolition [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013; Berenyi-Berkovits-Erőss].

- **political_feasibility**: No phase-out will achieve durability unless bundled with (a) decoupled standards-setting, (b) comparison-group pilots with published results, and (c) funded, tracked equity outreach — and even then reversal risk stays high. Why: teacher resistance is structural, middle-class parents demand falsifiable proof, and Hungary's electoral/union configuration makes reversal risk higher than Poland's [evidence: moderate — Round 06 discourse analysis; Jakubowski et al. 2016].

## What the experts agree on

- Roughly 8–12% of a cohort enters 6/8-year tracks at ages 10–12 via central written exam, and entrants are strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal].
- Hungary's SES share of PISA reading/maths variance (≈19–25%) is well above the OECD average (≈12–14%) and persists across cycles [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2012–2022].
- Since 2013 most state schools operate under tankerület centres while churches and foundations hold a growing, decentralized share, fragmenting any uniform structural mandate [evidence: strong — Public Education Act amendments; maintainer statistics].
- Structural reform costs are dominated by transition friction (retraining, facility realignment, administration), not steady-state operational costs [evidence: weak — budget chapters; expert estimates].
- Abolishing tracks alone does not equalize outcomes; sorting continues via catchment geography and parental choice [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013; school-choice literature].

## Minority positions

- **portuguese_reform (minority)**: Retain the tracks; pursue non-structural TEIP-style targeted funding and rigorous curriculum standards instead of altering selection age. Rationale: Portugal improved PISA outcomes 2006–2015 without structural reorganization, so the abolition debate may divert capacity from higher-impact classroom interventions [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trends; Crato 2020]. Holder's own uncertainty: transferability of TEIP mechanisms to Hungary and the effect of Hungarian teacher shortages remain unverified.

- **education_finance (minority)**: Retain the system; transition costs outweigh any steady-state savings. Rationale: per-pupil costs are flat across tracks and national wage grids mean staff reassignment does not lower wage bills, so consolidation front-loads cost without fiscal return [evidence: strong for flat costs; weak for magnitudes].

- **implementation_planning (minority)**: Retain in the medium term until teacher supply stabilizes. Rationale: the systemic maths/science teacher shortage makes redistribution across comprehensive schools functionally impossible without collapsing delivery in disadvantaged regions [evidence: strong — OECD; PSZ/PDSZ surveys].

- **legal_and_governance (minority)**: Do not default to either phase-out or retention; commission an independent equity audit and co-design a graduated pathway. Rationale: legislative entrenchment and maintainer plurality make legislated closure procedurally costly, and no recent Hungary-specific equity impact study exists to justify it [evidence: strong for entrenchment; weak for the equity-tension claim].

- **school_network_planning / demography (minority caveat within the phase-out camp)**: Non-state maintainers (church/foundation) can absorb phased-out selective tracks and bypass national consolidation, meaning a state closure may fragment rather than consolidate the network [evidence: moderate — maintainer statistics; assumption that church/private maintainers will prioritize selective tracks]. This directly contradicts any assumption that non-state maintainers can be restricted from absorbing phased-out capacity — an unresolved tension flagged repeatedly in prior rounds.

## Unresolved cross-cutting uncertainties

- Whether delayed selection alone (without full comprehensivization) suffices, and what the counterfactual policy mix is [confidence: low — international_comparison].
- The proportion of selection advantage attributable to the exam itself vs. prior family tutoring investment [confidence: low — hungarian_education_system].
- Actual nationwide implementation/enforcement capacity across the fragmented maintainer landscape, including the share of rural 6/8-year seats held by non-state maintainers [confidence: medium — hungarian_education_system, school_network_planning].
- Whether the middle class would remain in comprehensive schools or exit to private/alternative selective schooling [confidence: medium — finnish_reform].
- Whether the 2024+ government coalition holds through a full 5–7 year reform cycle, given Poland-style reversal risk [confidence: medium — political_feasibility].
- The precise cost of teacher retraining and facility reallocation [confidence: low–medium — education_finance, implementation_planning].
