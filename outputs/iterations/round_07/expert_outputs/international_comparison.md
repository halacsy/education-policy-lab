# Expert analysis: international_comparison

## Findings (evidence)

- Earlier between-school tracking (selection before age 11) increases within-country inequality of educational outcomes without raising mean achievement in cross-national comparison. [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006, *Economic Journal*]

- Hungary's socio-economic gradient in PISA reading and mathematics is substantially steeper than OECD average: 19–25% of score variance attributable to SES vs. 12–14% OECD-wide across 2012–2022 cycles. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA country notes]

- Countries with later selection (age 15+, comprehensive systems) show lower SES-achievement correlation while maintaining or exceeding mean PISA scores; Germany's two-tier system at age 10 sits between early-selection and comprehensive models, with persistent inequity but lower than Hungary's gradient. [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA comparative analysis, Brunello & Checchi 2007]

- The 6- and 8-year gimnazium track in Hungary operates de facto as early selection: entry typically at age 10–12 via competitive exams, academically stratifying cohorts before adolescence. [evidence: moderate — Hungarian education ministry data; OECD Education at a Glance Hungary profiles]

- Difference-in-differences studies of tracking reform (e.g., Sweden's 1960s comprehensive shift, Germany's Länder variation) show modest long-run equity gains post-reform, but effects take 10–15 years to fully manifest in cohort outcomes. [evidence: moderate — Meghir & Palme 2005; Pischke & Wachter 2008]

## Interpretation

Hungary's dual problem is both structural and acute: the SES–achievement gap is among the steepest in Europe, and the 6–8-year gimnazium system operationalizes this gap institutionally by sorting students at ages where family resources strongly predict test performance, not innate ability. The international evidence indicates that *maintaining* this early-selection structure will perpetuate—not solve—inequality.

The Hanushek–Woessmann finding is critical: countries do not trade equity for excellence by postponing selection. Conversely, delayed tracking does not guarantee equity gains if socio-economic sorting persists through informal channels (tutoring, school choice, residence).

## Assumptions

- Hungary's PISA SES gradient reflects structural tracking effects, not unmeasured ability differences. [assumption]
- Transferability of European diff-in-diff evidence to Hungary assumes comparable labour-market incentives and absence of parallel private-selection systems. [assumption]
- The 6–8-year gimnazium system is the primary institutional driver of Hungary's inequality (vs. unequal primary schooling quality). [assumption — needs testing]

## Position

Hungary should postpone formal between-school selection from age 10–12 to age 14–15 and broaden comprehensive lower-secondary structures, because early tracking demonstrably increases inequality without raising mean outcomes, and Hungary's already-steep SES gradient suggests selection at intake amplifies family-resource disadvantage.

## Uncertainties

- **What is the causal role of the gimnazium system vs. primary-school quality gaps?** [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: regression discontinuity analysis around gimnazium entry thresholds and primary attainment data]

- **How large are equity gains from delayed selection in Hungary specifically?** [confidence: low — would be reduced by: pilot reform in one county with 10-year outcome tracking, or synthetic control study using regional variation]

- **Do informal selection mechanisms (private tutoring, school choice) offset formal-tracking abolition?** [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: household survey data on tutoring uptake post-reform and school-choice patterns in countries that eliminated early selection]

- **Timeline to measurable equity improvement post-reform?** [confidence: low — would be reduced by: longitudinal microsimulation model calibrated to Hungarian labour-market returns and cohort-flow data]
