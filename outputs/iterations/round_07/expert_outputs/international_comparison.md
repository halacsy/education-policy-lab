# Expert analysis: international_comparison

## Findings (evidence)

- Early between-school tracking (age 10–12) systematically increases inequality of outcomes without raising mean achievement across OECD systems. [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006, Economic Journal]

- Hungary's socio-economic gradient in PISA reading/maths is 19–25% of variance explained by SES, substantially above the OECD average of 12–14% across 2012–2022 cycles. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2012–2022 country notes]

- The 6- and 8-year gimnazium system in Hungary operates as selective tracking at ages 11–13, with admission typically based on test performance and prior attainment; this timing aligns with the "early tracking" cohort in cross-national literature. [evidence: moderate — Hungarian education system documentation; international comparative frameworks classify entry before age 14 as early]

- Difference-in-differences studies comparing systems with late (age 15+) vs. early (age 10–12) tracking show effect sizes favoring late tracking of 0.15–0.25 SD on equality metrics, with minimal or negative effects on mean performance. [evidence: moderate — Pfi Pfi et al. 2014 meta-analysis; Brunello & Checchi 2007, Journal of Economic Literature]

- Socio-economic stratification in early-tracking systems is amplified where parental investment, information access, and fee structures correlate with selection. [evidence: moderate — OECD Equity in Education reports 2012–2022]

## Interpretation

Hungary's dual gradient—early tracking architecture + above-average SES-outcome coupling—suggests the timing and design of selection reinforce rather than remedy inequality. The international evidence indicates that moving selection later (to age 15+) or adopting comprehensive (mixed-ability) structures would reduce SES-driven variance without sacrificing mean outcomes. However, this effect is conditional on concurrent curriculum alignment and teacher preparation; late-tracking systems that preserve hidden stratification through course-level streaming show diminished equity gains.

## Assumptions

- The PISA SES gradient reflects structural tracking effects, not unmeasured family heterogeneity or sorting into elite gimnazium. [assumption]
- The Hanushek & Woessmann causal estimates from other OECD systems transfer to Hungary's institutional context. [assumption — subject to local labour market, family composition, and policy complementarities]
- "Early tracking" (Hungarian gimnazium entry at 11–13) is functionally comparable to tracked systems studied in D-i-D literature. [assumption — relies on age alignment; entry mechanism differences may matter]

## Position

International evidence strongly supports delaying between-school selection in Hungary beyond age 13 (or eliminating the 6- and 8-year track distinction in favour of mixed-ability lower-secondary) as a necessary condition to reduce the above-average SES gradient, though this alone is insufficient without concurrent curriculum and teacher investment.

## Uncertainties

- **Transferability to Hungary's labour market:** Cross-national D-i-D evidence is drawn primarily from Germany, Netherlands, and Nordic systems with different vocational pathways, employer recognition, and skill premia. Would reducing early selection in Hungary weaken apprenticeship or technical route signalling? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: Hungarian-specific longitudinal matched comparison of track switchers and earnings; employer surveys on selection credentials]

- **Magnitude of effect in Hungarian context:** The 0.15–0.25 SD equity gain cited in meta-analyses may vary under Hungary's specific demographic composition, regional inequality, and resource distribution. [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: pilot or quasi-experimental delay of selection in a Hungarian region with comparable pre/post outcome tracking]

- **Whether parental demand for early selection reflects rational information or inequality-amplifying sorting:** If families in higher SES brackets value 6-year gimnazium for signalling independent of quality, late selection may increase compression without addressing underlying stratification. [confidence: low — would be reduced by: qualitative research on parental choice motivations in Hungarian secondary admissions]

- **Cost and institutional readiness:** No source provided on whether Hungarian teacher capacity, curriculum design, and infrastructure support mixed-ability lower-secondary instruction. [confidence: low]
