# Expert analysis: international_comparison

## Findings (evidence)
- Cross-country difference-in-differences evidence indicates earlier between-school tracking increases inequality of outcomes without raising mean performance. [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006, Economic Journal]
- In PISA, the share of reading/maths score variance explained by socio-economic status is among the highest in the OECD for Hungary (~19–25% across cycles vs ~12–14% OECD average). [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2012-2022 country notes]
- Countries that delayed tracking (e.g., comprehensive reforms in Finland, Sweden, Poland pre-2016) saw reductions in the SES-outcome gradient over subsequent PISA cycles without loss in mean scores. [evidence: moderate — cross-country PISA/DiD literature, general pattern known to me but I cannot cite exact effect sizes or specific study years with confidence]
- The Hungarian 6- and 8-year gimnazium system selects students at ages 10–12, earlier than most OECD systems (typical first tracking at 14–16 or later). [evidence: strong — OECD/Eurydice structural comparisons of education systems]
- Early-tracking systems tend to show a stronger association between parental education/income and the track a child enters, independent of measured ability. [evidence: moderate — general finding across German-, Austrian-, and Hungarian-style tracking literature; exact Hungarian-specific selection-equity studies not in hand]

## Interpretation
The cross-national pattern is consistent and directionally strong: earlier selection age correlates with higher inequality of outcomes, and Hungary's own PISA gradient is already an outlier on the high-inequality end among OECD comparators. This is compatible with — though does not on its own prove causally for Hungary — a story where the 6-/8-year gimnazium tracks amplify a selection effect already visible in the data. The comparative literature does not show that delaying selection lowers overall national performance; the tradeoff is primarily distributional, not (on average) about aggregate achievement.

## Assumptions
- Hungary's institutional and labor-market context is similar enough to comparator countries (Germany-Austria-style vs Nordic-style systems) for the DiD tracking-timing results to transfer. [assumption]
- Parental school-choice behavior in Hungary responds to selection incentives similarly to other early-tracking systems studied. [assumption]

## Position
International evidence supports raising the effective selection age (curtailing or phasing out 6-/8-year gimnazium entry before age 14-15) as the change most likely to reduce Hungary's outlier-level SES-outcome gradient without a demonstrated cost to mean achievement.

## Uncertainties
- No Hungary-specific causal (DiD/RDD) estimate of the gimnazium selection effect on inequality is in hand, only cross-country generalization. (confidence: low — megbízhatóság: alacsony; would be reduced by: a Hungary-specific quasi-experimental study exploiting admission cutoffs or regional variation in 6-/8-year school availability)
- Unknown whether Hungary's elite 6-/8-year track also serves a "keeping high performers in public education" function whose removal could trigger private-school flight, altering net equity effects. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: data on private-school enrollment trends in countries that abolished early tracking)
- Magnitude (not just direction) of achievement-inequality tradeoff specific to the Hungarian curriculum/labor market is unknown. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Hungary-specific replication of Hanushek & Woessmann-style analysis)
