# Expert analysis: international_comparison

## Findings (evidence)

- **Earlier between-school tracking increases inequality without raising mean achievement.** [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006, Economic Journal] Cross-country difference-in-differences designs show that systems selecting students into separate schools before age 15 amplify socio-economic gradients in outcomes.

- **Hungary already exhibits exceptionally steep socio-economic gradients in literacy and numeracy.** [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2012–2022] The share of reading/maths variance attributable to family SES is 19–25% in Hungary, compared to 12–14% OECD average. This gap persists across multiple assessment cycles, indicating structural rather than cyclical inequality.

- **The 6- and 8-year grammar school system selects at ages 12 and 10 respectively, earlier than most OECD peers.** [evidence: moderate — Hungarian education system documentation and OECD Education at a Glance comparisons] Most tracked systems select at 14–15; Hungary's entry window is younger and competitive (entrance exams).

- **Early selection in tracked systems correlates with larger SES–achievement gaps in international studies.** [evidence: moderate — PISA structural analysis; Brunello & Checchi 2007] Systems with early, rigid tracking (Germany, Austria pre-reform, Hungary) show stronger family-origin effects than late-tracking peers (Sweden pre-2011, Finland).

## Interpretation

Hungary's combination of (a) early selection, (b) high socio-economic stratification in PISA, and (c) international evidence of tracking-inequality link suggests that the 6/8-year grammar system reinforces rather than mitigates family advantage. The international evidence does not show mean-achievement gains offsetting this inequality cost.

The timing (ages 10–12) is a critical vulnerability: at this age, cultural capital, parental investment in test prep, and prior achievement gaps are already pronounced but still malleable. Selective entry amplifies these pre-existing differences into permanent school-type assignment.

## Assumptions

- [assumption] PISA gradients and labour-market outcomes in high-income OECD systems are generalizable to Hungary's economic context.
- [assumption] The causal direction (tracking → inequality) is not reversed by omitted variables (e.g., parent choice by ability).
- [assumption] Hungarian entrance exams measure potential rather than already-accumulated cultural-linguistic advantage.

## Position

**Hungary's early academic selection via 6/8-year grammar schools is likely increasing inequality of outcomes without raising average performance, and international evidence supports later selection or mixed-cohort schooling as an equity-efficiency improvement.**

## Uncertainties

- **What is the counterfactual policy mix?** [confidence: low] International comparisons show late tracking *and* comprehensive secondary design; Hungary could delay selection without full comprehensivization. Would be reduced by: cost–benefit analysis of hybrid models (e.g., selection at 14–15 instead of 10–12; mixed-ability primary schooling with tracked upper secondary). *Megbízhatóság: alacsony; csökkentené: magyar-releváns reformpilóták vagy szimulációs elemzések.*

- **Do Hungarian grammar schools provide genuine acceleration or primarily signalling/peer effects?** [confidence: low] International evidence on tracking separates achievement gains from composition effects; Hungarian data on whether grammar alumni outperform matched non-selected peers in value-added terms is opaque to me. Would be reduced by: longitudinal matched-cohort analysis controlling for prior achievement and family SES.

- **What is the political elasticity of the 6/8-year system?** [confidence: medium–high] The system is institutionally entrenched; reform resistance from parents and schools is predictable but degree unknown. Would be reduced by: stakeholder interview data; electoral sensitivity analysis.
