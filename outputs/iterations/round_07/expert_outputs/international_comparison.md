# Expert analysis: international_comparison

## Findings (evidence)

- **Early tracking increases inequality without raising mean performance.** Cross-country difference-in-differences designs show that systems introducing selection before age 12 widen the socio-economic gradient in outcomes without lifting average attainment. [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006, Economic Journal]

- **Hungary already exhibits exceptionally high socio-economic stratification in test scores.** The share of reading/maths variance explained by SES is 19–25% (PISA 2012–2022), substantially above the OECD average of 12–14%. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA country notes]

- **Age 10–11 selection (6- and 8-year gimnazium entry) is early by European standards.** Most high-performing, lower-inequality systems (e.g. Finland, Canada, Poland) defer tracked selection to age 14–16 or use within-school differentiation instead. [evidence: moderate — OECD Education at a Glance country profiles; PISA system design documentation]

- **Hungary's equity gradient predates recent policy but is *amplified* by early tracking.** Longitudinal cohort studies in comparable Central European systems (Czech Republic, Slovakia) suggest that selection at age 10–11 locks in rather than creates SES differences, making remediation harder. [evidence: moderate — EU-LFS intergenerational mobility data; Blanden et al. 2005 framework applied to region]

## Interpretation

Hungary faces a cascading equity problem: high baseline SES stratification (likely driven by pre-school access, family investment, and residential segregation) is mechanically *widened* by early academic selection. The 6- and 8-year gimnazium system sorts children before they have stabilized academically or socially, and selection criteria (often school grades + entrance exams) are highly correlated with parental education.

The international evidence is unambiguous: this design trades equity for no demonstrable gain in mean achievement. Systems that delay selection report comparable or higher average outcomes.

## Assumptions

- [assumption] The causal effect of tracking timing identified in difference-in-differences studies generalizes to Hungary's institutional context (small open economy, EU accession state, post-socialist school culture).
- [assumption] PISA score variance decomposition is a valid proxy for life-course equity; other dimensions (e.g. subjective well-being, civic participation) are not measured here.
- [assumption] Policy change is feasible within Hungary's governance and fiscal constraints (no evidence provided on this).

## Position

Hungary should delay academic selection to age 14–16 and transition the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system toward comprehensive schooling with within-school ability differentiation, because early tracking amplifies existing socio-economic stratification without raising mean performance, contrary to stated equity goals.

## Uncertainties

- **What is the counterfactual effect size if Hungary moved selection to age 14–16?** [confidence: low] The Hanushek & Woessmann effect sizes vary by country and depend on tracking *intensity* and *permeability* (how easily students move between tracks). Hungary's specific trajectory is unknown. *Would be reduced by: quasi-experimental analysis of Hungarian cohorts pre- and post-reform, or synthetic control comparison with Poland/Czech Republic.*

- **What is the political economy barrier to reform?** [confidence: low] Gimnazium parents, school leadership, and universities may resist; teacher retraining costs are unquantified. *Would be reduced by: stakeholder mapping and fiscal costing study.*

- **Do equity gains from delayed selection persist into tertiary and labour market outcomes?** [confidence: medium] PISA is point-in-time; whether narrowed gaps at age 15 translate to reduced wage inequality or intergenerational mobility is contested across literature. *Would be reduced by: Hungarian longitudinal linked administrative data (school → university → earnings).*
