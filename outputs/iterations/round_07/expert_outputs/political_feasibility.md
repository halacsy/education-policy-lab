# Expert analysis: political_feasibility

## Findings (evidence)

- **Urban middle-class entrenchment**: Early selection via 6- and 8-year gimnazium is perceived by politically active urban parents as a non-negotiable mobility/insurance asset. Prior curtailment attempts (1990s–2010s) triggered well-organised, sustained opposition that derailed reform. [evidence: moderate — domestic press and stakeholder analyses, 1990s–2010s]

- **Polish reversal precedent**: Poland's 1999 postponement of tracking to age 15 initially improved PISA outcomes; the reform was reversed 2016–2019 due to political pressure, not pedagogical failure. [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD]

- **Stakeholder fragmentation on durability**: From round 6 episodic memory, pedagogus_erdekvedo will withdraw conditional support if accountability or compliance audits are embedded in the reform (not just published guidance). kisgyerekes_szuloi will soften opposition only if pilot data on SES proxy gaming and teacher supply are documented *before* rollout. eselyegyenlosegi_civil will strengthen support only if low-SES and Roma outreach is measured, not assumed. [evidence: moderate — stakeholder discourse, rounds 1–6]

- **Teacher supply as binding constraint**: The scenario itself identifies teacher retraining and supply adequacy as a binding constraint, yet pedagogus_erdekvedo (S3) notes conditional support is not credible if this assumption is unverified. [evidence: moderate — policy scenario documentation and stakeholder critique]

## Interpretation

Political durability of a track-postponement reform hinges on three conditions that are currently misaligned:

1. **Credible delivery on middle-class reassurance**: If advanced-programme funding and selective entry to enriched tracks are decoupled from teacher-level gatekeeping and instead rely on transparent, centrally-managed assessment, urban opposition may not mobilise at pre-2010 intensity.

2. **Pre-rollout evidence on equity mechanisms**: SES-based intake rebalancing and Roma outreach must be piloted and evaluated before nationwide rollout, otherwise kisgyerekes_szuloi opposition will harden and delegitimise the reform as untested social engineering.

3. **Stakeholder buy-in on institutional redesign, not compliance audits**: Teachers will conditionally support if standards are guidance-based and planning time is allocated; they will withdraw if the reform introduces new performance audits disguised as quality assurance.

The Polish precedent shows that even successful pedagogical outcomes (rising PISA scores) do not guarantee durability if the political coalition fragments. Hungary's reform would likely face reversal pressure 4–6 years post-implementation if early indicators show either (a) perceived middle-class disadvantage, or (b) unvalidated equity proxies.

## Assumptions

- [assumption] Urban middle-class mobilisation capacity remains as high in 2024–2025 as in 1990s–2010s (has not diffused due to demographic or institutional change).
- [assumption] "Documented pilot data" on SES gaming and teacher supply can be generated within a realistic pre-rollout window (1–2 years) and will meaningfully shift stakeholder positions rather than entrench them.
- [assumption] A governance redesign (centrally-managed assessment, guidance-based standards) is administratively feasible and will be perceived as credible by both teachers and parents.

## Position

Early track-postponement in Hungary is politically durable only if bundled with: (i) pre-rollout pilot evidence on equity mechanisms and teacher supply, (ii) decoupling of standards from teacher-level accountability, and (iii) documented outreach to low-SES and Roma families—otherwise middle-class opposition will reverse the reform within 5–7 years, as occurred in Poland.

## Uncertainties

- **What credible "decoupling" from accountability looks like in practice**: The boundary between guidance-based standards and de facto performance expectations is contested in Hungarian education discourse. [confidence: low — would be reduced by: stakeholder interviews with inspectorate officials and union representatives on how guidance-based standards would be operationalised in practice]

- **Timing and feasibility of pilot evidence**: No timeline has been specified for SES proxy validation, teacher supply audits, or Roma outreach pilots. [confidence: low — would be reduced by: explicit project schedule with measurable pilot milestones and decision rules for Go/No-Go gates before rollout]

- **Sustainability of Polish-style outcomes in Hungarian institutional context**: Poland's PISA gains occurred under different curriculum, teacher-qualification, and regional-inequality profiles. [confidence: low — would be reduced by: comparative analysis of institutional preconditions (teacher CPD capacity, regional resource distribution) in Poland vs. Hungary 1999–2012]

- **Magnitude of middle-class defection under advanced-programme funding model**: Will selective enrichment within general schools substitute for 8-year gimnazium and retain urban buy-in, or will it be perceived as insufficient? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: stakeholder scenario-testing workshops or discrete-choice survey data on parent preference for enrichment-within-general vs. separate track]
