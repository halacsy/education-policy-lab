# Expert analysis: political_feasibility

## Findings (evidence)

- **Structural resistance is durable and well-mobilised.** Urban middle-class parents regard 8- and 6-year gimnazium places as a key mobility/insurance asset; prior attempts to curtail structures met intense, well-organised resistance [evidence: moderate — domestic press and stakeholder analyses, 1990s-2010s]. This constituency has organisational capacity and media access to sustain opposition campaigns.

- **International precedent shows reversal risk.** Poland's 1999 reform postponed tracking to age 15; PISA scores rose substantially by 2012, with analyses attributing part of the gain to delayed selection. The reform was reversed in 2016–2019 for political reasons [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD]. This demonstrates that even evidence-backed postponement can be reversed if political coalitions shift.

- **Stakeholder pivots are conditional, not unconditional.** Episodic memory from rounds 6–7 shows that pedagogus_erdekvedo, kisgyerekes_szuloi, and eselyegyenlosegi_civil groups have articulated specific, measurable conditions—not blanket positions. For example: pedagogus_erdekvedo S1 would support only if assessment is standardized centrally without teacher-level gatekeeping; kisgyerekes_szuloi S1 would move to strong support only if a pilot demonstrates ≥85% SES classification accuracy [evidence: strong — episodic memory, rounds 6–7].

- **Non-state sector expansion is a reversal vector.** Kisgyerekes_szuloi conditions explicitly flag that if sorting migrates to non-state (religious, private) schools under a comprehensive model, the policy goal collapses. Binding agreements with non-state maintainers are named as a prerequisite, not optional [evidence: strong — episodic memory, round 7, S2 and S4].

- **Teacher supply is a binding political constraint.** Pedagogus_erdekvedo S3 identifies teacher shortage as a core assumption vulnerability. Conditional support hinges on a published 10-year forecast showing stabilization *before* national rollout. Without this, opposition hardens [evidence: strong — episodic memory, round 7, S3].

## Interpretation

Political durability in Hungary's case depends on *disaggregation*—moving beyond binary support/opposition to **conditional mobilisation tied to measurable pilot outcomes**. The episodic record shows stakeholders are not locked into positions; they have named what evidence would unlock softening (e.g., SES classification audits, teacher-supply forecasts, non-state binding agreements).

However, two durability risks are acute:
1. **Reversal via non-state expansion.** If comprehensive intake rebalancing succeeds in state schools but non-state sectors (especially religious schools, expanding in Hungary) absorb selective demand, the reform fails materially. This requires *binding legal architecture*, not guidance.
2. **Electoral volatility.** Poland's reversal shows that even measurable gains do not immunise reforms from political reversal if government turnover occurs. A 2016–2019 Polish government reversed 1999 gains; Hungary's coalition dynamics are similarly volatile.

**Durability strategy:** Tie rollout to *irreversible institutional lock-in*—multi-year pilot data, legislative teacher-protection guarantees, non-state funding caps written into law, not ministerial decree. Short-term political survival is insufficient; durability requires making reversal costly.

## Assumptions

- Episodic conditions reflect genuine bottlenecks rather than rhetorical positioning [assumption]
- Urban middle-class resistance can be managed if non-state pathways remain open [assumption]
- A 2–5 year pilot window is politically feasible before national rollout pressure builds [assumption]
- Independent audit capacity exists in Hungary to evaluate coaching-resistance and SES classification [assumption]

## Position

**Hungary should pilot comprehensive intake rebalancing in 3–4 districts over 5 years, with mandatory independent evaluation of SES classification accuracy (≥85% threshold), teacher-supply stabilisation forecasts, and binding non-state sector caps, before any national rollout; without these gates, reversal risk under the next government is high.**
