# Expert analysis: political_feasibility

## Findings (evidence)

- Urban middle-class parents treat 6- and 8-year gimnazium places as a non-negotiable mobility asset and insurance mechanism. Prior curtailment attempts (1990s–2010s) triggered sustained, well-organised opposition. [evidence: moderate — domestic press and stakeholder analyses]

- Poland's 1999 reform delayed tracking to age 15; PISA gains materialised by 2012, partly attributed to postponed selection. The reform was reversed in 2016–2019 under political pressure despite educational gains. [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD]

- Hungarian pedagogus érdekvédő (teacher union) conditional support hinges on four hard decoupling requirements: (1) central standardisation of assessment without school-level gatekeeping; (2) planning-time allocation for advanced programmes not contingent on teacher-designed enrichment; (3) curriculum standards as guidance, not performance-audit triggers; (4) intake rebalancing explicitly separated from class heterogeneity mandates. [evidence: strong — round 06 episodic memory]

- Teacher supply is documented as a binding constraint in current planning; conditional support from pedagogus is not credible if that assumption holds. [evidence: strong — round 06 episodic memory]

- Kisgyerekes szülői (parents of young children) will only soften opposition to conditional if: (1) SES proxy piloting shows non-gameability; (2) advanced-programme funding in general schools is evaluated against comparison groups for high-achiever retention; (3) teacher supply audits and retraining uptake are completed and documented. [evidence: strong — round 06 episodic memory]

- Esélyegyenlőségi civil society strengthens support only if outreach and information campaigns targeting low-SES and Roma families in under-applying settlements are documented and measured by application rates. [evidence: strong — round 06 episodic memory]

## Interpretation

The political durability problem is not selection *per se*, but the vulnerability of any reform to reversal when stakeholder conditions remain unmet. Poland's experience shows that gains (PISA +) do not prevent rollback if political costs are high. Hungary faces three interlocking durability risks:

1. **Middle-class exit risk**: Terminating or materially narrowing the 6/8-year track risks capital flight to private schools or parental defection to opposition parties. This constituency is organised, vocal, and has demonstrated capacity to block reform.

2. **Implementation credibility collapse**: Teachers will interpret any ambiguity in "rigorous standards" or "mixed-cohort teaching" as performance audit creep. Pedagogus will withdraw cooperation if decoupling is not explicit and policed. Without compliance, advanced programmes collapse; parents lose confidence; support evaporates.

3. **Equity tokenism trap**: Civil society and disadvantaged-family trust depends on measurable outreach, not rhetorical commitment. Pilot data showing SES-proxy gaming or teacher resistance to mixed cohorts will trigger permanent opposition, making any reform politically toxic.

## Assumptions

- Pedagogus unions have veto power over implementation fidelity [assumption]. If decoupling directives are issued but unenforced, teachers will treat them as fiction.
- Middle-class parents view delayed selection as a material loss of choice, not a gain in fairness [assumption]. Framing alone will not shift this calculation.
- Low-SES and Roma family underapplication is remediable by campaign alone, not by deep structural barriers (e.g. transport, information asymmetry, prior family exposure) [assumption — weak].

## Position

Hungary should pursue selection postponement (moving to age 15) paired with irreversible decoupling of standards/assessment/funding from teacher accountability, explicit planning-time guarantees, and a pilot-evaluate-document loop for equity outreach; without these four conditions met and locked in before rollout, reversal risk is high and durability is not credible.

## Uncertainties

- **Will pedagogus union enforce decoupling compliance at school level?** [confidence: low]. Unions rarely monitor member behaviour after negotiation. *Would be reduced by:* baseline compliance audits in 2–3 schools, published quarterly, with explicit union enforcement consequences.

- **Can advanced-programme funding in general schools retain high-achievers without cream-skimming middle-class parents into selective schools?** [confidence: low]. No Hungarian comparison-group data exists. *Would be reduced by:* piloting in 15–20 general schools with randomised control schools, 3-year follow-up of student retention and SES composition.

- **What is the real cost of middle-class political defection?** [confidence: medium]. Organised opposition is real; electoral volatility is unknown. *Would be reduced by:* stakeholder polling pre-reform on exit thresholds, and simulation of coalition shifts under different reform scenarios.

- **Is low-SES underapplication due to information, transport, family confidence, or school-level gatekeeping?** [confidence: low]. The reform assumes information is the bottleneck. *Would be reduced by:* qualitative research in 5–10 under-applying settlements identifying stated and revealed barriers.

- **How reversible is a selection-postponement reform once implemented?** [confidence: medium]. Poland reversed; cost of reversal is not analysed in Hungarian context. *Would be reduced by:* stakeholder interviews with Polish reformers on political switching costs and veto-player coalition changes.
