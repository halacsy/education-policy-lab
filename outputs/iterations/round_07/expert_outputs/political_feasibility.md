# Expert analysis: political_feasibility

## Findings (evidence)

- Urban middle-class parents treat 6- and 8-year gimnazium places as irreplaceable mobility/insurance assets; prior reform attempts met sustained, well-organised political resistance. [evidence: moderate — Domestic press and stakeholder analyses, 1990s-2010s]

- Poland's delayed-selection reform (1999, age 15 entry) correlated with PISA gains by 2012; the reform was politically reversed 2016–2019 despite technical success. [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD]

- Hungarian teacher unions (pedagogus_erdekvedo) will condition support on **decoupling** standards-setting from teacher-level performance audits and gatekeeping; conditional pledges collapse if intake rebalancing or compliance audits remain bundled. [evidence: moderate — Round 06 discourse analysis]

- Early-childhood parents (kisgyerekes_szuloi) will soften opposition only if advanced-programme funding in general schools is **piloted against a comparison group** and documented to retain high-achievers; assumption-based reassurance does not shift the coalition. [evidence: moderate — Round 06 discourse analysis]

- Equity advocates (eselyegyenlosegi_civil) require **documented outreach campaigns** targeting low-SES and Roma families in under-applying settlements, with measured application uptake; bundling without enforcement is insufficient. [evidence: moderate — Round 06 discourse analysis]

## Interpretation

The political durability of any Hungarian reform hinges on three conditions that prior rounds have isolated:

1. **Decoupling institutional change from teacher accountability**: Teacher resistance is structural, not rhetorical. Linking curriculum standards, assessment, or intake rebalancing to performance metrics triggers defection. The successful pathway requires that standards are published as *guidance*, not compliance benchmarks.

2. **Evidence-based pilots with comparison groups**: Middle-class parents and equity stakeholders alike demand falsifiable proof. Poland's reversal suggests that even technical success is politically fragile if the winning coalition loses confidence in the counterfactual. Pilot designs matter more than final-stage advocacy.

3. **Active equity deployment, not passive inclusion**: Mentioning "under-represented groups" without funded, tracked outreach signals performative commitment. This coalition will defect if documentation shows application rates stagnate.

The Poland case is a cautionary template: delayed selection works technically but is politically reversible when one stakeholder (typically centre-right or parents of high-achievers) mobilises against it. Hungary's right-wing electoral stability and strong pedagogus union mean reversal risk is **higher**, not lower, than Poland 2016–2019.

## Assumptions

- Teacher supply remains a binding constraint; conditional support by pedagogus_erdekvedo is not credible if supply assumptions fail. [assumption]
- Pilot evaluation can be credibly independent and published in time to influence next-term school assignment. [assumption]
- SES-proxy instruments (used for intake rebalancing) are gameable; opposition softens only if piloting data show weak gaming, not if theory suggests it is unlikely. [assumption]

## Position

**No reform postponing or curtailing 6- and 8-year gimnazium selection will achieve political durability in Hungary unless it is bundled with (a) decoupled standards-setting, (b) comparison-group pilots with published results, and (c) funded, tracked equity outreach—and even then, reversal risk remains high because the losing coalition (urban middle-class parents) has consistent electoral voice.**
