# Expert analysis: political_feasibility

## Findings (evidence)

- Urban middle-class parents treat 6- and 8-year gimnazium places as mobility insurance and a non-negotiable private good; prior reform attempts (1990s–2010s) faced well-organised, sustained opposition that blocked implementation. [evidence: moderate — Domestic press and stakeholder analyses, 1990s-2010s]

- Poland's 1999 reform postponed tracking to age 15; PISA gains were documented by 2012, with selection-delay attributed as a causal factor. However, the reform was politically reversed in 2016–2019 despite evidence of success. [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD]

- From episodic memory (Round 6): Pedagogus érdekvédő (teacher unions) condition support on decoupling standards from teacher-level accountability and decentralising compliance audits; without these guarantees, conditional support collapses to opposition. [evidence: moderate — Stakeholder discourse analysis, Round 6]

- From episodic memory: Kisgyerekes szülői (young-family parents) will soften opposition only if advanced-programme funding in general schools is piloted with comparison-group evaluation; coaching pressure and SES-proxy gaming are named as reversal triggers. [evidence: moderate — Stakeholder discourse analysis, Round 6]

- From episodic memory: Esélyegyenlőségi civil society will strengthen support only if outreach to low-SES and Roma families is documented and tracked by application uptake; absence of this mechanism is a credibility threshold. [evidence: moderate — Stakeholder discourse analysis, Round 6]

## Interpretation

The evidence points to a **durability paradox**: structural reforms that delay selection (Poland case) produce measurable equity and achievement gains, but are vulnerable to reversal when affluent stakeholders mobilise. Hungary's prior failures (1990s–2010s) suggest that without active political insulation—not just evidence—reversals will occur within 5–10 years.

The episodic memory reveals that **conditional support is weaponised**. Stakeholders are not offering flexibility; they are listing pre-conditions that, if unmet, convert them to opposition. Teacher unions require institutional decoupling; affluent parents require pilot validation; civil society requires outreach documentation. Bundling these into a single, transparent implementation roadmap is the only path to durable coalition-building.

The Poland reversal (2016–2019) is particularly instructive: evidence of success did not prevent political reversal, implying that **durability depends on sustained stakeholder buy-in, not retrospective outcomes**.

## Assumptions

- That stakeholder "conditions" reflect genuine negotiating positions, not performative resistance. [assumption]
- That documentation and transparency (pilot evaluation, outreach tracking) will change stakeholder calculus rather than deepen mistrust. [assumption]
- That teacher supply is a binding constraint (named in Round 6 scenario), and thus teacher-union cooperation cannot be bypassed. [assumption]

## Position

Hungary should pursue delayed selection (moving tracking to age 15) bundled with **transparent, stakeholder-co-authored implementation roadmaps** specifying (a) teacher-accountability decoupling, (b) pilot evaluation of advanced-programme funding in general schools, and (c) documented outreach to low-SES and Roma families; without these, the reform will face reversal within 8 years, replicating the Poland and Hungary historical pattern.

## Uncertainties

- **How credible is teacher-union conditional support?** Whether decoupling standards from accountability will genuinely convert union opposition to active support, or merely reduce resistance. [confidence: low — would be reduced by: structured dialogue with union leadership and commitment documents signed pre-piloting]

- **What triggers affluent-parent mobilisation?** Whether pilot evidence of high-achiever retention in general schools (the condition) will actually satisfy parents, or whether the condition was a proxy for deeper preference for cream-skimming. [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: ethnographic or survey research on affluent parents' underlying decision criteria]

- **Durability of political will.** Whether the current or successor government will sustain the reform post-implementation if affluent stakeholders mount opposition. [confidence: low — would be reduced by: cross-party political commitments or constitutional embedding of the reform]

- **Low-SES and Roma family response to outreach.** Whether documented outreach will materially increase application rates, or whether structural barriers (information, transport, cultural trust) will persist despite campaigns. [confidence: low — would be reduced by: pilot data on outreach efficacy and application-rate elasticity by SES and ethnicity]
