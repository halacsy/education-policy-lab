# Expert analysis: hungarian_education_system
## Findings (evidence)

- **Selection rate and timing:** Approximately 8–12% of each cohort enters 6- or 8-year gimnazium tracks via centralized written examination at age 10 or 12. [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics]

- **Socioeconomic stratification:** Entrants to selective tracks are strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families. [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics]

- **Persistence of sorting under choice regimes:** Free school choice combined with early selective tracks produces measurable between-school stratification already at primary stage in urban areas; track abolition alone would not eliminate sorting because choice and catchment mechanisms remain active. [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013; Berenyi-Berkovits-Erőss school choice studies]

- **Maintainer fragmentation:** Since 2013, most state schools operate under state school district centres (tankerulet), but churches and foundations maintain and control a growing proportion of schools. This institutional diversity constrains uniform structural mandates. [evidence: strong — Public Education Act amendments; maintainer statistics]

- **No early selection in many European comparators:** Several EU systems (e.g., Finland, Poland until recently) delay tracking to age 15–16 or eliminate it entirely, yet maintain strong academic outcomes and lower inequality. [evidence: moderate — OECD Education at a Glance; national policy reviews]

## Interpretation

Hungary's 6/8-year gimnazium system functions as an institutional mechanism for early academic stratification. The combination of (i) entrance at age 10–12, (ii) selection via centralized examination, and (iii) a two-tier curriculum (selective vs. non-selective secondaries) creates an early, durable partition in student trajectories. The strong SES correlation suggests the system translates family advantage into institutional sorting before students have had equal exposure to developmental learning.

However, abolition of early selection alone would not flatten the system: free choice and residential catchment already fragment schools by composition in cities. Effective equalization would require either restricting choice, enforcing balanced intake (e.g., lottery admission), or eliminating the denominational/foundation schools that function as choice-compatible selectivity.

The growing maintainer diversity (state, church, foundation) means that any reform must navigate multiple governance actors with different incentives and legal status.

## Assumptions

- The centralized exam is designed and administered to be comparable across all applicants. [assumption]
- The 8–12% selection rate reflects actual capacity constraints, not policy choice. [assumption]
- "Urban" sorting via choice operates under similar mechanisms in all cities studied. [assumption]

## Position

**Hungary should couple elimination of early selective tracks (6/8-year gimnazium at ages 10–12) with binding intake rules (randomized or balanced admission) and consolidation of maintainer governance to reduce institutional fragmentation, because track abolition alone will not reduce inequality if choice and residential sorting remain unconstrained.**

## Uncertainties

- **What fraction of current SES gap in secondary outcomes reflects selection at entry vs. differential pedagogy within tracks?** [confidence: low — would be reduced by: longitudinal value-added analysis comparing matched cohorts pre- and post-abolition in a comparable country]

- **How many students currently in selective tracks would be reassigned under randomized admission, and what would happen to their outcomes?** [confidence: low — would be reduced by: microsimulation of intake changes and pilot evidence from comparable systems]

- **Do church and foundation schools use their autonomy to select in ways that state schools cannot, and would this behavior persist under abolition of early tracks?** [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: audit of actual admission criteria and intake data by maintainer type]

- **What is the elasticity of residential choice to track structure?** I.e., how much of current urban sorting reflects track availability vs. school reputation more broadly? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: quasi-experimental evidence from cohorts crossing policy changes]

- **Are there political coalitions (church, urban middle class, pedagogical consensus) that would support or block reform?** [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: stakeholder interviews and policy window analysis]
