## Findings (evidence)

- **Selection rate and timing:** Approximately 8–12% of a cohort enters 6- or 8-year gimnazium tracks via centralized written examination at age 10 or 12. [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics]

- **Socioeconomic skew:** Entrants to selective tracks are strongly concentrated among high-SES, urban families. [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics]

- **Sorting persists beyond tracks:** Free school choice combined with early selective admission produces strong between-school sorting at primary stage in cities; track abolition alone would not eliminate this sorting through catchment and choice mechanisms. [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013; Berenyi-Berkovits-Erőss school choice studies]

- **Fragmented governance:** Since 2013, most state schools operate under state school district centres (tankerulet); churches and foundations maintain and expand school provision, constraining uniform structural intervention. [evidence: strong — Public education act amendments; maintainer statistics]

- **No direct evidence on learning outcomes by track:** The curated sources do not provide comparative attainment data (completion rates, qualification quality, labor-market positioning) for students in selective vs. mixed tracks. [evidence: strong absence — silence in registry]

## Interpretation

Early selection in Hungary operates within a complex system where governance fragmentation and market-driven school choice already generate substantial sorting independent of formal tracks. Abolishing 6/8-year gimnazium without addressing catchment design, choice architecture, or maintainer incentives risks displacing rather than eliminating stratification—creating de facto selection through school type or geography instead of examination.

The concentration of selective track access among high-SES urban cohorts suggests the tracks function as a mechanism for reproducing advantage, but *whether they amplify or merely reflect underlying inequality* cannot be determined from available evidence.

## Assumptions

- Tracking and school choice operate as substitutable sorting mechanisms; dismantling one requires coordinated reform of others. [assumption]
- The 8–12% figure represents stable, recent cohort behavior; demographic or policy shifts may alter this. [assumption]
- "Abolition" is technically feasible given multi-maintainer governance; political and contractual obstacles are not quantified. [assumption]

## Position

Hungary should not unilaterally abolish early selective tracks without simultaneous reform of school choice catchments and maintainer transparency, as track removal alone will shift selection into less visible, harder-to-monitor mechanisms while leaving SES-based sorting intact.
