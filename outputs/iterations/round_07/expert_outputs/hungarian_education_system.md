## Findings (evidence)

- Approximately 8–12% of each age cohort enters 6- or 8-year gimnazium tracks via centralized written examination at ages 10–12. [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics]

- Entrants to selective tracks are strongly skewed toward high-SES and urban families; this demographic sorting predates and reinforces broader within-system inequality. [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics]

- Early selection operates within a fragmented maintainer landscape: since 2013, most state schools are administered by regional school district centres (tankerületi intézmények), while churches, foundations, and private operators maintain a growing minority of places. [evidence: strong — Public education act amendments; maintainer statistics]

- Free school choice combined with selective tracks produces strong between-school sorting at primary stage in urban areas independent of formal track selection; abolition of tracks alone would not eliminate this sorting through catchment and choice mechanisms. [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013; Berényi–Berkovits–Erőss school choice studies]

- The 6/8-year gimnázium system is embedded in a decentralized funding and governance structure where uniform policy levers (e.g., track abolition) face implementation variance across maintainers. [evidence: moderate — maintainer structure and regulatory heterogeneity; weak on specific enforcement data]

## Interpretation

Early selection in Hungary functions as a sorting mechanism that crystallizes pre-existing SES advantage rather than identifying previously hidden talent. The centralized examination partially masks but does not eliminate choice-based and catchment-based sorting, meaning that formal track abolition would address only one layer of segmentation. The maintainer landscape creates a secondary fragmentation: state sector reforms do not automatically apply to church or foundation schools, limiting policy uniformity.

Any reform must account for this dual segmentation (selection + choice/catchment + maintainer heterogeneity) rather than treating track structure as an isolated lever.

## Assumptions

- The examination selection at ages 10–12 is treated as meritocratic by families and policymakers, even though intake skew toward SES suggests cream-skimming effects dominate.
- Current free school choice policy remains in place; analysis does not consider mandatory catchment assignment.
- Maintainer diversity persists; analysis does not assume re-centralization of church and foundation schools under state governance.

## Position

Abolition of the 6/8-year gimnázium tracks would reduce early stigmatization and SES-sorting at the selection stage, but would not eliminate between-school segregation without complementary reforms to school choice and catchment boundaries, and would apply unevenly across the fragmented maintainer system.
