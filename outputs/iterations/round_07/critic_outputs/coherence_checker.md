# Critique: coherence_checker

## S1.assumptions
Objection: The scenario's goal explicitly says "no workable SES indicator has yet been demonstrated at application time," while the assumptions field then requires that "a workable SES indicator can be built at application time." That is not merely uncertain; it is the central enabling condition for quotas/reweighting and is contradicted by the mechanism and uncertainties, which say no usable student-level SES register exists and proxies are gameable.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Reframe SES-conscious quotas as a pilot-only option conditional on first validating a robust application-time SES proxy, or remove quotas/reweighting from the main mechanism.

## S2.equity_impact
Objection: The claim that residual selective places become "even more SES-skewed" is presented as mechanical and near-certain, but the mechanism does not establish that the same applicant pool persists as seats shrink. If shrinking places changes application behavior, non-state supply, or admission thresholds, the direction and size of residual skew are not mechanically derivable from "fewer seats" alone.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Downgrade this from "mechanical and near-certain" to a plausible risk, and specify the needed condition: residual skew increases if applicant composition remains high-SES and admission rules are unchanged.

## S3.implementation_steps
Objection: The implementation steps include "Civil society — document and track outreach to low-SES and Roma families by application uptake," but the scenario abolishes 6/8-year entry and postpones formal selection to age 14-15. Tracking "application uptake" is an S1-style admission-reform lever and is internally mismatched with S3's structural mechanism unless it refers to later age-14 admissions or school-choice monitoring.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Replace this step with monitoring enrolment flows, school-choice patterns, and non-state transfers by SES/Roma status during the transition.

## S4.expected_benefits
Objection: "Lowest structural and legislative disruption" is supported by "no Nkt. structural amendment required," but the mechanism also requires rigorous national curriculum standards, TEIP-style targeting, ring-fenced multi-year funding, and teacher recruitment incentives. The evidence supports lower structural disruption, not necessarily lower legislative or administrative disruption, because funding guarantees and national standards may still require legal or budgetary changes.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Narrow the benefit to "lowest disruption to the 6/8-year track structure" and separately flag that durable funding and standards rollout may still involve substantial legal-budgetary work.
