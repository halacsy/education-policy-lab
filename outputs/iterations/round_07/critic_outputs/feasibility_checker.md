# Critique: feasibility_checker

## S1.implementation_steps
Objection: The year 2-4 step to “negotiate and legislate maintainer-uniform admission rules covering church/foundation schools” is operationally under-specified and likely too compressed. The evidence base says maintainer fragmentation is strongly evidenced and legally consequential, while S1’s own anti-migration mechanism is only [evidence: contested]. The scenario treats binding non-state maintainers as a schedulable ministry task, but the cited evidence supports only the risk of migration and fragmentation, not the feasibility of securing uniform rules within that lead time.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Split this into a prior legal-governance phase with explicit Nkt. amendment pathway, maintainer consultation requirements, enforcement mechanism, and a fallback design if church/foundation schools cannot be bound by year 4.

## S2.implementation_steps
Objection: The step “Government + legislature — enact a multi-party pact / legislative lock” in year 1 is unsupported as an implementation capacity claim. The evidence supports that Poland’s reform was reversed and that political durability is a problem [evidence: moderate], but it does not support the availability of a Hungarian multi-party pact or a legal lock strong enough to bind successor governments. This is not merely uncertain; it is a load-bearing feasibility assumption presented as an executable first-year step.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Recast the pact as an uncertainty or precondition, and add a concrete alternative such as phased statutory caps with automatic reporting, budgetary earmarks, and reversal-cost mechanisms that do not depend on cross-party agreement.

## S3.implementation_steps
Objection: The teacher-supply sequence is internally infeasible: S3 requires a 10-year teacher-supply forecast before rollout, but also schedules differentiation retraining in shortage-worst schools during years 1-4. The implementation-planning evidence says acute maths/science teacher shortages make redistribution across comprehensive schools infeasible without degrading disadvantaged regions [evidence: strong], while S3 labels retraining throughput only an [assumption]. The source supports a major capacity constraint, not confidence that the hardest schools can be sequenced first on this timeline.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Make retraining throughput a hard gate after a regional workforce audit, delay shortage-worst district rollout until staffing thresholds are met, and specify annual teacher-release capacity so retraining does not reduce classroom coverage.

## S4.cost_categories
Objection: S4 identifies recurring targeted funding as the dominant cost but gives no administrative or teacher-capacity cost for delivering TEIP-style intensive support under Hungarian shortages. The Portugal evidence supports non-structural gains in a different institutional setting [evidence: moderate], while transfer to Hungary is explicitly an [assumption]. As written, the cost model counts money but not the specialist staff, school-improvement teams, diagnostic capacity, and monitoring bandwidth needed to turn funding into implementation.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Add explicit recurring capacity categories for intervention staff, diagnostic support, school-improvement coaching, evaluator workload, and local administrative burden, with feasibility gated against Hungarian teacher-supply and qualification data.
