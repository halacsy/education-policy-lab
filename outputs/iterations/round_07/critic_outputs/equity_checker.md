# Critique: equity_checker

## S1.equity_impact
Objection: The field correctly notes that non-applicants see no benefit, but it underplays the equity-negative implication: the reform may redistribute a tiny number of scarce selective seats among applicants while leaving the largest excluded group untouched. The evidence tags support current SES skew in admissions, not the claim that the reachable population includes disadvantaged pupils at meaningful scale; without application-rate evidence by SES and geography, the projected equity gain is unsupported.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add that the reachable population is limited to low-SES pupils who already apply, require evidence on low-SES application rates before claiming equity gains, and state that non-applicant disadvantaged pupils may remain the dominant excluded group.

## S2.equity_impact
Objection: The claim that residual selective places become “even more SES-skewed” is too strong as written. Current KSH/OH statistics support that selective tracks are already SES-skewed, but they do not by themselves prove that reducing seats mechanically worsens the SES composition; that depends on how applicant pools, cutoffs, geography, and non-state substitution respond. The risk is plausible, not “near-certain” on the cited evidence.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Downgrade “mechanical and near-certain” to a plausible high-risk outcome, and specify the missing evidence needed: SES composition of marginal admitted pupils under shrinking quotas and projected non-state absorption by SES.

## S3.equity_impact
Objection: The field says S3 has “the largest expected aggregate equity gain” despite the scenario’s own evidence status saying the Hungary-specific peer-effect mechanism is weak/contested and sorting migration may offset the reform. That comparative ranking is unsupported: cross-national delayed-tracking evidence supports possible inequality reduction, but not that S3 dominates S1, S2, or S4 under Hungary’s fragmented maintainer and school-choice conditions.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Replace the comparative claim with a conditional one: S3 has the largest potential equity gain if sorting migration is contained through choice-architecture and maintainer-transparency reforms; otherwise the net gain is indeterminate.

## S4.equity_impact
Objection: The field focuses on partial narrowing of the SES gradient but does not identify who is reached inside disadvantaged general schools and who is still left out. TEIP-style compensation may benefit pupils in targeted schools while missing disadvantaged pupils in non-targeted mixed schools, small rural schools, or schools whose disadvantage is hidden by aggregate indicators. The Portuguese evidence supports general improvement from targeted support, not accurate targeting under Hungary’s early-selection and fragmented-maintainer context.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Add a targeting-risk sentence specifying which disadvantaged pupils may be excluded by the funding formula, and require school-level and pupil-level disadvantage measures before claiming broad equity reach.
