# Critique: equity_checker

## S1.equity_impact
Objection: The field correctly says the non-selected 88–92% majority receives no direct benefit, but it still claims S1 “directly helps high-ability low-SES pupils” without confronting the likely exclusion of low-SES pupils who are not already identified as high-ability, well-informed, or able to reach the qualifying threshold. The evidence tags support SES skew and selection effects, not that a threshold-plus-lottery design will reach the most disadvantaged pupils rather than only a thin subset already near the current admissions margin.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Specify that S1 benefits only low-SES pupils who clear the qualifying threshold and have application access; add outreach, transport, information, and threshold-monitoring conditions, or downgrade the equity claim to a marginal access gain for near-threshold applicants.

## S2.equity_impact
Objection: The field says S2 “widens the pool exposed to mixed-ability lower-secondary,” but it does not identify who may be harmed during the decade-long transition. Cohorts in schools losing selective-track prestige or resources before general-school advanced programmes are functioning could face a degraded offer, especially in disadvantaged or rural areas. The evidence only moderately supports later tracking equity gains and weakly supports family retention; it does not support assuming transition cohorts are equity-positive.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Add a transition-equity condition: phase-down should proceed only where reinvested general-school programmes are operational before caps fall, with monitoring of disadvantaged/rural schools for course availability, teacher quality, and enrolment loss.

## S3.equity_impact
Objection: The equity-negative threshold focuses on top-SES exit, but it ignores a second equity-negative pathway: disadvantaged pupils could be concentrated in the weakest comprehensive schools if teacher shortages and catchment choice are not fixed first. The cited evidence supports delayed selection internationally, but the scenario itself marks teacher capacity, peer-effect asymmetry, and catchment reform as contested or assumed; the equity_impact field overstates system-wide reach unless it names these distributional failure modes.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add explicit equity gates for disadvantaged-region teacher supply, within-school ability grouping, and catchment enforcement, not only top-SES enrolment retention.

## S4.equity_impact
Objection: The field says S4 improves absolute outcomes for the non-selected majority in disadvantaged schools, but the evidence only supports targeted funding and curriculum mechanisms moderately in Portugal, not in Hungary under teacher shortages and selection at age 10/12. The field should not state this as an expected reach claim without saying that the lowest-capacity schools may be least able to convert funding into gains.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Recast the equity impact as conditional: S4 may improve outcomes where disadvantaged schools have staffing and implementation capacity; add a risk that the most disadvantaged schools receive funds but not effective support, leaving gaps intact or widening relative inequality.
