# Critique: equity_checker

## S1.equity_impact
Objection: The equity impact correctly says benefits are limited to low-SES pupils who already apply, but it leaves out Roma pupils and rural pupils as distinct groups despite the mechanism explicitly naming outreach to low-SES and Roma families. The field therefore understates a major equity-negative pathway: if applications do not rise among Roma and rural families, the reform can redistribute a small number of seats among existing applicants while leaving the most excluded groups unreached.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Specify that the reachable group is high-ability low-SES applicants already in the applicant pool, and add that Roma, rural, and non-applicant families remain largely unreached unless outreach produces verified application increases.

## S2.equity_impact
Objection: The claim that residual selective places become “even more SES-skewed” is too strong as stated. The evidence supports a mechanical reduction in state selective seats and a strong risk of non-state offset, but it does not establish that the remaining state selective seats necessarily become more SES-skewed; that depends on applicant composition, admissions rules, and whether high-SES demand exits to non-state schools. The equity-negative risk is real, but the source supports it as plausible, not “mechanical and near-certain.”
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Recast the residual-skew claim as a high-risk but unproven outcome, and distinguish it from the better-supported mechanical effect that more pupils remain in the common track.

## S3.equity_impact
Objection: The field says this scenario has the “largest expected aggregate equity gain,” but the evidence record only supports that delayed tracking generally reduces inequality and that Poland/Finland-style gains require bundled reforms. It does not support ranking S3 above all other scenarios once teacher shortage, political reversal, and non-state exit are included. The statement overclaims certainty on who benefits before resolving whether disadvantaged pupils receive the required teacher-quality and support investments.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Say S3 has the largest theoretical equity upside if teacher supply, support systems, and exit containment are solved, rather than the largest expected aggregate gain.

## S4.equity_impact
Objection: The field identifies that S4 leaves the sorting mechanism intact, but it does not say who is actually reached: pupils who remain in disadvantaged general schools. It also omits who is left out: disadvantaged pupils in non-targeted schools, pupils already diverted by early selection, and schools unable to use funds because of teacher shortages. Given the strong teacher-shortage evidence, the equity impact should treat under-delivery in the target schools as a central equity-negative risk, not just a general uncertainty.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add that benefits are concentrated among pupils in eligible disadvantaged general schools, while impact is weakened where staffing shortages prevent the compensatory package from reaching the intended pupils.
