# Critique: evidence_checker

## S1.goal
Objection: The goal tags "access to selective places is less strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families" as strong evidence from KSH/Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics, but the registry's `gimn_share [strong]` supports only the cohort share, ages, and central written admission mechanism. The high-SES/urban skew is supported at most indirectly by `school_choice [moderate]` and `pisa_escs [strong]`, not by the cited KSH/OH admission-statistics claim.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Downgrade the SES-skew admission claim to moderate and cite `school_choice`; keep KSH/OH only for the 8-12% cohort share and age-10/12 selection facts.

## S2.equity_impact
Objection: The claim that residual selective places becoming "even more SES-skewed" is "mechanical and near-certain" is stronger than the evidence warrants. The registry supports current early-selection share and sorting, but it does not prove that reducing seats necessarily worsens the SES composition of remaining seats; that depends on applicant behavior, non-state substitution, and admission rules.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Recast as a plausible risk, not a near-certain mechanical result, and tag it weak or moderate: "Residual places may become more SES-skewed if high-SES demand remains concentrated and admission rules are unchanged."

## S3.mechanism
Objection: The first mechanism bullet cites "OECD/Eurydice structural comparisons" as strong evidence that Hungary selects earlier than most OECD systems, but OECD/Eurydice is not in the curated registry. Under the registry rule, this is an unsupported registry-backed claim unless explicitly marked as model knowledge or tied to a listed source.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Either mark the OECD/Eurydice comparison as model knowledge/uncited background, or replace it with registry-backed claims: `gimn_share [strong]` for Hungary's age-10/12 selection and `tracking_inequality [strong]` for the cross-country tracking-inequality relationship.

## S4.expected_benefits
Objection: "Politically less disruptive to implement than S2 or S3" is tagged contested, but the supporting discussion cites only internal political-risk reasoning, not a registry-backed source. The registry does support `parent_attachment [moderate]` for opposition to curtailing selective gimnazium places, but it does not establish that a Portuguese-style compensation package would be politically easier or less disruptive in Hungary.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Downgrade to weak/model-knowledge or reframe narrowly: "May avoid the specific parent-attachment backlash associated with reducing selective places, but its fiscal and legitimization risks remain unsupported by direct Hungarian evidence."
