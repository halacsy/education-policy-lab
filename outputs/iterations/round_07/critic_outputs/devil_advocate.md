# Critique: devil_advocate
## S3.goal
Objection: The goal overclaims that moving toward delayed tracking is "associated internationally with lower SES-outcome gradients" as if this directly supports abolishing Hungary's 6-/8-year gimnazium entry points. The cited evidence supports a cross-national association and broad delayed-tracking pattern, but the scenario itself admits no Hungary-specific causal estimate and flags sorting migration through school choice/non-state maintainers. The core claim is therefore unsupported at the Hungary-specific policy level.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Recast the goal as a hypothesis: abolish early gimnazium entry to test whether delayed tracking can reduce Hungary's SES gradient, conditional on accompanying school-choice and maintainer-governance safeguards.

## S2.assumptions
Objection: The first assumption collapses the scenario: "Caps on state-maintained schools are not simply offset by non-state maintainers expanding selective intake" is directly contradicted by the mechanism's own strong evidence tag that governance fragmentation and maintainer expansion are well documented. If the offset is likely, the phase-down reduces only state-sector selection, not aggregate early selection.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Make non-state offset the central design problem: include binding or negotiated caps across maintainers, or explicitly downgrade the expected equity effect to state-sector-only.

## S4.mechanism
Objection: The mechanism claims compensation can "offset" early sorting, but the evidence tag is weak and says no country has demonstrated offsetting at Hungary's selection age of 10-12. The Portuguese evidence supports improvement through targeted funding and curriculum standards in Portugal, not neutralization of inequities produced by early selective tracking. This is not merely weak evidence; it fails to support the load-bearing transfer claim.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Replace "offset" with "partially mitigate downstream harms" and require a pilot that measures whether compensated general schools close SES-gradient gaps relative to matched controls.

## S1.mechanism
Objection: The assessment-redesign claim assumes a less preparation-sensitive admission exam will materially reduce high-SES advantage, but the cited contested evidence actually supports a different point: Kertesi & Kezdi and Horn indicate observed track advantage is mostly selection effect, not that exam format drives the SES skew. The source does not establish that changing the exam changes who applies, who is selected, or how families compensate through private preparation.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Separate exam redesign from access reform: treat exam changes as unproven unless paired with applicant outreach, SES-based weighting, and pilot evidence on coaching sensitivity and applicant-pool composition.
