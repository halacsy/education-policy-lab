# Critique: devil_advocate

## S1.assumptions
Objection: The scenario collapses on its own SES-indicator assumption. It proposes SES-conscious quotas or reweighting while explicitly stating that Hungary has no student-level SES register usable at application time and that proxies are gameable. The cited evidence supports SES-skewed intake, not an administratively usable targeting mechanism, so the core lever is unsupported rather than merely uncertain.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Recast SES-conscious reweighting as a pilot-only option conditional on a validated, auditable application-time indicator; otherwise limit the scenario to assessment redesign and outreach.

## S2.equity_impact
Objection: The claim that the equity impact is “directionally positive” is unsupported because the scenario’s dominant risk is that non-state maintainers absorb displaced selective demand. The evidence tag for maintainer offset is strong, while the peer-composition benefit is contested. On the scenario’s own evidence, the net effect could be zero or negative if selective capacity migrates rather than shrinks.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Change the equity impact to “indeterminate absent binding non-state intake limits” and make any positive equity claim conditional on verified non-state containment.

## S3.expected_benefits
Objection: “Largest aggregate equity gain of the four scenarios” is too strong. The evidence supports the general proposition that earlier tracking increases inequality, but the Hungary-specific scenario depends on three unresolved conditions: sorting not migrating to school choice/non-state schools, political durability across two electoral cycles, and teacher supply stabilization. Those are not side constraints; failure of any one defeats the expected benefit.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: State that S3 has the largest theoretical equity upside, not the largest expected equity gain, and condition expected gains on explicit containment, workforce, and durability thresholds.

## S4.mechanism
Objection: The mechanism’s load-bearing claim that targeted funding and standards can “offset” early sorting is unsupported. The Portugal evidence supports improvement without structural change in Portugal, not compensation for Hungary’s age-10/12 selective drain. The scenario itself admits no country has demonstrated offsetting at Hungary’s selection age, so “offset” should not be treated as an evidenced mechanism.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Replace “offset” with “mitigate downstream harms” and downgrade any equity mechanism claim to weak unless backed by Hungary-specific or early-selection-system evaluation evidence.
