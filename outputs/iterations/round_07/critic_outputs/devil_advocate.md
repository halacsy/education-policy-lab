# Critique: devil_advocate

## S3.mechanism
Objection: S3’s core mechanism rests on asymmetric peer effects, but the scenario admits that this claim is contested and unverified for Hungary. The strong international evidence supports delayed tracking reducing inequality, not the stronger claim that Hungarian mixed-ability classrooms will create net learning gains rather than redistribute peer advantage from previously selected pupils to non-selected pupils.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Reframe S3 as an equity-redistribution scenario unless Hungary-specific peer-effect evidence or a pilot result supports net positive asymmetric effects.

## S2.implementation_steps
Objection: The “multi-party pact / legislative lock” is treated as an implementation step, but the evidence cited actually shows the opposite risk: Poland’s reform was reversed despite earlier gains. Nothing in the scenario supports that Hungary can create a durable lock against electoral turnover, especially in a fragmented maintainer system.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Replace the pact/lock step with a weaker, evidence-aligned condition: phase-down proceeds only while annual parliamentary and maintainer commitments are renewed, with reversal risk treated as unresolved.

## S4.mechanism
Objection: The load-bearing claim that compensation can offset early-selection segregation is unsupported. Portugal’s TEIP and curriculum evidence supports gains without changing selection age in Portugal, but it does not support offsetting Hungary’s selection at ages 10/12; the scenario correctly says no analogue exists, so the offset claim should not be presented as a mechanism.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: State that S4 can improve absolute outcomes in general schools but cannot be expected to neutralise the equity deficit of early selection without direct Hungarian evidence.

## S1.expected_benefits
Objection: “No loss of value-added for admitted pupils” is stronger than the evidence supports. Evidence that current advantages are largely selection effects weakens the case for large track-specific value-added, but it does not prove that changing the admitted peer mix and admission rule leaves value-added unchanged.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Change this benefit to: “limited expected loss of average outcomes is plausible if current measured advantage is mostly selection, but this requires pilot validation.”
