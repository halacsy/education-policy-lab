# Critique: devil_advocate

## S1.goal
Objection: The core goal depends on reducing SES skew while preserving the selective-track structure, but the scenario admits that its main targeting device, an application-time SES indicator, may not be buildable or non-gameable. If the indicator fails, the quota mechanism collapses and the scenario becomes only an assessment redesign with weak evidence that coaching advantages would fall. The cited Kertesi & Kezdi 2013 and Horn 2013 evidence supports selection effects, not the feasibility of fair within-structure redistribution.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Recast S1 as conditional on a validated SES proxy pilot, or replace SES quotas with a targeting mechanism already administratively available and explicitly state what equity gain remains without quotas.

## S2.expected_benefits
Objection: The claimed peer-composition benefit is unsupported at the level needed for the scenario. Shrinking selective seats mechanically keeps some high-achievers in common schools, but the evidence tag is contested because the scenario has not shown that retained high-achievers improve outcomes for non-selected pupils in Hungarian upper-primary settings. Without that effect, S2 reduces early sorting only numerically while possibly making the remaining selective places more SES-skewed.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Downgrade the peer-composition benefit to an uncertainty, and make the expected benefit primarily “lower share sorted early,” not improved outcomes for pupils left in common schools.

## S3.assumptions
Objection: The scenario’s equity claim is load-bearing on the assumption that sorting will not simply migrate to school choice, residential sorting, and non-state maintainers, but the scenario’s own equity_impact and political_risks identify this as the largest risk with moderate evidence. The international delayed-tracking evidence supports an association between later selection and equity, but it does not prove that Hungary’s fragmented maintainer system can prevent substitute selection channels.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Make binding intake rules and enforceable maintainer constraints part of the mechanism, not a negotiated implementation add-on, and downgrade expected equity gains unless those constraints are politically and legally specified.

## S4.goal
Objection: The claim that Hungary can “neutralise” the equity effects of early selection downstream is unsupported. The Portuguese evidence supports attainment gains from targeted funding, standards, and clustering, but it does not support offsetting selection at age 10–12 because Portugal is not a comparator with Hungary’s early selective gimnázium partition. This is not merely weak evidence; the central offsetting claim has no demonstrated source support.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Replace “neutralise” with “partially mitigate,” and specify measurable downstream outcomes that TEIP-style funding can plausibly affect without claiming to offset the sorting mechanism itself.
