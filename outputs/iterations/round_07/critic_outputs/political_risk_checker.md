# Critique: political_risk_checker

## S1.political_risks
Objection: The maintainer-exemption risk is named but not entrenched. The scenario says maintainer-uniform admission rules mitigate migration to church/foundation schools, yet the evidence tag is explicitly [evidence: contested] and the overview says non-state maintainers can absorb selective capacity unless bound by law [evidence: strong]. As written, S1 treats a politically hardest veto point as a negotiable implementation step rather than a core reversal/failure risk.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: State that S1 is not viable unless maintainer-uniform admissions are enacted as binding law with sanctions before rollout; downgrade the mitigation from assumed solution to unresolved political precondition.

## S2.political_risks
Objection: The “multi-party pact / legislative lock” is asserted as a reversal mitigation, but the evidence base does not support that it would be durable in Hungary. The scenario itself marks durability of a multi-party pact as low confidence, and the Poland precedent supports the opposite: even evidence-backed structural reform can be reversed under electoral turnover. The political risk section therefore overstates the protection supplied by step 1.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Reframe the legislative lock as partial mitigation only, and specify harder entrenchment mechanisms such as supermajority provisions where legally possible, independent budget commitments, binding maintainer agreements, and compensation constituencies that make reversal costly.

## S3.political_risks
Objection: Reversal risk is underdeveloped for the scenario with the highest political capital demand. S3 names “reversal under electoral turnover despite measurable gains,” but offers no entrenchment design comparable to its scale: pilots, gates, and teacher protections do not stop a successor government from restoring early tracks before national rollout or after partial rollout. The Poland evidence cited as [evidence: moderate] supports a severe reversal risk, not a generic listed risk.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add a dedicated entrenchment design: phased legal closure tied to cohort guarantees, ring-fenced teacher-support funding, binding non-state caps before rollout, and stakeholder compensation for affected elite gimnazium constituencies.

## S4.political_risks
Objection: The scenario understates the political risk that compensatory funding becomes a legitimising cover for continued stratification. It notes selection skew persists and targeted funding is vulnerable, but does not connect these into the central reversal path: once early selection remains untouched, future governments can cut TEIP-style funding while preserving the selective structure, leaving the equity deficit worse protected than under structural reform. The cited budget-vulnerability evidence supports this stronger risk.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: State that S4 requires legally ring-fenced multi-year funding and independent equity triggers; if funding falls below the intensity threshold, the package should automatically reopen structural reform rather than continue as symbolic compensation.
