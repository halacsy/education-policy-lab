# Critique: political_risk_checker

## S1.political_risks
Objection: The maintainer-plurality risk is named but not operationalized as a reversal/avoidance pathway. The field says non-state maintainers may decline reformed rules [evidence: strong], but it does not spell out that this could leave affluent families with an exit route into unreformed selective admissions, making the state-sector reform politically easy to bypass rather than merely “undermining uniformity.”
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Recast the risk as an explicit avoidance channel: church/foundation schools and possibly elite state schools with informal screening preserve selective access unless the admission mandate binds all maintainers and bans substitute screening practices.

## S2.political_risks
Objection: The scenario understates the reversal risk from prestigious state gimnaziums by tagging exemptions as merely “plausible.” Given the scenario’s mechanism depends on a decade-long cap, organised exemption-seeking by elite schools is not a side risk; it is a direct route to hollowing out the cap. The cited political-feasibility evidence on prior organised opposition supports more than a speculative note.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Upgrade the exemption risk to evidence: moderate, and specify that exemptions, special-status programmes, or delayed implementation for flagship schools would create precedents that unravel the annual cap.

## S3.political_risks
Objection: The field identifies Poland-style reversal but gives no entrenchment design despite the evidence saying evidence of success did not prevent reversal. “Cross-party or constitutional insulation” appears in uncertainties, but the political-risk field does not state what institutional lock would be required before implementation. This leaves the scenario’s biggest durability condition unmitigated.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add a required entrenchment package: cross-party pact, statutory supermajority or constitutional protection where feasible, grandfathering guarantees, and binding maintainer rules before national scale-up.

## S4.political_risks
Objection: The funding durability risk is too weakly evidenced and too vaguely designed. The claim that ring-fenced equity funding is “the first cut” is admitted as asserted from finance inputs, but the scenario then relies on sustained multi-year funding as a core assumption without proposing any budgetary entrenchment. The source supports fiscal exposure, not the specific “first cut” claim.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Replace “first cut” with “highly exposed to annual budget retrenchment unless legally multi-year and formula-based,” and add a mitigation requiring statutory ring-fencing, indexed allocations, and public annual reporting.
