# Critique: political_risk_checker

## S1.political_risks
Objection: The reversal risk is underdeveloped. The field names middle-class mobilisation and union resistance, but it does not explain how a quota-based admission reform would survive the first legal, ministerial, or electoral challenge once high-SES families frame it as unfair exclusion from scarce selective seats. The cited moderate domestic-press/stakeholder evidence supports likely mobilisation, not durability of the reform.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add an explicit entrenchment design: statutory quota rules, transparent appeals limits, independent monitoring, and a cross-party or maintainer pact specifying that quotas cannot be annually revised by ministerial decree.

## S2.political_risks
Objection: The field correctly identifies non-state maintainer expansion as a defeating risk, but it understates the implication: without binding caps across church and foundation maintainers, the core cap mechanism is politically reversible in practice even if state schools comply. The cited school_network_planning/maintainer evidence supports capacity absorption by non-state providers, not merely a marginal implementation problem.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Recast this as a core viability condition, not one risk among others: specify legal instruments for applying caps to non-state maintainers, consequences for non-compliance, and whether public funding would be conditioned on respecting selective-seat limits.

## S3.political_risks
Objection: The reversal risk is named but not mitigated. Citing Poland 2016-2019 shows that measured gains do not protect a structural reform from political reversal; the scenario still lacks any entrenchment mechanism capable of surviving two electoral cycles, even though its assumptions admit this is required.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add concrete reversal barriers: multi-year grandfathering, locked curriculum/exam sequencing, budget commitments for comprehensive-school supports, stakeholder compensation for affected gimnázium communities, and a cross-party pact or staged audit trigger before full abolition.

## S4.political_risks
Objection: The budget vulnerability risk is too weakly specified for a scenario whose mechanism depends on recurring targeted funding. The evidence tag admits only weak expert estimates and contested magnitude; this supports identifying fiscal exposure, but not confidence that TEIP-style compensation would be politically sustained during consolidation. If funding is cut, the scenario legitimises early selection while removing the compensatory lever.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Make durability of targeted funding a gating condition: require ring-fenced multi-year appropriations, statutory allocation formulas for disadvantaged schools, independent publication of cuts, and an explicit fallback if funding drops below the level needed for compensation.
