# Critique: political_risk_checker
## S1.political_risks
Objection: The non-state maintainer opt-out risk is named but not matched with any entrenchment design. The field says opt-out would undermine uniform impact and tags governance fragmentation as strong evidence, but the scenario offers only “voluntary alignment” in implementation. Strong evidence of fragmented legal authority supports a stronger claim: voluntary negotiation is structurally inadequate unless paired with enforceable funding, accreditation, admissions-transparency, or public-subsidy conditions.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add a concrete entrenchment mechanism tying non-state participation to public funding, accreditation, or admission-data transparency, and downgrade expected reach if such levers are legally unavailable.

## S2.political_risks
Objection: The scenario understates reversal risk by treating the decade-long phase-down mainly as an electoral-cycle durability problem. The stronger risk is that shrinking state seats creates a concentrated, recurring annual constituency for repeal before benefits are visible. The Polish precedent supports reversibility under institutional and middle-class pressure, but does not by itself support confidence that gradualism lowers risk; gradualism may instead multiply veto points across ten admission cycles.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Recast the risk as recurring annual repeal pressure during each quota-reduction cycle, and add lock-in measures such as statutory quota schedules, automatic reductions, cross-party pacting, and transparent biennial equity reporting.

## S3.implementation_steps
Objection: The listed “choice-architecture and maintainer-transparency safeguards” are too vague to mitigate the scenario’s own largest political risk: middle-class and church/foundation-school flight. The evidence cited supports that sorting can relocate through school choice and maintainer plurality, but the implementation field does not specify enforceable controls over admissions, catchments, capacity expansion, or funding conditions. Without those, the abolition design lacks the entrenchment needed to prevent visible losses from generating backlash and partial reversal.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Specify the safeguards: common admissions rules across publicly funded maintainers, capacity controls on replacement selective tracks, mandatory SES/admission reporting, and sanctions or funding conditions for non-compliance.

## S4.political_risks
Objection: The budget-vulnerability risk is tagged weak because no Hungary-specific budget-cut history is cited, but the scenario’s mechanism depends on sustained high recurring funding. That means the political risk is not merely evidentiary weakness; it is a core design failure unless the funding is insulated. The source support only establishes a general asserted pattern, not Hungary-specific reversal probability, so the claim should not be treated as a side risk while the implementation assumes ring-fencing will solve it.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Either provide Hungary-specific evidence on education-targeted funding durability or redesign the package with statutory multi-year appropriations, automatic indexation, and independent reporting on cuts; otherwise mark the compensation mechanism as politically fragile.
