# Critique: assumption_checker

## S2.equity_impact
Objection: The claim that residual selective places become “even more SES-skewed” is asserted as “mechanical and near-certain,” but the cited evidence only establishes that existing intake is SES-skewed and that caps retain more pupils in the common track. It does not show that reducing seats with the same applicant pool necessarily increases SES skew; that depends on the admissions rule, applicant response, and whether marginal admitted pupils are lower- or higher-SES.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Downgrade this to an uncertainty or conditional risk: “Residual selective places may become more SES-skewed if admissions rules and applicant composition remain unchanged.”

## S3.expected_benefits
Objection: “Lower SES-achievement correlation over the long run without loss of mean performance” is tagged strong with Hanushek & Woessmann 2006, but the expert record says that comparative evidence supports the direction that early tracking harms equity, not that Hungary would achieve lower SES correlation without mean-performance loss under this package. The scenario also conditions benefits on teacher supply, non-state exit containment, and political durability, all unresolved.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Reclassify as moderate and conditional: “Comparative evidence suggests delayed tracking can reduce inequality without necessarily lowering mean performance, but Hungary-specific effects depend on teacher capacity and containment of substitute sorting.”

## S4.mechanism
Objection: The statement “Portugal raised PISA outcomes without structural change via TEIP and standards” over-attributes causality. The expert record gives Portuguese non-structural improvement only as moderately evidenced, and the synthesis says Portugal is a precedent, not proof that TEIP and standards caused the gains or that they transfer to Hungary’s much earlier selection context.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Change to: “Portugal’s PISA outcomes improved during a period of TEIP-style targeting and standards reform, but causal attribution and transferability to Hungary are uncertain.”

## S1.assumptions
Objection: The first assumption says “A workable SES indicator can be built at application time,” while the goal explicitly says no workable SES indicator has yet been demonstrated and the mechanism says no usable register exists and proxies are gameable. This is not merely an assumption; it is a direct contradiction embedded in the scenario’s feasibility logic.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Replace with a feasibility condition: “The reform cannot proceed with SES-conscious quotas unless an independently audited, legally usable, gaming-resistant SES proxy is first demonstrated.”
