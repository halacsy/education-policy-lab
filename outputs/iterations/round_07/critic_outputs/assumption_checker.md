# Critique: assumption_checker

## S2.assumptions
Objection: The claim that non-state offset is a "near-certain response" overstates the evidence. The cited record strongly supports governance fragmentation and the possibility that church/foundation maintainers could absorb displaced demand, but it does not establish certainty or quantify likely expansion capacity, parental switching rates, or legal/financial constraints on non-state growth.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Replace "near-certain response" with "material risk" or "plausible offset risk," and state that the scale of non-state expansion is unquantified.

## S2.equity_impact
Objection: The statement that residual selective places becoming more SES-skewed is "mechanical and near-certain" is unsupported. Fewer seats with unchanged admissions could intensify competition, but the direction of SES skew depends on applicant behavior, geographic distribution of remaining seats, non-state substitution, and any concurrent admissions changes. KSH/OH statistics support current skew, not the inevitability of worsened skew under quota reduction.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Recast this as a plausible risk: "Residual places may become more SES-skewed if demand and admissions behavior remain unchanged," and identify required monitoring data.

## S3.expected_benefits
Objection: "Alleviation of demographically driven rural school-network collapse" is too strong for the cited evidence. Demography and school-network experts support cohort shrinkage as an independent consolidation pressure, but abolishing selective tracks would not necessarily stabilize rural schools if population decline, teacher shortages, parental school choice, and non-state enrollment continue. The source supports possible mitigation of urban selective drain, not alleviation of rural network collapse.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Narrow the benefit to "may reduce selective outflow from some local schools," and remove the broader claim about alleviating rural school-network collapse unless supported by district-level enrollment modeling.

## S4.assumptions
Objection: The assumption that non-structural interventions are politically easier is imported from the portuguese_reform expert but contradicted by this scenario's own cost and risk profile: high recurring targeted funding, ring-fencing across budget cycles, and teacher-supply constraints. The expert record supports lower disruption from not changing tracking age, but not overall political ease or durability in Hungary.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Split the assumption: "less disruptive to families benefiting from current selection" versus "politically and fiscally difficult to sustain," and downgrade the evidence tag from expert-backed assumption to contested.
