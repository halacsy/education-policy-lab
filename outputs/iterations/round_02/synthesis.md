## Overview

The expert record addresses whether and how Hungary should reform early academic selection via the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system (entry at age 10–12). Experts converge on the descriptive picture: early selection is heavily SES-stratified and coexists with an exceptionally high socio-economic achievement gradient. They diverge sharply on causal attribution, the appropriate reform pathway (abolition, deferral, pilot, or non-structural targeted support), and whether structural change is politically and administratively viable. Demographic decline and maintainer fragmentation are treated as cross-cutting constraints. No expert claims Hungary-specific causal evidence isolating the track effect exists; several international analogues (Finland, Poland, Portugal) are offered with differing lessons.

## Disagreement map

- **international_comparison, equity_and_social_mobility, education_finance**: Early between-school selection should be delayed or moved within-school; the international causal evidence (Hanushek–Woessmann) and SES-gradient data indicate the system amplifies inequality without raising mean performance. Why: cross-country difference-in-differences and Hungarian value-added studies suggest the track advantage is largely a selection effect, so deferral reduces stratification at low performance cost.

- **finnish_reform (minority)**: Hungary should NOT adopt comprehensive schooling on the Finnish model unless bundled with costed teacher professionalization and integrated support. Why: the Finnish equity effect is entangled with teacher policy and support systems; structure-only transfer replicates a known failure mode.

- **portuguese_reform (minority)**: Hungary can reduce equity costs WITHOUT abolishing gimnaziums, via curriculum standardization, TEIP-style targeted support, and retention reduction. Why: Portugal achieved large PISA gains without raising selection age, showing a non-structural pathway exists provided targeted investment is substantial and sustained.

- **polish_reform**: Hungary should pilot delayed selection (to age 15) in selected regions with embedded monitoring and legitimacy-building. Why: Poland's 1999 reform correlated with PISA gains, but its 2016–2019 reversal shows reforms fail without sustained political coalition.

- **implementation_planning, school_network_planning**: Do not implement structural reform nationwide first; pilot mixed-ability lower-secondary in well-resourced districts alongside teacher recruitment. Why: teacher shortages are acute and geographically uneven, and premature nationwide change concentrates selection pressure and worsens regional inequality.

- **legal_and_governance (minority)**: Commission a Hungary-specific evaluation BEFORE committing to structural reform, negotiating voluntary alignment with non-state maintainers. Why: no curated Hungary-specific outcome data exists, and statutory anchoring plus maintainer fragmentation create high switching costs.

- **political_feasibility**: Durable reform requires either a supermajority absorbing 3–5 years of parent mobilization or a choice-protecting reframe that risks Poland-style reversal. Why: gimnazium places function as signalling/insurance assets for a mobilisable urban middle class.

- **hungarian_education_system**: Track removal alone will not reduce stratification without simultaneous school-choice and catchment reform. Why: catchment design, choice asymmetries, and maintainer diversity sustain between-school sorting independently of track existence.

- **demography**: Selection policy does not alter total cohort contraction (~25–30% by 2035–2040); it only determines who bears consolidation. Why: birth-cohort shrinkage is structurally locked in through 2040 and independent of selection policy.

## What the experts agree on

- Early selection at age 10–12 is heavily concentrated among high-SES, urban families. [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics]
- Hungary's SES-achievement gradient is exceptionally high (19–25% of PISA variance vs. 12–14% OECD). [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2012–2022 country notes]
- Much of the measured gimnazium advantage is a selection effect rather than causal value-added. [evidence: moderate — Horn 2013; Kertesi & Kezdi]
- Maintainer fragmentation (tankerület vs. church/foundation) prevents uniform top-down structural mandates. [evidence: strong — Public Education Act amendments; maintainer statistics]
- No Hungary-specific study isolates the causal contribution of track existence net of choice/catchment effects. [evidence: low/absent — noted across multiple agents]
- Structural reform carries high transition costs that dominate modest steady-state funding differences. [evidence: moderate — international reform comparisons; Hungarian vocational restructuring 2010–2015]
- Any structural reform requires a multi-year implementation timeline (6–8 years) due to statutory anchoring and enrolled-pupil guarantees. [evidence: strong — Nkt. structural requirement]

## Preserved uncertainties

- **Causal contribution of track existence to inequality, net of choice and catchment.** [confidence: low — no Hungarian study isolates this effect]
- **Decomposition of stratification across parental choice vs. catchment vs. track selection.** [confidence: low — studies show interaction but not isolated magnitudes]
- **Mechanism of SES sorting in gimnazium selection** (test performance vs. choice vs. gatekeeping). [confidence: low]
- **Magnitude of peer-effect losses for non-selected pupils.** [confidence: low — Hungarian data appears absent]
- **What actually drove the Finnish equity effect** (selection delay vs. teacher policy vs. support). [confidence: low]
- **Effect size of delayed tracking on Hungary specifically.** [confidence: low]
- **Longevity of any reform under Hungarian political conditions.** [confidence: low]
- **Teacher readiness/capacity for mixed-ability instruction.** [confidence: medium]
- **Transition-phase budget requirement (3–5 years); ~7–15 billion HUF estimate.** [confidence: low]
- **Elasticity of parental exit to private/international schools if selection changes.** [confidence: medium]
- **Interaction of reform with maintainer fragmentation and tankerület governance.** [confidence: low]
- **Regional heterogeneity of selection intensity and demographic contraction.** [confidence: medium]
- **Migration flows and fertility response affecting cohort size.** [confidence: low]
- **Portuguese TEIP-model transferability and dosage/cost in Hungary.** [confidence: low]
- **How much of Hungary's SES gradient is attributable to the gimnazium system vs. other factors.** [confidence: medium]
