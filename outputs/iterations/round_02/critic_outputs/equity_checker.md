# Critique: equity_checker

## S1.equity_impact
Objection: The field claims gains "accrue to high-ability low-SES pupils" but this is the group least reachable by admission-rule tweaks: low-SES high-ability pupils are precisely those who under-apply and self-select out before admission (aspiration/information barriers), so a fairer admission rule reaches the low-SES pupils who already apply — a more advantaged subset. The scenario needs an application-stage outreach mechanism, or the equity claim overstates who is actually reached. (Evidence tag on the benefit is [moderate], but the abroad-documented rule effects concern intake among *applicants*, not the composition of the applicant pool.)

## S2.equity_impact
Objection: The field concedes residual selective places become "even more SES-skewed" but treats this as secondary. For the highest-SES cohort this scenario is plausibly equity-negative during the decade-long transition: the political_risks field itself states scarcer places "intensify the admission arms race," which raises the SES gradient of who wins those places. So the transition years may worsen equity at the top gate precisely for the cohorts the reform is supposed to protect — this should be named as a distinct equity-negative window, not folded into a net-positive claim.

## S3.equity_impact
Objection: The field names middle-class exit to non-state schools as the risk but omits the within-school equity threat that its own mechanism relies on: "in-school differentiation (setting)" reproduces SES sorting *inside* the common school if setting tracks map onto SES (well documented for tuition-based setting). The equity_impact field should acknowledge that the differentiation mechanism [evidence: contested] can convert between-school sorting into within-school sorting, leaving the SES gradient partly intact even without exit.

## S4.equity_impact
Objection: The claim that the package "helps the disadvantaged majority where they actually are" assumes the targeting formula reaches them, but the assumptions field admits stigmatisation risk and the mechanism relies on premia moving experienced teachers *into* disadvantaged schools. If premia are insufficient (uncertainties field rates this confidence: medium), the equity_impact is not merely "partial" — it is near-zero for instruction quality, since the funding reaches buildings but not better teaching. The field overstates delivered equity by conflating funding allocation with realised benefit.
