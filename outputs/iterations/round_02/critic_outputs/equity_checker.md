# Critique: equity_checker

## S1.equity_impact
Objection: The claim of "moderately improves equity within selective tracks" rests on evidence tags (Kertesi & Kezdi 2013; Berényi-Berkovits-Erőss) that document existing sorting patterns, not the causal efficacy of admissions-quota mechanisms — the cited literature does not test what happens when quotas are imposed within a multi-maintainer system. More importantly, the field never addresses who is structurally excluded regardless of quota design: students in tankerületek with no local non-state alternative and no selective track nearby (deep-rural, often Roma-concentrated districts) gain nothing from reweighted admission scores, since the reform only reallocates *who* enters existing selective seats, not *where* seats or quality exist. The equity claim should be scoped to "urban/regional selective-track composition" rather than "equity" generally.

## S2.equity_impact
Objection: "Significantly improves equity in the long run" is a stronger claim than the evidence_status ("moderate") or the scenario's own mechanism support (evidence: moderate — OECD PISA notes; Kertesi & Kezdi 2013) justifies, and it ignores a distinct equity risk: within-school ability grouping. Comparative literature on delayed tracking (Jakubowski et al., cited in S3) shows that when between-school selection is removed, schools frequently reintroduce streaming/ability grouping internally, reproducing SES sorting inside the "comprehensive" primary track. This scenario's equity_impact never considers whether reduced *between*-school sorting could be offset by increased *within*-school sorting, which would leave the most disadvantaged pupils no better off.

## S3.equity_impact
Objection: The claim to "maximize educational equity" conflates SES-gradient reduction (well supported by Hanushek & Woessmann and Pekkarinen et al., which measure general socio-economic variance in achievement) with Roma-specific school segregation, which is a distinct and well-documented equity problem in Hungary operating largely *within* the general-school system (residential and within-primary segregation), not solely via the 6/8-year gimnazium pathway. None of the cited evidence (international tracking-age studies) speaks to Roma segregation dynamics, so the scenario cannot claim to address "equity" comprehensively — it only addresses the elite-track dimension of sorting, leaving the largest and most severe segregation channel unexamined. This is an unsupported extension of the cited evidence, not merely a weak one.
