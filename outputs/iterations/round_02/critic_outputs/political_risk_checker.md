# Critique: political_risk_checker

## S1.political_risks
Objection: The listed risks omit the most consequential entrenchment failure for this scenario: an SES-aware admission rule (weighted lottery / district quotas) is trivially reversible by decree, since S1 requires no structural change and leaves the selective tracks intact. A successor administration hostile to "punishing talent" (the very framing named as opposition) can revert the admission decree in a single cycle at near-zero cost, undoing intake-composition gains within one or two admission rounds. The field names opposition but ignores that this scenario has the weakest entrenchment of the reversible options — no felmenő guarantee, no sunk cost, no constituency created — yet the risk section treats reversibility as absent.

## S2.political_risks
Objection: The reversal risk is named ("a successor government can freeze or reverse the trajectory cheaply, Polish precedent") but no entrenchment design is offered to counter it, and the scenario's own uncertainties field concedes the mitigation ("cross-party pact or statutory entrenchment attempts") is low-confidence. This is a stated-but-unmitigated reversal risk: a decade-long trajectory whose equity payoff arrives late is maximally exposed to mid-course reversal, yet the political_risks field proposes no locking mechanism (e.g., statutory floor on freed-capacity reinvestment, cross-party pact as a precondition to enactment). Naming the Polish precedent without a design response understates the risk.

## S3.political_risks
Objection: The field asserts "Polish-style reversal after government change destroys sunk transition costs" but overstates the deterrent value of sunk costs as implicit entrenchment. The Polish 2016 reversal itself demonstrates that a completed structural reform with large sunk transition costs was reversed — sunk costs did not protect it. The scenario therefore should not treat the decade of transition investment as self-entrenching; it needs an explicit entrenchment mechanism (constitutional/qualified-majority anchoring, or a genuine cross-party pact secured before phase-out begins, not merely "attempted" as the implementation_steps hedge). As written, the strongest-equity scenario carries the strongest reversal exposure with no locking design named.

## S4.political_risks
Objection: The two named risks (budget vulnerability, legitimising selection) miss the structurally decisive entrenchment asymmetry: because S4 is "additive" and touches no structure, it creates no protected constituency and no institutional lock — its funding lines are precisely the reversible category the field admits are "first cut in fiscal consolidation." The scenario claims (expected_benefits) it is "implementable under any government [evidence: strong]," but easy reversibility is the flip side of easy implementability: a package with low political capital cost is also one no future government pays a price to dismantle. The political_risks field should register that "implementable under any government" and "abandonable by any government" are the same property.
