# Critique: devil_advocate

## S2.evidence_status
Objection: The mechanism labels the sorting reduction "strong [evidence: strong]" and rests the scenario on it, but this conflates a definitional truth with a causal one. Capping entry places lowers the *count* of pupils sorted at 10/12 only if displaced demand does not reappear as sorting elsewhere — via school choice between general schools, relocation, or non-state entry. The scenario's own equity_impact and S3's uncertainty on "sorting migration" concede this. So the load-bearing claim is not "strong" but tautological-at-best: it measures inputs (places offered), not the outcome (early sorting of the cohort). The real evidence status for *sorting reduction as an equity outcome* is weak.

## S4.expected_benefits
Objection: "Improved outcomes in disadvantaged general schools within 5 years (Portuguese trajectory) [evidence: moderate]" is contradicted by the scenario's own uncertainty field, which states "no country has demonstrated it at Hungary's selection age." Portugal did not compensate for selection this early; it operated a later-tracking system. Transplanting the Portuguese effect size onto a system where the highest-ability, highest-SES pupils have *already exited* the disadvantaged schools is precisely the untested step. The benefit tag should be "weak/unsupported for the Hungarian selection age," not "moderate" — the moderate evidence supports the components in a *different structural context*.

## S3.assumptions
Objection: The assumption "teacher capacity for differentiated instruction can be built at national scale within the transition window" is the true single point of failure, yet it is stated as a precondition rather than tested. The mechanism explicitly makes in-school differentiation the *substitute* for between-school tracks ("substitutes... if teachers are trained for it"). If this assumption fails, the scenario does not merely underperform — it produces common schools with worse instruction for everyone and no offsetting equity gain, i.e., strictly worse than baseline. The scenario treats this as one assumption among several; it is the hinge on which the entire equity case swings, and no evidence tag or capacity estimate is attached to whether national-scale retraining has ever succeeded on this timescale.

## S1.assumptions
Objection: "Elite gimnaziums comply rather than route selection through informal channels" is listed as an assumption and separately flagged as a low-confidence uncertainty, but the scenario's mechanism and expected benefits are calculated *as if compliance holds*. Given the scenario itself names church/private maintainer exemption as a political risk and informal-selection as the top uncertainty, the "more balanced intake within 2-3 cycles [evidence: moderate]" benefit assumes away the very failure mode most likely to occur. The evidence abroad cited for admission-rule effects presumes enforceable single-authority admissions — a condition Hungary's fragmented maintainer landscape does not satisfy — so the "moderate" transfer is unsupported.
