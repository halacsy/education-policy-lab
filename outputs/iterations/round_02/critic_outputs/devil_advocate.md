# Critique: devil_advocate

## S1.mechanism
Objection: The "moderate" evidence tag misrepresents its sources. Kertesi & Kezdi 2013 and the Berényi–Berkovits–Erőss school-choice studies document *that* sorting occurs and *how* parental choice drives it; neither evaluates a quota-based admissions intervention or score-reweighting scheme. There is no cited study showing that imposing equity quotas or reweighted exam scores actually reduces sorting inside a fragmented, multi-maintainer system — the mechanism's efficacy claim is essentially untested, not moderately supported. The tag should read "weak/untested policy lever, moderate descriptive baseline."

## S2.assumptions
Objection: The assumption "non-state school maintainers can be restricted from absorbing the phased-out selective capacity" is directly contradicted by the synthesis's own hungarian_education_system disagreement note, which states that constraining state selective tracks pushes selective demand into church/foundation schools. Nothing in S2's implementation steps gives the state legal power to compel this restriction — "renegotiate agreements with church maintainers" is voluntary, and the uncertainty section itself rates the maintainers' willingness at only medium confidence. Without enforceable non-state restriction, the scenario's "significant long-term equity improvement" claim is unsupported, since the entire mechanism assumes away the leakage channel that a rival scenario treats as near-certain.

## S3.evidence_status
Objection: Tagging overall evidence_status "strong" conflates two different claims. The strong/moderate citations (Hanushek & Woessmann 2006, Pekkarinen et al. 2009, Jakubowski et al. 2016) support the general cross-national finding that postponing tracking reduces SES effects on achievement — they say nothing about Hungary specifically. The scenario's own assumptions list flags, as unverified assumptions, that Hungary's institutional/cultural context resembles the studied countries, that Hungarian teachers can handle the heterogeneity Finland's could, and that Poland is structurally comparable. These are exactly the links needed to turn "strong general evidence" into "strong evidence this will work in Hungary," and they carry no evidence tag at all — they're bare assumptions. Labeling the scenario's evidence_status "strong" therefore overstates confidence in the actual recommended policy for Hungary; it should be split into strong (general mechanism) / weak (domestic applicability).
