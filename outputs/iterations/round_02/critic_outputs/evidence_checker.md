# Critique: evidence_checker

## S2.mechanism
Objection: The cap-driven sorting reduction is tagged [evidence: strong], but "strong" overstates what a mechanical identity supports. A cap on entry places only guarantees fewer pupils enter selective tracks by administrative arithmetic; it does not evidence that early *sorting* (the SES-outcome mechanism of concern) falls, since displaced high-SES demand can re-sort via school-choice within general schools or exit to non-state maintainers — a channel the scenario's own equity_impact and S3 flag. A mechanical count is not empirical evidence about the sorting outcome; the tag conflates definitional certainty with evidential strength.

## S3.mechanism
Objection: The asymmetric-peer-effects claim is tagged [evidence: contested], which is honest, but the first bullet's "Later between-school selection weakens the SES-performance link" is tagged [evidence: strong] on "cross-country tracking evidence." That cross-country literature (e.g., PISA-based tracking-age comparisons) is correlational and confounded by whole-system differences; it supports a *direction* claim at best, not a "strong" causal warrant for a Hungarian phase-out. The evidence_status field itself concedes "Hungarian implementation capacity are genuinely disputed" — inconsistent with a "strong" tag on the headline mechanism.

## S4.expected_benefits
Objection: "Politically inexpensive; implementable under any government [evidence: strong]" is a political-feasibility prediction, not an empirically evidenced benefit. Nothing in the cited Portuguese (TEIP) source speaks to Hungarian cross-government durability; indeed the scenario's own political_risks ("first cut in fiscal consolidation") and uncertainties ("Durability of the budget line... confidence: low") directly contradict a "strong" tag. The evidence grade and the scenario's own uncertainty assessment cannot both stand.

## S4.mechanism
Objection: The mechanism states targeted resources "improved outcomes in Portugal without changing selection age [evidence: moderate]," but Portugal does not select between schools at 10/12 as Hungary does — so the Portuguese case cannot evidence offsetting *early selection* effects. The evidence_status field concedes exactly this ("whether they can offset selection as early as Hungary's is the open question"), which means the moderate tag applies to Portuguese outcome gains generally, not to the offsetting-early-selection claim the mechanism actually makes. The tag is attached to a stronger claim than the source supports.
