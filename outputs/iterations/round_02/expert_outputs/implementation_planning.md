# Expert analysis: implementation_planning

## Findings (evidence)

- Hungary's teacher shortage is acute and geographically uneven, with science and maths disproportionately affected. [evidence: strong — OECD Education at a Glance; PSZ/PDSZ surveys]
- The teaching workforce is ageing; replacement cohorts are smaller. [evidence: strong — same sources]
- Disadvantaged regions face the most severe teacher scarcity. [evidence: strong — same sources]
- Any structural reform to selection or secondary tracks will require teacher reallocation or retraining. [evidence: strong — logical inference from above]
- Per-pupil funding differences between track types are modest in steady state; transition costs (retraining, building repurposing, administration) dominate reform budgets. [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates]

## Interpretation

Early academic selection systems (6- and 8-year gimnazium entry at ages 10–12) require either:
1. Parallel teaching capacity at entry points, or
2. Rapid triage and differentiation within schools.

Both create concentrated demand for qualified maths/science teachers precisely where supply is tightest. Moving away from early selection toward comprehensive entry followed by later tracking *could* reduce this bottleneck by deferring specialization, but only if:
- Secondary schools can absorb mixed-ability cohorts without requiring additional teachers in core subjects (unlikely), or
- The reform includes funded teacher development and recruitment specifically for comprehensive lower-secondary.

The transition period (3–5 years) will be expensive: staff retraining, facility conversion, curriculum redesign, and temporary overstaffing during cohort overlap. Current budgetary evidence is weak, so cost predictability is low.

## Assumptions

- Teacher supply elasticity is low in the short to medium term (2–7 years). [assumption]
- Existing per-pupil funding formulas would apply to any new structure without renegotiation. [assumption]
- Disadvantaged regions cannot easily attract additional qualified teachers through wage incentives alone. [assumption]

## Position

Hungary should not implement structural reform to early selection without first **piloting mixed-ability lower-secondary in one or two well-resourced districts, concurrent with a regional teacher-recruitment and retention programme**; premature nationwide change will concentrate selection pressure into fewer, more competitive entry points and worsen regional inequality.

## Uncertainties

- **What is the actual transition-phase budget requirement (3–5 years)?** [confidence: low — would be reduced by: detailed costing of teacher retraining, facility repurposing, and temporary staffing overlaps in pilot districts]
- **How much of the teacher shortage is due to early tracking, vs. wage, working conditions, and demographic decline?** [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: comparative analysis of teacher turnover and recruitment in early-selection vs. comprehensive systems in similar economies (e.g., Slovakia, Czechia)]
- **What is the causal link between 6/8-year gimnazium and equity outcomes?** [confidence: low — would be reduced by: longitudinal tracking of cohorts entering each system, controlling for prior attainment and family SES]
- **Can disadvantaged regions absorb reform coordination costs during a teacher shortage?** [confidence: low — would be reduced by: administrative capacity audit and baseline data on district-level reform implementation success in recent years]
