# Expert analysis: Early academic selection and 6/8-year gimnazium system in Hungary

## Findings (evidence)

- The 6- and 8-year gimnazium (secondary grammar school) forms are statutory anchored in the Public Education Act (Nkt.) and maintainer agreements; structural change requires legislative amendment. [evidence: strong — Nkt. analysis]

- Any phase-out or restructuring must include multi-year notice periods and transitional guarantees for pupils already enrolled, creating a minimum 6–8 year implementation timeline. [evidence: strong — Nkt. structural requirement]

- School maintenance is fragmented: state schools operate under state school district centres (tankerület) since 2013; churches, foundations, and private entities maintain a growing share of schools (precise current proportion unknown). [evidence: strong — Public Education Act amendments; maintainer statistics]

- Fragmented maintenance means uniform structural mandates cannot be imposed top-down on non-state maintainers without negotiation or legislative conflict. [evidence: moderate — logical consequence of maintainer plurality]

- Early selection (typically at age 10–11 for 6/8-year entry) is contested on equity grounds internationally: research shows it correlates with socioeconomic stratification and reduced social mobility in tracked systems. [evidence: moderate — OECD education policy literature; however Hungary-specific impact studies not cited in curated sources]

- No curated source provides Hungary-specific outcome data on the 6/8-year system's effect on achievement, equity, or labour market outcomes. [evidence: weak — absence of evidence in curated registry]

## Interpretation

The policy question presents a governance and equity tension:

1. **Structural lock-in**: Legislative and contractual anchoring creates high switching costs. Reform requires statutory amendment, multi-stakeholder negotiation, and pupil-level safeguards.

2. **Maintainer fragmentation**: Non-state sectors (churches, foundations) cannot be uniformly mandated; policy must either negotiate sector-by-sector or tolerate heterogeneity.

3. **Equity vs. autonomy**: Early selection serves institutional choice and parental preference but creates tracking that may amplify inequality. No Hungary-specific evidence quantifies this trade-off.

## Assumptions

- That "early academic selection" refers to entry at age 10–11 into 6/8-year tracks, distinguishing them from comprehensive 4-year secondary options. [assumption]
- That the curated sources represent the core legal/governance constraints but not the full empirical or international comparative picture. [assumption]
- That "what should Hungary do" invites both feasibility (legal/governance) and normative (equity/access) framing. [assumption]

## Position

Hungary should commission a Hungary-specific evaluation of the 6/8-year system's effects on equity and labour market outcomes before committing to structural reform; in parallel, negotiate with non-state maintainers on voluntary alignment toward later selection or mixed-track models, while preserving statutory guarantees for currently enrolled pupils.
