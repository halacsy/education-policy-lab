# Expert analysis: Early Selection and Secondary Grammar Schools in Hungary

## Findings (evidence)

- Poland postponed academic tracking from age 11 to age 15 via its 1999 reform, eliminating early selection into separate secondary pathways. [evidence: strong — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD]

- PISA reading and mathematics scores in Poland rose substantially between 1999 and 2012, with measurable gains occurring in the post-reform cohorts. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA data cited in Jakubowski et al. 2016]

- Decomposition analyses attribute a portion of Poland's PISA gains to the delayed-tracking mechanism itself, though the effect size remains contested and is confounded with concurrent socioeconomic and curriculum changes. [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016]

- Poland reversed the delayed-tracking system in 2016–2019, reinstating early selection pathways, primarily for political and ideological reasons rather than empirical performance data. [evidence: moderate — OECD policy documentation; timing and stated rationales]

- Delayed tracking typically reduces inequality in standardized test outcomes (between-school variance) but does not guarantee gains in absolute attainment, especially if quality or teacher capacity remains unequal across schools. [evidence: moderate — broader European tracking literature; not Hungary-specific]

## Interpretation

Poland's case demonstrates that institutional design (timing of selection) correlates with test performance gains in a middle-income EU context, and that such gains can be reversed by political decisions divorced from performance evidence. The 2016–2019 reversal signals that durability of equity-oriented reforms depends on sustained political coalition and public legitimacy, not empirical superiority alone. Hungary's 6- and 8-year grammar school system (early selection at age 10–12) mirrors the pre-1999 Polish model; the Polish trajectory suggests both measurable short-term gains from unification and long-term vulnerability to reversal.

## Assumptions

- That PISA gains in Poland are causally attributable to delayed tracking rather than confounded improvements in teacher training, curriculum breadth, or socioeconomic conditions. [assumption]

- That Hungary's institutional context (education budget, teacher expertise, regional inequality) is sufficiently similar to Poland's to allow policy transfer. [assumption]

- That test-score gains are the primary legitimate policy goal; distributional equity and reduced inequality between regions/schools are treated as co-equal objectives. [assumption]

## Position

Hungary should pilot a delayed-selection model (postponing grammar school entry to age 15) in selected regions, because Poland's evidence shows measurable learning gains and reduced inequality, but should simultaneously build political legitimacy through inclusive dialogue and embed performance monitoring, because political reversal negated Poland's gains.

## Uncertainties

- **Effect size of delayed tracking on Hungary specifically**: Polish PISA gains are documented, but their magnitude relative to Hungarian baseline performance and the causal weight of selection timing remain unknown. [confidence: low — would be reduced by: dedicated quasi-experimental analysis of Hungarian cohorts or matched regional pilots]

- **Longevity of reform under Hungarian political conditions**: Poland's reversal occurred within 17 years; whether Hungary's political economy makes reform more or less durable is unquantified. [confidence: low — would be reduced by: comparative analysis of Hungarian education reform sustainability and current legislative support for equity measures]

- **Teacher readiness for mixed-ability instruction in delayed-selection classrooms**: Poland's success assumes adequate training and resources; Hungary's current capacity is not documented in curated sources. [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: workforce survey and pilot-phase fidelity monitoring]

- **Interaction with vocational/technical pathways**: Delayed selection affects grammar schools; effects on and compatibility with parallel vocational tracks remain unspecified. [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: Poland–Hungary sectoral mapping and outcome data for non-academic routes]
