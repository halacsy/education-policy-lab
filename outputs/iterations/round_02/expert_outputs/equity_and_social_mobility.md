# Expert analysis: equity_and_social_mobility

## Findings (evidence)

- Hungary exhibits exceptionally strong SES gradients in academic outcomes: socio-economic status explains 19–25% of reading and maths score variance in PISA, compared to 12–14% OECD average. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2012–2022 country notes]

- Early selection into 6- and 8-year gimnazium tracks (at age 10 or 12) enrolls 8–12% of cohorts; admission is heavily concentrated among high-SES, urban families via centralised written examination. [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics]

- Hungarian value-added research shows that the measured achievement advantage of early-selective tracks is predominantly a selection effect rather than causal value-added; pupils admitted to gimnazium would likely outperform even in non-selective tracks. [evidence: moderate — Horn (2013); Kertesi & Kezdi school-effect studies]

## Interpretation

The system functions as an inequity amplifier. Early selection at age 10–12 mechanically sorts high-SES pupils into advantaged tracks, locking in distributional inequality before puberty. The modest independent effect of the gimnazium track itself means the inequality gain is purchased largely through *selection*, not through superior pedagogy. This leaves two distributional harms: (1) non-selected pupils suffer peer-effect losses (removal of higher-ability/motivated peers and reduced classroom composition diversity); (2) selected pupils' advantage reflects parental wealth and test-prep access, not merit alone, thereby reducing social mobility.

## Assumptions

- [assumption] The causal effect estimates from Hungarian value-added studies are robust to unobserved confounding (e.g., unmeasured family investment or peer quality).
- [assumption] The 8–12% enrollment figure is stable across recent cohorts and reflects consistent selection intensity.
- [assumption] PISA SES gradient measures survey-wave sampling; trends may differ from administrative enrollment data.

## Position

Hungary should narrow or delay early academic selection below age 14 and strengthen non-selective secondary tracks through targeted peer-diversity and resource-balancing measures, because the evidence shows current early selection amplifies SES gradients without proportional value-added returns and thereby reduces social mobility.

## Uncertainties

- **What is the magnitude of peer-effect losses for non-selected pupils after removal to early-select tracks?** [confidence: low — would be reduced by: longitudinal value-added studies tracking non-selected cohorts before/after introduction of selection; Hungarian data appears absent]

- **How much of the SES gap in selection success reflects test-prep inequality vs. underlying prior achievement?** [confidence: low — would be reduced by: audit studies or analysis of admission exam scores net of prior standardised test performance; currently unclear from curated sources]

- **What is the counterfactual social mobility rate under delayed or abolished early selection?** [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: comparative case studies (e.g., Germany länder reforms, Finland late-selection system); no Hungarian quasi-experimental evidence cited]

- **Does selection intensity vary substantially by region (urban/rural) and does this modify inequality conclusions?** [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: stratified KSH analysis of admission by region and SES quartile]
