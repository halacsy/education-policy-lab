# Expert analysis: school_network_planning

## Findings (evidence)

- Annual births fell from ~125k (early 1990s) to ~85–95k; cohort shrinkage forces school-network consolidation decisions in the 2020s–2030s regardless of selection policy. [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks]

- Since 2013 most state schools are run by state school district centres (tankerület); churches and foundations maintain a growing share of schools, complicating any uniform structural mandate. [evidence: strong — Public education act amendments; maintainer statistics]

- Early selection into 6- and 8-year grammar schools (typically at age 10–12) concentrates high-performing pupils, leaving primary and mainstream secondary schools with narrower ability ranges and smaller revenue per pupil in consolidation scenarios. [evidence: moderate — comparative education research on tracked systems (Germany, Austria); Hungarian school funding follows pupil headcount]

- Commuting tolerance varies sharply by region: urban agglomerations (Budapest, Debrecen, Szeged) absorb 30–60 min commutes; rural and small-town families tolerate 20–40 min; beyond that, participation drops. [evidence: moderate — Hungarian mobility surveys; OECD PISA contextual data]

- Selective grammar schools generate measurable peer-effect and credential advantages for admitted pupils; non-admitted pupils in resource-constrained secondaries show no compensatory gain. [evidence: moderate — TIMSS/PISA trend analysis; limited Hungarian longitudinal data]

## Interpretation

Cohort shrinkage will eliminate ~15,000–25,000 secondary places per year by 2030. Selection policy cannot postpone this math. The real choice is whether to:

1. **Consolidate around selective hubs** (keep 6/8-year schools, close low-demand mainstream secondaries): Reduces per-pupil overhead but leaves non-selective schools under-resourced and spatially stranded in rural zones.

2. **Shift to common lower secondaries + deferred selection at age 14–16**: Spreads consolidation pain more equitably, but requires retraining staff and risks losing elite pupils to private/international pathways.

The maintainer fragmentation (state districts vs. church/foundation schools) means no single authority can force uniform closure. Selective schools, often prestigious and often church-run, will survive consolidation; mainstream state schools will shrink.

## Assumptions

- Pupil mobility follows current patterns; policy does not shift family/employment geography. [assumption]
- Selection criteria remain entrance exams and prior grades; no radical shift to lottery or portfolio-based admission. [assumption]
- Funding formulas remain headcount-based; no ring-fencing for disadvantaged secondaries. [assumption]

## Position

Hungary should decouple selection policy from network consolidation: phase selection to age 14+ (not 10–12) to allow common lower secondaries to stabilize enrollment, then apply rigorous closure criteria to mainstream schools only if maintainers consent, while protecting rural access via transport subsidies or satellite campuses.

## Uncertainties

- **Regional viability thresholds for secondary schools**: At what cohort size do secondaries become economically or educationally non-viable? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: detailed cost–quality audits in shrinking regions]

- **Impact of selection policy on private-school migration**: Do non-admitted pupils migrate to private schools in significant numbers, or accept mainstream state placement? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: 5-year panel tracking of non-admitted pupils' enrollment choices]

- **Maintainer responsiveness to consolidation mandates**: Will churches/foundations cooperate with closure or resist, fragmenting the network further? [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: interviews with maintainer leadership; analysis of past merger/closure negotiations]

- **Peer-effect magnitude in Hungarian data**: How much of grammar-school advantage is selection bias vs. peer effect? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: matched longitudinal cohort analysis comparing admitted and near-miss pupils]
