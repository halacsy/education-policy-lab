# Expert analysis: international_comparison

## Findings (evidence)

- Between-school tracking introduced or intensified at younger ages causally increases socio-economic gradients in achievement without raising mean performance in cross-country difference-in-differences designs. [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006, Economic Journal]

- Hungary's socio-economic gradient in PISA reading and mathematics is 19–25% of score variance explained by SES, substantially above the OECD average of 12–14% across 2012–2022 cycles. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA country notes 2012–2022]

- The 6- and 8-year gimnazium system constitutes early between-school tracking (age 11–13 entry), concentrating advantaged students in selective streams while consigning lower-SES peers to vocational or general tracks. [evidence: moderate — Hungarian education system structure; international tracking typology (Brunello & Checchi 2007)]

- Post-2012 tracking reforms in several European systems (Germany: Länder-level tracking delays; Austria: some Länder experimentation with later selection) show modest reductions in SES gradient when combined with within-school support, but effects are context-dependent and modest. [evidence: moderate — European education policy reviews; not included in curated sources—flagged as model knowledge]

## Interpretation

Hungary's exceptionally high SES-achievement gradient coexists with early, rigid tracking via the gimnazium system. The Hanushek–Woessmann causal evidence indicates that the 6–8 year system is likely *amplifying* rather than mitigating inequality. The gradient is not driven primarily by selection into elite schools (which is limited), but by the structural bifurcation of the secondary market, which sorts lower-SES students into lower-status tracks at age 11–13—a period when parental resources and early cognitive gaps are still malleable.

The question for Hungary is not whether tracking per se is harmful (the evidence is mixed by context), but whether *early, between-school* tracking, in a system with high SES sorting, can be reformed without dismantling the gimnazium brand.

## Assumptions

- The PISA SES gradient in Hungary is not primarily driven by measurement error or test bias. [assumption]
- The 6–8 year gimnazium system functions as effective between-school tracking (i.e., sorting occurs and is durable). [assumption]
- Causal evidence from other systems transfers meaningfully to Hungary, subject to institutional and labour-market differences. [assumption]

## Position

Early entry into the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system is likely exacerbating Hungary's already exceptional socio-economic inequality in achievement; international evidence suggests delaying or broadening selection, or moving selection within schools rather than between schools, would reduce this gradient without sacrificing mean performance, though context-specific Hungarian evidence is absent.

## Uncertainties

- **What is the actual mechanism of SES sorting in the gimnazium system?** Is selection driven by test performance (confounded with SES), family choice, or gatekeeping? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: qualitative case studies of selection processes and parental choice behaviour in Hungarian cities]

- **What would be the labour-market and prestige consequences of delaying or broadening gimnazium entry?** [confidence: low — would be reduced by: employer surveys and long-term graduate tracking by track type and entry age]

- **How much of Hungary's SES gradient is attributable to the gimnazium system vs. other factors (neighbourhood segregation, preschool access, teacher quality)?** [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: Hungarian education production-function study decomposing variance sources]

- **Are there within-country variation studies (e.g., across Hungarian regions or cohorts) that provide quasi-experimental evidence on tracking effects specific to Hungary?** [confidence: low — would be reduced by: systematic review of Hungarian education research and administrative data studies]
