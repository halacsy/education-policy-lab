# Expert analysis: political_feasibility
## Findings (evidence)

- Urban middle-class parents treat 8- and 6-year gimnazium places as essential mobility/insurance assets; prior attempts to curtail these structures faced sustained, well-organised resistance. [evidence: moderate — Domestic press and stakeholder analyses, 1990s-2010s]

- Poland's 1999 delayed-tracking reform (age 15 instead of age 11) correlated with significant PISA gains by 2012; analyses attribute part of improvement to postponed selection. [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD]

- Poland reversed its delayed-tracking reform in 2016–2019 despite PISA evidence, citing political pressure from parents and regional actors. [evidence: moderate — OECD; Polish education policy record]

- Early selection (age 10–12) in Hungary is associated with high correlation between SES and track placement, limiting social mobility for lower-income cohorts. [evidence: moderate — Hungarian education equity analyses; comparative PISA microdata]

- Gimnazium system consolidation or elimination requires legislative supermajorities or sustained cross-party consensus in Hungary; single-majority governments have rarely succeeded with comprehensive education restructuring. [evidence: weak — Hungarian parliamentary precedent; assumption-dependent]

## Interpretation

The political economy of early selection in Hungary rests on a narrow but mobilisable constituency: affluent urban parents for whom early gimnazium entry is a **signalling and certainty mechanism**, not merely a pedagogical choice. This mirrors Poland's 2010s experience: even evidence of equity gains and PISA improvements could not sustain the reform once political cost (parent mobilisation, regional opposition) exceeded perceived benefit to the governing coalition.

Any Hungarian reform faces a **durability trap**: if it reduces parental choice or certainty, resistance will be immediate and well-resourced; if it merely adds options without removing gimnazium, it will fail to address equity while absorbing political capital.

The Polish reversal is instructive not as inevitable outcome, but as a **credible threat to reform advocates**. It shows that PISA gains alone do not guarantee political survival.

## Assumptions

- That "middle-class resistance" documented in 1990s–2010s Hungarian press remains a mobilisable force in 2024. [assumption]
- That Polish 1999–2019 cycle is sufficiently analogous to Hungarian context to yield actionable lessons. [assumption]
- That PISA equity improvements would be valued similarly by current Hungarian political actors as by OECD technocrats. [assumption]

## Position

**Political durability of any tracked-to-comprehensive shift in Hungary requires either: (a) a supermajority willing to absorb 3–5 years of parent mobilisation, or (b) a reframe that protects perceived choice/status while postponing selection—but (b) risks repeating Poland's reversal cycle if framing unravels.**

## Uncertainties

- **What proportion of middle-class parent preference for gimnazium is pedagogically motivated vs. signalling-driven?** [confidence: low — would be reduced by: discrete choice experiments or qualitative parent surveys disaggregating motivation] Current evidence conflates asset value with actual educational quality preference.

- **Has Hungarian political party consensus on equity shifted since 2010, or would a 2024 reform still face 1990s-level organised resistance?** [confidence: low — would be reduced by: stakeholder interviews with current parent organisations and party education advisors]

- **What is the counterfactual PISA trajectory for Poland absent 1999 reform?** [confidence: low — would be reduced by: synthetic control or matched cohort analysis isolating reform effect from secular trends]

- **Do Hungarian regional/rural parents and educators hold different views on early selection than urban middle class?** [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: stratified opinion polling and regional education authority consultation]
