# Expert analysis: demography

## Findings (evidence)

- Annual births in Hungary fell from ~125,000 (early 1990s) to 85–95,000 (2010s–2020s), representing a 28–32% cumulative decline. [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks]
- This cohort shrinkage is structurally locked in through 2040 because the smaller cohorts of women born in the 1990s–2000s are now entering peak reproductive years. [evidence: strong — demographic momentum; KSH population projections]
- Early selection (at age 10–12 via the 6- and 8-year gimnazium intake) concentrates demand for grammar-school places into fewer, larger institutions. [evidence: moderate — common practice in Central Europe; intake mechanics documented in Hungarian education ministry reports]
- Declining cohorts + concentrated demand = surplus capacity in lower-tier secondary schools and geographic mismatch between supply and demand. [evidence: moderate — implicit in cross-European school consolidation trends during similar demographic transitions]

## Interpretation

The demographic driver (birth-cohort shrinkage) is independent of selection policy. Whether Hungary keeps early selection or shifts to late selection, the total secondary-school population will contract by ~25–30% by 2035–2040.

However, selection *design* shapes *where* that contraction hits:
- **Early selection concentrates pressure**: Fewer, larger grammar schools absorb high-achievers; lower-tier schools face enrollment collapse and closure risk in rural/peripheral regions.
- **Late selection distributes pressure**: All secondary schools shrink proportionally; capacity adjustments are more uniform but harder to time.

The policy question conflates two separable challenges: (1) managing absolute cohort decline (unavoidable), and (2) choosing who bears the consolidation burden (avoidable).

## Assumptions

- Reproductive behaviour and migration remain near current trends. [assumption]
- Selection policy does not significantly alter human-capital outcomes at the population level (contested in international literature, but not the demographer's domain). [assumption]
- "School-network decisions" refers to facility closures, mergers, and program consolidation. [assumption]

## Position

Cohort decline will force substantial secondary-school consolidation by 2035 regardless of selection policy; early selection will concentrate that consolidation into non-grammar schools in peripheral regions, while late selection will distribute it more evenly—but the demographic pressure is inescapable.

## Uncertainties

- **Migration flows (confidence: low)**: Will net emigration of young adults accelerate or stabilize? Would be reduced by: detailed cohort-tracking data and scenario-based population projections from KSH with migration sensitivity analysis.
- **Regional heterogeneity (confidence: medium)**: Will urban cohorts stabilize while rural cohorts collapse further? Would be reduced by: municipality-level birth and outmigration data for 2015–2025.
- **Intake elasticity (confidence: low)**: How much does early selection policy reduce or increase grammar-school demand relative to a late-selection regime? Would be reduced by: comparative enrollment data from Hungary and peer countries (e.g., Austria post-reform).
- **Fertility response to policy (confidence: low)**: Could family-support or education-access policy meaningfully raise birth rates? Would be reduced by: causal econometric studies of Hungarian fertility responses to policy changes (limited literature exists).
