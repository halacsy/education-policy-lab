# Expert analysis: Finnish peruskoulu transfer to Hungarian early selection context

## Findings (evidence)

- Finland's peruskoulu reform (1972–1977) bundled comprehensive schooling (no tracking to age 16), teacher professionalization, and integrated support systems; equity gains are documented but **not attributable to comprehensive structure alone**. [evidence: moderate — Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009]

- The reform's equity effect (reduced family-background dependence on outcomes) is **entangled with teacher policy and support systems**, meaning the mechanism is not transparent from the Finnish case. [evidence: moderate — Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009]

- Early selection (6- and 8-year grammar schools in Hungary) operates on a fundamentally different institutional and timing logic: selection at age 10–12 vs. Finland's age-16 transition; this is a structural, not marginal, difference. [evidence: strong — definitional; flagged as model knowledge]

## Interpretation

The Finnish case is often cited as evidence that "comprehensive schooling works." The evidence is real but partial. What *actually carried* the equity effect remains unclear from the curated source: was it:
- The delay of selection itself (postponing differentiation)?
- The concurrent teacher quality and training overhaul?
- The integrated support infrastructure (SEN, counseling, remediation)?
- A combination, with no dominant factor?

For Hungary's 6/8-year grammar school question, this ambiguity is critical. Simply eliminating early selection **without** simultaneous investment in teacher professionalization, support services, and curriculum differentiation would replicate a common failure mode: comprehensive structure without comprehensive support.

## Assumptions

- Equity effects in Finland are attributable partly to structure and partly to complementary systems. [assumption]
- Hungary's early selection context (institutional, curricular, and resource) is sufficiently different from 1970s Finland that direct transfer is high-risk. [assumption]
- The question assumes "doing something" about selection; an evidence-based answer must address what bundle of reforms, not structure alone. [assumption]

## Position

Hungary should not adopt comprehensive schooling (eliminating the 6/8-year grammar system) based on the Finnish model unless accompanied by credible, costed commitments to teacher professionalization and integrated support services; the Finnish reform's success was bundled, and structure-only transfer will likely fail.

## Uncertainties

- **What mechanism in the Finnish reform actually drove equity gains?** [confidence: low] — Would be reduced by: decomposition studies isolating the effect of selection delay from teacher policy and support infrastructure; quasi-experimental or synthetic-control designs.

- **Is Hungary's teacher workforce, funding, and support capacity sufficient to implement comprehensive schooling *with* the necessary complements?** [confidence: low] — Would be reduced by: detailed audit of teacher training, per-pupil spending by school type, and existing support services capacity.

- **What are Hungary's documented equity gaps and their drivers (selection, resources, teacher quality, or other)?** [confidence: medium] — Would be reduced by: national diagnostic assessment linking outcomes to school type, entry timing, and student background.

- **Have other countries attempted to transfer the Finnish model, and with what results?** [confidence: medium] — Would be reduced by: systematic review of comprehensive-schooling adoptions outside Scandinavia post-1990.
