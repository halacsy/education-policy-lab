# Expert analysis: Portuguese_reform pathway for Hungarian early selection

## Findings (evidence)

- Portugal achieved +49 PISA points (reading) and +40 (math) between 2006–2015 without raising selection age, instead via curriculum standardisation, school clustering, and targeted support (TEIP). [evidence: strong — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020]

- TEIP (Territories of Educational Priority) targeted disadvantaged schools with extended instruction time, teacher professional development, and reduced pupil–teacher ratios. [evidence: moderate — Portuguese Ministry of Education implementation records cited in Crato 2020]

- Portugal reduced grade retention from ~35% (2006) to ~15% (2015) through diagnostic early intervention and remedial pathways rather than system-wide streaming. [evidence: moderate — OECD Education at a Glance; Crato 2020]

- Hungarian 6- and 8-year gimnaziums select ~20–25% of cohort at age 10–11; early selection is associated with widened inequality but not definitively with absolute performance gains when controlling for intake. [evidence: moderate — Hungarian Central Statistical Office; OECD PISA 2018]

- No randomised trial directly compares delayed selection versus early selection + targeted support in Central European contexts. [evidence: weak — model knowledge; no registry study found]

## Interpretation

The Portuguese case demonstrates that **system-wide performance gains are achievable without structural deselection**, provided three conditions hold: (1) curriculum coherence and diagnostic tools reach all schools, (2) resource concentration targets disadvantaged cohorts, and (3) retention is replaced by progression with support. 

For Hungary, this suggests a non-structural pathway: keep early selection but (a) strengthen entrance assessment validity, (b) expand TEIP-equivalent targeted support in non-selected schools, and (c) reduce retention in primary and early secondary grades. The constraint is that Portuguese gains occurred in a relatively high-equity baseline (Gini ~0.35); Hungary's inequality (Gini ~0.28 but higher intergenerational persistence) may require stronger dosing.

## Assumptions

- Portugal's TEIP model is transferable to Hungarian institutional contexts (both EU, but different labour markets and teacher supply). [assumption]
- Early selection itself does not prevent absolute gains in non-selected cohorts if support intensity increases. [assumption]
- Hungarian retention reduction is politically feasible (currently ~10–12% annual rate; Portugal's 2006 baseline was higher, so may be easier to move). [assumption]

## Position

Hungary can improve aggregate PISA outcomes and reduce early selection equity costs simultaneously by adopting Portugal's standards + targeted support + retention reduction model, without abolishing gimnaziums—but only if investment in TEIP-equivalent programmes in lower-selecting schools is substantial and sustained (3–5 years minimum).

## Uncertainties

- **Dosage and cost**: What is the unit cost of Hungarian TEIP-equivalent programmes per disadvantaged student? Would be reduced by: comparative costing study (Portugal vs. Hungary labour, materials, scale). [confidence: low]

- **Selection mechanism causality**: Does Hungarian early selection *cause* lower performance in non-selected students, or is selection endogenous to prior attainment? Would be reduced by: quasi-experimental design (regression discontinuity at gimnazium cutoff scores). [confidence: medium]

- **Teacher capacity**: Do Hungarian primary and lower secondary teachers have diagnostic and remedial training comparable to Portugal post-2009? Would be reduced by: teacher survey + curriculum audit. [confidence: low]

- **Political economy**: Which stakeholder coalition would fund and sustain a 5-year targeted support programme if selection is nominally preserved? Would be reduced by: stakeholder analysis and pilot budget trajectory. [confidence: low]
