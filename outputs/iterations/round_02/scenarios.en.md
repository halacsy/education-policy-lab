# Policy scenarios

## S1 — Admission reform within the current structure
**Goal**
Reduce SES concentration at entry to 6/8-year gimnazium tracks by reforming admission criteria while preserving the existing institutional and legal structure.

**Mechanism**
- Entry to 6/8-year tracks is currently dominated by centralized written examination that filters for test-prep advantage and parental investment, and admission is heavily concentrated among high-SES, urban families [evidence: strong]
- The measured achievement advantage of early-selective tracks is predominantly a selection effect rather than causal value-added [evidence: moderate]
- Reforming admission criteria (e.g., SES-proportional quotas, validity-improved assessment) could reduce SES concentration at entry, but the effect of admission-rule changes on overall stratification is not directly evidenced for Hungary [evidence: contested]
- Abolition or reform of selective intake without simultaneous reform of catchment rules and school choice regimes would likely preserve or displace rather than eliminate stratification [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
Mixed — the SES concentration and selection-effect mechanisms are strongly/moderately evidenced, but the causal effect of admission-rule changes on overall stratification is contested and unevidenced for Hungary.

**Assumptions**
- SES-proportional or validity-improved admission criteria can be defined and enforced across fragmented maintainers (state centres, churches, foundations). [assumption]
- Test-prep inequality is a substantial component of SES gap in selection success, rather than underlying prior achievement. [assumption — unverified; see uncertainties]
- The 8–12% enrollment figure and selection intensity are stable across recent cohorts. [assumption]
- Church and foundation maintainers can be brought into a common admission framework rather than exempted. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Potential reduction of SES concentration at entry without dismantling institutional structures, though the effect on overall stratification is not directly evidenced for Hungary [evidence: contested]
- Lower transition-cost burden than structural reform since buildings and staffing remain in place, though per-pupil funding differences between track types are modest and this cost claim rests on weak budgetary evidence [evidence: weak]
- Improved transparency and validity of entrance assessment if test-prep advantage is reduced [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
Targets the most visible, bureaucratically explicit sorting mechanism (centralized exam). However, because free school choice and catchment boundaries produce between-school sorting independently of track existence [evidence: moderate], admission reform alone would likely displace rather than fully reduce stratification; residual sorting through choice and catchment is expected to persist.

**Cost categories**
- Administrative/legislative amendment costs for revising admission rules (scope likely underestimated given fragmented maintainer governance)
- Development and validation costs for a new/reformed entrance assessment instrument
- Ongoing monitoring and compliance costs to enforce criteria across state, church, and foundation maintainers
- Negotiation/coordination costs with non-state maintainers

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education — amend admission regulations to introduce SES-proportional quotas and/or validity-improved assessment (requires legislative amendment)
- Ministry of Education — negotiate equity thresholds with church and foundation maintainers (feasibility constrained: no single authority can mandate non-state maintainers without negotiation or legislative conflict)
- Oktatási Hivatal — redesign and pilot the entrance assessment to reduce test-prep advantage
- Oktatási Hivatal — establish monitoring of admission outcomes by SES quartile and region

**Political risks**
- Affluent urban parents treat gimnazium places as mobility/insurance assets and are likely to mobilise against changes that reduce perceived certainty of access [evidence: moderate]
- Maintainer-exemption risk: if church/foundation schools are exempt from reformed criteria, S1 creates an incentive to shift selective intake to exempt maintainers, worsening the stratification it targets
- Reform absorbing political capital while failing to address equity (the 'durability trap' identified for option that adds constraints without removing the structure)

**Uncertainties**
- How much of the SES gap in selection success reflects test-prep inequality vs. underlying prior achievement? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: audit studies or analysis of admission exam scores net of prior standardised test performance]
- What is the causal contribution of track existence to overall inequality, net of choice and catchment effects? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: longitudinal cohort studies holding choice/catchment constant]
- Will churches/foundations cooperate with a common admission framework or resist, fragmenting the system further? [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: interviews with maintainer leadership and analysis of past negotiations]
- What proportion of middle-class parent preference for gimnazium is pedagogically motivated vs. signalling-driven? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: discrete choice experiments or qualitative parent surveys]

## S2 — Gradual phase-down of 6/8-year entry places
**Goal**
Progressively reduce the number of 6/8-year gimnazium entry places over a multi-year trajectory, shifting selection later without an abrupt structural break.

**Mechanism**
- Early between-school tracking at younger ages causally increases socio-economic gradients in achievement without raising mean performance in cross-country designs [evidence: strong]
- Reducing early entry places shifts a growing share of the cohort into common lower-secondary settings, deferring differentiation [evidence: moderate]
- Legislative and contractual anchoring requires multi-year notice periods and transitional guarantees, creating a minimum 6–8 year implementation timeline that a staged cap trajectory can align with [evidence: strong]
- Partial retention of early places during phase-down means the sorting mechanism continues to operate on the retained share [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
Moderate — the direction (earlier tracking amplifies SES gradients) is strongly evidenced internationally and the legal timeline is strong, but the specific effect of a partial, staged cap in Hungary is not directly evidenced.

**Assumptions**
- Staged reform is expected to reduce reversal risk relative to abrupt change; this is untested and may instead merely extend the period of conflict. [assumption]
- Teacher recruitment/retraining can be front-loaded during the 2025–2035 retirement window, though no published cost/timeline estimate exists (see uncertainties). [assumption]
- Elite pupils will not exit to private/international pathways at a rate that undermines the equity aim during phase-down. [assumption]
- Cap trajectory can be enforced consistently across state and non-state maintainers. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Gradual reduction of the share of the cohort exposed to early between-school tracking, consistent with the direction of the international causal evidence [evidence: moderate]
- Lower short-run disruption than abrupt abolition, respecting statutory transitional guarantees for enrolled pupils [evidence: strong]
- More even distribution of demographic consolidation pressure across school types as the early-selected share shrinks [evidence: moderate]

**Equity impact**
Because the mechanism is a gradual reduction rather than removal of early places, residual stratification persists through the transition on the still-selected share; the sorting mechanism is diminished in scope year by year but not eliminated until the trajectory completes. Between-school sorting via choice and catchment would remain active independently [evidence: moderate].

**Cost categories**
- Multi-year transition budgeting for staff retraining in mixed-ability pedagogy (scale and per-teacher cost unquantified — see uncertainties)
- Temporary overstaffing costs during cohort-overlap years
- Curriculum redesign and alignment for expanded common lower-secondary provision
- Administrative/legislative costs of a staged cap and transitional guarantees
- Teacher recruitment in disadvantaged regions where science/maths shortages are most acute

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education — legislate a declining annual cap on 6/8-year entry places with multi-year notice and transitional guarantees for enrolled pupils
- Ministry of Education — front-load teacher recruitment/retraining during the 2025–2035 retirement window (cost/timeline not yet estimated — requires costing before commitment)
- Tankerület centres — plan phased conversion of released capacity into common lower-secondary provision
- Ministry of Education — negotiate cap participation with church/foundation maintainers

**Political risks**
- Reversal risk: a successor government can freeze or reverse the cap trajectory (as Poland reversed delayed tracking in 2016–2019 for political reasons) [evidence: moderate]
- No design feature currently raises the cost to a successor government of freezing the cap trajectory; reversal risk is identified but not mitigated in this scenario's design
- Sustained parent mobilisation over the multi-year window, with staged reform potentially extending rather than reducing the conflict period
- Migration of elite pupils to non-state or private provision during phase-down

**Uncertainties**
- Whether staged reform reduces reversal risk or merely extends the conflict period. [confidence: low — would be reduced by: comparative analysis of phased vs. abrupt education reform durability]
- How much teacher retraining would actually be required, and at what cost? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: pilot retraining programme with cost accounting and retention measurement]
- What is the elasticity of parental exit to private schools if early places are reduced? [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: survey data on willingness-to-pay and private-school capacity constraints]
- Longevity of the reform under Hungarian political conditions. [confidence: low — would be reduced by: comparative analysis of Hungarian education reform sustainability and current legislative support]

## S3 — Comprehensive school to age 14 (structural reform)
**Goal**
Abolish early academic selection by establishing common comprehensive schooling to age 14, deferring between-school selection, coupled with teacher and support investment.

**Mechanism**
- Early between-school tracking at younger ages causally increases socio-economic gradients in achievement without raising mean performance [evidence: strong]
- Poland postponed academic tracking from age 11 to age 15 in 1999 and PISA scores rose substantially in post-reform cohorts, with a portion attributed to delayed tracking though confounded with concurrent changes [evidence: moderate]
- Deferred selection combined with mixed-ability teaching reduces gaps without harming top performers, but requires 2–4 years before outcome stabilization [evidence: moderate]
- Abolition without simultaneous catchment and school-choice reform would likely preserve or displace stratification into different school types and locations [evidence: moderate]
- Finnish equity gains under comprehensive schooling are entangled with teacher policy and support systems, so comprehensive structure alone is not a transparent cause of equity [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
Mixed — the international direction is strong and Polish/Finnish cases are moderately evidenced, but the magnitude of gains for Hungary specifically and the isolable effect of structure vs. bundled supports remain unevidenced domestically.

**Assumptions**
- The Hanushek & Woessmann timing findings generalise to Hungary despite differences in school-quality variance, teacher composition, and curriculum coherence — this generalisation is itself an untested inference, not established for Hungary. [assumption]
- Political ownership survives two electoral cycles — this contradicts the political_feasibility position that no durable supermajority/consensus window is assured; the tension is unresolved. [assumption]
- Hungary's teacher workforce and support capacity can be brought to sufficiency with the necessary complementary investment. [assumption]
- Catchment and school-choice rules are reformed simultaneously, without which stratification is displaced rather than removed. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Potential reduction of the SES-score gradient, which in Hungary is elevated at 19–25% vs 12–14% OECD average; the elevated gradient is strongly evidenced but the reduction attributable to this reform is not directly evidenced for Hungary [evidence: moderate]
- Possible aggregate learning gains consistent with the Polish post-reform trajectory, though effect size for Hungary is unknown [evidence: moderate]
- More uniform distribution of demographic consolidation across all secondary schools rather than concentration in non-selective schools [evidence: moderate]

**Equity impact**
Directly removes the age-10–12 gatekeeper that mechanically sorts high-SES pupils into advantaged tracks. Gains are contingent on bundled teacher and support investment: the Finnish evidence shows equity effects are entangled with teacher policy and support systems [evidence: moderate], so without such investment inequality may reappear as within-school ability grouping — this is a plausible risk drawn from the entanglement evidence rather than a directly evidenced Hungarian causal link. Between-school sorting via choice/catchment persists unless reformed in parallel [evidence: moderate].

**Cost categories**
- Transition costs (staff retraining, building repurposing, administrative reorganization, curriculum alignment) which international experience suggests exceed steady-state savings by a factor of 3–5 over 5–10 years
- Teacher recruitment to offset the below-OECD staffing gap and ageing workforce (significant ongoing cost — magnitude not quantified in curated sources)
- Sustained investment in integrated support systems (SEN, counselling, remediation) as complements to structure
- Temporary overstaffing during cohort-overlap years
- Catchment/school-choice administrative reform costs

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education — legislate comprehensive schooling to age 14 with statutory transitional guarantees and a minimum 6–8 year timeline
- Ministry of Education — fund and deliver teacher professional development in mixed-ability pedagogy ahead of rollout
- Ministry of Education — reform free-choice rules and catchment boundaries in parallel to curb between-school sorting (significant administrative/political undertaking likely to face resistance)
- Tankerület centres and maintainers — negotiate conversion of 6/8-year schools, requiring agreement from non-state maintainers
- Ministry of Education — pilot in one or two well-resourced districts with fidelity monitoring before nationwide change

**Political risks**
- Reversal risk demonstrated by Poland's 2016–2019 reinstatement of early selection for political/ideological reasons despite PISA evidence [evidence: moderate]
- Requires legislative supermajority or sustained cross-party consensus that single-majority governments have rarely achieved for comprehensive restructuring [evidence: weak]
- Intense, well-resourced mobilisation by affluent urban parents who treat gimnazium places as mobility/insurance assets [evidence: moderate]
- Loss of elite pupils to private/international pathways if the gimnazium brand is dismantled

**Uncertainties**
- Effect size of delayed tracking on Hungary specifically. [confidence: low — would be reduced by: dedicated quasi-experimental analysis of Hungarian cohorts or matched regional pilots]
- What mechanism in the Finnish reform actually drove equity gains (structure vs. teacher policy vs. support). [confidence: low — would be reduced by: decomposition/synthetic-control studies isolating the effect of selection delay]
- Can mixed-ability teaching at scale maintain academic rigor for high achievers? [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: evidence from comparable European systems and context-specific Hungarian pilot data]
- Whether political ownership can survive two electoral cycles given documented reversal risk. [confidence: low — would be reduced by: stakeholder interviews and comparative reform-durability analysis]
- What is the actual transition-phase budget requirement (3–5 years)? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: detailed costing of retraining, facility repurposing, and staffing overlaps in pilot districts]

## S4 — Keep the structure, compensate general schools (Portuguese-style package)
**Goal**
Retain the 6/8-year gimnazium structure but raise outcomes and reduce equity costs by investing in non-selected schools via a Portuguese-style package (standards, targeted support, retention reduction).

**Mechanism**
- Portugal achieved +49 PISA points (reading) and +40 (math) between 2006–2015 without raising selection age, via curriculum standardisation, school clustering, and targeted support (TEIP) [evidence: strong]
- TEIP targeted disadvantaged schools with extended instruction time, teacher professional development, and reduced pupil–teacher ratios [evidence: moderate]
- Portugal reduced grade retention from ~35% (2006) to ~15% (2015) through diagnostic early intervention rather than system-wide streaming [evidence: moderate]
- No randomised trial directly compares delayed selection versus early selection + targeted support in Central European contexts [evidence: weak]

**Evidence status**
Mixed — the Portuguese aggregate gains are strongly evidenced and the TEIP components moderately so, but transferability to Hungary at its earlier selection age and the compensatory claim are weakly evidenced or untested.

**Assumptions**
- Compensation can offset, rather than merely mask, the equity costs of early selection — this is doing the central work in S4 yet no country evidence exists at Hungary's selection age, so it is unverifiable ex ante. [assumption]
- Portugal's TEIP model is transferable to Hungarian institutional contexts despite different labour markets and teacher supply. [assumption]
- Early selection itself does not prevent absolute gains in non-selected cohorts if support intensity increases. [assumption]
- Hungary's higher intergenerational persistence may require stronger dosing than Portugal's higher-equity baseline. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Potential aggregate PISA gains without structural deselection, on the model of the Portuguese trajectory, though transfer to Hungary's earlier-selection context is untested [evidence: weak]
- Reduced grade retention via diagnostic early intervention, consistent with the Portuguese record [evidence: moderate]
- Lower political-conflict cost than structural reform because the gimnazium structure and parental choice are preserved [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
Aims to raise the floor for non-selected pupils rather than remove the age-10–12 gatekeeper; it leaves the early-selection sorting mechanism and its high-SES skew intact, so it mitigates rather than reverses stratification. Whether compensation offsets rather than masks the equity cost is unverifiable ex ante at Hungary's selection age. The claim that this does not address the loss of political voice/resources following departed top students is plausible but speculative, not directly evidenced.

**Cost categories**
- Sustained targeted remedial funding (TEIP-style) for disadvantaged schools — a long-term ongoing fiscal commitment, not a one-off, of unquantified magnitude
- Teacher professional development and reduced pupil–teacher ratios in non-selected schools
- Diagnostic assessment and remedial-pathway infrastructure to replace retention
- Curriculum standardisation and coherence tools reaching all schools
- Programme monitoring and evaluation over the minimum 3–5 year investment horizon

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education — pilot intensive TEIP-style support in underperforming vocational and lower-track schools in selected regions (requires significant, sustained fiscal commitment)
- Ministry of Education — introduce curriculum standardisation and diagnostic tools across all schools
- Ministry of Education — replace grade retention with progression-plus-support pathways in primary and early secondary
- Ministry of Education — commission a comparative costing study of Hungarian TEIP-equivalent unit costs before scale-up
- Oktatási Hivatal — monitor outcomes in non-selected schools by SES quartile and region

**Political risks**
- Sustained multi-year funding commitment is vulnerable to budget cycles; the stakeholder coalition to fund a 5-year targeted programme when selection is nominally preserved is unidentified [evidence: weak]
- Preserving the structure while adding options risks the 'durability trap': absorbing political capital without addressing equity
- Perceived as entrenching the gimnazium system, drawing opposition from equity advocates while satisfying pro-selection constituencies

**Uncertainties**
- Dosage and cost: what is the unit cost of a Hungarian TEIP-equivalent programme per disadvantaged student? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: comparative costing study of Portugal vs. Hungary labour, materials, scale]
- Does Hungarian early selection cause lower performance in non-selected students, or is selection endogenous to prior attainment? [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: regression-discontinuity design at gimnazium cutoff scores]
- Do Hungarian primary and lower-secondary teachers have diagnostic and remedial training comparable to Portugal post-2009? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: teacher survey and curriculum audit]
- Which stakeholder coalition would fund and sustain a 5-year targeted support programme if selection is preserved? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: stakeholder analysis and pilot budget trajectory]
