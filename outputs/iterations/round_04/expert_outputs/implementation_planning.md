# Expert analysis: implementation_planning

## Findings (evidence)
- Hungary faces a sustained teacher shortage and an ageing teacher workforce, concentrated in disadvantaged regions and in maths/science; any structural reform competes for the same scarce teachers. [evidence: strong — OECD Education at a Glance; PSZ/PDSZ surveys]
- Per-pupil funding differences between track types are modest; the dominant costs of structural reform are transition costs (retraining, building use, administration), not steady-state costs. [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates]

## Interpretation
Implementation capacity, not design, is the binding constraint: teacher supply, administrative bandwidth of district centres, and timetable of legal notice periods dictate a 6-10 year horizon for any structural variant.

## Assumptions
- District centres retain current administrative capacity. [assumption]

## Position
Sequence: pilot, evaluate, legislate, then scale — never legislate first.

## Uncertainties
- Realistic retraining throughput per year for upper-primary specialist teachers. (confidence: medium; evidence that would reduce it: teacher-education capacity audit.)
