# Critique: devil_advocate

## S1.assumptions
Objection: The scenario rests on "a workable SES indicator exists at application time," but treats this as a minor precondition rather than a load-bearing claim. Every mechanism (SES-weighted lottery, district quotas, SES-aware rules) depends on a per-applicant SES measure that is accurate, gameable-resistant, and legally defensible. Hungary has no such validated individual-level SES index at admission; parental education/income self-reports are manipulable precisely by the high-SES families the reform targets. If the indicator is weak, the SES-aware rules either misfire or become a vector for gaming — collapsing the entire mechanism, not just weakening it.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Move the SES-indicator to a mechanism precondition with its own evidence tag; specify the concrete data source (e.g., settlement-level deprivation index vs. individual data) and add a fallback rule (area-based weighting) for when individual SES cannot be verified.

## S2.mechanism
Objection: The claim that caps "mechanically lower early sorting [evidence: strong]" is only true for state-maintained places. The scenario's own assumption concedes enforceability "across state, church and private maintainers" is uncertain, and political_risks flags exemption litigation. If church/private maintainers are exempt or expand to absorb capped demand, caps do not mechanically reduce sorting — they redistribute it to non-state providers. The "strong" tag applies to a within-sector arithmetic that the cross-sector reality may negate.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Downgrade the mechanism tag to "moderate, conditional on cross-sector cap enforcement"; add the non-state absorption channel explicitly and tie the strong tag only to the enforced-sector subset.

## S3.mechanism
Objection: The asymmetric peer-effects claim is tagged [evidence: contested] yet the equity_impact calls this "the largest expected equity gain of all scenarios." The load-bearing benefit (disadvantaged pupils gain more than advantaged lose) is precisely the contested link, meaning the scenario's headline advantage rests on its weakest evidence. If peer effects are symmetric or small, S3 imposes the highest cost and political risk of any option for a net-neutral outcome.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Reframe the equity_impact as conditional ("largest gain *if* asymmetric peer effects hold; net-neutral if symmetric"), and add a decision gate: proceed to national phase-in only if pilot-district NABC data confirm asymmetry.

## S4.expected_benefits
Objection: "Politically inexpensive; implementable under any government [evidence: strong]" contradicts the scenario's own political_risks, which identify budget vulnerability as "the first cut in fiscal consolidation." A recurring high-cost funding formula is not politically inexpensive — it is cheap to legislate but expensive to sustain, and the strong tag conflates the two. The evidence supports low enactment cost, not durable implementation.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Split the tag: "low enactment cost [strong]" vs. "sustained funding durability [weak, see uncertainties]"; remove "implementable under any government" or qualify it as "enactable, not necessarily sustainable, under any government."
