# Critique: devil_advocate

## S3.assumptions
Objection: The assumption "sorting does not simply migrate to school choice between general schools and to non-state maintainers" is the single load-bearing claim on which S3's entire equity case rests, yet it is asserted as a manageable risk rather than treated as already falsified. The synthesis's own disagreement map cites "growth of non-state maintainers" as an observed trend *under the current system*, i.e., before any structural reform removes the gimnazium outlet — meaning the migration channel is demonstrably active already, not merely a hypothetical failure mode. If middle-class exit to non-state schools is already occurring in the baseline, phasing out 6/8-year entry removes one exit option while leaving the underlying demand and the non-state supply untouched, making migration the *expected* outcome rather than a tail risk. The equity_impact field even concedes this ("segregation could worsen") but the assumption is still framed as something that merely needs to "not" happen, without any mechanism proposed to prevent it (e.g., non-state admission regulation is absent from implementation_steps).
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Either add a concrete non-state-maintainer admission/quota mechanism to implementation_steps, or reclassify this from "assumption" to "primary uncertainty/risk" and lower expected_benefits' confidence accordingly.

## S2.expected_benefits
Objection: "Stronger peer composition in urban general-school upper grades [evidence: moderate]" is tagged moderate, but this benefit is mechanically downstream of the claim that "general schools can absorb and challenge high-achievers if funded for it" — which evidence_status itself labels "the unevidenced link." A benefit cannot carry stronger evidence than the assumption it depends on; tagging it "moderate" while the underlying retention mechanism is admitted to be unevidenced is an internal inconsistency, not a defensible independent estimate. The uncertainties section confirms this by rating "whether general-school programmes actually retain high-achieving families" at confidence: low — directly contradicting the "moderate" tag two sections earlier.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Downgrade the peer-composition benefit tag to [evidence: weak] or explicitly mark it conditional on the retention assumption holding, and cross-reference the low-confidence uncertainty in the same field.

## S4.expected_benefits
Objection: "Politically inexpensive; implementable under any government [evidence: strong]" overclaims certainty for a claim that the scenario's own political_risks field contradicts: "Budget vulnerability: compensation lines are the first cut in fiscal consolidation." A policy that is the first casualty of fiscal tightening is not "implementable under any government" in any durable sense — it is implementable only under favorable fiscal conditions, which is a materially weaker and different claim. The "strong" evidence tag appears to attach to the observation that compensation packages face less *ideological* opposition than structural reform, but that is a distinct claim from budgetary durability, and the scenario conflates the two.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Split the claim into two separately-tagged benefits: (1) low political/ideological resistance [evidence: strong, if supported], and (2) budgetary durability across fiscal cycles [evidence: weak/contested, consistent with the uncertainties field's own "confidence: low" on budget-line durability].

## S1.assumptions
Objection: "A workable SES indicator exists at application time" is listed as a bare assumption with no evidence tag and no discussion of what such an indicator would actually be in the Hungarian administrative context (address-based deprivation proxy? free/reduced meal status? parental education self-report?). Given that the entire admission mechanism (weighted lottery or district quota above/below threshold) is architecturally dependent on this indicator existing and being non-gameable, its absence of evidentiary support is not a minor gap — it is comparable in severity to the compliance-channel risk that the scenario does flag (elite-school informal selection). Unlike that risk, this one gets no mention in uncertainties at all, despite being at least as consequential: a poorly-constructed SES proxy could be gamed by exactly the same SES-advantaged families the reform targets (address manipulation, selective self-reporting).
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Add an explicit uncertainty entry for indicator gameability/validity, and name a candidate indicator (e.g., existing eligibility registries) with its known limitations before treating the mechanism as ready to pilot.
