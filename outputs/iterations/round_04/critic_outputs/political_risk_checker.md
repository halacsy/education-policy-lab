# Critique: political_risk_checker

## S1.political_risks
Objection: The risk list omits the most consequential reversal vector for this scenario: an SES-aware admission rule (weighted lottery, district quotas) is a decree-level instrument that a successor government can silently revert to a plain point-ranked exam in a single admission cycle — cheaper and less visible than reversing S2/S3. The listed risks (parent opposition, maintainer exemption) are contemporaneous opposition, not reversal/entrenchment risks. There is no entrenchment design (e.g., embedding the SES weighting in primary legislation or tying it to an independent admission authority).
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add a reversal risk line and pair it with an entrenchment mechanism: "SES-weighting rests in an amendable ministerial decree; a successor can restore point-ranking in one cycle. Mitigation: anchor the weighting principle in the public education act and delegate parameter-setting to an independent admissions body with published change-justification duties."

## S2.political_risks
Objection: The stated reversal risk ("a successor government can freeze or reverse the trajectory cheaply (Polish precedent)") is asserted but paired with no entrenchment design anywhere in the scenario — the implementation_steps and uncertainties gesture at "cross-party pact or statutory entrenchment attempts" only as a way to *reduce uncertainty*, not as an actual mechanism. For a decade-long trajectory whose entire value depends on surviving multiple electoral cycles, the absence of a concrete entrenchment mechanism is the scenario's central unmitigated flaw, not a side risk.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Specify the entrenchment design and its limits: e.g., statutory cap trajectory with supermajority amendment lock, felmenő-rendszer vested-rights guarantees that raise reversal cost, and a pre-committed year-5 evaluation gate that makes freezing politically costly. State honestly that no mechanism fully binds a determined successor majority.

## S3.political_risks
Objection: The scenario names "Polish-style reversal after government change destroys sunk transition costs" but treats the cross-party pact (implementation_steps: "Government + opposition (pact attempt)") as sufficient entrenchment, when the Polish precedent is precisely a case where structural reform was reversed despite prior political consensus. The scenario does not confront that a pact is weak entrenchment against a determined successor; it lacks any harder mechanism (vested pupil rights, constitutional/organic-law status, irreversibility-by-sunk-infrastructure sequencing).
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Downgrade the pact from mitigation to necessary-but-insufficient, and add harder entrenchment: sequence irreversible investments (teacher retraining, building reconfiguration) early to raise reversal cost, grant vested rights to enrolled cohorts, and pursue organic-law status. Add the political_risks line that even these leave a determined majority able to reverse — this is a genuine residual risk, not a solved one.

## S4.political_risks
Objection: The scenario correctly flags budget vulnerability but understates its own reversal asymmetry: because S4 is "additive" and touches no structure, reversal requires no legislation at all — merely a line-item cut in an annual budget — making it the *least* entrenched of all four scenarios, the opposite of the "implementable under any government" durability claimed in expected_benefits. The political_risks field does not reconcile this contradiction with the benefit claim.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Reconcile the two fields: "implementable under any government" refers to ease of *adoption*, not *durability*; the same non-structural nature that eases adoption makes the policy maximally reversible via routine budgeting. Add entrenchment: statutory earmarking or a multi-year formula-funding guarantee with an automatic-continuation default.
