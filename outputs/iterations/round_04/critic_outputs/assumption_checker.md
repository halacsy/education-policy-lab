# Critique: assumption_checker

## S1.assumptions
Objection: The assumption "A workable SES indicator exists at application time" is treated as a binary given, but the scenario's entire equity mechanism (weighted lottery above a threshold, district quotas, SES weighting) depends on the indicator being both available AND non-gameable at age 10. The expert record supplies no evidence that Hungary has an application-time SES index that resists parental manipulation (address changes, declared income). This is not a minor caveat: if the indicator is gameable, the SES-aware rules relabel sorting rather than reduce it — exactly the failure the equity_impact field concedes "may relabel rather than reduce sorting if compliance is weak."
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Split the assumption into (a) indicator exists and (b) indicator resists gaming; add an uncertainty entry on indicator manipulability with "reduced by: audit of address/income declarations in pilot districts," and downgrade the weighted-lottery mechanism evidence tag until (b) is tested.

## S2.mechanism
Objection: The mechanism claims "Advanced-programme funding in general schools retains ambitious families in the common track [evidence: weak]" and the evidence_status field itself flags this as "the unevidenced link." Yet the entire equity case of S2 rests on it: if ambitious families are NOT retained, the freed capacity does not improve peer composition and the residual selective places become more SES-skewed (as equity_impact admits). Tagging the load-bearing causal link "weak" while building the scenario's benefits on it is an internal contradiction — the expected_benefits "stronger peer composition [evidence: moderate]" cannot be more evidenced than the retention mechanism it depends on.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Downgrade "stronger peer composition in urban general-school upper grades" from moderate to weak to match its dependency on the weak retention link, or cite specific evidence that funded advanced programmes retain high-SES families in comprehensive settings.

## S3.evidence_status
Objection: The evidence_status calls the peer-effect asymmetry "contested" but the mechanism field tags it "[evidence: contested]" while expected_benefits asserts a "weaker SES-outcome gradient within a decade [evidence: moderate]." The moderate benefit tag is stronger than the contested mechanism that produces it. If asymmetric peer effects are genuinely disputed, the direction and size of the SES-gradient benefit cannot be rated moderate — the Polish/Finnish "direction of effect" citation establishes direction, not magnitude within a decade.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Either separate the direction claim (moderate) from the magnitude/timing claim (weak/contested), or restate the benefit as "direction of effect toward a weaker gradient" without the decade timing at moderate confidence.

## S4.expected_benefits
Objection: "Improved outcomes in disadvantaged general schools within 5 years (Portuguese trajectory) [evidence: moderate]" transfers Portugal's TEIP result to Hungary, but the scenario's own uncertainties field states "no country has demonstrated it at Hungary's selection age." The moderate tag imports external validity that the scenario elsewhere explicitly denies: Portugal did not have age-10/12 between-school selection, so its trajectory does not evidence offsetting Hungary's earlier sorting. The tag conflates "the components worked in Portugal" with "the package offsets early selection," which is precisely the open question.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Downgrade to weak, or narrow the benefit claim to "improved outcomes in disadvantaged schools" (component effect) and drop the implication that this offsets early sorting, which the uncertainties field concedes is undemonstrated.
