# Critique: evidence_checker

## S2.mechanism
Objection: The claim "Advanced-programme funding in general schools retains ambitious families in the common track [evidence: weak]" is the load-bearing assumption for the entire scenario's equity case, yet is graded only "weak" — and the scenario's own uncertainties section confirms this ("Whether general-school programmes actually retain high-achieving families. confidence: low"). A weak tag is honest, but the expected_benefits then upgrade a directly dependent outcome ("Stronger peer composition in urban general-school upper grades") to [evidence: moderate]. The peer-composition benefit cannot be better-evidenced than the retention mechanism it depends on; the moderate tag is unsupported given the weak parent link.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Downgrade "Stronger peer composition in urban general-school upper grades" to [evidence: weak], or cite a distinct source for the peer-composition claim that does not route through the unevidenced retention link.

## S3.mechanism
Objection: The tag "Later between-school selection weakens the SES-performance link, per cross-country tracking evidence [evidence: strong]" overstates what tracking-age studies support. Cross-country evidence (e.g., PISA-based tracking-age correlations) is observational and confounded; it supports a "moderate," direction-of-effect claim, not "strong" causal grading. The scenario's own evidence_status field concedes the overall case is "contested" and that "Hungarian implementation capacity" is disputed — inconsistent with a strong tag on the core mechanism.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Downgrade to [evidence: moderate] and reword to "cross-country tracking evidence associates later selection with a weaker SES-performance link (observational)."

## S3.expected_benefits
Objection: "Weaker SES-outcome gradient within a decade of full phase-in (Polish/Finnish direction of effect) [evidence: moderate]" cites Finland as supporting evidence, but Finland never operated Hungarian-style 6/8-year early selection to reform away — it is a system-design comparator, not a reform-effect case. Only Poland offers an actual delayed-selection reform with measured effects, and its evidence is itself contested and reversal-prone (per the scenario's own political_risks). Citing Finland as a "direction of effect" for a phase-out inflates the evidence base.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Drop the Finnish citation from this benefit or relabel it a design comparator; ground the moderate tag on the Polish reform evidence alone, noting its contested status.

## S4.expected_benefits
Objection: "Politically inexpensive; implementable under any government [evidence: strong]" is a political-feasibility judgment tagged as if it were an empirical finding. The scenario's own political_risks field contradicts the "strong" grade ("Budget vulnerability: compensation lines are the first cut in fiscal consolidation"), and uncertainties rates budget durability "confidence: low." A strong evidence tag on cross-government implementability is inconsistent with the scenario's own admission that the funding is fiscally fragile.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Downgrade to [evidence: moderate] and split the claim — low upfront political cost is defensible, but "implementable under any government" is contradicted by the stated budget-cut vulnerability and should be qualified.
