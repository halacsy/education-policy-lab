# Critique: evidence_checker

## S2.expected_benefits
Objection: "Steadily falling early-sorting share of each cohort (measurable annually) [evidence: strong]" is tagged strong, but no registry source actually measures the effect of entry-place caps on sorting share — this is a mechanical/definitional inference from cap policy, not an empirical finding. The only "strong" registry items (gimn_share, tracking_inequality, demography) describe current intake share and general between-school-tracking effects, not the causal chain from caps to falling sorting share. Tagging a logical deduction as "strong" evidence misrepresents the registry's grading scale, which reserves "strong" for cited empirical studies (e.g., Hanushek & Woessmann 2006).
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Retag as "model inference (not registry-sourced)" or split the claim: the arithmetic fact that fewer selected places = fewer selected pupils can stand untagged as definitional, while any claim about system-level sorting reduction should drop to "weak/unsupported" pending evidence.

## S4.expected_benefits
Objection: "Politically inexpensive; implementable under any government [evidence: strong]" assigns the top evidence grade to a political-feasibility prediction, but nothing in the curated registry grades political implementability of compensation packages — this is the author's own political judgment, not a sourced empirical claim. No registry entry (fiscal, political, or governance) supports "strong" here; at most "political_risks" fields elsewhere acknowledge budget vulnerability, which undercuts rather than supports "politically inexpensive... under any government."
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Remove the evidence tag or relabel as (speculative); the adjacent Political risks section already flags budget vulnerability, which contradicts calling this "strong."

## S1.expected_benefits
Objection: "More balanced intake into selective tracks within 2-3 admission cycles [evidence: moderate]" attaches a specific, falsifiable timeline (2-3 cycles) to a moderate tag, but the only relevant registry sources (school_choice [moderate], tracking_inequality [strong]) describe sorting mechanisms and cross-country track-timing effects — none estimate a speed-of-change parameter for admission-rule reform. The timeline figure appears to be invented, not derived from any cited source, yet is graded as if it carries the same moderate evidential weight as the sourced claims.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Drop the "2-3 admission cycles" figure or mark it (speculative), keeping only the qualitative claim ("intake composition can shift with admission-rule reform") at moderate.

## S3.expected_benefits
Objection: "Later, better-informed track choice at 14 reduces misallocation of talent [evidence: moderate]" cites no registry source at all — poland_reform and finland_comprehensive (both moderate) discuss PISA score and equity outcomes, not "misallocation of talent" or informed-choice effects at the individual level. This is a distinct causal claim smuggled in under the same moderate tag as the adjacent, actually-sourced claim about SES gradients, without its own evidentiary basis.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Either cite a specific registry-consistent source for talent-misallocation effects or retag as (speculative)/model knowledge, separating it from the properly-sourced SES-gradient claim in the same bullet.
