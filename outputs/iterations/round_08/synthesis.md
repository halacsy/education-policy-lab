## Overview

Twelve domain experts examined Hungary's 6-/8-year gimnázium early-selection system from complementary angles (system description, international comparison, three national case studies, equity, demography, network planning, finance, law/governance, political feasibility, implementation). All agree Hungary selects earlier and more steeply along SES lines than OECD peers, but they diverge sharply on whether the answer is to phase the system out, delay it, keep it and patch around it, or defer action until preconditions (capacity, consensus, evidence) are met. Several load-bearing empirical questions — causal weight of tracking vs. choice/catchment, transferability of Finnish/Polish/Portuguese experience, teacher-exit risk, maintainer behavior under a mandate — remain at low or medium confidence across the record and are not resolved by this synthesis; they are carried forward below and in each expert's own uncertainty list.

## Disagreement map

- **international_comparison, finnish_reform, polish_reform, equity_and_social_mobility, demography, school_network_planning**: Hungary should phase out or substantially delay early selection (to roughly age 13–15), because early tracking raises the SES gradient without demonstrated mean-performance gains and Hungary's gradient already exceeds the OECD average. Why: cross-national difference-in-differences evidence (Hanushek & Woessmann 2006; Jakubowski et al. 2016; Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009) plus Hungary-specific value-added studies showing the raw gimnázium advantage is mostly a selection effect, not a causal gain.
- **portuguese_reform (minority)**: Hungary should retain the 6-/8-year gimnázium structure unchanged and instead pursue non-structural reforms (curriculum standards, school clustering, TEIP-style targeted funding for disadvantaged schools). Why: Portugal raised PISA outcomes substantially between 2006–2015 without altering selection age, suggesting structural reorganization may be a "distraction" from more tractable, less politically disruptive levers.
- **education_finance (minority)**: Hungary should retain the system because steady-state operational costs are roughly flat across track types, while transition costs (teacher retraining, facility repurposing) are large and front-loaded — the fiscal case for consolidation is weak. Why: centralized wage-grid and funding structures (Klebelsberg Centre) mean no local savings materialize from track abolition; the money is spent on the switch itself, not saved by it.
- **implementation_planning (minority)**: Hungary should retain the system in the medium term and defer structural change until the systemic teacher shortage (especially maths/sciences, marginalized regions) is resolved. Why: dismantling early tracks now would force redistribution of already-scarce specialized teachers into comprehensive schools, risking degraded delivery precisely where capacity is weakest.
- **hungarian_education_system (minority-adjacent)**: Hungary should not abolish tracks unilaterally; instead tighten SES-aware admission quotas, weaken school-choice/catchment sorting, and impose uniform accountability across state/church/foundation maintainers — treating structural abolition and choice-architecture reform as separable questions. Why: early tracking is only one layer of a multi-mechanism sorting system (choice + catchment + maintainer fragmentation also drive stratification), so removing tracks alone would not eliminate segregation.
- **legal_and_governance**: Hungary should commission an independent equity audit before any structural mandate, then co-design a graduated pathway (phased entry-age increase, transparent criteria, stronger primary–secondary bridges) that respects Nkt. legislative timelines and maintainer plurality. Why: the 6-/8-year form is anchored in statute and maintainer agreements, so uniform closure carries high procedural/legal cost, and no recent Hungary-specific impact study exists to justify overriding that cost.
- **political_feasibility**: Consolidation and delayed selection are achievable, but only if bundled simultaneously with pilot validation, a compressed 3–4-year phase-down, constitutional/legislative (not discretionary) funding guarantees, and multi-party political consensus. Why: Poland's 1999 reform produced real gains but was reversed in 2016–2019 once political consensus eroded — a reform that replicates Poland's structural design without locking in the political/financial bundle inherits its fragility.
- **On root cause of inequality — hungarian_education_system vs. equity_and_social_mobility / international_comparison**: hungarian_education_system holds that catchment- and choice-based sorting contribute substantially alongside tracking, so track abolition alone would displace rather than eliminate segregation; equity_and_social_mobility and international_comparison attribute the core equity problem more directly to the selection mechanism itself. Why the difference: no curated source isolates the tracking-specific causal share from choice/catchment effects (both sides flag this as an open uncertainty, not a settled point).
- **On maintainer fragmentation — hungarian_education_system vs. legal_and_governance / school_network_planning**: hungarian_education_system's reform position assumes church/foundation maintainers can be brought under uniform accountability rules; legal_and_governance and school_network_planning hold that these maintainers can and likely will absorb or bypass any state-mandated contraction of selective capacity, fragmenting a uniform mandate's reach. This tension is unresolved in the expert record — it has been flagged in prior synthesis rounds and persists without new evidence resolving it either way.
- **On urgency/driver — demography & school_network_planning vs. legal_and_governance & implementation_planning**: the former argue demographic cohort shrinkage makes school-network consolidation decisions unavoidable within the 2020s–2030s regardless of selection policy, making delay costly; the latter argue for slowing down — commissioning evidence first (legal_and_governance) or waiting for teacher-supply stabilization (implementation_planning) — treating urgency claims as insufficient justification for acting before capacity/evidence exists.

## What the experts agree on

- Hungary's 6-/8-year gimnázium tracks select roughly 8–12% of each cohort at ages 10–12 via centralized exam, and entry is strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families [evidence: strong — KSH/Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics; corroborated across hungarian_education_system, equity_and_social_mobility].
- Hungary's PISA SES gradient (≈19–25% of variance) substantially exceeds the OECD average (≈12–14%) [evidence: strong — OECD PISA country notes; corroborated across international_comparison, equity_and_social_mobility].
- Annual births have fallen from ~125,000 (early 1990s) to ~85,000–95,000 recently, and this cohort shrinkage will force school-network consolidation decisions in the 2020s–2030s independent of any selection-policy choice [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks; agreed by demography and school_network_planning].
- Governance is fragmented: since 2013 most state schools sit under centralized tankerület governance, while church and foundation maintainers hold and are expanding an independent share of school operations, complicating any uniform structural mandate [evidence: strong — Public education act amendments/maintainer statistics; agreed by hungarian_education_system, school_network_planning, legal_and_governance].
- No expert proposes abrupt, unconditional, unilateral structural change — every position, whether "phase out" or "retain," attaches explicit preconditions (bundled investment, phased timelines, audits, political pacts, or capacity thresholds) before or instead of action.
- The precise causal contribution of early tracking itself (versus school choice/catchment sorting, teacher-training quality, or funding allocation) to Hungary's inequality outcomes remains empirically unresolved across the record — flagged as a low-confidence uncertainty by hungarian_education_system, international_comparison, and finnish_reform alike.

## Minority positions

Roughly one-third of the expert panel (4 of 12: portuguese_reform, education_finance, implementation_planning, and hungarian_education_system in a qualified form) dissent from the majority lean toward phasing out or delaying selection. These are preserved in full, not folded into the majority framing:

- **portuguese_reform**: Retain the structural status quo entirely; invest instead in curriculum standards, school clustering, and TEIP-style targeted funding for disadvantaged schools. Rationale: Portugal's 2006–2015 PISA gains show equity and performance improvements are achievable without touching selection age, implying the structural debate may misallocate reform attention away from more transferable, less disruptive levers. Uncertainty the position itself flags: whether Portugal's TEIP mechanism and teacher-supply conditions transfer to Hungary is unconfirmed [confidence: high on teacher-shortage transferability risk; medium on political sustainability of targeted funding].
- **education_finance**: Retain the system on fiscal grounds — steady-state per-pupil costs are flat across tracks, so consolidation buys little ongoing savings while transition costs (retraining, facility repurposing) are large and immediate. Rationale: centralized wage grids and Klebelsberg Centre funding structures decouple track-level staffing changes from budget outcomes, undercutting the fiscal-efficiency case for reform. This is a purely cost-side objection, distinct from the equity-side minority reasoning of portuguese_reform.
- **implementation_planning**: Retain the system in the medium term specifically because of an acute, structural teacher shortage (math/sciences, marginalized regions); forcing redistribution now risks collapsing delivery in already-fragile comprehensive schools. Rationale: administrative capacity, not political will or evidence, is the binding constraint — a distinct rationale from both portuguese_reform (non-structural substitution) and education_finance (cost accounting).
- **hungarian_education_system**: A qualified dissent from "phase out" — proposes retaining tracks but tightening SES-aware admission and choice/catchment rules and imposing cross-maintainer accountability, treating structural abolition as neither necessary nor sufficient. Rationale: tracking is one layer among several (choice, catchment, maintainer plurality) driving sorting, so removing it alone would not close the equity gap. This position carries an internal, still-unresolved tension (see disagreement map) with legal_and_governance and school_network_planning over whether maintainer compliance with such accountability rules is realistic — the panel record does not settle this, and it should not be read as settled here.

These four positions differ from one another in rationale (evidence-substitution, cost-accounting, capacity-constraint, and mechanism-substitution respectively) and should not be collapsed into a single "anti-reform" bloc.
