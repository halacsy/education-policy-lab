# Synthesis

## Overview
Across nine expert analyses, a substantive consensus emerges that Hungary's 6- and 8-year grammar school system enacts early selection (ages 10 and 12) that concentrates high-socio-economic-status, urban pupils in a small set of prestigious schools, interacts with free school choice to amplify segregation, and correlates with one of the OECD's steepest SES-achievement gradients — yet whether this reflects genuine school quality or a bare selection/sorting effect remains empirically unresolved for Hungary specifically. International cases (Finland, Poland, Portugal) are read differently by different experts: some treat delayed selection as transferable evidence for phase-out, others stress that Finland's and Portugal's gains were bundled with resource/support investments that are analytically inseparable from structural change, and the political_feasibility and Polish-reform experts warn that Poland's 2016–2019 reversal shows structural reform is politically reversible absent binding, cross-party, statutory (not decree-based) commitments. A second cluster of disagreement concerns feasibility: demography, school_network_planning, education_finance, legal_and_governance, political_feasibility and implementation_planning agree that cohort decline forces network consolidation regardless of policy choice and that teacher supply, transition costs, and maintainer fragmentation (state/church/foundation) are binding constraints, but they diverge on whether a pilot-first, incremental approach or a legally binding staged phase-out schedule is the more credible route, and on how large a role decree-versus-statute reversibility risk should play. Uncertainty is pervasive and substantive rather than cosmetic: no Hungarian value-added study isolating school quality from intake composition exists, the scale of de facto status-hierarchy reconstitution under formal equality is unknown, and the fiscal and workforce cost of any transition has never been formally estimated by government.

## Disagreement map
### Whether international evidence (Finland, Poland, Portugal) is directly transferable to justify phasing out early selection in Hungary
- **international_comparison, finnish_reform, polish_reform, equity_and_social_mobility, demography**: Cross-country evidence on early tracking's inequality effects is mechanistically transferable to Hungary because the underlying causal channels (peer sorting, composition effects, selection vs. quality) operate similarly across institutional contexts. Why: Difference-in-differences and PISA gradient studies show consistent patterns across many systems; Hungary's exceptionally high SES-achievement gradient (19–25% vs 12–14% OECD) is itself evidence the mechanism is active domestically.
- **finnish_reform, portuguese_reform, polish_reform** (minority): Transferability is conditional and likely overstated: the Finnish and Portuguese equity gains were bundled with simultaneous resource, support-service and curriculum investments inseparable from the structural change, and Hungary's free-school-choice regime differs structurally from Finland's catchment-based model and Portugal's centralised system, so structural change alone would not replicate the gains. Why: Analytically, structure and bundled investment cannot be decomposed in the source studies; Poland's own reversal shows that even documented gains did not guarantee political durability, undermining simple transfer logic.

### Whether Hungary should pilot reform locally first or commit to a legally binding national phase-out schedule
- **hungarian_education_system, legal_and_governance, school_network_planning**: A monitored multi-year pilot in one or two large urban districts, with transparent value-added measurement, should precede any system-wide mandate, because the pedagogical value-added of the current tracks is not known and premature national mandates risk unmeasured harm. Why: No recent Hungarian value-added study exists to distinguish school quality from selection effect; piloting generates the missing evidence before committing irreversible political capital.
- **political_feasibility, demography** (minority): Without a multi-party statutory pact — binding timelines (≤4 years), teacher-retraining funding, and constitutional budget-floor guarantees — any decree-based or pilot-first reform remains politically reversible within a single electoral cycle and should not be the primary reform vehicle. Why: Hungarian education policy since 2024–2025 is delivered predominantly via ministerial decree, which is faster to reverse than statute; the Polish 2016–2019 reversal demonstrates that even evidence-backed reforms fail without durable cross-party commitment.

### Whether non-state (church/foundation) maintainers can realistically be restricted from absorbing phased-out selective capacity
- **hungarian_education_system, legal_and_governance, school_network_planning**: A phase-out design can, with cross-maintainer coordination and negotiated transitional guarantees, prevent church and foundation schools from simply absorbing the displaced selective demand. Why: Legislative amendment plus negotiated maintainer agreements are the standard mechanism for structural education reform in Hungary's plural-maintainer system.
- **political_feasibility** (minority): This restriction assumption is not credible: church and foundation maintainers, who account for a growing and already substantial share (with government-dependent private enrolment near 25.5%), constitute an independent veto-player likely to absorb high-achieving students into selective foundation schools regardless of state-track phase-down, undermining the equity rationale for reform. Why: Explicitly flagged in this round as an unresolved assumption contradicted by the maintainer-share evidence; church/foundation growth is structural and not subject to state legislative control in the same way as state-school-district-centre schools.

## What the experts agree on
- Hungary's 6- and 8-year grammar schools select pupils at ages 10 and 12 via a centralized written admission examination, and entrants are strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families. [evidence: strong]
- Hungary's socio-economic-status gradient in PISA achievement (19–25%) is markedly steeper than the OECD average (12–14%). [evidence: strong]
- Cohort decline (births falling from ~125,000 in the early 1990s to 85,000–95,000 by the 2020s) forces school-network consolidation decisions independent of any selection-policy choice. [evidence: strong]
- No recent, comprehensive Hungarian study isolates the causal value-added of 6/8-year grammar schools from pupil-composition (selection) effects. [evidence: weak]
- Teacher shortage (pedagógushiány) and an aging, structurally constrained teacher workforce limit the feasibility of rapidly scaling up comprehensive-school capacity regardless of the chosen reform path. [evidence: moderate]
- Since 2013 most state schools are maintained by state school district centres (tankerületi központok), while church and foundation maintainers hold a growing share, structurally complicating any uniform national reform mandate. [evidence: strong]
- No detailed, publicly available cost-benefit analysis or transition-cost estimate (teacher retraining, building reallocation, administrative restructuring) specific to a Hungarian phase-out of early selection currently exists. [evidence: weak]
- Any credible phase-out of early selection must be implemented cohort-by-cohort over a multi-year horizon (roughly one pupil-generation), pre-announced several admission cycles ahead, and paired with explicit teacher-staffing and building-use planning. [evidence: moderate]
