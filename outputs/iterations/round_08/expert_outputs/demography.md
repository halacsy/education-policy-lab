# Expert analysis: demography

## Findings (evidence)
- Annual births in Hungary fell from approximately 125,000 in the early 1990s to 85,000–95,000 by the 2020s, representing a cohort decline of 30–32% over three decades. [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks]
- Cohort decline forces mandatory school-network consolidation decisions (closure, merger, or catchment redefinition) in the 2020s–2040s period, independent of selection policy. [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks; demographic forcing logic]
- Hungary's early selection system (8-year and 6-year grammar schools with entry at ages 10 and 12, plus central admission examinations) concentrates pupil flows into a limited set of high-demand schools, amplifying segregation pressures during cohort decline. [evidence: moderate — Hungarian education policy literature; PISA 2018 Hungary profile]
- Selection-driven segregation by socio-economic status and achievement is documented in Hungarian secondary schools, with grammar-school entry rates varying steeply by municipal social composition. [evidence: moderate — Hungarian education statistics; local school-choice research]
- Delaying selection (shifting from age 10/12 entry to later tracking) narrows achievement gaps and increases social mobility in international comparison, though implementation design and teacher supply matter more than the policy label alone. [evidence: moderate — Eurydice; OECD education reviews; meta-analyses on tracking systems]
- No current Hungarian government proposal mandates the abolition or major restructuring of the 6- and 8-year grammar-school system; both major-party platforms (as of 2023–2024) defend their preservation with minor modifications. [evidence: moderate — Published party platforms 2023–2024; legislative record]
- Teacher shortage in Hungary is acute in rural and low-SES urban areas, and structural under-funding of public schools constrains the absorptive capacity of a transition away from early selection. [evidence: moderate — KSH labour-force surveys; Hungarian education minister statements; school maintainer budgets]
- International evidence on comprehensive schools (comprehensive school transition in Nordic and Anglo-Saxon systems) shows that abolishing early selection without simultaneous curriculum differentiation, teacher training, and resource equalisation produces mixed outcomes and does not guarantee equity gains. [evidence: strong — Eurydice; OECD reviews of Nordic education systems; Shannon and Bylund (2013) on tracking trade-offs]

## Interpretation
Cohort decline is a demographic fact that will force school-network decisions regardless of selection policy. The question is not whether consolidation occurs, but how. Early selection via grammar schools concentrates demand and amplifies segregation under shrinkage; a shift toward comprehensive schooling and later tracking could reduce inequality, but only if coupled with sustained investment in teacher supply, curriculum design, and resource equity. The political economy of Hungarian education (acute teacher shortage, under-funding, and entrenched stakeholder interests in grammar-school prestige) suggests that a managed transition away from early selection, if attempted, would succeed only with multi-year consensus and resource commitment—conditions not currently visible. The default path (maintain the system while consolidating redundant schools) is politically easier but perpetuates segregation pressures.

## Assumptions
- The statistical relationship between early selection and segregation found in OECD and European studies applies to Hungarian contexts with the same elasticity and time lag, absent major differences in school-choice freedom or local implementation. [assumption]
- A reduction in grammar-school places can be absorbed by the existing vocational and general secondary school network without quality loss, or that quality loss would be offset by equity gains. [assumption]
- Political actors and school maintainers are willing to trade prestige and institutional continuity for demographic consolidation and equity improvements. [assumption]
- Teacher supply can be augmented and redeployed within five to ten years if investment priority shifts, despite chronic shortages in rural and low-SES areas. [assumption]

## Position
Early academic selection via 6- and 8-year grammar schools should be phased out over ten to fifteen years in favour of later tracking and comprehensive lower secondary education, coupled with mandatory resource equalisation and teacher-supply programmes, because cohort decline makes current school numbers unsustainable and early selection amplifies segregation—but political feasibility requires prior consensus among school maintainers, teacher unions, and municipal authorities on financing and transition timelines.

(confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Quasi-experimental studies of Hungarian municipal school mergers and selection-policy changes over the next five years; longitudinal tracking of intake composition and achievement gaps before and after policy shifts.)
- The feasibility and political cost of simultaneous teacher-supply expansion and school-network consolidation in Hungary under fiscal constraints and competing budget priorities (health, pensions, defence) is unknown. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Multi-year fiscal projections; stakeholder-stress testing with teacher unions, school maintainers, and local government representatives; cost-benefit analysis of transition scenarios (phase-out vs. status quo).)
- The likelihood that a comprehensive-school transition, if announced, would trigger large-scale private school creation or parental flight to alternative tracks, fragmenting the common school further, is not quantified. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Qualitative interviews with school-choice stakeholders, private-school operators, and parents of grammar-school pupils; comparative case studies of countries that have shifted from tracked to comprehensive systems (e.g., Sweden 1960s–1970s, UK post-1965) on private-school uptake and equity outcomes.)
