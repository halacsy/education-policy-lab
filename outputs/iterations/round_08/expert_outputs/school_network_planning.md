# Expert analysis: school_network_planning

## Findings (evidence)
- Annual births fell from approximately 125,000 in the early 1990s to 85,000–95,000 by the 2020s, forcing school-network consolidation decisions independent of selection-policy choices. [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks]
- Since 2013, most state primary and secondary schools operate under state school district centres (tankerületi központok); churches and foundations maintain and expanding share, complicating any uniform structural mandate on selection tracks. [evidence: strong — Public Education Act amendments; maintainer statistics]
- Hungary's public education framework maintains three entry points for akademic secondary tracks: age 10 (8-year gimnazium), age 12 (6-year gimnazium), and age 14 (4-year secondary), creating de facto early selection regardless of formal policy intent. [evidence: moderate — Public Education Act structure; domain knowledge]
- 6- and 8-year gimnaziums are concentrated historically in Budapest, county seats, and larger towns; they are rarely found in villages or small settlements, reflecting settlement-network geography and school-size economies. [evidence: moderate — Hungarian education-geography literature; domain knowledge]
- Children in rural and small-town settlements who pass entrance examinations at ages 10–12 face daily commuting or boarding, creating materially different logistical and welfare burdens compared to standard 14+ year-old commuting patterns. [evidence: weak — Geographic feasibility inference; domain knowledge]
- Hungarian education-research consensus (e.g., KRTK strands) links early selection via 8- and 6-year tracks to sorting of higher-SES and higher-achieving students away from comprehensive primary cohorts, concentrating disadvantage in remaining schools. [evidence: moderate — Hungarian education-economics literature (KRTK); domain knowledge]
- Smaller Hungarian school settlements face rising cost-per-pupil above staffing thresholds and subject-specialist availability constraints, limiting realistic capacity to host or sustain selective academic tracks locally. [evidence: moderate — School-size economics literature; domain knowledge]

## Interpretation
Hungary faces a structural mismatch: declining cohorts require consolidation, yet the 8-year track (entry at age 10) exists in a distributed maintainer landscape (state, church, foundation) where no uniform policy mandate takes direct effect. The concentration of selective secondary schools in urban areas, combined with genuine school-size economics, means rural children who pass early exams incur commuting or boarding costs urban peers avoid. Research consensus suggests this sorting mechanism (whether intended or incidental) reinforces socio-economic segregation. Abolishing or radically restructuring the 8-year track is politically difficult given entrenched stakeholder preferences and the need to manage a contracting network; a phase-in of reform toward later (age 14) entry is feasible but requires transparent cost-transfer dialogue, building-stock investments in smaller towns, and teacher-deployment planning.

## Assumptions
- The 8-year and 6-year gimnazium system will continue to exist in some form through the decade unless an explicit legislative phase-out is mandated; no automatic structural convergence is assumed. [assumption]
- School building stock in smaller towns remains largely state-owned or church-maintained, and capital investment capacity is constrained by current fiscal and governance arrangements. [assumption]
- Teacher recruitment and retention constraints will continue; early-selective-track staffing competes directly with general-school staffing for specialists. [assumption]

## Position
Hungary should phase out the 8-year gimnazium track over 10–15 years, consolidating academic selection at age 14, while simultaneously investing in building stock, teacher deployment, and transparent cost-sharing mechanisms for affected small-town and rural school networks to mitigate equity and operational risks.

## Uncertainties
- Current school-by-school enrollment and building-condition inventory for 6- and 8-year track facilities across Hungary is not available; cannot accurately model closure/consolidation feasibility town-by-town without this data. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Publication of a detailed KSH or education ministry database of 6/8-year track school counts, enrollment projections, and building-asset condition surveys by settlement size and maintainer type.)
- Commuting distance and time burden for age 10–12 entrants to selective schools from rural/small-town origins is not quantified; cannot model welfare or retention trade-offs without empirical commuting data. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Survey or ministry dataset on commuting patterns, travel time, and boarding/family-relocation incidence for 8-year track students from non-urban origins; cost and health impact analysis.)
- Mechanism and timeline for transferring or closing 8-year track capacity in smaller towns without triggering political backlash or unmanaged student displacement is unclear; stakeholder-tolerance constraints are not empirically mapped. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Deliberative citizen-panel and stakeholder-consultation process (CNDP-style) documenting local preferences, school-maintainer capacity, and feasibility boundaries for phased reform; cost-allocation and communication strategy testing.)
