# Expert analysis: polish_reform

## Findings (evidence)
- Poland's 1999 reform postponed academic tracking from age 10–12 to age 15, extending comprehensive education through the gimnazjum (lower secondary school). [evidence: strong — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD]
- Poland's PISA scores rose substantially between 1999 and 2012, with analyses attributing part of the gain to delayed selection. [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD]
- Poland reversed the 1999 reform in 2016–2019, restoring early tracking options (6- and 8-year grammar schools), primarily for political reasons rather than evidence-based grounds. [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD; model knowledge on Polish policy context]
- Early selection (age 10–12) is correlated in comparative research with larger achievement gaps, especially for low socio-economic status pupils, though causation is not definitively isolated. [evidence: moderate — model knowledge; international comparative PISA/TIMSS patterns]
- Hungary maintains a 6- and 8-year grammar school (gimnazium) system with early selection and admission examinations (felvételi vizsga), inherited from pre-1999 Polish and Central European tradition. [evidence: strong — Hungarian education law and public knowledge]
- Hungary's school choice (szabad iskolaválasztás) system allows selection by examination, concentrating advantage in higher-SES families and contributing to segregation between schools. [evidence: moderate — model knowledge; Hungarian education policy literature]
- Poland's 2016–2019 reversal did not produce published longitudinal outcome data on achievement gaps or social mobility within the timeframe available. [evidence: weak — absence of cited evidence in curated sources; model knowledge]
- Teacher shortage and capacity constraints exist in Hungary, creating implementation pressure regardless of tracking policy choice. [evidence: moderate — model knowledge on Hungarian education system constraints]

## Interpretation
Poland's 1999 reform—delaying academic selection to age 15—was associated with improved equity-relevant outcomes (PISA gains, narrower achievement gaps); its 2016–2019 reversal was driven by political factors, not evidence. Hungary faces a parallel decision: whether to phase out or retain early tracking via 6- and 8-year grammar schools. The Polish experience suggests delayed selection correlates with better equity, but Hungarian context (school choice culture, teacher shortages, entrenched examination system) differs, and post-reversal Polish evidence on consequences remains sparse. Any Hungarian reform should clarify whether the goal is equity, social mobility, or school autonomy—different goals may require different mechanisms.

## Assumptions
- Polish reform design and outcomes are causally relevant to Hungarian context—that is, delays in selection in Hungary would produce similar equity effects as in Poland. [assumption]
- The achievement-gap measures used in Polish PISA analysis (socio-economic status gaps, reading/mathematics disparities) apply with the same sensitivity to Hungarian pupil populations. [assumption]
- Political reversal in Poland (2016–2019) did not occur because empirical evidence had accumulated that early tracking was beneficial—it reversed despite evidence suggesting the opposite. [assumption]
- Administrative capacity and teacher supply constraints in Hungary are endogenous and can be partially addressed by reform design (e.g., phase-in timelines). [assumption]

## Position
Hungary should pilot a phase-in of delayed selection (moving the first admission checkpoint from age 10–12 to age 14–15) in volunteer districts, measuring equity and achievement effects before national rollout, and coupling any reform with teacher-supply and curriculum investments to ensure implementation does not fail on capacity grounds.

## Uncertainties
- What is the magnitude and distribution of Poland's PISA score improvement attributable to the 1999 reform versus other concurrent policy changes, demographic shifts, or economic growth? (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Causal decomposition studies isolating the reform effect from confounders using regression discontinuity, synthetic control, or instrumental variable methods.)
- Why did Poland reverse the 1999 reform in 2016–2019? Was it due to evidence of unintended consequences, political capture by private grammar school interests, or ideological preference for selection? (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Interviews with Polish policymakers and legislative historians; analysis of lobby group positions and funding; longitudinal administrative data on outcomes post-reversal (2019–2023).)
- How would a Hungarian phase-in of delayed selection interact with the existing school choice (szabad iskolaválasztás) and admission examination (felvételi vizsga) system? Would it reduce segregation or merely redistribute it? (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Administrative simulation or pilot data on how selective secondary grammar schools would fill capacity under delayed-selection rules; tracking of application patterns and acceptance rates by SES quintile before and during pilot.)
- What is the teacher-supply constraint in Hungary's secondary education system, and would removing early-selection pressure on grammar schools exacerbate or mitigate teacher shortages? (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Current and projected teacher shortage data by school type and region; forecasts of demand under alternative tracking scenarios; analysis of whether grammar school status attracts or repels experienced teacher recruitment.)
- Would Hungary's commitment to the school-choice principle and transparent admission criteria permit a credible, acceptable shift to later selection, or would it require legislative change that faces political veto? (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Legal and constitutional review of school-choice and selection rights under Hungarian law; stakeholder interviews with school maintainers (fenntartók), parent associations, and private grammar school operators; draft legislative scenarios and feasibility assessments.)
