# Expert analysis: portuguese_reform

## Findings (evidence)
- Portugal reduced early school leavers from 14.7% (2007) to 6.2% (2023) using non-structural reforms: curriculum standards, school clustering, targeted support (TEIP), and retention reduction, without changing the age of initial selection. [evidence: strong — Eurostat/INE; OECD PISA trend reports 2006–2015; Crato 2020; EACEA Youth Wiki]
- Portugal maintains comprehensive schooling to age 15 with no early tracking; TEIP is a territorial priority programme providing additional resources and pedagogical support to disadvantaged schools. [evidence: moderate — OECD 2014 policy outlook; Portuguese Ministry of Education documentation]
- TIMSS 2023 results show a concerning decline in grade 8 mathematics and science achievement in Portugal; the percentage of low-SES students with high performance fell from 21% (2015) to 16% (2024). [evidence: moderate — Education & Training Monitor 2025; TIMSS 2023 data]
- Portugal historically experienced one of the OECD's highest grade repetition rates; later retention-reduction reforms were designed to address this structural inefficiency. [evidence: moderate — OECD comparative education statistics; domain knowledge]
- One peer-reviewed analysis of 15 years of Portuguese national exam data raised questions about whether TEIP's compensatory support reduced performance gaps in practice. [evidence: weak — MDPI 2024 study (national exam trends 2009–2024)]
- Hungary operates 6- and 8-year grammar schools (hatosztályos and nyolcosztályos gimnazium) with admission examinations at ages 12 and 10 respectively, combined with school choice, creating early, selective tracking distinct from Portugal's model. [evidence: strong — Hungarian education law; public education statistics; OECD Education at a Glance]
- Portugal did not abolish grammar schools or late-entry selection but rather integrated standards-based curriculum, data-driven school support, and retention policy as complementary levers to improve population outcomes without wholesale structural change. [evidence: moderate — OECD 2014 policy outlook; Portuguese Ministry 3rd-generation TEIP documentation]

## Interpretation
Portugal's experience demonstrates that substantial equity and retention gains (8.5 percentage points over 16 years) are achievable without eliminating early selection, provided that *non-selective entry does not occur at an even earlier age*. The model rests on three pillars: (1) curriculum and assessment standardisation to set a common floor; (2) targeted, data-driven resource allocation to disadvantaged schools; (3) active reduction of grade repetition and dropout through pedagogical and social supports. Hungary's 6- and 8-year gymnasium system is structurally earlier and more selective than Portugal's pathway, and operates within a school-choice regime that Portuguese policy does not rely on to the same degree. The recent widening of low-SES achievement gaps in Portugal (TIMSS 2023), however, suggests that compensatory support alone may not be sufficient to offset selection effects if selection occurs at formative ages. Non-structural reform is a complement to, not a replacement for, decisions about the timing and intensity of academic selection.

## Assumptions
- That pedagogical and systemic support (standards, TEIP, retention reform) can be implemented in Hungary with similar rigour and data-infrastructure quality as in Portugal, which has national examinations and a centralised education monitoring system. [assumption]
- That non-structural reform can operate independently of decisions about selection age and intensity; in reality, early selection may offset some benefits of compensatory support. [assumption]
- That teacher capacity, school leadership quality, and curriculum-implementation fidelity in Hungary are sufficient to sustain a standards-based, targeted-support model without massive initial investment. [assumption]
- That reducing grade repetition in Hungary will not lower standards or mask unresolved learning deficits if coupled with pedagogical differentiation and early intervention. [assumption]
- That the Hungarian school-choice system allows for equitable resource distribution to TEIP-like programmes in the same way a more centralised system (like Portugal's) does. [assumption]
- That the underlying demand for early selection in Hungary (for parental aspirations, perceived school quality, and social status) will not erode the effectiveness of non-structural compensatory measures. [assumption]

## Position
Portugal's non-structural improvement path (standards, targeted support, retention reform) achieved measurable equity and retention gains without eliminating early selection, but this evidence does not settle whether Hungary should retain its earlier-age, higher-intensity 6- and 8-year grammar system, because the timing and selection intensity matter as much as support quality for long-term social mobility outcomes.

## Uncertainties
- Whether Portugal's non-structural model can be transplanted to Hungary's constitutionally protected school-choice regime without creating greater equity stratification, because choice and selection may reinforce each other. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A comparative study of equity outcomes in school-choice systems with and without compensatory targeted support (e.g. Netherlands, Czech Republic, Austria vs. Portugal); data on parental choice stratification by SES in Hungary.)
- Whether TEIP-like targeted support can meaningfully narrow achievement gaps if early selection (at ages 10–12) has already identified and separated cohorts by prior attainment; the question is whether the selection effect will dominate the support effect. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Longitudinal value-added analysis in systems with early selection: does within-track support narrow gaps, or do between-track gaps determine long-term inequality? Quasi-experimental evidence from countries that have moved selection age (Finland, Germany).)
- The actual current rate of grade repetition in Hungary and whether it is a primary driver of early school leaving or a symptom of early-age selection; Portugal's success may reflect reduction of a specific inefficiency that is not the same magnitude in Hungary. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: National comparative data on grade repetition rates Hungary vs. Portugal (OECD Education at a Glance); Hungarian cohort tracking study showing the causal pathway from selection to early school leaving.)
