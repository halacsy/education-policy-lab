# Expert analysis: international_comparison

## Findings (evidence)
- Cross-country difference-in-differences evidence shows earlier between-school tracking increases inequality of outcomes without raising mean achievement. [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006, Economic Journal]
- In Hungary, socio-economic status explains 19–25% of variance in PISA reading and mathematics scores, among the highest in the OECD; OECD average is 12–14%. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2012–2022 country notes]
- Hungary operates 6- and 8-year grammar schools (gimnaziumok) with entry via competitive admission examination, creating early selection at ages 10 and 12. [evidence: strong — Hungarian Education Act 2011; OECD Education Statistics]
- PISA gradient studies indicate that when early tracking is present, school type explains a larger share of achievement variance than in comprehensive systems, consistent with selection rather than school quality differences. [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA analytical reports 2015–2022; Hanushek & Woessmann 2011, Journal of Economic Literature]
- No randomized or quasi-experimental evidence currently available compares outcomes of early selection removal specifically in the Hungarian context. [evidence: weak — Model knowledge; Hungarian education policy literature]

## Interpretation
International evidence demonstrates a robust tradeoff: earlier between-school selection (characteristic of Hungary's 6/8-year gimnazium system) is associated with greater inequality without efficiency gains. Hungary's exceptionally high SES-achievement gradient (nearly double the OECD average) suggests that selection mechanisms amplify rather than mitigate disadvantage. The causal mechanism identified in difference-in-differences studies is selection effect—peer composition sorting rather than differential teaching quality. However, the transferability of these findings to Hungary depends on unmeasured institutional and labour-market factors specific to the Hungarian context.

## Assumptions
- The relationship between early tracking and inequality observed in OECD countries is not nullified by Hungary's distinctive free school choice (szabad iskolaválasztás) regime or by the high degree of academic selection within state schools. [assumption]
- Selection effects (composition) can be distinguished from school-quality effects in the Hungarian context using the same methods that prove valid in international comparisons. [assumption]
- The absence of Hungarian-specific quasi-experimental evidence does not invalidate the policy relevance of cross-country findings, as the underlying mechanisms (peer effects, teacher sorting, curriculum differentiation) operate in Hungary as elsewhere. [assumption]
- The labour market returns to different school types (gimnazium vs. vocational paths) have not materially changed in ways that would reverse the equity implications of early selection. [assumption]

## Position
Hungary's 6- and 8-year grammar school system, implemented via competitive admission at ages 10 and 12, amplifies socio-economic inequality without raising mean achievement, and this mechanism is transferable from international evidence unless Hungary's institutional context (labour-market structure, school-choice regime, or within-school tracking) substantially decouples selection from segregation.

## Uncertainties
- The exact magnitude of the selection effect (vs. unmeasured quality differences) in Hungary's 6/8-year sistema is not quantified. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: A Hungarian panel study (e.g., longitudinal tracking of admitted vs. near-threshold rejected cohorts) or a decomposition of PISA score variance by intake SES and school type, controlling for prior attainment.)
