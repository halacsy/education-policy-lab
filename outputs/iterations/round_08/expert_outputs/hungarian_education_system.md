# Expert analysis: hungarian_education_system
## Findings (evidence)

- Approximately 8–12% of each cohort enters 6- or 8-year gimnazium tracks via centralized written examination at age 10–12 [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics].

- Admission to selective tracks is strongly skewed toward high-SES and urban families [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics].

- Early selection through selective tracks does not operate in isolation; free school choice and catchment-based sorting already generate significant between-school stratification in urban primary education [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013; Berenyi-Berkovits-Erőss school choice studies].

- Abolition of early selective tracks alone would not eliminate between-school sorting, since catchment residence and parental choice mechanisms remain [evidence: moderate — same sources].

- Since 2013, the majority of state schools operate under state school district centre (tankerulet) governance; churches and foundations maintain and are expanding their school operations, fragmenting structural uniformity [evidence: strong — Public education act amendments; maintainer statistics].

- Selective tracks create two distinct pedagogical and curricular pathways that diverge sharply in pace, content depth, and peer composition from age 10/12 onwards [evidence: moderate — domain knowledge; formal comparative curriculum analysis not cited in curated sources].

## Interpretation

Hungary's early academic selection system operates within a multi-layered sorting landscape. The 6/8-year gimnazium system is not the sole mechanism of educational stratification; it interacts with school choice, catchment effects, and maintainer diversity to produce cumulative inequality. Removing selective tracks without addressing choice and catchment dynamics risks displacing rather than eliminating sorting, while maintainer fragmentation (state, church, foundation) complicates any uniform reform mandate.

The strength of SES skew in track entry suggests selection reflects both genuine aptitude differences and unequal access to exam preparation, information, and cultural capital. Whether selection identifies merit or reproduces advantage remains contested empirically.

## Assumptions

- Early selection is justified primarily on pedagogical grounds (pace differentiation) rather than on equity grounds [assumption].
- Structural reform (track abolition) is considered separately from choice architecture reform [assumption].
- The curated sources represent current policy-relevant evidence; recent legislative or demographic shifts may not be fully captured [assumption].

## Position

Hungary should not abolish selective tracks unilaterally; instead, it should simultaneously tighten socioeconomic equity filters in track admission (transparent, SES-aware quotas or weighted selection), weaken school choice and catchment mechanisms that duplicate sorting effects, and impose uniform pedagogical and equity accountability across all maintainer types (state, church, foundation).
