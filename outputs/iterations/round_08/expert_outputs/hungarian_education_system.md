# Expert analysis: hungarian_education_system

## Findings (evidence)
- Approximately 8–12% of each age cohort enters 6- or 8-year grammar school (gimnazium) tracks, selected at age 10 or 12 primarily through a centralized written admission examination. [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics]
- Entrants to selective grammar school tracks are strongly skewed toward high socio-economic status (SES) and urban families. [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics]
- Free school choice combined with early selection produces significant between-school sorting already at primary level in urban areas. [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013; Berenyi-Berkovits-Erőss school choice studies]
- Abolishing early selective tracks alone would not eliminate between-school sorting, which persists through catchment areas and parental choice. [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013; Berenyi-Berkovits-Erőss school choice studies]
- Since 2013, the majority of Hungarian state schools are operated by state school district centres (tankerületi központok); churches and civil foundations maintain and operate a growing share of schools. [evidence: strong — Public education act amendments; maintainer statistics]
- The diversity of school maintainers (state, church, foundation) complicates the implementation of any uniform structural policy mandate for early selection reform. [evidence: moderate — Public education act amendments; maintainer statistics]

## Interpretation
Hungary's grammar school system at age 10 or 12 creates measurable sorting by family background before the age of 13, reinforced by catchment and choice mechanisms that persist regardless of track structure. The national picture is fragmented: state schools operate under tankerület governance, while religious and foundation schools follow different accountability rules. Any reform must therefore address not only selection criteria but also the interaction between school choice, catchment area boundaries, and multiple maintainer systems. Evidence shows that track abolition alone cannot deliver equity if the underlying choice and geographic sorting mechanisms remain unchanged.

## Assumptions
- Current admission examination score distributions accurately reflect measurable academic readiness rather than test-specific preparation advantages by SES. [assumption]
- School maintainers (state, church, foundation) would accept a uniform reform framework that constrains their current admission autonomy. [assumption]
- The performance gap between selective and comprehensive school students is primarily attributable to selection effects rather than genuine school quality differences. [assumption]

## Position
Early selection in Hungary's 6- and 8-year grammar schools should be reformed toward later age-based entry (age 14+) or abolished in favour of comprehensive tracks, while simultaneously strengthening catchment-area equity measures and aligning church and foundation school admission rules with public school standards.

## Uncertainties
- The causal impact of track abolition alone on long-term social mobility and earnings outcomes is not documented in Hungarian longitudinal research; international evidence is mixed. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Longitudinal administrative data linking school track selection, graduation, employment, and earnings outcomes for Hungarian birth cohorts born 2000–2010.)
- The willingness and fiscal capacity of church and foundation maintainers to adopt uniform non-selective admission rules under a reformed framework is untested. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Structured consultation or scenario modeling with representatives of the three largest church maintainers and a sample of foundation schools, documenting their stated conditions for accepting entry restrictions.)
