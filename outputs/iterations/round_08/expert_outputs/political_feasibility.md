# Expert analysis: political_feasibility

## Findings (evidence)

- Urban middle-class parents regard 8- and 6-year gimnazium places as key mobility and insurance assets; prior attempts to curtail these structures have met intense, well-organized resistance. [evidence: moderate — domestic press and stakeholder analyses, 1990s-2010s]

- Poland's 1999 reform postponed tracking to age 15; PISA scores rose substantially by 2012, with analyses attributing part of the gain to delayed selection. [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD]. The reform was reversed in 2016-2019 for political reasons.

- **TRANSFERABILITY NOTE**: Poland's gains occurred alongside broader curriculum and teacher-training changes in a near-uniform public system without a large church/foundation maintainer sector. The 2016-2019 political reversal shows that even measurable academic gains are not self-protecting without sustained political consensus—a critical constraint for Hungary's context.

- Stakeholder discourse (episodic memory, rounds 6–7) reveals conditional support patterns: teachers, young-family parents, and equality advocates will soften or reverse opposition **only if** reform is bundled with:
  - Credible pilot validation by Oktatási Hivatal (teachers' requirement)
  - Phase-down compressed to 3–4 years, not 8–10 (time-bounds for workforce planning)
  - Constitutional or special-budget locks on targeted funding (survives fiscal discretion)
  - Verifiable SES-proxy design and pilot data showing gaming resistance (parent requirement)
  - Multi-party political pact with measurable commitments in year 1–2 (durability requirement)

## Interpretation

Stakeholders are not inherently opposed to consolidation and delayed selection; they are opposed to unilateral, under-resourced, or politically unstable implementation. The Poland case reinforces this: structural gains exist, but reversal occurred because consensus was never locked in legislatively or politically.

Hungary's conditions suggest durability requires **simultaneous bundling** of three elements:
1. **Institutional**: pilot validation, teacher retraining, SES-proxy testing
2. **Financial**: constitutional or legislative guarantees (not discretionary budget items)
3. **Political**: multi-party endorsement and public commitment

Absent this bundling, the reform replicates Poland's 1999 strengths (delayed selection, measurable gains) but inherits its 2016-2019 fragility (political reversal under pressure).

## Assumptions

- Oktatási Hivatal has or can acquire capacity to conduct large-scale pilot validation [assumption]
- Constitutional budget floors in Hungary are credible, durable constraints on fiscal consolidation [assumption]
- Multi-party consensus on education reform is achievable despite current government-opposition polarization [assumption]
- SES-proxy mechanisms can be designed and implemented to resist institutional gaming under Hungarian conditions [assumption]

## Position

Hungary can implement consolidation and delayed selection, but only as a bundled reform with constitutional financial guarantees, pilot-validated selection mechanisms, 3–4 year phase-down, and multi-party legislative consensus—not as a standalone structural change.

## Uncertainties

- Capacity of Oktatási Hivatal for this scale of pilot validation [confidence: medium; would be reduced by: public audit of their recent pilot capacity and current staffing]
- Achievability of multi-party political pact given current polarization [confidence: low; would be reduced by: joint public statements from government and major opposition parties acknowledging shared education goals]
- Durability of constitutional budget commitments under fiscal crisis or EU/IMF consolidation pressure [confidence: medium; would be reduced by: historical analysis of Hungary's constitutional budget commitments during the 2008–2011 crisis]
- SES-proxy gaming risk under Hungarian administrative and social-service conditions [confidence: low; would be reduced by: 18–24 month pilot data from matched schools showing SES proxy stability and absence of informal selection proliferation]
