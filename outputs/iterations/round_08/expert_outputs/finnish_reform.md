# Expert analysis: finnish_reform

## Findings (evidence)
- Finland's peruskoulu comprehensive-school reform (phased 1972–1977) eliminated early academic selection and is associated with reduced dependence of student outcomes on family socio-economic status. [evidence: moderate — Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009]
- The Finnish reform bundled comprehensive organisation (single common school through age 16) with simultaneous investment in teacher training, support services (special education, counselling), and curriculum standardisation; these elements are analytically inseparable in outcome measurement. [evidence: moderate — Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009; domain literature on Finnish education reform implementation]
- Early selection in Hungarian grammar schools (hatosztályos and nyolcosztályos gimnazium at ages 12 and 10) and the central admission examination (központi írásbeli) create measurable composition and sorting effects that are difficult to decompose from teaching quality or resource variation. [evidence: moderate — Domain knowledge of Hungarian educational structure; PISA and Hungarian educational research literature]
- The equity effect of comprehensive organisation depends critically on whether support services, teacher capacity, and curriculum breadth are held equal across schools; without these preconditions, comprehensive structure alone does not guarantee reduced outcome inequality. [evidence: moderate — International comparative education literature; Finnish implementation context]
- Hungary's 'free school choice' (szabad iskolaválasztás) system and selective school admission create institutional conditions that differ structurally from Finland's catchment-area based comprehensive model, reducing direct policy transferability. [evidence: strong — Hungarian education policy structure; OECD education reviews]
- No peer-reviewed large-scale study directly compares the effect of eliminating early selection in Hungary on equity outcomes, controlling for simultaneous changes in support services and teacher supply. [evidence: weak — Literature search; absence of evidence acknowledged]

## Interpretation
Finland's peruskoulu reform reduced outcome inequality by combining institutional change (ending early selection) with systemic investment in teacher quality, support services, and curricular breadth. The equity effect is not attributable to structure alone. Hungary faces a structurally different starting point: selective secondary grammar schools operate within a framework of free school choice, which amplifies sorting effects. Transferring the Finnish model would require not just ending early selection but simultaneously strengthening support capacity and equalising resources across all secondary schools—a bundled intervention that has not yet been piloted at scale in a setting similar to Hungary's institutional and labour-market context.

## Assumptions
- Equity gains from institutional reform are transferable across different free-choice and catchment-based school systems if underlying support capacity is held equal. [assumption]
- Hungarian secondary education can absorb broad curricular and pedagogical expectations across all track types without a period of quality degradation in other tracks. [assumption]
- The causal direction between comprehensive structure and equity is established; structure is not merely a correlate of unobserved factors (e.g. political stability, teacher professionalism) that drive equity. [assumption]
- Teacher supply in Hungary can be expanded and quality maintained simultaneously during a transition away from selective secondary entry. [assumption]

## Position
Hungary should pilot a phased elimination of early secondary selection (hatosztályos and nyolcosztályos gimnazium intake exams) paired with mandatory equal investment in universal support services and teacher capacity across all secondary-school types before scaling nationally, because the Finnish equity effect depended on systemic bundling, not structure alone.

## Uncertainties
- Does the political economy of Hungarian school maintainers (fenntartók) and teacher labour-market constraints allow simultaneous elimination of early selection and expansion of support services, or will a phase-out trigger resource deallocation from comprehensive schools? (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A detailed institutional mapping of fenntartó budgeting behaviour during previous secondary transitions (e.g. 2012 curriculum reform); cost accounting studies of support-service provision under different school-system configurations.)
- What is the quantified effect of admission examinations (központi írásbeli) versus school-based sorting on outcome inequality in Hungary, holding socio-economic status and prior attainment constant? (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Causal decomposition studies using quasi-experimental designs (regression discontinuity, propensity-score matching) comparing cohorts subject to different exam thresholds; longitudinal value-added analysis of schools with and without selective intake.)
- Would eliminating early selection in Hungary reduce the incentive for middle-class families to exit the public system to private or specialist schools, or would it trigger further stratification through school choice? (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: School-choice behaviour studies before and after comparable policy reforms in Central European contexts; household preference surveys conditioning on removal of early selection.)
