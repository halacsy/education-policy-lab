# Policy scenarios

## S1 — Admission reform within the current structure
**Goal**
Reduce the socio-economic skew of entrants to 6- and 8-year gimnazium tracks without changing the tracks' existence, legal basis, or entry ages.

**Mechanism**
- Replacing or supplementing the central written admission exam with a less coaching-sensitive assessment format would reduce the weight of family test-prep resources in selection, though no Hungarian evidence confirms any specific alternative format (aptitude, portfolio, lottery-among-qualified) resists SES advantage in practice. [evidence: weak]
- Introducing SES-weighted quotas or bonus-point schemes at admission could mechanically raise the share of disadvantaged entrants, following logic used in some international affirmative-admission designs, but no Hungarian pilot has tested this and a usable student-level SES register does not yet exist. [evidence: weak]
- Because free school choice and catchment-area effects independently drive segregation, admission-rule changes alone would not eliminate sorting even if they reduce the SES skew of exam-based entry specifically. [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
weak — The core mechanism (a less coachable, gaming-resistant admission design that measurably shifts SES composition) has no validated Hungarian instrument or pilot; supporting claims about exam-driven concentration are strong, but the reform lever itself is untested.

**Assumptions**
- That a usable, gaming-resistant SES proxy can be constructed and legally deployed at the point of application, despite Hungary currently lacking a student-level SES register.
- That uniform admission-equity rules can be applied across state, church, and foundation maintainers despite maintainer plurality being a structural, not merely administrative, complication documented across multiple expert findings.
- That an alternative assessment format would not simply shift, rather than reduce, the advantage of high-SES families (e.g. toward portfolio-building or interview-coaching markets).

**Expected benefits**
- Potentially higher share of disadvantaged entrants in selective tracks if a validated SES-sensitive admission mechanism is successfully designed and enforced. [evidence: weak]
- Avoids the legislative, transitional, and stakeholder-negotiation costs of abolishing or phasing down the tracks, since the structure itself remains intact. [evidence: moderate]

**Equity impact**
May modestly raise disadvantaged representation among applicants who qualify under new rules, but does nothing for the segregation driven by school choice and catchment effects outside the exam itself; middle-SES or rural pupils without qualifying SES-proxy status could face reduced access if quotas displace them, and non-applicants (those deterred by information costs, distance, or awareness gaps) remain entirely outside any benefit.

**Cost categories**
- SES-proxy data infrastructure: low-to-medium cost for a verified self-report workflow versus high cost for an administrative data-linkage/register build, with no current budget line for either.
- Assessment redesign and psychometric validation (medium, one-off, multi-year).
- Legal review and potential litigation exposure over quota or proxy mechanisms (medium, contingent).

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Interior / education authority (Oktatási Hivatal) — Commission design and small-scale piloting of an alternative, less-coaching-sensitive assessment format and a candidate SES-proxy, explicitly as an unvalidated pilot, not a national instrument. (timeline: year 1-3)
- Independent research institute (e.g. KRTK) with Ministry — Evaluate pilot outcomes for validity, gaming-resistance, and SES-composition shift; publish findings before any legal codification. (timeline: year 3-4)
- Parliament / Ministry of Education — If pilot validity is confirmed, legislate uniform admission-equity rules applicable across state, church, and foundation maintainers, with explicit enforcement mechanism for non-state maintainers. (timeline: year 4-5)
- Klebelsberg centres and non-state maintainers — National rollout of the validated admission mechanism, with monitoring of compliance and displacement effects by maintainer type. (timeline: year 5-6)

**Political risks**
- Non-state maintainers may resist uniform equity mandates; maintainer fragmentation is treated in the expert record as a structural complication, not merely a difficult-but-solvable administrative matter, so resistance risk is at least moderate rather than weak.
- Urban middle-class parents who view selective tracks as a mobility and status asset may organise against any mechanism perceived to reduce their children's admission odds.
- Because implementation proceeds by ministerial decree rather than statute in the current environment, a subsequent government could reverse the mechanism within a single electoral cycle.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether any alternative assessment design (aptitude, portfolio, lottery-among-qualified) actually resists SES advantage in the Hungarian context remains unverified. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A multi-year Hungarian pilot comparing admission outcomes and SES composition under alternative assessment formats versus the current exam, with independent evaluation.)
- Whether a usable, legally defensible, gaming-resistant SES proxy can be built given the current absence of a student-level SES register. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A funded feasibility study and small-scale data-linkage pilot assessing accuracy, gaming resistance, and legal defensibility of candidate SES-proxy designs.)

## S2 — Gradual phase-down of 6/8-year entry places
**Goal**
Progressively reduce the number of 6- and 8-year gimnazium entry places over a multi-year timetable, while explicitly acknowledging that non-state maintainers may absorb displaced demand and that the share of the cohort exposed to early sorting overall may not fall proportionally.

**Mechanism**
- Legislating year-on-year caps on new 8-year and then 6-year cohort admissions reduces state-sector selective capacity, but the legal basis only supports that phase-out requires legislative change, not that a specific cap trajectory is administratively straightforward. [evidence: strong]
- Reduced state-sector selective capacity is expected to lower average exposure to early sorting only if church, foundation, and private maintainers do not expand their own selective offerings to absorb displaced demand; this offsetting risk is real but unquantified. [evidence: weak]
- Residual selective places are likely to become more SES-skewed as competition intensifies for a shrinking pool, consistent with general selection-effect logic though not separately tested for phase-down scenarios specifically. [evidence: weak]

**Evidence status**
weak — The legal mechanism for reducing places is strong, but the causal chain from fewer state-sector places to lower cohort-wide early-sorting exposure is contested because maintainer-plurality offsetting behaviour is neither ruled out nor quantified.

**Assumptions**
- That church and foundation maintainers can be regulated or incentivized not to expand selective places to offset the state-sector reduction, despite maintainer plurality being a structural constraint on uniform mandates.
- That the phase-down timetable can proceed on a fixed legislative schedule despite acknowledged uncertainty about non-state absorption dynamics.

**Expected benefits**
- Reduces the absolute number of state-sector selective places, which is a directly legislatable and monitorable lever, independent of whether overall cohort exposure falls proportionally. [evidence: strong]
- May incrementally improve peer composition in urban general-school upper grades if displaced high-achieving pupils remain in the comprehensive stream, though this rests on the peer-effect mechanism, which the international literature treats as contested and Hungarian-specific evidence is absent. [evidence: contested]

**Equity impact**
Residual selective places likely become more SES-skewed as competition intensifies for fewer places, and lower-SES pupils in general schools who previously might have gained access to selective seats lose that access without receiving any compensating investment under this scenario alone; if non-state maintainers absorb displaced demand, cohort-wide early-sorting exposure may not fall at all, meaning the equity gain is conditional and unguaranteed rather than assured.

**Cost categories**
- Compensatory funding or capacity controls for general schools absorbing retained high-achievers and displaced pupils during the phase-down (medium-to-high, multi-year, currently unbudgeted).
- Monitoring and regulatory-lever infrastructure to track non-state maintainer intake for offsetting expansion (medium, recurring).
- Legal and administrative costs of legislating year-on-year admission caps and managing transitional guarantees for currently enrolled pupils (medium, one-off).

**Implementation steps**
- Parliament — Legislate a fixed multi-year schedule of year-on-year caps on new 8-year, then 6-year, cohort admissions in state-maintained schools, with transitional guarantees for enrolled pupils. (timeline: year 1)
- Ministry of Education / Klebelsberg centres — Implement the first cohort-cap reduction and establish a named set of regulatory levers (e.g. capital-grant conditionality, per-pupil funding adjustments, licensing review) to monitor and, if needed, discourage offsetting expansion by non-state maintainers. (timeline: year 1-2)
- Ministry of Education, general-school maintainers — Roll out compensatory funding and capacity-building for general schools receiving displaced and retained pupils, phased in step with each admission-cap reduction. (timeline: year 2-6)
- Independent monitoring body (e.g. KRTK or Oktatási Hivatal research unit) — Publish annual reports on non-state maintainer intake shifts and cohort-wide early-sorting exposure to determine whether the phase-down is achieving its stated goal or being offset. (timeline: year 2-8)

**Political risks**
- Urban middle-class families and school maintainers may lobby to slow or halt the cap schedule once early cohort effects become visible.
- Non-state maintainers have both the incentive and, given maintainer plurality, plausible latitude to expand selective offerings, which would blunt the policy's equity intent without violating its letter.
- Because the schedule proceeds by statute, reversal requires legislative repeal rather than mere decree change, offering more durability than S1 but still vulnerable to a parliamentary majority change during the multi-year rollout.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether non-state maintainers will expand selective places to absorb demand displaced by the state-sector cap, offsetting the intended reduction in cohort-wide early-sorting exposure. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Annual administrative tracking of non-state maintainer enrollment by track type during the first two cohort-cap cycles, compared against a pre-registered counterfactual projection.)
- Whether the peer-composition benefit for comprehensive-stream pupils materializes at all, given the contested status of the underlying peer-effect mechanism. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A Hungarian quasi-experimental study comparing achievement and motivation in comprehensive-school cohorts before and after a phased admission-cap reduction in a pilot region.)

## S3 — Comprehensive school to age 14 (structural reform)
**Goal**
Abolish 6- and 8-year gimnazium entry and consolidate academic preparation into a comprehensive school structure through age 14, moving Hungary's selection age closer to the later-tracking pattern associated in cross-national research with lower SES gradients, while explicitly not claiming this alone would align Hungary with an international consensus, since the cited cross-national evidence supports association, not a guarantee of transferable causal effect.

**Mechanism**
- Cross-national difference-in-differences evidence associates earlier between-school tracking with greater inequality of outcomes without raising mean achievement, but this evidence establishes a general cross-national association, not that abolishing Hungary's specific 6/8-year system would produce the same effect size in Hungary's distinct institutional context. [evidence: moderate]
- Removing the age-10/12 admission examinations would eliminate one specific sorting channel, but because free school choice and catchment-area effects operate independently, sorting is likely to migrate into school choice, non-state maintainers, and residential/catchment mechanisms rather than disappear. [evidence: moderate]
- Poland's 1999 postponement of tracking to age 15 correlates with PISA score gains attributed partly to delayed selection, offering a relevant but not decisive precedent given Poland's later reversal for political, not evidence-based, reasons. [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
contested — The equity direction of delayed/comprehensive tracking is moderately supported cross-nationally, but Hungary-specific causal evidence is absent, the sorting-migration risk is substantial, and the Polish precedent demonstrates political reversibility rather than settled success.

**Assumptions**
- That sorting does not simply migrate to school choice, non-state maintainers, catchment effects, or private tutoring markets to the point of neutralizing the equity gain — an assumption directly in tension with this scenario's own equity_impact, political_risks, and uncertainties, which identify exactly this migration as the largest risk.
- That teacher supply can be expanded and redeployed within the implementation timeline despite Hungary's documented aging workforce and acute regional shortages.
- That a multi-party political pact securing statutory (not decree-based) commitment can be negotiated and sustained across electoral cycles, given the Polish reversal precedent and Hungary's documented post-2010 veto-player fragmentation.

**Expected benefits**
- Potentially the largest reduction among the four scenarios in the specific sorting channel operated by admission examinations, since this is the only scenario that removes the exam-based mechanism entirely rather than modifying or shrinking it. [evidence: weak]
- Aligns Hungary's selection age with systems associated cross-nationally with lower SES-achievement gradients, though this benefit is conditional on sorting not migrating to other channels and on complementary investment, neither of which is guaranteed by structural change alone. [evidence: contested]

**Equity impact**
This scenario removes the exam-based sorting channel most directly among the four options, but the largest identified risk is that sorting relocates into school choice, non-state maintainer expansion, catchment-area effects, and private tutoring markets, meaning the net aggregate equity gain is uncertain rather than clearly the largest among scenarios; whether net equity improves depends on unresolved questions about migration magnitude and complementary investment.

**Cost categories**
- Early diagnostic and remedial support system build-out (high, recurring; no numeric fiscal range or duration currently specified).
- Teacher retraining for heterogeneous, mixed-ability classrooms (high, one-off plus recurring; capacity-constrained by documented aging workforce and regional shortages).
- Building and network reconfiguration to consolidate 6/8-year track facilities into comprehensive schools (high, one-off, multi-year, unquantified against demographic decline savings).
- Legal, legislative, and transitional-guarantee costs for currently enrolled pupils (medium, one-off).

**Implementation steps**
- Parliament — Legislate abolition of 8-year, then 6-year, gimnazium admission, with staggered notice periods and transitional guarantees for enrolled pupils, contingent on securing a cross-party pact. (timeline: year 1)
- Ministry of Education, with independent monitoring body — Establish a segregation-tracking dashboard measuring sorting migration into school choice, non-state maintainers, and catchment effects, published annually to test whether the equity gain is being realized or offset. (timeline: year 1-8)
- Ministry of Education, teacher-training institutions — Begin a staged, capacity-realistic teacher-retraining programme for heterogeneous classrooms, explicitly scoped against documented workforce-aging and regional-shortage constraints rather than an idealized timeline; initial phase targets high-shortage regions with additional incentive funding, not simultaneous national coverage. (timeline: year 2-8)
- Klebelsberg centres, church and foundation maintainers — Consolidate building stock and reassign staff cohort-by-cohort as gimnazium entry cohorts phase out, coordinated across maintainer types via negotiated agreements rather than unilateral state mandate. (timeline: year 3-9)

**Political risks**
- Teacher unions may resist a retraining and workload-restructuring mandate; the only curated registry evidence directly supporting resistance risk is a sustained teacher shortage finding, not a specific discourse-analysis source, so this risk is asserted at moderate rather than strong confidence.
- Reversal risk is substantial and only partially mitigated: the Polish 1999-2016/19 reversal is moderate evidence that structural reforms of this kind are politically vulnerable, and this scenario's only entrenchment device — a cross-party political pact — is not yet specified with statutory safeguards, independent monitoring triggers, or budget-lock mechanisms that would make it credible.
- Urban middle-class families and government-dependent private/church schools (an estimated 25.5% enrolment share) represent a substantial veto-player bloc with both incentive and capacity to resist or circumvent abolition.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether sorting migrates into school choice, non-state maintainers, and catchment effects to an extent that neutralizes the equity gain from abolishing exam-based selection. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A decomposition analysis of segregation-index changes under scenarios that separately vary exam abolition and school-choice restriction, using administrative pupil-flow data.)
- Whether a cross-party statutory pact with credible entrenchment mechanisms (budget locks, independent monitoring triggers) can actually be negotiated in Hungary's current politically fragmented environment. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Structured interviews with parliamentary party leaders and ministry staff on concrete safeguard conditions, plus comparative case study of durable cross-party education pacts in other Central European states.)
- Whether teacher retraining capacity can realistically meet the timeline given the documented aging workforce and acute regional shortages, or whether the plan must be slowed or regionally sequenced further. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A current workforce-demand model by subject and region linked to teacher-preparation pipeline data and retraining-capacity estimates.)

## S4 — Keep the structure, compensate general schools (Portuguese-style package)
**Goal**
Retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system unchanged while implementing a bundled package of curriculum standardisation, targeted resource allocation to disadvantaged schools, and grade-retention reduction, aiming to improve outcomes for pupils outside the selective tracks without claiming this would neutralize the socio-economic inequities produced by age-10/12 selection, since the Portuguese evidence supports system-level improvement under a broad reform bundle without early selection, not offsetting inequities caused by selection itself.

**Mechanism**
- Portugal reduced early school leavers from 14.7% to 6.2% (2007-2023) using curriculum standards, school clustering, targeted support (TEIP), and retention reduction, without changing the age of initial selection, demonstrating that non-structural reform can produce system-level gains absent structural change. [evidence: strong]
- TEIP-style targeted support is expected to narrow achievement gaps in disadvantaged schools, but recent TIMSS 2023 data show a decline in the share of high-performing low-SES pupils in Portugal (21% to 16%, 2015-2024), and one peer-reviewed analysis of 15 years of Portuguese exam data raised questions about whether TEIP's compensatory support reduced performance gaps in practice. [evidence: contested]
- Whether TEIP-like targeted support can meaningfully narrow gaps if early selection has already separated cohorts by prior attainment is an open question, since the Portuguese model operates without Hungary's age-10/12 selection mechanism, meaning the mechanism's applicability to a system that retains early tracking is unverified. [evidence: weak]

**Evidence status**
contested — The Portuguese retention/dropout gains are strong evidence for a distinct system without early tracking; applying the same bundle atop Hungary's retained selection mechanism is unverified, and Portugal's own recent TIMSS decline weakens confidence that compensatory support alone offsets selection effects.

**Assumptions**
- That pedagogical and systemic support (standards, TEIP, retention reform) can be implemented in Hungary with similar rigour and data-infrastructure quality as in Portugal, despite Hungary lacking Portugal's centralised education monitoring system.
- That non-structural reform can operate independently of decisions about selection age and intensity, despite the scenario's own mechanism field noting that early selection may offset some benefits of compensatory support — a tension the scenario carries rather than resolves.
- That funding commitments for TEIP-style targeted programmes will be durable and not the first line cut during fiscal consolidation, despite political-risk evidence suggesting exactly this vulnerability.

**Expected benefits**
- May reduce early school leaving and grade repetition among pupils outside the selective tracks, following the direction of the Portuguese precedent, though Hungary's structural starting point (retained early selection) differs materially from Portugal's. [evidence: weak]
- Politically less disruptive than structural reform since it does not require abolishing or reducing gimnazium places, avoiding direct confrontation with maintainer and middle-class veto-player interests, though this should not be read as evidence the package is politically risk-free, since targeted funding lines are themselves identified as fiscally vulnerable to being cut first in consolidation. [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
Equity gains are bounded not only by the depth and permanence of funding commitments but also by the more fundamental risk that this scenario legitimizes and stabilizes the early selection mechanism while offering only compensatory, not structural, redress; Portugal's own recent widening of low-SES high-performer gaps under TIMSS 2023 suggests compensatory support may be insufficient once selection has already sorted cohorts, so this scenario could be equity-neutral or even equity-negative net of its entrenchment effect on the status quo.

**Cost categories**
- TEIP-style targeted funding line for disadvantaged schools (no numeric fiscal range available; identified as a likely first target for cuts during fiscal consolidation, making its effective cost highly uncertain and politically contingent).
- Curriculum standardisation and assessment infrastructure (medium, one-off plus recurring maintenance).
- Retention-reduction pedagogical and social support programmes, including remedial teaching and counselling (medium-to-high, recurring).

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education — Design and legislate a national curriculum-standardisation framework applicable to all secondary tracks, drawing on Portuguese standards design but adapted to Hungary's maintainer plurality. (timeline: year 1-2)
- Ministry of Education, Klebelsberg centres (KK) — Establish a TEIP-style targeted-funding mechanism for disadvantaged general schools, with an explicit statutory (not merely decree-based) funding floor to reduce first-line-cut vulnerability, and begin a phased, capacity-realistic rollout of grade-retention-reduction and diagnostic supports that is explicitly sequenced against, not assumed independent of, the documented teacher-shortage constraint — starting in a limited number of pilot regions rather than simultaneous national coverage. (timeline: year 2-4)
- Independent evaluation body — Conduct interim evaluation of pilot-region outcomes on early leaving, retention rates, and low-SES high-performer share, explicitly testing for the TIMSS-2023-style risk that compensatory support fails to offset selection effects, before any funding-floor renewal decision. (timeline: year 4-5)
- Ministry of Education, KK — Based on interim evaluation, scale the package nationally only in regions with demonstrated teacher-capacity readiness, deferring further rollout elsewhere pending workforce investment. (timeline: year 5-8)

**Political risks**
- Targeted TEIP-style funding lines are identified as likely first-line cuts during fiscal consolidation, meaning the scenario's central benefit-delivery mechanism is inherently budget-vulnerable, undermining any framing of the package as low-risk simply because it avoids structural confrontation.
- Teacher shortage constraints strongly undermine credible delivery of grade-retention-reduction and diagnostic-support rollout at the pace and scale the Portuguese model assumed, since Portugal's implementation context had different workforce conditions.
- By stabilizing the status quo selection mechanism, this scenario may face renewed equity-advocacy criticism if outcomes show the TIMSS-2023-style pattern of widening low-SES performance gaps despite the compensatory package, reducing political durability of continued funding.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether Hungary's documented teacher shortage will blunt Portuguese-style interventions to the point of nullifying expected benefits; this is genuinely unresolved and should not be labelled with high confidence given the interventions have not been piloted in Hungary. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A pilot-region implementation with explicit teacher-capacity tracking, comparing delivery fidelity and outcome gains against regions with better staffing ratios.)
- Whether TEIP-like targeted support can meaningfully narrow achievement gaps when early selection (ages 10-12) has already separated cohorts by prior attainment, given Portugal's own recent TIMSS-2023 decline in low-SES high performers. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Longitudinal value-added analysis in systems with early selection testing whether within-track support narrows gaps or whether between-track gaps dominate long-term inequality; quasi-experimental comparison with countries that shifted selection age.)
- Whether a statutory funding floor for TEIP-style programmes can actually be secured and sustained, given the political-risk finding that such lines are likely first-line cuts during fiscal consolidation. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Multi-year fiscal projections and stakeholder-stress testing with the Ministry of Finance on the durability of statutory education funding floors during past consolidation episodes.)
