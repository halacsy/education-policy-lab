# Critique: evidence_checker

## S0.mechanism
Objection: The first mechanism cites `legal_and_governance` for an audit using admission and PISA microdata, but `legal_and_governance` is not in the curated source registry. The registry supports `legal` only for statutory anchoring and legislative requirements, not data-linkage feasibility or whether an audit could isolate causal effects. The tag should not imply registry-backed support for audit feasibility.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Regrade the audit-feasibility claim as unsupported/model knowledge, or cite only that no registry source establishes audit feasibility; keep `legal [strong]` only for the statutory-change point.

## S1.mechanism
Objection: The first mechanism is tagged `strong` for the claim that SES-weighted admissions or quotas could shift entrant composition. The registry’s `gimn_share [strong]` supports only the existence and scale of early gimnazium entry and selection; it does not establish SES skew, and it does not support the causal effectiveness of SES weighting or quotas. At most, the diagnosis draws on `school_choice [moderate]` or `pisa_escs [strong]`, while the corrective mechanism remains untested.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Split the sentence: tag the current sorting diagnosis with `school_choice [moderate]`/`pisa_escs [strong]`, and tag SES weighting or quotas as `weak` or unsupported in Hungary.

## S2.evidence_status
Objection: The scenario says non-state maintainers “can and likely will expand” selective places, and uses that to downgrade the central equity claim. The registry’s `governance [strong]` supports maintainer fragmentation, and `school_choice [moderate]` supports sorting under choice, but no registry fact supports a likely behavioral response of church/foundation maintainers expanding selective capacity after state place cuts. The “likely will” claim is stronger than the cited support warrants.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Change “can and likely will expand” to “may expand,” mark it as an uncertainty, and cite `governance [strong]` only for the structural possibility created by maintainer fragmentation.

## S3.expected_benefits
Objection: The claim that abolition “removes the specific legal exposure created by age 10-12 differentiation under Article 66/a and EU equal-treatment scrutiny” cites Hungarian Constitutional Court and EU frameworks, but no such source appears in the curated registry. The registry’s `legal [strong]` supports that 6/8-year forms are anchored in law and phase-out requires legislative change; it does not support a legal-exposure or equal-treatment-risk claim.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Remove the legal-exposure benefit or reframe it as unregistered legal analysis/model knowledge; use `legal [strong]` only for the legislative process and grandfathering implications.
