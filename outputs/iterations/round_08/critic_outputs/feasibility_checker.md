# Critique: feasibility_checker

## S1.implementation_steps
Objection: The year 1-2 build-and-validate step for a provisional SES proxy is not feasible as written because the scenario itself says Hungary lacks a student-level SES register and may need welfare/tax/residency linkage. That is not just a technical task for Oktatási Hivatal; it requires legal basis, data-controller agreements, privacy review, verification workflows, appeals handling, and school-level administrative capacity before the first admission cycle. The evidence tags correctly mark efficacy as weak/contested, but the implementation schedule understates the legal and administrative lead time needed to create a usable proxy.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Split SES-proxy creation into a separate pre-pilot feasibility phase of 18-24 months, including legal authorization, data-sharing agreements, privacy review, appeals process, and administrative burden testing before any admissions use.

## S2.implementation_steps
Objection: The scenario depends on regulatory levers over non-state maintainers by year 1-2, but the record cited in the scenario says those levers do not currently exist and their legal feasibility is untested. That makes the phase-down schedule over-specified: monitoring non-state intake from year 2 onward is not a remedy unless the state already has enforceable authority to cap, condition, or sanction non-state expansion. The source support establishes maintainer fragmentation and legal entrenchment; it does not support rapid creation of enforceable cross-maintainer place caps.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Make non-state regulatory authority a gating condition before the state-side phase-down begins, or revise benefits downward to state-maintained schools only and explicitly model offsetting non-state expansion as the base case.

## S3.implementation_steps
Objection: The teacher retraining timeline is infeasible relative to the scenario's own evidence. It says full national coverage in 4 years is unlikely, yet still lists year 1-4 as the national retraining window while simultaneously abolishing entry through legislation in year 1-2. Given the cited strong evidence of systemic teacher shortages, especially in shortage regions and key subjects, this creates a capacity mismatch: pupils would be moved into heterogeneous comprehensive classrooms before the trained workforce exists.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add workforce-supply milestones as binding prerequisites for regional rollout, extend the national transition beyond 4 years, and sequence abolition only after minimum staffing/retraining thresholds are met by region.

## S4.implementation_steps
Objection: The national rollout of grade-retention reduction and diagnostic support in years 2-5 is administratively underpowered. The scenario acknowledges Hungary's teacher shortage will blunt delivery and that shortage-affected regions may lag beyond year 5, but it still treats national rollout as the core implementation path. Portugal-style targeted support is labor-intensive; the evidence cited supports Portugal's bundled gains, not Hungary's capacity to staff diagnostic/remedial systems at national scale under current shortages.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Replace national rollout with a staged capacity-based rollout, budget explicit additional specialist staffing and training, and make year 4-5 evaluation judge implementation fidelity before expansion rather than only funding durability and outcome trends.
