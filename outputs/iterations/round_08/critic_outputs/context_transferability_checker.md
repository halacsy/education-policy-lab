# Critique: context_transferability_checker

## S0.expected_benefits
Objection: The claim that an audit deadline could build buy-in and reduce “reversal risk of the kind seen in Poland” over-generalizes the Polish case. The registry’s Poland transferability note says Poland’s gains were not durably locked in even with a broader reform bundle and occurred in a near-uniform public maintainer landscape unlike Hungary’s fragmented church/foundation sector. S0 does not explain why a Hungarian audit-plus-deadline process would create the missing political durability or cross-maintainer settlement that Poland lacked.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Reframe this benefit as speculative and state the missing precondition directly: reversal risk would only fall if the audit process produced binding cross-party and cross-maintainer commitments, not merely a published evidence base or statutory deadline.

## S3.evidence_status
Objection: The field correctly notes that Finnish/Polish evidence is conditional, but it still frames the missing bundle mainly as “teacher-training and choice/catchment reforms.” For the Polish source, the registry’s transferability note also identifies a very different maintainer landscape: Poland’s reform operated in a near-uniform public system, while Hungary has a large church/foundation sector that can absorb or recreate selective demand. That precondition is not named in this field despite Poland being used as part of the support for delayed tracking.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Add that Polish transferability depends not only on curriculum/teacher/catchment complements, but also on Hungary’s ability to prevent non-state maintainers from recreating selective tracks outside the state mandate.

## S4.expected_benefits
Objection: The claim that Portugal shows “system-level PISA gains achievable without the political and legal disruption of structural reform” is too transferable as written. The registry’s Portugal note says Portugal did not have Hungary’s 6/8-year gimnazium selection structure in the first place, so it is evidence that non-structural levers can improve outcomes somewhere, not that they can deliver comparable gains while Hungary retains age-10/12 selection. The field mentions unknown gap-narrowing magnitude for TEIP, but not the deeper missing precondition: Portugal was not trying to offset this specific early-selection mechanism.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: State that Portugal supports only bounded system-improvement potential, and that comparable Hungarian gains would require showing that curriculum/TEIP/retention reforms can work despite an unchanged age-10/12 selective track structure.

## S4.assumptions
Objection: The assumption that Portuguese TEIP and curriculum mechanisms are transferable despite “differing selection structures and teacher-supply conditions” names the issue but does not specify the registry’s operative precondition. The Portugal transferability note says TEIP’s effect is entangled with the whole reform bundle and not isolated; therefore Hungary cannot assume a TEIP-style funding stream plus selected pieces of the package will reproduce Portugal’s gains unless the full bundle and delivery capacity are present.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Replace the broad transferability assumption with a conditional one: Portuguese-style gains require implementing the package as an integrated bundle with adequate delivery capacity, and Hungary’s teacher shortage makes that precondition uncertain or partly absent.
