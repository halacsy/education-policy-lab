# Critique: political_risk_checker

## S0.political_risks
Objection: The field names the risk that parties may prefer indefinite deferral, but it does not specify any entrenchment mechanism beyond the same statutory deadline whose enforceability the scenario itself rates as weak and unsupported by precedent. The cited political_feasibility evidence supports polarization and deferral incentives, not the claim that a deadline can survive repeal, extension, or noncompliance.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add a concrete enforcement design: automatic parliamentary debate, mandatory publication sanctions, judicially reviewable reporting duty, or budgetary trigger if the deadline is missed; otherwise state plainly that S0 has no credible anti-deferral protection.

## S1.political_risks
Objection: The opposition risk is understated because the scenario treats high-SES parent mobilization as a general resistance risk rather than as a direct threat to pilot validity. If organized families opt out, litigate, or concentrate pressure on the 2-3 pilot districts, the pilot may no longer test the reform mechanism but instead test political containment capacity. The moderate political_feasibility evidence supports organized historical resistance, not merely abstract concern.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Add pilot-specific opposition pathways: strategic nonparticipation, legal challenge under equality rules, pressure on maintainers to withdraw, and local political campaigning that biases the admitted cohort or prevents implementation.

## S3.political_risks
Objection: The reversal-risk discussion correctly invokes Poland but leaves the entrenchment design almost empty. A “multi-party pact” is named, while the same field admits there is no constitutional or supermajority lock. Given the evidence tag says Poland’s gains were reversible when consensus collapsed, the source supports the opposite of relying on a nonbinding pact.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Replace the generic pact with binding mechanisms: supermajority amendment threshold for restoring age-10/12 entry, statutory grandfathering plus multi-year earmarked transition funds, independent implementation authority, and cross-party signed commitments with named sanctions or automatic continuation rules.

## S4.political_risks
Objection: The field identifies funding cuts as a risk but does not confront the larger political-risk mechanism: S4 may create a durable coalition for preserving early selection by buying off criticism with compensatory funding. That is not merely an equity-impact issue; it is a political entrenchment risk. The cited evidence is weak on Hungary-specific TEIP durability, so the scenario should not imply that lower near-term conflict equals political robustness.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add an explicit entrenchment risk: selective-track beneficiaries support S4 because it preserves access, while disadvantaged-school funding remains discretionary and cuttable. Specify ring-fencing, entitlement status, or sunset-review triggers that prevent compensation from becoming a permanent substitute for structural reform.
