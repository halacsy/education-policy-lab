# Critique: equity_checker

## S0.equity_impact
Objection: The field correctly says the audit creates no equity change, but it understates the equity-negative effect of delaying action despite strong diagnostic evidence already available. The evidence base strongly supports that current admissions are SES-skewed and that Hungary’s PISA SES gradient is unusually high; what is weak is only the audit/deadline mechanism, not the existence of the inequity. Treating delay mainly as “one more cohort” obscures that the scenario knowingly preserves a strongly evidenced inequitable allocation while waiting for weaker additional evidence.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Explicitly state that S0 is equity-negative during the audit period because it preserves a strongly evidenced SES-skewed selection system, and clarify that the weak evidence tag applies to the governance remedy, not to the diagnosis of inequity.

## S1.equity_impact
Objection: The field focuses on middle-SES/rural applicants who may lose out, but it does not adequately assess the largest excluded group: lower-SES pupils who never apply to selective tracks because of information, travel, tutoring, or expectation barriers. The scenario’s own mechanism only reaches applicants, so an SES-aware admissions pilot could look more equitable among applicants while leaving pre-application exclusion intact. That is not just “most of the cohort nationally sees no equity change”; it is a core reach failure in the intervention design.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add that S1’s equity impact is limited to the applicant pool unless paired with outreach, application support, transport support, and monitoring of application rates by SES/rurality; otherwise it may improve entrant composition without reaching the most excluded pupils.

## S2.equity_impact
Objection: The field identifies non-state offsetting and residual-place competition, but it misses a direct equity-negative pathway for displaced lower- and middle-SES high achievers: as state selective places shrink, high-SES families can substitute into church/foundation/private options, while less-resourced families may lose their only accessible selective route. The evidence tags support maintainer fragmentation and likely non-state expansion as moderate/strong structural risks; the equity field should therefore treat unequal substitution capacity as a central impact, not only as institutional relocation of sorting.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add a distinct affected group: capable lower-/middle-SES pupils displaced from state-maintained selective tracks who cannot afford or access non-state alternatives, and specify mitigation such as transport, scholarship, or cross-maintainer access rules.

## S4.equity_impact
Objection: The field says S4 may be equity-neutral-to-negative if it entrenches selection and funding is non-durable, but it underplays who is actually reached by the compensatory package. TEIP-style funding reaches disadvantaged general schools, not disadvantaged pupils who are already excluded from high-status selective peer networks, nor disadvantaged pupils in non-disadvantaged schools. The Portugal evidence supports system-level gains, not neutralizing Hungary’s age-10/12 selection gradient, so the field should not imply broad equity reach without specifying which disadvantaged pupils are outside the funding logic.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Specify that S4 primarily reaches pupils enrolled in targeted general schools, while leaving out disadvantaged pupils outside eligible schools and all pupils affected by selective-track peer/resource separation; require eligibility and coverage analysis before claiming meaningful equity gains.
