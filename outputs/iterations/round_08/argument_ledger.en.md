### S1
- **pedagogus_erdekvedo** — conditionally supports [value-modeled: workload protection] — Fairer admissions are acceptable if they add no unpaid administrative burden on schools. Condition: No new reporting duties without paid time.
- **kisgyerekes_szuloi** — conditionally supports [value-modeled: Parental concern for predictability and fairness of the admission process directly affecting where and how a child attends school, plus safety/commuting stability for the specific cohort subject to new rules] — A family in a pilot district planning around the existing exam-based admission process could see the rules of the game change under their own child's application cycle, with a proxy or assessment design that is explicitly acknowledged as unvalidated. Families need to know well in advance of application deadlines what criteria will apply and have assurance that a gaming-detection failure won't retroactively disadvantage compliant applicants. Condition: Would move to support if pilot districts commit to publishing final admission criteria at least one full school year before the affected application cycle, and to oppose if criteria changes are announced with less lead time or applied retroactively to families already in the pipeline.
- **eselyegyenlosegi_civil** — conditionally supports [value-modeled: field experience with partial reforms] — Admission reform is a first step, but alone it re-labels the queue instead of shortening it. Condition: Pair it with enforced desegregation and measurable intake targets.
- **konzervativ_ertekvedo** — conditionally supports [value-modeled: merit as the legitimate criterion for access to advanced academic tracks, and subsidiarity/maintainer autonomy protecting church and foundation schools' admission decisions from uniform state mandates] — SES-weighted quotas risk displacing genuinely able pupils in favor of a demographic proxy that the scenario itself admits is unvalidated and gameable, undermining the meritocratic function the gimnazium track exists to serve; compelling non-state maintainers into a uniform admission-equity rule, even at pilot scale, oversteps the subsidiarity and maintainer autonomy the current legal framework protects. Additive support (tutoring, preparation resources) for disadvantaged but talented applicants, layered on top of the existing merit threshold, would be far more acceptable than a quota that bypasses it. Condition: Would support if the pilot replaced SES quotas with needs-based preparatory support that still requires meeting the same merit bar, and if participation by non-state maintainers remained fully voluntary rather than mandated.
- **digitalisgazdasagi** — conditionally supports [value-modeled: documented employer concern that the current admission system screens on tutoring access and cultural capital rather than aptitude (general skills-shortage literature and OECD analyses of exam-prep-driven selection) implies an interest in widening the pool of students identified for advanced STEM/analytic tracks, provided identification of genuine aptitude is not weakened] — A pilot that broadens access to selective tracks without diluting the identification of genuine STEM/analytic aptitude would be a net gain for the future skilled-labour pool. But the scenario itself flags that a 'less-coachable' assessment design is unvalidated for whether it still identifies real aptitude versus merely different demographic groups; if the redesigned assessment fails to predict subsequent achievement, the sector loses a reliable aptitude signal without gaining a larger effective talent pool. Condition: Would support scale-up if pilot evaluation shows the new assessment identifies students who go on to strong STEM/analytic outcomes at rates comparable to or better than the current exam, across a wider SES range; would oppose rollout if evaluation shows the redesigned assessment is a weaker predictor of subsequent skill development or is easily gamed by moderately-resourced families rather than reaching the intended pool.
- **egyhazi_fenntartoi** — conditionally supports [value-modeled: Denominational and foundation schools' documented legal discretion over admission criteria reflecting their pedagogical/religious mission, protected under maintainer agreements and Nkt. provisions distinguishing state and non-state maintainer governance] — The scenario's own assumptions concede that 'no legal mechanism currently compels non-state maintainers to adopt uniform equity criteria' and that the pilot may need to be 'confined to state-maintained schools' for this reason. A pilot confined to state schools raises no objection from this interest. But if a uniform SES-weighted admission mandate is extended to church/foundation schools via ministerial decree or a new legal instrument, it would override maintainers' existing discretion to weigh mission-relevant criteria (e.g. sustained faith-community commitment) alongside or instead of an SES proxy imposed from outside. Condition: Would support inclusion if participation is voluntary, preserves maintainer discretion to combine SES criteria with existing mission-based criteria rather than replacing them, and is negotiated with maintainer associations rather than imposed by decree.
- **oktataspolitikai_reformmozgalom** — conditionally supports [value-modeled: Kockás könyv trajectory] — A fairer gate on the same building is progress, but the documented reform trajectory this archetype draws on aims to need no gate before 14 at all. Condition: Only as an explicit transition step towards the comprehensive model.
- **pedagogus_szakszervezeti_hang** — conditionally supports [value-modeled: organized-labour interest in workload management and professional autonomy; union concern that pilots are often unfunded and teachers absorb design and implementation costs invisibly] — SES-aware admission pilot is conditionally acceptable if: (a) pilot-district teachers are granted union participation in assessment-redesign working groups (not post-hoc consultation), (b) pilot schools receive demonstrable resource increase (e.g., class-size reduction if SES weighting increases compositional heterogeneity), (c) any new assessment development includes teacher time and external expertise, not added to existing teaching load, (d) union has binding (not advisory) role in mid-pilot feasibility review and timeline adjustment. Selective-track pilots historically concentrate high-achieving pupils with adequate resource; SES weighting that increases heterogeneity without proportional resources increases workload risk for pilot-school teachers. Condition: union supports if pilot is fully resourced and teacher-designed; opposes if pilot is framed as 'no additional cost' or imposes assessment change without consultation or workload adjustment; would shift to support if government commits to scaling resources proportional to any compositional changes detected in pilot data
- **kormanyzati_reformrealizmus** — conditionally supports [value-modeled: Programmatic preference for piloting before national rollout, minimizing legal and administrative exposure, and treating maintainer plurality (state/church/foundation) as a governance constraint requiring negotiated consultation rather than override.] — A geographically bounded pilot within the existing legal framework (no Nkt. amendment required) is exactly the kind of low-commitment, evidence-generating step a durability-minded government can defend to both reform advocates and status-quo constituencies, provided it does not get read publicly as a precursor to a decision already made. Support is conditioned on the pilot genuinely functioning as evidence-gathering, not as a politically pre-committed first phase of national rollout. Condition: Would shift to oppose if non-state maintainers cannot be brought in through negotiation and the pilot proceeds state-schools-only in a way that publicly signals differential treatment by maintainer type, or if legal review flags material Article 66/a exposure before pilot launch — in either case the political and legal cost would exceed the evidence gained.
- **fuggetlen_szakpolitikai_kutatomuhely** — conditionally supports [value-modeled: An independent policy-research institute values learning from evidence and is honest about evidence gaps. If the pilot is designed to *rigorously study* these assumptions—including monitoring for gaming incidence, proxy stability, and assessment design effectiveness—it becomes valuable learning. If it assumes these mechanisms work and reports an outcome, it wastes the opportunity and discredits the entire approach. The scenario's own flagged risk (absence of an existing SES register, need to build a proxy as part of the pilot) means this is a bet on inventing workable infrastructure during a compressed pilot window. Condition: Strong support if: (1) explicitly framed as learning about proxy design and assessment effectiveness, not testing a fix; (2) intensive monitoring of gaming, proxy stability, and assessment SES-sensitivity; (3) results published regardless of outcome; (4) evaluation does not assume the assessment is sound but empirically tests it. Oppose if: (1) compressed into unrealistic timeframe; (2) non-state participation is low-consent and unrepresentative; (3) pressure to declare success and roll out before evidence is conclusive; (4) failure findings downweighted versus success in rollout decision; (5) findings on proxy gaming or assessment brittleness are suppressed.

### S2
- **pedagogus_erdekvedo** — conditionally supports [value-modeled: job security] — A phase-down is bearable only with retraining guarantees and no forced redundancies. Condition: Enforceable teacher guarantees written into the law.
- **kisgyerekes_szuloi** — conditionally supports [value-modeled: Parental value placed on preserving school choice as a mobility and safety-net option, and on not overcrowding the general schools their other children may attend] — A shrinking pool of state-maintained selective places, without matched capacity investment in general schools, risks larger class sizes or resource strain for the children who stay in or are pushed into general schools — a direct child-wellbeing and safety concern, not just an abstract equity one. At the same time, if non-state maintainers expand to absorb demand, the choice that families valued doesn't disappear so much as become costlier or less geographically convenient, which affects commuting and family budgets differently across income levels. Condition: Would support if compensatory general-school capacity funding is confirmed and disbursed ahead of each year's place reduction (as the scenario's own implementation steps intend); would oppose if capacity funding lags the schedule, since that directly risks overcrowding for children with no other option.
- **eselyegyenlosegi_civil** — supports [value-modeled: peer effects on the left-behind] — Every cohort spared early sorting is a cohort whose weakest members keep their stronger peers. Condition: Phase-down speed must not outrun support for receiving schools.
- **konzervativ_ertekvedo** — opposes [value-modeled: tradition and continuity of the 6/8-year gimnazium as an established talent-development pathway, parental choice, and subsidiarity/maintainer autonomy against extension of state place-count regulation to non-state schools] — A legislated year-on-year cut to selective places directly narrows the options available to families who choose this path, resting on a peer-composition benefit the scenario's own evidence rates as weak and contested. More troubling, the scenario explicitly calls for new regulatory levers over non-state maintainers (subsidy conditionality, extended place caps) specifically to stop them absorbing displaced demand — this is a direct extension of state control over church and foundation schools that currently enjoy independent maintainer status, contrary to subsidiarity. Condition: Would reconsider only if place reductions were purely voluntary and demographically driven (e.g., tracking genuine cohort decline) rather than a legislated cap, and if no new compulsion mechanism were extended over non-state maintainers.
- **digitalisgazdasagi** — conditionally supports [value-modeled: documented, strongly evidenced systemic teacher shortage in mathematics and sciences (OECD Education at a Glance; PSZ/PDSZ surveys cited in the scenario) implies an interest in not shrinking specialized STEM-track capacity without a credible substitute source of advanced STEM instruction] — Reducing selective-track places is only equity-neutral for the skills pipeline if general schools receiving retained high-achieving pupils have adequate specialist STEM/digital-subject teaching capacity to sustain advanced instruction — and the scenario's own cost and political-risk sections concede this compensatory capacity is neither currently funded nor guaranteed to survive fiscal consolidation. Absent that guarantee, phase-down risks narrowing the advanced-STEM pipeline exactly where the shortage is worst. Condition: Would support if compensatory capacity funding for general-school STEM/digital instruction is legislated as durable (not merely announced) and monitoring shows advanced-track STEM output is maintained through the transition; would oppose if compensatory funding lapses or if non-state maintainers absorbing displaced demand are the only source of continued advanced STEM instruction, since that would fragment rather than secure the pipeline.
- **egyhazi_fenntartoi** — opposes [value-modeled: Maintainer autonomy over enrollment and place-count decisions as protected by individual church-state agreements and Nkt.'s maintainer-differentiated governance structure] — The scenario explicitly proposes 'regulatory levers aimed at non-state maintainers, such as subsidy conditionality tied to aggregate selective-place caps or extending place-count rules to state-subsidized non-state schools' precisely because it anticipates and wants to prevent maintainers from expanding to meet demand. That is a direct claim on maintainer decision-making that the scenario's own legal_and_governance basis says 'no such levers currently exist' to support, and its own political-risk section acknowledges maintainers 'may resist any extension of place-count regulation to non-state schools as an infringement on maintainer autonomy.' Responding to parental demand for selective places is a legitimate exercise of an institution's mission and agreement-protected discretion, not a loophole to be closed. Condition: Would move toward no_position or conditional acceptance if any capacity constraint were negotiated directly with maintainer associations as a voluntary agreement rather than imposed via unilateral subsidy conditionality, and if it applied only to new state-subsidized capital expansion rather than existing enrollment discretion.
- **oktataspolitikai_reformmozgalom** — supports [value-modeled: CKP Kockás könyv (2016), ckpinfo.hu/kockaskonyv] — The documented reform proposal this archetype draws on proposes exactly this: transferring the 6/8-year forms into the comprehensive model gradually, with broad social consensus. Condition: Gradualism must have a fixed end-state, not become permanent limbo.
- **pedagogus_szakszervezeti_hang** — opposes [value-modeled: organized-labour interest in job security, voluntary mobility, and avoiding forced redeployment; union concern that phase-down of selective entry without binding protections will force gymnasium-trained teachers into general schools against preference, and will increase general-school class sizes without compensatory funding given Hungary's documented teacher shortage] — phase-down without guarantees risks: (1) forced moves of gymnasium teachers trained for selective cohorts into general classes; (2) class-size increases in general schools if phase-down shrinks gymnasium places but new 'retained/displaced' pupils are absorbed without additional funding; (3) acceleration of attrition given Hungary's documented systemic teacher shortage (OECD, PSZ/PDSZ data). Union requirement for conditional support: (a) no forced redeployment—gymnasium teachers retain right to exit or retrain voluntarily with funded retraining program; (b) general schools absorbing displaced pupils receive demonstrable capacity investment (new positions or class-size caps, verified in advance, not promised post-hoc); (c) non-state maintainer place expansion is legally capped or monitored so offsetting does not undermine the state-side reduction. Without binding guarantees on all three, phase-down is workload transfer in shortage conditions. Condition: union would shift to conditional support only if government legislates (not administratively commits to) binding guarantees on redeployment voluntariness (with union arbitration over disputes), class-size protections (e.g., statutory cap on general-school class sizes during phase-down period), and regulatory instruments to limit non-state offsetting expansion. A multi-year agreement co-signed with union leadership would be necessary, not a ministry working group.
- **kormanyzati_reformrealizmus** — conditionally supports [value-modeled: Governing-majority preference for gradualism and legislated predictability over abrupt structural change, tempered by fiscal-discipline concerns about locking in multi-year recurring compensatory spending and by wariness of ceding regulatory ground to non-state maintainers.] — The scenario's own record concedes non-state maintainers may absorb displaced demand, meaning the equity gain is conditional on regulatory levers that do not yet exist and whose legal viability against maintainer agreements is untested — a governing majority weighing durability would not legislate a fixed multi-year schedule against maintainers before that legal groundwork is done, since a legislated schedule that later proves unenforceable against non-state offsetting expansion looks like a broken promise rather than a working policy. Condition: Would move to support if regulatory levers on non-state maintainer place expansion are legally validated in advance and compensatory general-school funding is ring-fenced against fiscal-consolidation cuts before the schedule is legislated; would move to oppose if neither condition can be secured, since then the reform relocates stratification rather than reducing it while still bearing full legislative and political cost.
- **fuggetlen_szakpolitikai_kutatomuhely** — opposes [value-modeled: An independent policy-research institute values intellectual honesty about assumptions. S2's equity logic is predicated on preventing an outcome that the scenario's own evidence (school_network_planning, legal_and_governance) treats as structurally likely, not hypothetical. Supporting a reform that bets on preventing probable outcomes is not evidence-driven.] — S2's central claim—reduce the share of state-maintained cohort exposed to early sorting—is entirely conditional on regulatory levers successfully constraining non-state maintainer expansion. The scenario's own evidence treats this expansion as a structural likelihood given maintainer fragmentation and growth trends, yet proposes no regulatory levers that currently exist. If you are betting on preventing an outcome the evidence assesses as likely, this is wishful thinking, not evidence-based policy. Additionally, S2 creates specific distributional losers: lower-SES pupils in general schools who currently access residual selective places. If sorting migrates to non-state tracks, residual state-selective places become scarcer and more competitive (higher-SES only), flipping a potential benefit into a harm for the group the reform aimed to help. Condition: Support if: (1) regulatory levers to cap non-state selective expansion are designed and legally validated *before* state phase-down begins, not as a parallel aspiration; (2) general schools absorbing retained/displaced pupils receive genuine capacity and resource investment; (3) a compensation mechanism exists for lower-SES pupils no longer accessing shrinking residual state-selective places (e.g., advanced curriculum in general schools). Strongly oppose if launched without these conditions, as it risks entrenching stratification in less-visible, less-regulated, less-transparent institutions.
