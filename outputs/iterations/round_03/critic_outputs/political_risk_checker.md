# Critique: political_risk_checker

## S1.political_risks
Objection: The listed risks omit the most consequential reversal vector: this reform relies on a "SES weighting" defined in an admission decree, which a successor government can silently neutralize by adjusting the weighting parameter to near-zero without repealing anything or triggering visible opposition. The framing risks (lottery as "punishing talent") are real but recoverable; parametric gutting is invisible and permanent. No entrenchment design (e.g., statutory floor on the weighting, or a published parameter tied to measured intake composition) is offered.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add a risk line naming parametric reversal of the SES weighting via decree amendment, and add an entrenchment mechanism (e.g., primary-legislation floor on weighting or an automatic parameter linked to a published intake-composition target).

## S2.political_risks
Objection: The scenario correctly names cheap reversal (Polish precedent) and lists "cross-party pact or statutory entrenchment attempts" under uncertainties, but the political_risks field offers no actual entrenchment design against the reversal it identifies — it states the risk and stops. For a decade-long trajectory whose entire value depends on completion, an unmitigated reversal risk is a fatal gap, not a footnote. The mechanism (published trajectory) actually worsens reversal ease by making the remaining cuts a visible target each year.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add concrete entrenchment options to political_risks (statutory trajectory with supermajority repeal lock, back-loaded costs that raise reversal price, or binding the fund to intake outcomes) and assess each option's durability.

## S3.political_risks
Objection: The scenario names Polish-style reversal and pins the mitigation on a "cross-party pact" in implementation_steps, but political_risks never assesses what happens when the pact attempt fails — which the Polish precedent suggests is the modal outcome. The entire reform's survival is staked on an unassessed contingency; there is no fallback entrenchment or phased-commitment design that delivers partial equity gains if political ownership collapses mid-transition.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add a risk line for pact failure as the base case, and specify a fallback design (e.g., sequencing that front-loads irreversible-but-beneficial steps like teacher retraining, or degrading gracefully to an S2 cap trajectory) so partial gains survive reversal.

## S4.political_risks
Objection: The scenario identifies budget vulnerability ("first cut in fiscal consolidation") as the primary risk but leaves it entirely unmitigated in political_risks, despite the uncertainties field pointing to "statutory earmarking attempt." Given that expected_benefits claims the package is "implementable under any government [evidence: strong]," the unaddressed budget-reversal risk directly contradicts that strong-tagged durability claim: a line that is first to be cut is not durably implementable.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Either downgrade the "implementable under any government [evidence: strong]" benefit to reflect budget fragility, or add an entrenchment mechanism to political_risks (statutory earmark, multi-year budget guarantee, or ring-fenced levy) and reconcile the two fields.
