# Critique: feasibility_checker

## S2.assumptions
Objection: The assumption "Caps are enforceable across state, church and private maintainers" is asserted without engaging the capacity reality that the scenario's own political_risks and the synthesis flag: church maintainers have constitutional/concordat protections that make cap enforcement legally contestable. The scenario treats enforceability as a binary assumption when the binding constraint is the legal lead time and litigation window before a cap becomes effective against non-state maintainers. Without a stated mechanism for how caps bind non-state actors, the entire "mechanical" sorting reduction in the mechanism field is confined to the state sector, where roughly half the selective demand can migrate out of reach.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add an assumption-level dependency: caps only bind state maintainers unless a separate legal instrument (concordat renegotiation or per-maintainer agreement) is secured first, and re-scope the "mechanical" sorting-reduction claim to the state-sector share only, or add an implementation step establishing the non-state legal basis before caps take effect.

## S3.implementation_steps
Objection: The step "launch differentiation retraining at scale; pilot comprehensive model in 2-3 districts" collapses two operations with incompatible timelines into one line. National-scale differentiation retraining of the existing teacher workforce cannot be delivered within the phase-in window given real teacher-supply and trainer-of-trainers bottlenecks — the assumptions field itself concedes this is uncertain. The implementation sequence presents retraining as a launchable input rather than a decade-long throughput constraint that gates when the common-school model can legally take first pupils.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Split into two sequenced steps with explicit throughput figures: (a) a capacity build-out phase estimating annual teacher-retraining throughput vs. the number of teachers needing it, and (b) tie the legislated "named year" for stopping 6/8-year entry to a retraining-coverage threshold rather than a fixed calendar date.

## S4.implementation_steps
Objection: The step "introduce premia and mentoring pipelines for disadvantaged schools" assumes the teacher supply exists to be redirected. The scenario's own uncertainties field flags that the premium size needed to move experienced teachers is unknown — but the deeper feasibility gap is that in a national teacher shortage, premia reshuffle a fixed scarce pool rather than expand it, so gains in targeted schools come at the cost of the schools teachers leave. The implementation steps present the pipeline as additive capacity when it is redistributive under real supply constraints.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Add an implementation step assessing net teacher availability in the sending schools, and pair premia with a supply-expansion measure (retention bonuses or trainee pipelines) so the package does not simply relocate scarcity; alternatively, scope expected_benefits to acknowledge zero-sum teacher movement.

## S1.implementation_steps
Objection: The step "run first reformed admission cycle with monitoring" omits the administrative-bandwidth cost of building and validating a "workable SES indicator at application time" (named as an assumption). Constructing a legally defensible, non-gameable SES index usable at the point of application is a substantial data-governance and administrative task with its own lead time, not a byproduct of running an admission cycle. Absent this, the "SES-aware admission rules" mechanism has no operational instrument.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Insert a prior implementation step: data-protection authority and statistical office jointly specify and validate the application-time SES indicator (data sources, legal basis, anti-gaming safeguards) before the first reformed cycle, with a lead time reflected in the trajectory.
