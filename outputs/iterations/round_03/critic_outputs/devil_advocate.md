# Critique: devil_advocate

## S2.evidence_status
Objection: The scenario labels the cohort-level sorting reduction "strong" and frames only the general-school retention as "the unevidenced link." But the load-bearing claim for equity is not that caps mechanically reduce the *count* of selected pupils — it's that this reduces *net sorting* rather than displacing it. The scenario's own equity_impact concedes residual selective places become "even more SES-skewed," and the arms race intensifies. If displacement dominates, the "strong" mechanical reduction produces near-zero equity gain. The mechanical count is strong; the equity inference from it is not, and the evidence tag conflates the two.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Downgrade the equity-relevant claim to moderate and split the tag: "count reduction [strong]; net-sorting reduction [weak], conditional on S1-type admission reform running concurrently."

## S3.mechanism
Objection: The third mechanism bullet — in-school differentiation "substitutes for between-school tracks if teachers are trained for it" — carries no evidence tag at all, yet it is the operational hinge of the entire scenario. If differentiation fails, S3 collapses into a lower-performing common school, not a comprehensive success. The two tagged mechanisms (later selection [strong], asymmetric peer effects [contested]) are irrelevant if the untagged one fails. An untagged load-bearing claim is worse than a weak-tagged one.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add an explicit tag to the differentiation bullet — the honest reading is [weak] for national-scale delivery within the transition window — and state that S3's benefit case is gated on this being achieved.

## S4.expected_benefits
Objection: "Politically inexpensive; implementable under any government [evidence: strong]" contradicts the scenario's own cost_categories and political_risks. Cost lists targeted funding as "high — this is the package" and premia as "medium-high" recurring; political_risks flags these lines as "the first cut in fiscal consolidation." A recurring high-cost program that is first to be cut is not "politically inexpensive" — it is fiscally cheap to *legislate* and expensive to *sustain*. The "strong" tag rewards a claim the scenario elsewhere refutes.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Split the claim: "cheap to enact [strong]; durable across fiscal cycles [weak, see budget-vulnerability risk]." Remove "politically inexpensive" as an unqualified benefit.

## S1.assumptions
Objection: The assumption "a workable SES indicator exists at application time" is stated as a binary existence condition, but the real load-bearing requirement is that the indicator be *non-gameable* by exactly the high-SES families the reform targets. The political_risks and uncertainties both flag informal selection channels and elite-school adaptation; a gameable SES indicator (self-reported address, income thresholds families can restructure) is the same failure in another form. If the indicator can be gamed, the weighted lottery/quota reweights toward families who can manipulate it — potentially anti-equity.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Restate the assumption as "a workable *and manipulation-resistant* SES indicator exists," and add an uncertainty on indicator-gaming with a validation step in the pilot (e.g., audit reported vs. administrative SES data).
