# Critique: equity_checker

## S1.equity_impact
Objection: The field claims gains "accrue to high-ability low-SES pupils" but never confronts a distributional problem internal to its own mechanism: a weighted lottery above a threshold reaches only those low-SES pupils who clear the ability threshold — precisely the group already most likely to gain admission under any regime. The pupils left furthest behind (low-SES, below-threshold, in the general-school majority) get nothing, yet the field frames the reform as equity-positive rather than equity-neutral-to-most. This is a concentration-of-benefits flaw, not a phrasing issue.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: State explicitly that benefits reach only the low-SES sub-population near the admission margin, quantify that group's approximate size relative to the low-SES cohort, and reclassify the reform's equity reach as narrow rather than general.

## S2.equity_impact
Objection: The field concedes residual selective places become "even more SES-skewed" without S1, but does not reckon with the transition-year equity cost its own political_risks field names: scarcer places "intensify the admission arms race." During the decade-long phase-down, low-SES families face a harder, more coached competition for shrinking places — a plausibly equity-NEGATIVE interval for exactly the pupils the reform targets. The equity_impact field presents only the steady-state gain and omits this transitional harm.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add a transition-period equity assessment covering the phase-down years, acknowledging the arms-race intensification as a temporary equity-negative effect on low-SES applicants, and pair the cap trajectory with S1-type admission reform to neutralise it.

## S3.equity_impact
Objection: The field names middle-class exit to non-state schools as the countervailing risk, but the assumptions field also flags that "sorting does not simply migrate to school choice between general schools" — i.e., residential/within-sector sorting. The equity_impact field treats non-state exit as the only segregation channel and is silent on general-school-to-general-school sorting via school choice and residential sorting, which can worsen segregation even if non-state exit is contained. The "largest equity gain" claim rests on an [evidence: contested] peer-effect asymmetry, so the upside is weaker than the field's framing implies.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Broaden the equity-negative channels to include residential and between-general-school sorting, and downgrade the "largest expected equity gain" claim to conditional on the contested asymmetric-peer-effect evidence holding.

## S4.equity_impact
Objection: The field asserts compensation "helps the disadvantaged majority where they actually are," but the scenario's own uncertainties field states no country has demonstrated compensation offsetting selection at Hungary's early age (confidence: low). The equity_impact claim therefore asserts a reach the evidence does not support — at most compensation could partially narrow within-school outcomes; the "helps the majority" framing implies a demonstrated benefit that is actually unproven at this selection age.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Qualify the benefit as unproven at Hungary's selection age, distinguishing the individually-evidenced components from the untested claim that they offset early sorting, and mark the majority-benefit as a hypothesis to be tested by the pre-registered evaluation.
