# Critique: assumption_checker

## S2.assumptions
Objection: The assumption "Caps are enforceable across state, church and private maintainers" contradicts the expert record and the scenario's own political_risks field, which states church maintainers "may litigate or negotiate exemptions." An assumption cannot be asserted as a working premise when the same scenario lists its failure as a live risk. The legal_and_governance and political_feasibility experts flag non-state maintainer exemption as a recurring structural obstacle, so enforceability is precisely the unverified crux, not a background assumption. If caps are not enforceable against the maintainers most likely to absorb displaced selective demand, the "mechanical" sorting reduction tagged [evidence: strong] does not follow at the system level.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Demote enforceability from an assumption to an open uncertainty with a confidence rating, and qualify the [evidence: strong] mechanism tag to "strong for state-maintained places only; system-level effect contingent on non-state compliance."

## S3.mechanism
Objection: The mechanism claims asymmetric peer effects ("raises outcomes of disadvantaged pupils more than it lowers those of advantaged ones") and tags it [evidence: contested] — but then the expected_benefits and equity_impact fields build on this as if it were established, calling S3 "the largest expected equity gain of all scenarios." A contested causal link cannot simultaneously carry the largest claimed benefit. The synthesis records that peer-effect asymmetry is "genuinely disputed," so the benefit magnitude is unsupported, not merely uncertain.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Recalibrate the equity_impact claim to a conditional ("largest potential gain if asymmetric peer effects hold, which is contested"), and align the expected_benefits tag with the contested status of its underlying mechanism.

## S4.assumptions
Objection: The assumption "Compensation can meaningfully offset, not just mask, the effects of early sorting" is directly contradicted by the scenario's own uncertainty field, which states "no country has demonstrated it at Hungary's selection age." This is not an assumption that can be granted — it is the exact unverified claim on which the whole scenario rests. Listing it as an assumption disguises a load-bearing unknown as a premise, and the synthesis explicitly frames this as the open question dividing experts.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Remove this from assumptions and state it as the scenario's central unresolved uncertainty; downgrade expected_benefits "[evidence: moderate]" to reflect that the Portuguese evidence does not cover Hungary's selection age.

## S1.expected_benefits
Objection: The benefit "Reduced test-preparation arms race pressure on age-10 children [evidence: weak]" is undercut by the scenario's own uncertainty that reduced coachability may not "survive the test-prep industry's adaptation" (confidence: low). Worse, S2's political_risks note that scarcer selective places "intensify the admission arms race" — if S1 leaves the number of selective places unchanged while merely changing the assessment, the arms race may migrate rather than reduce. The benefit is unsupported given that competition for a fixed number of elite seats persists.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Qualify the benefit to "may shift the form of preparation pressure rather than reduce it, since selective-seat scarcity is unchanged," or mark it (speculative) pending repeat coachability studies.
