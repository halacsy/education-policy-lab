# Critique: devil_advocate

## S1.assumptions
Objection: The scenario's equity claim is load-bearing on "A workable SES indicator exists at application time," yet this is asserted, not established. Hungary lacks a validated individual-level SES measure usable at admission; proxies (address, parental declaration) are either gameable by mobile middle-class families or legally fraught. If no clean indicator exists, the SES-weighted lottery and district quotas — the entire equity mechanism — cannot be operationalized as designed, collapsing the goal into a structure-preserving cosmetic reform.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Downgrade expected_benefits to [evidence: weak] pending a named, tested SES proxy, and add an implementation step that validates the indicator against gaming before the mechanism is claimed to work.

## S2.mechanism
Objection: The mechanism admits its own load-bearing failure: "Advanced-programme funding in general schools retains ambitious families in the common track [evidence: weak]" and the evidence_status calls this "the unevidenced link." But the equity case depends entirely on it — if ambitious families exit rather than stay, capping selective places drives them to private/church maintainers or residential sorting, worsening segregation. The scenario tags the sorting reduction "strong" (correct, it's mechanical) but the net equity outcome inherits the weak link's uncertainty and should not be presented as a benefit.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Re-tag expected_benefit "Stronger peer composition in urban general-school upper grades" from [evidence: moderate] to [evidence: weak], conditional on the retention mechanism, and state that without demonstrated retention the net effect on segregation is indeterminate.

## S3.mechanism
Objection: The asymmetric-peer-effects claim is tagged [evidence: contested], yet it is the mechanism that makes S3 "the largest expected equity gain of all scenarios." If peer effects are in fact symmetric or the disadvantaged-pupil gain is modest, the scenario trades advantaged-pupil outcomes for uncertain gains — precisely the objection its critics raise. The scenario is honest about the tag but then builds the equity_impact superlative on top of the contested link without discounting it.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Condition the equity_impact "largest expected gain" claim explicitly on the asymmetric-peer-effect assumption holding, and present a symmetric-effects downside case as a named branch, not merely as an exit-risk aside.

## S4.uncertainties
Objection: The scenario concedes "no country has demonstrated [compensation offsetting selection] at Hungary's selection age," which is the load-bearing empirical claim for the entire goal. This is not merely low-confidence — it is an absence of positive evidence, yet the Portuguese analogy is used throughout to imply transferability. Portugal does not select between schools at 10; using its trajectory to project "improved outcomes within 5 years" [evidence: moderate] overstates what the source supports, since the Portuguese evidence covers a system without early between-school selection to offset.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Downgrade "Improved outcomes in disadvantaged general schools within 5 years" to [evidence: weak] and restate the Portuguese evidence as supporting compensation effectiveness generally, not compensation *offsetting early between-school selection* specifically.
