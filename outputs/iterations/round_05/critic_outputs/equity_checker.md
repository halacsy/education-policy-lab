# Critique: equity_checker

## S1.equity_impact
Objection: The equity_impact claims "gains accrue to high-ability low-SES pupils" but the mechanism only reweights who is selected among applicants — it says nothing about who applies in the first place. Rural low-SES families face travel-time and information barriers to reaching gimnazium campuses that a weighted lottery or SES-quota cannot fix; the evidence tag for SES-aware admission rules [moderate] supports composition effects on the applicant pool, not on application rates. As written, the claim overstates reach to pupils who never enter the pool, and is silent on whether this is a distinct equity-negative gap the reform leaves untouched.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Add a sentence distinguishing "composition among applicants" from "access to apply," and flag geographic/informational barriers as a separate equity gap not addressed by admission-rule reform alone.

## S2.equity_impact
Objection: The equity_impact notes residual selective places become "more SES-skewed" but does not address transition-cohort harm: as caps tighten, incumbent higher-SES families (who have more information and preparation resources) are best positioned to adapt quickly and capture the shrinking number of places, meaning the pupils most harmed by scarcity are precisely the marginal low-SES applicants the policy is meant to help, in the years before S1-type admission reform is paired in. This is a distinct, foreseeable equity cost during the transition window, not merely a residual-state description, and it is left unstated. (speculative on magnitude, but the mechanism is a direct implication of the stated interaction with S1)
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Add explicit acknowledgment that absent simultaneous S1-style admission reform, the transition years themselves — not just the "residual" steady state — carry a foreseeable SES-skewing risk, and name this as a sequencing dependency rather than an afterthought.

## S3.equity_impact
Objection: The equity_impact identifies flight-to-non-state-schools as the central risk but does not name which subgroup absorbs the downside if that risk materializes: middle-class exit does not harm the exiting families, it concentrates disadvantaged and immobile (including rural and Roma) pupils in the remaining common schools, worsening their peer composition specifically. The current wording ("segregation could worsen relative to baseline") treats this as a diffuse system property rather than identifying the pupils who would bear the concentrated cost, which is a different and more consequential equity claim than the one stated.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Name the subgroup at risk explicitly (pupils unable to exit to non-state alternatives — rural, low-income, Roma) and state that for them the downside scenario is not merely "no gain" but active concentration/re-segregation.

## S4.equity_impact
Objection: The equity_impact assumes targeting formulas reach "the disadvantaged majority where they actually are," but the assumptions field only flags stigma risk, not the more consequential risk that SES-based targeting formulas systematically under-identify or politically exclude Roma-segregated schools — a well-documented pattern in Hungarian compensatory schemes where explicit ethnic targeting is avoided for political reasons, causing SES proxies to miss the most segregated schools. The evidence tag [moderate] supports that Portuguese-style targeted funding improved outcomes generally, but says nothing about whether an SES-only formula in the Hungarian context reaches the specific schools carrying the largest equity cost.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: State explicitly that SES-based (rather than ethnicity- or segregation-index-based) targeting risks systematically under-reaching the most segregated schools, and flag this as a design choice requiring its own evaluation criterion, not folded into the general stigma caveat.
