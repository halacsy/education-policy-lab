# Critique: equity_checker

## S1.equity_impact
Objection: The field concedes the reform "does nothing for the majority who remain in general schools," yet frames the net equity effect as positive. This omits a plausibly equity-negative pathway: making elite tracks more meritocratic can *raise* their prestige and signalling value, increasing the penalty attached to non-selection and widening the outcome gap for the excluded majority. A gate that admits high-ability low-SES pupils more fairly still concentrates the ablest peers away from general schools — the equity_impact treats the entry-gate gain as the whole ledger while ignoring the composition loss it imposes on those left behind.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add an explicit equity-negative pathway: legitimising/strengthening selective tracks may worsen relative outcomes for the non-selected majority via prestige and peer-drain effects; note this is why S1 is presented as a complement to S2/S4, not a standalone equity win.

## S3.equity_impact
Objection: The field names middle-class exit to non-state schools as the key risk but asserts "the largest expected equity gain of all scenarios" without specifying *for whom* the gain lands. The Hungarian free school-choice and maintainer landscape means the disadvantaged pupils most likely to benefit are also those least able to follow the exiting middle class, so the residual common schools could concentrate disadvantage even as the national gradient nominally improves. The equity_impact does not distinguish between-region/between-maintainer distribution of the gain — a national average improvement can coexist with worsened segregation for the poorest pupils.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Disaggregate the claimed gain by who is reached: state which pupils/regions capture it and acknowledge that under free choice, gains may bypass the most disadvantaged if they cannot access the strengthened common schools; tie the magnitude claim to the exit-monitoring uncertainty already listed.

## S4.equity_impact
Objection: The field claims the package "helps the disadvantaged majority where they actually are," but the assumptions flag that targeting formulas may stigmatise schools — an equity-negative pathway the equity_impact never carries through. SES-indexed targeting can trigger further middle-class flight from labelled schools, accelerating the very sorting the compensation is meant to offset, so the reach could be narrower than stated (only the already-concentrated poor) and self-undermining. The evidence tag on the Portuguese trajectory [moderate] concerns aggregate outcome gains, not distribution across who stays vs. flees.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: State the stigma-driven flight pathway in equity_impact and note that reach depends on avoiding a visible "poor school" label; flag that the Portuguese evidence does not speak to composition effects at Hungary's selection age.

## S2.equity_impact
Objection: The field acknowledges residual selective places become "even more SES-skewed" without S1, but does not weigh who bears the transition cost: during the phase-down, scarcer places intensify the arms race (as the political_risks field itself states), which is regressive — low-SES families cannot buy into an escalating test-prep market. The equity_impact treats the end-state peer-composition gain as the equity story while ignoring that the multi-year transition may be *equity-negative* for the exact low-SES high-ability pupils S1 aims to protect.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Add the transition-period distributional cost: note that arms-race intensification during phase-down disadvantages low-SES applicants, making the S1+S2 pairing not merely complementary but necessary to avoid an equity-negative transition.
