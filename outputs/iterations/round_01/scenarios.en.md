# Policy scenarios

## S1 — Admission Reform Within the Current Structure
**Goal**
Mitigate socioeconomic segregation and early sorting by reforming the centralized written admissions process for 6- and 8-year gimnaziums without changing school structures.

**Mechanism**
- Reforming the centralized admissions criteria and introducing quota systems for disadvantaged pupils reduces SES-based sorting [evidence: moderate — Model knowledge / Hungarian educational policy literature].
- Introducing strict socio-economic quotas or modifying the weighting of the central written admission examination limits the siphoning off of high-SES, urban pupils [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics].

**Evidence status**
Overall moderate — Admissions statistics strongly show high-SES skew, but the effectiveness of admissions adjustments on overall segregation is contested due to primary-stage sorting.

**Assumptions**
- Elite gimnaziums will not bypass reformed admissions criteria through secondary oral exams or alternative assessment pathways.
- Middle-class parents will accept admissions quotas rather than seeking to bypass them through church- or foundation-run school entry.

**Expected benefits**
- Increased representation of low-SES and rural students in elite 6- and 8-year tracks [evidence: moderate].
- Reduced concentration of privilege in urban gimnaziums without incurring high transition costs [evidence: weak].

**Equity impact**
Moderately improves equity within the selective tracks, but does not address the broader systemic sorting occurring at the primary school level.

**Cost categories**
- Admissions administration and test design adjustments
- Monitoring and enforcement compliance costs for tankerületek

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Public Education — Amend the ministerial decree governing secondary school admissions to introduce SES-based quotas or modify written test weightings.
- Educational Authority (Oktatási Hivatal) — Redesign the central written admission examinations to reduce bias and measure raw potential rather than acquired privilege.
- School District Centres (Tankerületek) — Monitor and enforce compliance with new admissions rules and quotas across state schools.

**Political risks**
- Intense resistance from urban middle-class parents who view these slots as critical mobility assets.
- Potential legal challenges from non-state school maintainers regarding institutional autonomy in student selection.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether modifying admissions criteria can significantly reduce sorting given that free school choice and residential segregation are already entrenched at the primary level.
- The willingness of church and foundation school maintainers to voluntarily align with reformed admissions criteria.

## S2 — Gradual Phase-Down of 6- and 8-Year Entry Places
**Goal**
Gradually reduce and eventually phase out entry places in 6- and 8-year gimnaziums using a felmenő rendszer (gradual phase-out) to transition towards a standard age 14 selection model.

**Mechanism**
- Gradually reducing enrollment caps in 6- and 8-year tracks limits early academic selection at ages 10 and 12 [evidence: strong — Model knowledge / Hungarian Public Education Act].
- Phasing out these entry points decreases early socioeconomic sorting by keeping higher-performing students in the primary system longer [evidence: moderate — Model knowledge / Hungarian educational policy literature].

**Evidence status**
Overall strong to moderate — High-quality national administrative data confirms tracking ages and demographics, while the impact of delayed tracking is supported by international evidence.

**Assumptions**
- A gradual phase-out (felmenő rendszer) satisfies the constitutional principles of legal certainty and acquired rights.
- Dwindling cohort sizes make the reduction of selective entry places structurally necessary to maintain local school network viability.

**Expected benefits**
- Reduces early socioeconomic sorting by delaying selection to age 14 [evidence: moderate].
- Stabilizes the demographic and academic viability of rural primary school upper-cycles by preventing peer drainage [evidence: moderate].

**Equity impact**
Significantly improves equity by keeping high-performing and high-SES students in standard primary schools until age 14, reducing between-school sorting.

**Cost categories**
- Bilateral contract renegotiations with non-state maintainers
- Primary school curriculum and staff adjustment costs during the transition

**Implementation steps**
- Parliament — Amend the Public Education Act (Nkt.) to mandate a gradual reduction in 6- and 8-year gimnazium slots over a multi-year period.
- Ministry of Public Education — Renegotiate bilateral agreements with church and foundation school maintainers to apply uniform enrollment caps.
- School District Centres (Tankerületek) — Manage local school network planning to absorb remaining students in standard primary school upper-cycles.

**Political risks**
- High risk of middle-class backlash and political mobilization against the reduction of highly coveted academic tracks.
- Legal disputes and compensation claims from church and foundation maintainers over operational autonomy.

**Uncertainties**
- The legal liability and compensation claims if bilateral agreements with church/foundation maintainers are unilaterally altered.
- Whether middle-class parents will bypass the reform by moving children to private or church schools that resist state mandates.

## S3 — Comprehensive School to Age 14 (Structural Reform)
**Goal**
Abolish the 6- and 8-year gimnazium structures and implement a unified, comprehensive lower-secondary school system for all students until age 14 (or age 16, following the Finnish model).

**Mechanism**
- Postponing student tracking to a later age reduces the inequality of educational outcomes without lowering overall performance [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006].
- Delaying academic tracking is associated with substantial gains in test scores, particularly for lower-performing students [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD].

**Evidence status**
Overall strong — Backed by solid international difference-in-differences analyses and regional case studies, though implementation capability in Hungary is weak.

**Assumptions**
- The Polish and Finnish structural reform outcomes are applicable to the Hungarian institutional and socio-political context.
- Standard primary schools can support and stretch high-achieving pupils if early selection is eliminated.

**Expected benefits**
- Substantial reduction in the share of performance variance explained by student socioeconomic status [evidence: strong].
- Improved system-wide educational equity and higher average performance for lower-performing students [evidence: moderate].

**Equity impact**
Maximizes educational equity and minimizes socio-economic sorting by completely eliminating the structural pathways that sort students at ages 10 and 12.

**Cost categories**
- Major teacher retraining and pedagogical support programs
- School building reorganization and administrative transition costs

**Implementation steps**
- Parliament — Rewrite the Public Education Act to abolish 6- and 8-year gimnazium tracks and establish a comprehensive lower-secondary tier.
- Ministry of Public Education — Launch a nationwide teacher retraining program and reallocate school buildings to fit the comprehensive model.
- Kleibelsberg Centre (Klebelsberg Központ) — Administer the redistribution of teaching staff and school resources to handle unified classrooms.

**Political risks**
- Extreme resistance from the vocal urban middle class, creating a high risk of a Polish-style political reversal in subsequent election cycles.
- Severe teacher union opposition due to administrative disruption during an ongoing teacher shortage crisis.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether Hungary has the fiscal headroom and teacher labor market capacity to implement the required pedagogical support systems.
- The precise capacity of regional school infrastructures to absorb building reallocations without requiring expensive new construction.

## S4 — Keep the Structure, Compensate General Schools (Portuguese-Style Package)
**Goal**
Retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system intact but deploy targeted resources, curriculum standards, and support to disadvantaged primary schools to counteract early selection inequities.

**Mechanism**
- Establishing clear standards, school clustering, targeted support for disadvantaged schools (TEIP), and reducing grade retention can improve educational outcomes without changing selection ages [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020].
- Redirecting funds to target interventions in standard schools addresses equity issues within the existing structural framework [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates].

**Evidence status**
Overall moderate to weak — Supported by Portuguese case studies, but the capacity of the centralized Kleibelsberg Centre to implement flexible, targeted programs is highly uncertain.

**Assumptions**
- Administrative capacity exists within the Hungarian centralized school system to coordinate targeted school support programs successfully.
- The peer effects and social stratification inherent in early selection can be neutralized through targeted non-structural funding adjustments.

**Expected benefits**
- Avoids substantial front-loaded structural transition costs [evidence: weak].
- Improves student performance in disadvantaged areas without triggering middle-class resistance or political backlash [evidence: moderate].

**Equity impact**
Attempts to improve equity by raising the quality of standard primary schools, though it leaves the primary engine of early socioeconomic sorting untouched.

**Cost categories**
- Targeted funding programs for schools in disadvantaged areas (TEIP-style)
- Development and implementation of national curriculum standards and remedial support infrastructure

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Public Education — Design and fund a targeted support framework for underperforming schools, focusing on resources and teacher incentives.
- Kleibelsberg Centre (Klebelsberg Központ) — Implement school clustering and coordinate targeted resource allocation to disadvantaged regions.
- Educational Authority (Oktatási Hivatal) — Oversee new national curriculum standards and programs aimed at reducing grade retention.

**Political risks**
- Very low risk of middle-class backlash as elite tracks are fully preserved.
- Potential criticism from reform advocates that the policy fails to address the root structural causes of educational segregation.

**Uncertainties**
- The extent to which Hungary's highly centralized school management system can flexibly implement targeted support programs analogous to the Portuguese model.
- Whether peer effects and socioeconomic segregation can be mitigated without structural changes to the early tracking system.
