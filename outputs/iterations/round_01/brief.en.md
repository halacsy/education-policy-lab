## Evidence

* The 6- and 8-year gimnazium tracks select 8–12% of a student cohort at ages 10 and 12 via a centralized exam, with participants strongly skewed toward high socio-economic status (SES), urban families, segregating students earlier than the standard age 14 transition [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics; Public education act amendments; OECD PISA country notes; model knowledge].
* Hungary’s share of reading and mathematics score variance explained by student SES is among the highest in the OECD, standing at approximately 19–25% compared to the OECD average of approximately 12–14% [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2012-2022 country notes].
* Since the 2013 public education act amendments, most state schools are run by centralized state school district centres (tankerület), while churches and foundations maintain a growing share of schools, which complicates uniform structural mandates [evidence: strong — Public education act amendments; maintainer statistics].
* Shrinking cohort sizes, with annual births down from approximately 125,000 in the early 1990s to approximately 85,000–95,000, will force school-network consolidation in the 2020s and 2030s regardless of the tracking policy chosen [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks].
* The Hungarian public education system faces a sustained teacher shortage and aging workforce, particularly concentrated in mathematics, science, and disadvantaged regions [evidence: strong — OECD Education at a Glance; PSZ/PDSZ surveys].
* Per-pupil funding differences between track types are modest; the primary financial impacts of structural tracking reform are transitional (retraining, building adaptation, administrative reorganization) rather than long-run steady-state costs [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates].
* Reforming centralized admissions criteria and introducing quota systems for disadvantaged pupils reduces SES-based sorting [evidence: moderate — Model knowledge / Hungarian educational policy literature].
* Introducing strict socio-economic quotas or modifying the weighting of the central written admission examination limits the siphoning off of high-SES, urban pupils [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics].
* Increased representation of low-SES and rural students in elite 6- and 8-year tracks is expected under admissions reform [evidence: moderate].
* Modifying admissions weightings reduces the concentration of privilege in urban gimnaziums without incurring high transition costs [evidence: weak].
* Gradually reducing enrollment caps in 6- and 8-year tracks limits early academic selection at ages 10 and 12 [evidence: strong — Model knowledge / Hungarian Public Education Act].
* Phasing out these entry points decreases early socioeconomic sorting by keeping higher-performing students in the primary system longer [evidence: moderate — Model knowledge / Hungarian educational policy literature].
* A gradual phase-out of entry places reduces early socioeconomic sorting by delaying selection to age 14 [evidence: moderate].
* Phasing out entry places stabilizes the demographic and academic viability of rural primary school upper-cycles by preventing peer drainage [evidence: moderate].
* Postponing student tracking to a later age reduces the inequality of educational outcomes without lowering overall performance [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006].
* Delaying academic tracking is associated with substantial gains in test scores, particularly for lower-performing students [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD].
* Implementing a comprehensive school model to age 14 results in a substantial reduction in the share of performance variance explained by student socioeconomic status [evidence: strong].
* Unified lower-secondary schooling leads to improved system-wide educational equity and higher average performance for lower-performing students [evidence: moderate].
* Establishing clear standards, school clustering, targeted support for disadvantaged schools (TEIP), and reducing grade retention can improve educational outcomes without changing selection ages [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020].
* Redirecting funds to target interventions in standard schools addresses equity issues within the existing structural framework [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates].
* Portuguese-style targeted funding packages avoid substantial front-loaded structural transition costs [evidence: weak].
* Targeted funding packages improve student performance in disadvantaged areas without triggering middle-class resistance or political backlash [evidence: moderate].

## Interpretation

* Early academic selection in 6- and 8-year gimnaziums functions primarily as a socio-economic sorting mechanism rather than an academic value-adder, which exacerbates inequality, drains regional school networks, and intensifies geographic segregation amidst demographic decline [interpretation].
* Educational performance and equity can be improved within the existing structure through non-structural adjustments (such as targeted resources and curriculum standards), thereby avoiding massive front-loaded transition costs, intense middle-class resistance, legal liabilities, and administrative strain during an acute teacher shortage [interpretation].
* Academic selection should not be immediately abolished, but rather transitioned gradually via a phased transition (*felmenő rendszer*) over at least eight years while renegotiating maintainer agreements to respect constitutional principles of legal certainty and acquired rights [interpretation].
* Modifying admissions criteria or weightings moderately improves equity within selective tracks, but does not address the broader systemic sorting occurring at the primary school level [interpretation].
* Gradually phasing down entry places significantly improves equity by keeping high-performing and high-SES students in standard primary schools until age 14, reducing between-school sorting [interpretation].
* Implementing a comprehensive school model to age 14 maximizes educational equity and minimizes socio-economic sorting by completely eliminating the structural pathways that sort students at ages 10 and 12 [interpretation].
* Deploying targeted resources to general schools attempts to improve equity by raising the quality of standard primary schools, though it leaves the primary engine of early socioeconomic sorting untouched [interpretation].

## Assumptions

* Elite gimnaziums will not bypass reformed admissions criteria through secondary oral exams or alternative assessment pathways [assumption].
* Middle-class parents will accept admissions quotas rather than seeking to bypass them through church- or foundation-run school entry [assumption].
* A gradual phase-out (felmenő rendszer) satisfies the constitutional principles of legal certainty and acquired rights [assumption].
* Dwindling cohort sizes make the reduction of selective entry places structurally necessary to maintain local school network viability [assumption].
* The Polish and Finnish structural reform outcomes are applicable to the Hungarian institutional and socio-political context [assumption].
* Standard primary schools can support and stretch high-achieving pupils if early selection is eliminated [assumption].
* Administrative capacity exists within the Hungarian centralized school system to coordinate targeted school support programs successfully [assumption].
* The peer effects and social stratification inherent in early selection can be neutralized through targeted non-structural funding adjustments [assumption].
* High sorting leads to suboptimal outcomes, test scores proxy potential, and peer effects shape learning [assumption].
* Non-structural interventions can mitigate inequities, teacher supply is inelastic, and Klebelsberg Center capacity is constrained [assumption].

## Recommendations

* **Option A: Admission Reform (Within Current Structure)**
  * Amend the ministerial decree governing secondary school admissions to introduce SES-based quotas or modify written test weightings.
  * Redesign the central written admission examinations via the Educational Authority to reduce bias and measure raw potential rather than acquired privilege.
  * Direct School District Centres to monitor and enforce compliance with the new admissions rules across state schools.

* **Option B: Gradual Phase-Down of Selective Entry**
  * Amend the Public Education Act to mandate a gradual, phased reduction (felmenő rendszer) in 6- and 8-year gimnazium slots over a multi-year period.
  * Renegotiate bilateral agreements with church and foundation school maintainers to apply uniform enrollment caps.
  * Manage local school network planning through School District Centres to absorb remaining students in standard primary school upper-cycles.

* **Option C: Structural Transition to Comprehensive Lower-Secondary Schooling**
  * Rewrite the Public Education Act to abolish 6- and 8-year gimnazium tracks entirely and establish a unified comprehensive lower-secondary tier until age 14 or 16.
  * Launch a nationwide teacher retraining program and reallocate school buildings to fit the comprehensive model.
  * Administer the redistribution of teaching staff and school resources through the Kleibelsberg Centre to handle unified classrooms.

* **Option D: Retain Structure and Compensate General Schools**
  * Retain the existing 6- and 8-year tracks but design and fund a targeted support framework (similar to the Portuguese TEIP) for underperforming general schools.
  * Coordinate school clustering and targeted resource allocation to disadvantaged regions through the Kleibelsberg Centre.
  * Develop and implement national curriculum standards and remedial support infrastructure through the Educational Authority to reduce grade retention.

## Open questions

* Can modifying admissions criteria significantly reduce sorting given that free school choice and residential segregation are already deeply entrenched at the primary level?
* Are church and foundation school maintainers willing to voluntarily align with reformed admissions criteria, and what are the legal liabilities if their bilateral agreements are unilaterally altered?
* Will middle-class parents bypass admissions or phase-out reforms by moving their children to private, church, or foundation schools that resist state mandates?
* Does Hungary possess the fiscal headroom, regional school infrastructure, and teacher labor market capacity to implement the required pedagogical support systems and teacher retraining under a comprehensive model?
* To what extent can the highly centralized Kleibelsberg Centre flexibly and successfully implement targeted support programs and school clustering analogous to the Portuguese model?
* Can peer effects and socioeconomic segregation in primary education be meaningfully mitigated through targeted funding alone, without structural changes to the early tracking system?
