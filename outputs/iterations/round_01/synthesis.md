## Overview

The expert record examines Hungary's early academic selection via the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system (entry at ages 10–12), which admits roughly 8–12% of each cohort predominantly through centralized written examination. Experts converge on a shared factual picture—early selection is heavily skewed toward high-SES/urban families and Hungary's SES-achievement gradient (19–25% of score variance) substantially exceeds the OECD average (12–14%)—but diverge sharply on whether, how fast, and by what mechanism to reform it. Positions range from substantial narrowing/abolition of selection, through deferral to age 14–16, to non-structural improvement of within-track supports, to incremental negotiated reform that preserves the legal forms. Cross-cutting constraints named across analyses include demographic contraction, teacher shortage, fragmented governance, transition costs, and political durability risk.

## Disagreement map

- **equity_and_social_mobility, international_comparison**: Early selection at age 10–12 should be substantially narrowed or eliminated; it amplifies SES inequality while delivering only modest causal learning gains to selected pupils. Why: value-added studies find the selective-track premium is predominantly a selection artifact [evidence: moderate], and early tracking raises SES stratification without mean-performance gains across OECD systems [evidence: strong].
- **implementation_planning, school_network_planning, education_finance**: Defer selection to age 14–16 rather than abolish, phased and conditional on transition funding and teacher supply protection. Why: reform requires teacher reallocation/retraining under acute shortage [evidence: strong], transition costs dominate over marginal steady-state savings [evidence: weak], and deferral lets mixed-ability secondaries retain critical mass under demographic decline [evidence: moderate].
- **portuguese_reform**: (minority) Hungary could achieve equity/quality gains by intensifying non-structural supports (curriculum standards, school clustering, TEIP-style targeted funding, retention alternatives) before or instead of dismantling selection. Why: Portugal raised PISA scores 2006–2015 without lowering selection age, suggesting structural abolition is not a necessary condition for improvement [evidence: moderate].
- **legal_and_governance**: (minority) Preserve the legal forms; pursue incremental negotiated reform with diagnostic (non-selective) assessment and flexible re-assignment, rather than statutory abolition. Why: the forms are statutory and durable, maintainer fragmentation makes uniform mandates unenforceable, and abolition faces legal/contractual barriers [evidence: strong].
- **hungarian_education_system**: Track integration with deferred selection (post-14) must be coupled with simultaneous reform of free-choice rules and catchment boundaries; abolition alone would displace rather than reduce stratification. Why: choice, catchment, and maintainer diversity fragment the system independently of track existence [evidence: moderate].
- **finnish_reform**: Removing selection alone cannot transfer an equity dividend; it must be bundled with teacher-quality investment, integrated support, and transparent within-school inclusion. Why: Finland's peruskoulu equity effect was bundled with teacher professionalization and support, not attributable to selection abolition alone [evidence: moderate].
- **polish_reform, political_feasibility**: Reform durability, not technical efficacy, is the binding constraint; delayed selection is reversible within an electoral cycle unless locked in via workforce retraining, parental communication, or credible compensation for displaced middle-class families. Why: Poland delayed tracking to age 15 with measurable PISA gains yet reversed 2016–2019 under middle-class political pressure [evidence: moderate], and the defending constituency is small, concentrated, and politically articulate [evidence: strong/implicit].
- **demography, school_network_planning**: Cohort decline forces school-network consolidation regardless of selection policy; early selection increases the social cost of necessary closures. Why: births fell ~28–32% and will eliminate ~25–30% of pupil places by 2040 [evidence: strong], while selective schools concentrate demand and deplete standard schools [evidence: moderate].

## What the experts agree on

- Entry to 6/8-year gimnazium tracks is heavily skewed toward high-SES and urban families; selection admits only ~8–12% of the cohort. [evidence: strong]
- Hungary's SES-achievement gradient (19–25% of score variance) substantially exceeds the OECD average (12–14%). [evidence: strong]
- Early tracking (age 10–12) sits within the international window associated with increased SES stratification without systematic mean-performance gains. [evidence: strong/moderate]
- Selection abolition alone is insufficient: sorting persists via choice, catchment, within-school ability grouping, or bundled-support absence unless jointly addressed. [evidence: moderate]
- Structural reform is not primarily a cost-saving measure; transition costs dominate and reform must be justified on equity/learning grounds. [evidence: weak/moderate]
- Governance is fragmented (tankerület state centres, churches, foundations since 2013), making uniform structural mandates administratively and legally complex. [evidence: strong]
- Demographic contraction independently forces network consolidation across the 2020s–2040 period regardless of selection policy. [evidence: strong]

## Preserved uncertainties

- No robust causal evidence on total inequality/sorting outcomes if tracks are abolished while choice and catchment remain unchanged (hungarian_education_system, equity_and_social_mobility).
- The magnitude of equity gain attributable specifically to selection abolition versus bundled supports is not decomposed in the Finnish or Portuguese sources (finnish_reform, portuguese_reform).
- International elasticities (Poland, Finland, Portugal) may not transfer to Hungary's institutional context; Hungarian-specific value-added and peer-effect estimates are limited (international_comparison, equity_and_social_mobility, polish_reform).
- Whether Hungary's high SES gradient stems primarily from selection timing versus within-school resource allocation, curriculum differentiation, or family behaviour remains open (international_comparison).
- Reversibility/transition costs, teacher-retraining cost and timeline (~5,000–8,000 teachers), and whether regional teacher shortage is an addressable or hard constraint are unquantified (implementation_planning, education_finance).
- Whether church/foundation maintainers would accept deferred-selection mandates or resist via admissions practice, and the enforceability of maintainer agreements, is unstudied in the provided sources (hungarian_education_system, legal_and_governance).
- The political appetite for legislative amendment, redistribution, and transition funding—and whether staged reform reduces or merely prolongs reversal risk—is unknown (political_feasibility, legal_and_governance).
- Regional variation in birth decline, migration effects, and prestige-school demand elasticity to selection age are unquantified (demography, school_network_planning).
