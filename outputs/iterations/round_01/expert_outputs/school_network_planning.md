# Expert analysis: school_network_planning

## Findings (evidence)

- **Demographic constraint is binding.** Annual births fell from ~125k (early 1990s) to ~85–95k; cohort shrinkage forces school-network consolidation decisions in the 2020s–2030s regardless of selection policy. [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks]

- **Governance is fragmented.** Since 2013 most state schools are run by state school district centres (tankerulet); churches and foundations maintain a growing share of schools, complicating any uniform structural mandate. [evidence: strong — Public education act amendments; maintainer statistics]

- **Selection at age 11–13 creates winners and losers early.** Early academic tracking in 6- and 8-year programmes sorts pupils into prestige-path and non-prestige cohorts before secondary school entry, locking in unequal trajectories. [evidence: moderate — international literature on tracking effects (Hanushek & Wößmann); Hungarian-specific outcome data not cited in curated sources]

- **Network consolidation and selection policy interact.** Towns losing pupils must choose: close neighbourhood secondary schools (forcing longer commutes), consolidate with remaining grammar schools, or maintain fragmented provision. Selection systems that concentrate prestige pupils in central locations intensify this tension. [evidence: moderate — school choice literature; Hungarian-specific feasibility data not in curated sources]

## Interpretation

Cohort shrinkage is not optional—it will eliminate ~25–30% of pupil places by 2040. The real policy choice is **not whether to consolidate, but whether early selection accelerates or buffers that consolidation's social cost.**

If 6- and 8-year selection pathways are retained:
- Prestige grammar schools concentrate in larger towns; smaller towns lose academically-streamed cohorts.
- Non-selected pupils remain in underfunded secondary schools with narrower curricula and weaker labour-market outcomes.
- Commuting tolerance (how far families will send children) becomes a binding constraint in rural/small-town areas.

If selection is deferred (e.g. to age 15–16) or abolished:
- Mixed-ability secondaries can retain critical mass longer, reducing small-town school closures.
- Network consolidation is still necessary, but distributes pain more evenly.

## Assumptions

- Commuting tolerance for secondary pupils is ~30 min / ~15 km in rural areas, higher in urban regions. [assumption]
- Selection policy effectiveness (value-added to top-stream pupils) is sufficient to justify the stratification cost. [assumption — not verified in curated sources]
- Church and foundation schools will not be mandated into a uniform system; policy must work within pluralist governance. [assumption]

## Position

Hungary should decouple network consolidation (necessary due to demography) from early selection (not necessary and complicates town-by-town feasibility): defer selection to age 15–16, maintain mixed-ability secondaries in smaller towns as hubs, and reserve 6–8-year grammar pathways for verified high-capacity students identified after age 13, when reliability improves.

## Uncertainties

- What is the actual commuting-distance tolerance by town size and region? [unknown]
- How many pupils currently benefit (in earnings/attainment) from early selection vs. how many are mislabelled at age 11? [unknown — no Hungarian longitudinal data cited]
- Will fragmented governance (state centres, churches, foundations) permit a coherent network plan? [unknown — depends on ministry coordination]
- What is the elasticity of prestige-school demand to selection age? [unknown]
