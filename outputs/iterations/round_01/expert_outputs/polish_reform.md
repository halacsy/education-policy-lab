# Expert analysis: Early selection and grammar school tracking in Hungary

## Findings (evidence)

- Poland delayed academic tracking from age 11 to 15 via the 1999 gimnazjum reform; PISA scores rose substantially by 2012, with peer-reviewed analysis attributing a portion of gains to postponed selection. [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD]

- Poland's 2016–2019 reversal occurred despite positive test-score evidence, indicating that selection timing is determined by political coalition priorities, not solely by measured learning outcomes. [evidence: moderate — institutional record; OECD reversal documentation]

- Early tracking (age 11) concentrates disadvantaged students in lower streams, amplifying initial disparities; delayed tracking (age 15) permits longer exposure to mixed-ability environments, reducing inequality of opportunity. [evidence: moderate — comparative European education research; Dupriez et al. structural findings]

- Hungary currently operates 6- and 8-year secondary grammar schools (gimnazium selection at age 10–12), creating two-tier segmentation similar to pre-1999 Poland. [evidence: moderate — Hungarian education system structure; OECD country profile]

## Interpretation

Poland's trajectory offers a cautionary case: technical evidence of reform success does not guarantee institutional durability when political costs (e.g., elite dissatisfaction, parental choice constraints) exceed electoral salience of equity gains. The reversal despite measured gains suggests that delayed-tracking reforms require sustained political consensus or voter commitment to equity, not just evidence of efficacy.

For Hungary, the Poland precedent implies:
1. A shift toward later selection would likely improve average PISA performance and reduce tracked-stream inequality, but only if maintained across electoral cycles.
2. Early reversal is a plausible political risk, particularly if middle-class parent coalitions mobilize against mixed-ability instruction.

## Assumptions

- PISA gains in Poland post-1999 were causally linked to delayed tracking (not confounded by contemporaneous curriculum, teacher investment, or measurement effects). [assumption]
- Hungarian student composition and institutional capacity are sufficiently similar to Poland's 1999 baseline to permit analogous outcomes. [assumption]
- Political durability of reform depends on sustained public legitimacy or structural lock-in (e.g., teaching workforce reorientation). [assumption]

## Position

Hungary should delay selection to age 15 if willing to lock in reform via workforce retraining and parental communication campaigns; otherwise, early selection reversal is likely within one electoral cycle, neutralizing equity and efficiency gains.

## Uncertainties

- I do not have quantified Hungarian baseline equity metrics or comparative selection-timing data for Hungary.
- The specific political coalitions and voter preferences in Hungary are outside my training domain; reversibility risk cannot be estimated without local political mapping.
- Causal attribution of Poland's PISA gains to tracking delay versus other reforms is contested; I cannot isolate the effect size.
- Long-term labour market outcomes of delayed versus early selection in Poland post-reversal are unknown to me.
