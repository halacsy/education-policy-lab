# Expert analysis: portuguese_reform
## Findings (evidence)
- Portugal has implemented targeted support programs for schools in disadvantaged areas (TEIP) to improve educational outcomes [evidence: strong — OECD (2019). PISA 2018 Results (Volume I): What Students Know and Can Do].
- Efforts to reduce grade repetition in Portugal have shown some success, contributing to improved retention rates [evidence: moderate — European Commission (2020). Education and Training Monitor 2020: Portugal].
- Non-structural improvements, such as pedagogical support and curriculum adjustments, can complement structural reforms in education [evidence: strong — OECD (2019). PISA 2018 Results (Volume I): What Students Know and Can Do].
## Interpretation
The Portuguese experience suggests that focusing on non-structural improvements, such as targeted support for struggling schools and initiatives to reduce grade repetition, can yield positive results in educational systems. These measures, while not altering the fundamental structure of schooling, address key challenges that can hinder student success and equity. The effectiveness of these approaches lies in their ability to provide tailored interventions that meet specific needs within the existing educational framework.
## Assumptions
- The effectiveness of targeted support programs like TEIP is transferable to different national contexts, assuming similar underlying challenges exist. [assumption]
- Reducing grade repetition is a desirable outcome that correlates with improved educational attainment and efficiency. [assumption]
## Position
Hungary should consider implementing targeted support for schools facing challenges and strategies to reduce grade repetition as complementary measures to any structural reforms of its early academic selection and grammar school system.
## Uncertainties
- The specific impact of Portugal's TEIP program on long-term academic achievement and equity is not fully detailed in the provided evidence.
- The optimal design and implementation of grade repetition reduction strategies for the Hungarian context are unknown.
- The extent to which non-structural improvements can compensate for potential drawbacks of early academic selection in Hungary remains to be analyzed.
