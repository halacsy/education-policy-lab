# Expert analysis: portuguese_reform

## Findings (evidence)

- Portugal raised PISA reading, mathematics and science scores substantially between 2006 and 2015 without lowering the age of academic selection or dismantling early-tracked secondary structures. [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020]

- Portugal's improvement strategy combined four non-structural levers: curriculum standards alignment, school clustering (agrupamentos), targeted intensive support (TEIP) for disadvantaged schools, and reduction of grade retention rates. [evidence: moderate — Crato 2020]

- Grade retention in Portugal fell from ~35% (1990s) to ~12% (2015), concurrent with PISA gains, suggesting retention reduction may amplify effect of other interventions. [evidence: moderate — OECD Education at a Glance; national statistical reports]

- Early selection systems (age 10–12) persist in Hungary, Germany, Austria and Czech Republic; systems without early selection (e.g. Spain, Portugal post-reform framing) show variable outcomes, indicating selection timing alone does not determine performance. [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA cross-country analysis]

## Interpretation

Portugal's case demonstrates that structural abolition of tracked entry is not a necessary condition for performance improvement. Instead, **quality within tracks and better support at the lower end** can offset selection's stratifying effects. The TEIP programme—intensive investment in low-performing schools—may be the operative factor: it reduces the equity cost of early selection by narrowing the skill gap before tracking decisions.

For Hungary, this suggests a staged alternative to immediate grammar school reform: strengthen curriculum coherence across all secondary pathways, pilot intensive support in underperforming vocational and lower-track schools, and measure whether selection effects narrow before structural change.

## Assumptions

- Portugal's curriculum and teacher-quality baseline are comparable enough to Hungary's that TEIP-style support would transfer. [assumption]
- Retention reduction works partly through confidence/motivation effects, not only through skill acquisition, and these mechanisms operate similarly across Central European contexts. [assumption]
- Hungary's political appetite for targeted school funding (necessary for TEIP) matches Portugal's 2006–2015 fiscal commitment. [assumption]

## Position

Hungary could achieve meaningful equity and quality gains by intensifying non-structural supports (curriculum standards, school clustering, targeted remedial funding, retention alternatives) before or instead of dismantling early grammar school selection.

## Uncertainties

- What proportion of Portugal's gain came from retention reduction *vs.* TEIP *vs.* standards alignment? The sources do not decompose effects.
- Does Hungary's specific grammar school structure (6- and 8-year pathways, entry age 10–12) create different stratification dynamics than Portugal's lycée model?
- How long does a TEIP-style intervention need to run before effects on downstream selection outcomes are visible (5, 10, 15 years)?
- Are there Hungarian pilot data on targeted support efficacy, or would this be an untested import?
