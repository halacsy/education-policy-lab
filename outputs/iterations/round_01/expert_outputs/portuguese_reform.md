# Expert analysis: portuguese_reform

## Findings (evidence)
- Portugal significantly improved its PISA results between 2006 and 2015 without altering its academic selection age. [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020]
- The Portuguese improvement path relied on establishing clear curriculum standards, school clustering, targeted support for schools in disadvantaged areas (TEIP), and a focus on reducing grade retention. [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020]

## Interpretation
Structural reorganization, such as abolishing the Hungarian 6- and 8-year gimnazium system, is politically contentious and complex. Portugal's experience suggests that educational outcomes and equity can be significantly enhanced within an existing structural framework. By shifting the policy focus from structural dismantling to targeted assistance (e.g., funding and resources for underperforming schools) and rigorous standards, Hungary can address the equity issues associated with early tracking without the disruption of a major institutional overhaul.

## Assumptions
- The administrative capacity exists within the Hungarian educational system to coordinate targeted school support programs similar to Portugal's TEIP. [assumption]
- The inequities in Hungary's early selection system are primarily driven by resource disparities and varying school quality, which can be mitigated through targeted non-structural interventions. [assumption]

## Position
Hungary should retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system but complement it with non-structural reforms—specifically, targeted resource allocation to disadvantaged schools, national curriculum standards, and policies to reduce grade retention—to mitigate selection inequities.

## Uncertainties
- The extent to which Hungary's centralized school management system (Kleibelsberg Centre) can flexibly implement targeted support programs analogous to the Portuguese model.
- Whether the peer effects and social stratification inherent in Hungary’s early selection can be fully neutralized solely through non-structural adjustments.
