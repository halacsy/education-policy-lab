# Expert analysis: Hungarian early academic selection and 6/8-year gimnazium system

## Findings (evidence)

- **Selection scale and timing:** Approximately 8–12% of each cohort enters 6- or 8-year gimnazium tracks, selected at age 10 (6-year) or 12 (8-year) predominantly through centralized written examination. [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics]

- **Socioeconomic skew:** Entrants to these tracks are strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families, creating an early segmentation mechanism that correlates with family resources rather than ability alone. [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics]

- **Selection does not exhaust sorting:** Free school choice plus early selective tracks produces substantial between-school sorting already at primary stage in cities; abolition of the gimnazium tracks alone would not eliminate tracking, as choice and catchment-based sorting would persist. [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013; Berenyi-Berkovits-Erőss school choice studies]

- **Maintainer fragmentation:** Since 2013, most state schools operate under state school district centres (tankerulet); churches and foundations maintain a growing share of schools, making uniform structural mandates administratively complex. [evidence: strong — Public education act amendments; maintainer statistics]

## Interpretation

Early selection at ages 10–12 via high-stakes examination serves as a gatekeeper that legitimizes and locks in existing socioeconomic advantage. The examination format filters for test-prep advantage and parental investment, not purely innate ability. However, the sorting problem is **structural and multi-layered**: choice, catchment, and maintainer diversity all fragment the system independently of track existence. Removing tracks without addressing choice architecture and catchment effects would likely displace rather than reduce stratification.

## Assumptions

- The examination-based selection mechanism is the primary driver of early tracking effects (not school choice or catchment alone). [assumption]
- "Early selection" is treated as a policy lever distinct from broader school segregation. [assumption]

## Position

Hungary should integrate the 6/8-year gimnazium tracks into a unified secondary system with deferred selection (post-14), coupled with simultaneous reform of free choice rules and catchment boundaries to reduce between-school sorting; track abolition alone risks masking rather than addressing stratification.

## Uncertainties

- **Counterfactual outcomes:** No robust causal evidence on what happens to total inequality or sorting if tracks are abolished while choice/catchment remain unchanged.
- **International comparability:** Evidence on Hungarian outcomes vs. comparable systems (Czechia, Slovakia, Poland) with different track ages is not cited in curated sources; model knowledge only.
- **Maintainer compliance:** Whether church/foundation schools would accept deferred selection mandates or resist via admissions practice is unstudied in provided sources.
- **Mechanism of SES skew:** Is advantage primarily examination score, application rate, or parental navigation of choice? Unclear from citations.
