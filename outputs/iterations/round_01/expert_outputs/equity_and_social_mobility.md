# Expert analysis: equity_and_social_mobility

## Findings (evidence)

- Hungary exhibits among the highest SES gradients in academic achievement in the OECD: socio-economic status explains 19–25% of reading and mathematics score variance, nearly double the OECD average of 12–14%. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2012-2022 country notes]

- Early selection into 6- and 8-year gimnazium tracks (at ages 10–12) is restricted to 8–12% of each cohort and is heavily skewed toward high-SES and urban families. [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics]

- The observed achievement premium of early-selective tracks is predominantly a selection artifact rather than a causal effect: value-added studies isolating peer and institutional effects find modest genuine gains for admitted pupils. [evidence: moderate — Horn (2013); Kertesi & Kezdi school-effect research]

- Early selection concentrates high-ability and high-SES peers in selective tracks, leaving non-selected pupils in schools depleted of both cognitive diversity and resources. [evidence: moderate — implicit in selection mechanics and peer-effect literature; direct peer-effect estimates for Hungary flagged as uncertain below]

## Interpretation

The evidence points to a system that amplifies pre-existing inequality without generating proportional learning gains. Selection at age 10–12 locks in family advantage: families with cultural capital and wealth navigate entry; schools then reinforce this through peer composition rather than pedagogy. The high national SES gradient suggests selection functions as a *sorting mechanism, not an equity corrector*. Non-selected schools lose both top students and the resources (political voice, motivated parents, teacher quality) that follow them.

The moderate value-added finding is critical: if most of the raw advantage is selection-driven, the system is succeeding at stratification but not at value creation. This raises efficiency *and* equity concerns.

## Assumptions

- [assumption] The KSH/Hivatal statistics accurately capture SES composition at entry; unmeasured advantages (test prep, language exposure) may be even more concentrated.
- [assumption] Horn and Kertesi & Kezdi estimates of value-added are causal under their identification assumptions; unmeasured school inputs may still confer real gains not captured in score-based models.
- [assumption] The question frames early selection as a Hungarian policy choice; in fact, selective tracks are embedded in broader structural choices (curriculum, teacher allocation, regional inequality) that would need joint reform.

## Position

Early academic selection at age 10–12 in Hungary should be substantially narrowed or eliminated, because it amplifies SES-based inequality while delivering only modest causal learning gains to selected pupils and actively harming peer composition in non-selective schools.

## Uncertainties

- **Peer effects on non-selected pupils**: I cannot quantify how much non-selective schools lose cognitively or socially when top students depart. Hungarian-specific peer-effect estimates are not in the curated sources.
- **Counterfactual school quality**: What would non-selective schools look like with equal resources and no cream-skimming? Evidence on de-selection reforms (e.g., Germany's Länder moves to comprehensive systems) exists but is not curated here.
- **Teacher supply and allocation**: Do selective tracks draw disproportionately experienced or qualified teachers? This affects both selected and non-selected pupils but is not addressed in the curated evidence.
- **Age 10–12 predictive validity**: How stably does selection at this age predict later outcomes relative to later assessment? This bears on whether earlier selection reduces or amplifies inequality.
