# Expert analysis: equity_and_social_mobility

## Findings (evidence)
- In PISA, the share of reading/maths score variance explained by socio-economic status is among the highest in the OECD for Hungary, standing at ~19-25% across cycles compared to the OECD average of ~12-14% [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2012-2022 country notes].
- Approximately 8-12% of a cohort enters 6- or 8-year gimnazium tracks, selected at age 10 or 12 largely via the central written admission examination, with entrants strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics].
- Hungarian value-added studies find the raw academic advantage of early-selective gimnazium tracks is mostly a selection effect, meaning the causal (value-added) gain for admitted pupils is modest [evidence: moderate — Horn (2013); Kertesi & Kezdi school-effect studies].
- General tracking literature shows that earlier selection ages exacerbate socio-economic sorting and depress the achievement of students left in non-selective tracks due to negative peer effects [evidence: moderate — Model knowledge / general economic literature on tracking].

## Interpretation
The 6- and 8-year gimnazium system functions primarily as a mechanism for socio-economic segregation rather than academic value creation. By siphoning off high-SES and high-performing students at ages 10 and 12, the system concentrates disadvantage in standard primary schools. Because the causal value-added of these selective schools is modest, the high performance of their pupils is a reflection of intake composition rather than superior educational efficacy. Consequently, early tracking amplifies Hungary's exceptionally high SES-gradient in student outcomes without yielding substantial net academic returns.

## Assumptions
- Standardised test scores (PISA, central exams) are valid proxies for student potential and academic performance [assumption].
- Peer composition significantly influences the learning environment and outcomes of non-selected pupils [assumption].
- System-level equity can be improved by altering structural sorting mechanisms [assumption].

## Position
Hungary should phase out the 6- and 8-year gimnazium tracks and defer academic selection to age 16 to mitigate socio-economic segregation and reduce the equity gap in student performance.

## Uncertainties
- The precise magnitude of negative peer effects on students remaining in the standard 8-year primary schools following the departure of the top 8-12% of peers.
- The capacity and readiness of the standard primary school system to support and stretch high-achieving pupils if early selection is eliminated.
- The political and social feasibility of reform, given the high concentration of middle- and upper-class parental interest in preserving selective tracks.
