# Expert analysis: legal_and_governance

## Findings (evidence)
- The 6- and 8-year gimnazium forms are anchored in the public education act and maintainer agreements; phase-out requires legislative change, multi-year notice, and transitional guarantees for enrolled pupils [evidence: strong — Nkt. (public education act) analysis].
- Since 2013, most state schools are run by state school district centres (tankerület), while churches and foundations maintain a growing share of schools, which complicates any uniform structural mandate [evidence: strong — Public education act amendments; maintainer statistics].
- Any immediate restructuring of educational tracks without transition periods violates constitutional principles of legal certainty and acquired rights for students currently enrolled [evidence: strong — Model knowledge (Hungarian Constitutional Court practice on "felmenő rendszer")].

## Interpretation
The legal landscape of Hungarian public education prevents rapid structural changes. Because authority is split between state district centres (tankerületek) and non-state maintainers (churches/foundations), a uniform command-and-control phase-out of the 6- and 8-year systems is legally complex. Non-state maintainers operate under bilateral agreements that cannot be unilaterally terminated overnight without triggering legal liabilities or political-legal disputes. Furthermore, the constitutional principle of *felmenő rendszer* (gradual phase-in/phase-out) guarantees that students already enrolled in these programs must be allowed to complete their studies under the rules in force at their time of entry.

## Assumptions
- Non-state maintainers (churches and foundations) will legally contest unilateral state interventions that infringe upon their operational autonomy or school structures [assumption].
- The Hungarian parliament retains the legislative sovereignty to amend the Public Education Act (Nkt.), provided it respects constitutional transition guarantees [assumption].

## Position
Hungary should not immediately abolish early selection, but rather implement a legally phased transition (*felmenő rendszer*) over a minimum of eight years, while simultaneously renegotiating maintainer agreements with churches and foundations to align structural mandates.

## Uncertainties
- The precise legal liability and financial compensation claims that foundation and church maintainers could lodge if their bilateral agreements are unilaterally altered.
- The degree of political willingness within the legislative branch to challenge the growing structural autonomy of church-run secondary schools.
