# Expert analysis: political_feasibility

## Findings (evidence)
- Urban middle-class Hungarian parents view 8- and 6-year gimnazium slots as a critical mobility and insurance asset, and previous policy attempts to restrict these tracks triggered intense, well-organized stakeholder resistance. [evidence: moderate — Domestic press and stakeholder analyses, 1990s-2010s]
- Poland's 1999 education reform successfully delayed tracking until age 15, resulting in substantial PISA score gains by 2012 that researchers partially linked to delayed selection. [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD]
- The structural changes introduced by Poland's 1999 reform were fully reversed between 2016 and 2019 due to political reasons. [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD]

## Interpretation
The Hungarian 6- and 8-year gimnazium structures are politically fortified by the concentrated interests of the vocal urban middle class. While international precedents like Poland demonstrate that delaying tracking can yield significant, measurable pedagogical benefits (such as higher PISA scores), they also highlight severe long-term vulnerability to political reversal if stakeholder alignment is not sustained. In Hungary, any attempt to abruptly abolish or restrict early academic selection will trigger concentrated resistance from influential parents. Conversely, the diffuse beneficiaries of a more equitable, delayed-selection system (e.g., lower-income or rural families) lack equivalent organizational mobilization, creating a severe political asymmetry.

## Assumptions
- The Hungarian urban middle class possesses sufficient political influence and media access to veto unpopular structural education changes under the current electoral regime. [assumption]
- PISA score improvements alone are insufficient to build a self-defending political coalition for educational reforms. [assumption]

## Position
Hungary should retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium structures but restrict their future expansion while simultaneously upgrading standard 8-grade schools, as outright abolition is politically undeliverable and highly prone to reversal.

## Uncertainties
- The precise threshold of political pressure at which the current Hungarian government would compromise on education structures to protect its urban voter base.
- Whether teacher unions or municipal administrations in Hungary would align with parents or the state if reform compensation packages were offered.
