# Expert analysis: political_feasibility

## Findings (evidence)

- Urban middle-class parents treat early grammar school selection (6- and 8-year gimnazium places) as a non-negotiable asset for social mobility and risk protection; prior reform attempts faced organised, sustained political opposition that successfully blocked change. [evidence: moderate — Domestic press and stakeholder analyses, 1990s-2010s]

- Poland's 1999 reform delayed tracking to age 15 and achieved measurable PISA gains by 2012, but this reform was reversed 2016-2019 despite evidence of effectiveness, driven by political pressure from middle-class constituencies who perceived delayed selection as a threat to advantage. [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD]

- Early selection in Hungary (age 10–12) concentrates advantaged cohorts in grammar schools, while systematically excluding lower-SES children even when academically capable; this reproduces inequality but benefits a politically mobilised minority. [evidence: strong (implicit in design); source: structural feature of system documented in education policy literature]

## Interpretation

Any Hungarian reform that reduces early selection faces a credibility problem: the Polish precedent shows that even evidence-backed improvements can be reversed within 5–10 years if the beneficiary middle class perceives loss of positional advantage. Hungary's middle-class parents are not irrational—they are correctly identifying that delayed selection redistributes scarcity (elite school places) downward.

The durability risk is acute because:
1. The constituency defending early selection is small, concentrated, politically articulate, and well-resourced.
2. Reversals are cheaper than sustained opposition (Poland's reversal was a political win for conservatives).
3. Evidence of system-level gains (equity, reduced early dropout, PISA improvements) may not outweigh perceived individual loss for the mobilised group.

## Assumptions

- The middle-class resistance documented in 1990s–2010s Hungary would activate again under a similar reform threat. [assumption]
- Political parties see middle-class education preferences as a non-negotiable constituency issue rather than a contestable policy choice. [assumption]
- "Success" in delayed selection requires sustained commitment across electoral cycles; a single reversal wipes out gains. [assumption]

## Position

Early selection reform in Hungary is technically feasible and evidence-supported (per Poland's model), but politically durable only if bundled with *credible compensation mechanisms* for displaced middle-class families—e.g., expanded elite school capacity, parallel high-quality vocational pathways with tertiary access, or staged implementation with grandfathering—that prevent zero-sum perception.

## Uncertainties

- What proportion of middle-class resistance is ideological vs. genuinely rooted in local labour market returns to early selection? No Hungarian cost-benefit analysis is cited.
- Would staged reform (e.g., raising selection age to 12, then 14) reduce reversal risk, or merely extend the conflict?
- What is the current political appetite for redistribution in education among Hungarian governing parties? Unknown without current party positioning.
- Could evidence of improved tertiary access for non-selected cohorts shift middle-class calculation, or is positional advantage itself the target? Untested.
