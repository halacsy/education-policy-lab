# Expert analysis: implementation_planning

## Findings (evidence)

- Hungary operates a dual-track secondary system (6- and 8-year gimnazium selection at ages 11–12) that stratifies access by family wealth and parental education, producing persistent inequality in outcomes. [evidence: moderate — international comparative education literature; PISA/TIMSS trend data]

- Teacher shortage is acute and structural: Hungary has ~15% fewer teachers per pupil than OECD average, with severe regional concentration in disadvantaged areas and critical gaps in maths/science. [evidence: strong — OECD Education at a Glance; PSZ/PDSZ union surveys]

- The teacher workforce is ageing (median age ~48–50); retirement cohorts over the next 7–10 years will exceed new entrant supply under current recruitment conditions. [evidence: strong — Hungarian Statistical Office; OECD data]

- Per-pupil funding differences between track types are modest in steady state; transition costs (curriculum redesign, teacher retraining, building repurposing, administrative restructuring) dominate near-term budget impact. [evidence: weak — budget line items and expert estimates; no published transition-cost analysis exists for Hungary]

- Early selection (age 11–12) is associated internationally with larger achievement gaps and reduced intergenerational mobility in systems with high tracking. [evidence: moderate — Causa & Chapuis (OECD); Brunello & Checchi]

## Interpretation

Any structural reform—whether delaying selection, merging tracks, or broadening access—requires reallocation of teachers to new institutions/curricula and retraining in mixed-ability pedagogy. Under current teacher shortage conditions, this reallocation is zero-sum: teachers moved to reform implementation are unavailable for existing school maintenance, particularly in already-under-resourced regions.

The timing problem is critical: retiring cohorts (2025–2035) create a narrow window where workforce transitions *could* align with structural reform, but only if recruitment and retraining are front-loaded now.

## Assumptions

- Teacher retraining is feasible within 2–3 years for pedagogical method shift (mixed-ability teaching). [assumption]
- Regional teacher mobility can be influenced through incentives (housing, salary differentials) but will remain incomplete. [assumption]
- Early selection's equality costs outweigh its sorting efficiency gains for Hungary's policy objectives. [assumption]

## Position

Hungary should delay formal academic selection from age 11–12 to age 14–15 and pilot integrated lower-secondary tracks in 3–4 regions with recruitment/retraining support, conditional on securing dedicated transition funding and protecting teacher supply in disadvantaged areas; otherwise, reform will hollow out rural schools.

## Uncertainties

- No published estimate of the cost and timeline for retraining ~5,000–8,000 teachers in mixed-ability pedagogy exists for Hungary.
- Whether political will exists to fund transition costs separately from operating budgets is unknown.
- Parental demand effects (flight to private or selective institutions) under delayed selection are not modelled for Hungary's specific context.
- Whether regional teacher shortage is addressable through incentives or represents a hard constraint is contested among stakeholders.
