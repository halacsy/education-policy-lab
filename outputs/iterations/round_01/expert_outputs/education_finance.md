# Expert analysis: education_finance

## Findings (evidence)

- Per-pupil funding differences between grammar school (gimnazium) and general secondary tracks are modest in magnitude [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates]. This means steady-state recurrent cost savings from track consolidation are unlikely to be large.

- Structural reform costs are dominated by transition rather than ongoing budget items: staff retraining, building repurposing, administrative restructuring, and redundancy management [evidence: weak — expert estimates]. These are one-time or multi-year front-loaded expenses, not permanent savings.

- Early selection systems (age 10–12 entry to 6- or 8-year gimnazium) sort by parental education and socioeconomic status at least as much as by academic aptitude [evidence: moderate — OECD reviews of Central European tracking; Hungarian data from КОК/ОКМ]. This implies equity effects will dominate cost effects in any reform evaluation.

- Grammar school capacity in Hungary is oversubscribed in urban centers and underutilized in rural areas [evidence: moderate — Ministry of Education administrative data; regional enrollment patterns]. A system redesign must address geographic mismatch, not just aggregate numbers.

## Interpretation

The finance lens narrows the reform problem: Hungary cannot justify structural change on cost grounds alone. Steady-state savings are marginal. Any reform must be evaluated primarily on **equity and learning outcomes**, with transition costs as a secondary constraint to manage.

If early selection persists, the finance case for change is weak. If Hungary moves toward later common schooling (e.g., age 14+), the transition cost burden falls on the 2–4 years immediately after reform. Retraining secondary teachers in mixed-cohort pedagogy, redeploying grammar school buildings, and managing staff surpluses are the real budget impacts.

## Assumptions

- Funding formulas remain unchanged during transition [assumption]
- Teacher labor market absorbs retraining costs without large wage increases [assumption]
- Building repurposing or closure does not trigger large infrastructure write-downs [assumption]

## Position

Delaying early selection to age 14+ is financially feasible but requires front-loaded transition budgeting; it is not a cost-saving reform, and should be justified on equity grounds, not budget relief.

## Uncertainties

- Exact per-pupil cost differences by track type (no transparent costing by track in public Hungarian budget documents found in sources)
- Regional demand elasticity if grammar school access changes
- Teacher retraining cost per FTE and reabsorption timeline
- Whether building repurposing generates offsetting revenue or land-use gains
