# Expert analysis: international_comparison

## Findings (evidence)

- Early between-school tracking (selection before age 11) increases socio-economic stratification of outcomes without raising mean achievement across OECD systems. [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006, Economic Journal]

- Hungary's reading and mathematics score variance attributable to socio-economic status is substantially elevated: 19–25% vs. 12–14% OECD average across PISA cycles 2012–2022. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA country notes 2012–2022]

- The 6- and 8-year grammar school system in Hungary selects students at ages 11–12, placing selection timing within the early-tracking window identified in comparative literature as linked to equity loss. [evidence: moderate — structural familiarity; explicit comparative timing analysis not in curated sources]

- Postponing or eliminating early selection (moving to late tracking or comprehensive systems) shows modest or neutral effects on mean performance but consistently reduces SES-score gradient in meta-analyses. [evidence: moderate — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006 plus subsequent replication literature; not explicitly curated]

## Interpretation

Hungary faces a direct alignment problem: its early selection system operates within the international evidence window flagged as equity-costly, and its SES gradient already exceeds OECD norms substantially. The curated evidence suggests that the current 6/8-year grammar school architecture is not a unique high-performing model—early tracking systems do not systematically outperform late-tracking or comprehensive peers on average outcomes. Removing or delaying selection would predictably flatten the SES-outcome gradient, though the magnitude of equity gain specific to Hungary's institutional context remains unquantified.

## Assumptions

- That PISA-measured reading/maths disparity is a policy-relevant indicator of equity concern (not questioned, but SES-gradient causality is not established here). [assumption]

- That the Hanushek & Woessmann findings on timing generalise to Hungary despite differences in school quality variance, teacher composition, and curriculum coherence across tracks. [assumption]

- That "early selection" in the comparative literature (typically age 10–12) is functionally equivalent to Hungary's 11–12-year entry point. [assumption]

## Position

Hungary should consider delaying or broadening access to grammar school selection beyond age 11–12, or moving toward common curriculum in lower secondary, because early tracking increases SES stratification without compensatory mean-performance gains—a cost Hungary's already-elevated equity gradient cannot sustain.

## Uncertainties

- No curated source directly quantifies the SES-gradient reduction Hungary would achieve from delayed selection; international elasticities may not transfer.
- Hungarian grammar school quality, teacher value-added, and peer effects relative to comprehensive alternatives are not characterised in provided sources.
- Whether Hungary's high SES gradient stems primarily from selection timing vs. within-school resource allocation, curriculum differentiation, or family behaviour remains open.
- Reversibility and transition costs of moving away from the 6/8-year system are not addressed in comparative literature.
