# Expert analysis: international_comparison
## Findings (evidence)
- Early academic selection is associated with increased educational inequality, as measured by PISA performance gradients [evidence: strong — OECD PISA reports].
- Tracking systems, particularly those that are rigid and implemented early, tend to exacerbate socioeconomic disparities in educational outcomes [evidence: strong — UNESCO reports, various academic studies].
- The age of selection is a critical factor; later selection is generally linked to more equitable outcomes [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA reports].
- While some high-performing systems employ tracking, their success often depends on robust compensatory mechanisms and high overall quality across all tracks, which are not universally transferable [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA reports].
- The Hungarian system's early selection into grammar schools (gimnazium) at age 10 (after 4 years of primary) and 12 (after 6 years of primary) is earlier than in many comparable OECD countries [evidence: strong — Eurydice Network reports].
## Interpretation
The international evidence strongly suggests that early academic selection, as practiced in Hungary, is likely to contribute to significant educational inequalities. The earlier the point at which students are streamed into different educational pathways, the more pronounced the impact of socioeconomic background on their academic achievement tends to be. While some countries with tracking achieve high average performance, this is often coupled with substantial within-country variation linked to social origin. The transferability of successful tracking models is questionable without careful consideration of the specific context, including the quality of all educational pathways and the presence of strong equity-focused support systems.
## Assumptions
- The goal of Hungarian education policy is to improve equity and reduce the impact of socioeconomic background on student outcomes. [assumption]
- PISA gradients and difference-in-differences literature provide relevant insights into the potential consequences of Hungary's selection system. [assumption]
## Position
Hungary should consider delaying academic selection to mitigate the negative equity impacts observed in international comparisons of early tracking systems.
## Uncertainties
- The precise magnitude of the impact of Hungary's specific selection ages (10 and 12) on current educational inequality, beyond general trends.
- The extent to which compensatory mechanisms exist or could be effectively implemented within the Hungarian system to offset potential equity issues arising from early selection.
- The specific characteristics of successful international tracking systems that might be transferable to the Hungarian context, if any.
