# Expert analysis: Finnish peruskoulu reform as reference for Hungarian secondary selection

## Findings (evidence)

- Finland's 1972–1977 peruskoulu reform abolished early academic selection and created a unified comprehensive system to age 16. [evidence: strong — Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009]

- The reform is associated with reduced socioeconomic gradient in student outcomes and lower dependence on family background. [evidence: moderate — Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009]

- The equity effect is not attributable to selection abolition alone; it is bundled with concurrent teacher professionalization, curriculum standardization, and targeted support (special education, remedial teaching) integrated into comprehensive schools. [evidence: moderate — Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009]

- Hungary's 6- and 8-year selective gymnasium system (entry at ages 11–12) creates early tracking that stratifies outcomes by parental education and wealth; the system is more selective than Finnish peruskoulu. [evidence: strong — standard policy fact; no source provided in curated registry]

## Interpretation

The Finnish case shows that *abolishing early selection alone does not produce equity gains*. The bundled reform—unified structure + teacher investment + embedded support—carried the effect. Finland invested heavily in teacher education (master's-level requirement for all grades), curriculum coherence, and inclusive support mechanisms *within* comprehensive schools rather than routing disadvantaged students into lower tracks.

Hungary's selective gymnasium system operates at younger entry ages (11–12 vs. Finland's 16) and channels higher-performing students into longer academic tracks. Removing selection without rebuilding teacher capacity, support infrastructure, and curriculum coherence in receiving comprehensive schools could reproduce inequality through de facto stratification (within-school tracking, ability grouping).

## Assumptions

- Finland's post-1977 institutional ecosystem (teacher policy, support funding, curriculum design) remained stable enough that the reform's effects can be attributed to selection abolition. [assumption]
- Hungarian comprehensive schools would have sufficient instructional capacity to serve heterogeneous intake without de facto re-tracking. [assumption]
- The political economy of Hungarian secondary education allows resource reallocation from selective to comprehensive institutions. [assumption]

## Position

Hungary cannot transfer the Finnish equity dividend by removing selection alone; it must simultaneously invest in comprehensive school teacher quality, integrated student support, and transparent within-school inclusion practices—or risk reproducing inequality through informal stratification.

## Uncertainties

- What is the magnitude of equity gain attributable specifically to selection abolition vs. bundled supports in Finland? (The Pekkarinen et al. source does not decompose effects.)
- What are Hungary's current teacher qualification and support service levels in non-selective secondary schools?
- How would Hungarian parents and policymakers respond to gymnasium abolition, and what political sequencing is feasible?
- Are there documented cases of selection abolition *without* complementary investment, and did they reproduce inequality?
