# Expert analysis: demography

## Findings (evidence)

- Hungary's annual births declined from approximately 125,000 in the early 1990s to 85–95,000 in recent years, representing a 28–32% contraction. [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks]

- This cohort shrinkage creates an **inexorable forcing function** for school-network consolidation decisions across the 2020s–2040 period, independent of selection policy choices. [evidence: strong — demographic momentum; birth cohorts aged 0–15 are already determined]

- The 6- and 8-year gimnazium system (early secondary entry at ages 10–12) concentrates enrollment in selective schools, creating dual problems under decline: underutilized standard secondary schools in many regions, and high vacancy rates in selective-school capacity by 2030s. [evidence: moderate — structural feature of Hungarian education system; specific enrollment forecasts unavailable]

- Early selection amplifies *demographic inequality*: as total cohort shrinks, competitive pressure on selection tests remains high, but rural/economically disadvantaged regions lose selective-school options through closure, reducing genuine access even for high-ability students. [evidence: moderate — logical consequence of selective-school economics under contraction; empirical distribution mapping not cited]

## Interpretation

The policy question conflates two distinct problems:

1. **Demographic necessity** (unavoidable): Consolidation of underenrolled schools, closure of redundant capacity, and network redesign.
2. **Selection regime choice** (contingent): Whether early academic streaming accelerates or buffers adjustment costs.

Under cohort decline, the 6/8-year gimnazium system creates brittleness: selective schools maintain high entrance standards despite shrinking applicant pools, while standard schools absorb remaining demand with declining per-pupil resources. This pattern is visible in many OECD countries (Germany, Japan) but Hungary's degree of early stratification (age 10) is steeper than EU median.

## Assumptions

- Birth rates remain in the 85–95k range through 2040 (no major fertility recovery). [assumption]
- Current selective-school capacity (~15–20% of secondary cohort) does not contract as rapidly as overall cohort. [assumption]
- Regional school networks are geographically constrained (students cannot easily commute to distant selective schools). [assumption]

## Position

Cohort decline will force consolidation regardless of selection policy, but early academic selection (6/8-year gimnazium) *increases the social cost of necessary closures* by concentrating remaining demand in competitive pathways, leaving weaker standard schools to absorb demographic losses.

## Uncertainties

- Precise forecast of selective-school enrollment demand under 15% cohort shrinkage (elasticity unknown).
- Regional variation in birth decline (Budapest vs. periphery).
- Impact of recent migration inflows on school-age cohorts (not addressed in curated source).
- Counterfactual: what consolidation pattern would emerge under late selection (age 14–15)?
- Evidence on whether early selection delays or accelerates teacher workforce contraction.
