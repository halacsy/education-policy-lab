# Rejected Framings

## S1 — Admission Reform Within the Current Structure
- CHOSEN: Mitigate socioeconomic segregation and early sorting by reforming the centralized written admissions process for 6- and 8-year gimnaziums without changing school structures.
- REJECTED: Fully abolish selective entry points at ages 10 and 12 under the guise of admission reform — reason: This goes beyond modifying admission criteria and constitutes a structural change, which violates the constraint of keeping the current school structure intact.
- REJECTED: Rely on voluntary, non-binding admission guidelines for elite schools — reason: Voluntary guidelines lack enforceability and are highly likely to be ignored or bypassed by elite schools facing middle-class parental pressure, resulting in no measurable reduction in SES-based sorting.

## S2 — Gradual Phase-Down of 6- and 8-Year Entry Places
- CHOSEN: Gradually reduce and eventually phase out entry places in 6- and 8-year gimnaziums using a felmenő rendszer (gradual phase-out) to transition towards a standard age 14 selection model.
- REJECTED: Immediately terminate all 6- and 8-year entry tracks in a single academic year — reason: An immediate shutdown violates the constitutional principle of legal certainty and acquired rights, and it would cause severe systemic capacity shocks in both primary schools and gimnaziums.
- REJECTED: Phase down entry places only in state-run gimnaziums while exempting church and foundation schools — reason: Exempting non-state maintainers would lead to middle-class flight to these exempted sectors, reinforcing rather than reducing socioeconomic sorting.

## S3 — Comprehensive School to Age 14 (Structural Reform)
- CHOSEN: Abolish the 6- and 8-year gimnazium structures and implement a unified, comprehensive lower-secondary school system for all students until age 14 (or age 16, following the Finnish model).
- REJECTED: Create a dual-track system where students are sorted internally within comprehensive schools based on academic ability — reason: Internal tracking replicates the socioeconomic stratification of the early selection system, defeating the primary goal of establishing a unified, non-selective comprehensive tier.
- REJECTED: Establish comprehensive schools without teacher retraining or curriculum reform — reason: Merely changing the structural labels without adjusting pedagogical support would lead to a drop in system performance and fail to support high-achieving or disadvantaged students in unified classrooms.

## S4 — Keep the Structure, Compensate General Schools (Portuguese-Style Package)
- CHOSEN: Retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system intact but deploy targeted resources, curriculum standards, and support to disadvantaged primary schools to counteract early selection inequities.
- REJECTED: Equalize funding per capita across all schools without targeted compensation — reason: Standard flat funding fails to address the resource deficits and negative peer effects concentrated in disadvantaged primary schools under an early-selection regime.
- REJECTED: Implement a school voucher system to let low-SES students buy their way into elite tracks — reason: Vouchers do not scale to address systemic inequities in primary schools and are often captured by middle-class families, failing to improve equity within standard general schools.
