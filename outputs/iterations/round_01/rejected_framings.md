# rejected_framings.md

## S1 — Admission reform within the current structure

- CHOSEN: Flattened entry criteria with diagnostic scoring and equity-weighted selection, preserving 6/8-year legal forms and phased negotiation with maintainers.

- REJECTED: Pure quota system (e.g., fixed SES-band entry caps) — reason: Administratively rigid across fragmented maintainers (state centres, churches, foundations); no evidence that hard quotas reduce SES skew without triggering legal disputes or gaming via catchment/choice. The assumption that quotas alone would survive church/foundation resistance is unsupported.

- REJECTED: Centralised admissions instrument without legislative amendment — reason: Oktatási Hivatal redesign of scoring alone cannot enforce equity thresholds without binding rule changes; prior administrative reforms have been absorbed or reversed. Legislative baseline is necessary (stated in assumptions).

- REJECTED: Abolishing written examination entirely in favour of primary-school GPA only — reason: Creates perverse incentive for primary schools to inflate grades (moral hazard); loses signal on test-prep advantage (which is precisely the mechanism to diagnose and counter). Diagnostic framing preserves detection of advantage while reforming the gate.

- REJECTED: Admission reform coupled with immediate choice/catchment reform — reason: Scope creep; S1's design goal is to test whether admission-rule change alone can move the needle. Conflating multiple levers obscures the causal pathway and complicates legislative passage. Choice/catchment reform belongs in S3 (structural).

## S2 — Gradual phase-down of 6/8-year entry places

- CHOSEN: Annual scheduled reduction of early-entry places (target: 10–15% per cycle over 10–12 years) with grandfathering for enrolled pupils and front-loaded teacher retraining during the 2025–2035 retirement window.

- REJECTED: Immediate halving of early places with no transition period — reason: Triggers enrolled-pupil guarantees and legal transition costs explicitly cited as an advantage of the phased approach. Abrupt change would incite political reversal before benefits accrue (Polish precedent applies equally to abrupt and phased paths, but phased spreads the political pain).

- REJECTED: Place reduction without corresponding teacher retraining in mixed-ability pedagogy — reason: Leaves state and non-selective schools without instructional capacity to absorb pupils from phased-out places; de facto re-tracking would occur. Retraining is a bundled necessity, not an optional add-on (assumption critically depends on this).

- REJECTED: Phase-down with automatic reallocation of freed capacity to other selective streams (e.g., vocational early entry) — reason: Displaces rather than reduces early tracking; the equity problem is the age of selection and concentration of resources, not the label of the track. Maintains the stratification mechanism at a different point.

- REJECTED: Phase-down triggered by demographic decline alone, with no fixed schedule — reason: Introduces uncertainty for planning and allows political sabotage via manipulation of cohort-shrinkage forecasts. The scenario's strength is the *predictable* reduction independent of short-term demographic variance.

## S3 — Comprehensive school to age 14 (structural reform)

- CHOSEN: Integrated secondary to age 14 with post-14 selection, coupled with simultaneous reform of free-choice rules, catchment boundaries, and front-loaded teacher/support investment.

- REJECTED: Track abolition without curriculum standardization or support infrastructure — reason: Leaves within-school ability grouping and de facto re-tracking as residual stratification pathways (explicitly identified as a major risk in assumptions). Finland's peruskoulu bundled abolition with teacher professionalization and curriculum coherence; both are necessary. This framing treats selection abolition as sufficient, violating the evidence.

- REJECTED: Post-14 selection but with choice/catchment rules unchanged — reason: Postponing selection does not prevent cream-skimming if affluent families navigate choice systems to pre-sort into mixed-ability schools. The mechanism of stratification shifts from formal age-10 selection to informal age-14 choice-based sorting. S3's equity case depends on *joint* reform of both timing and choice.

- REJECTED: Comprehensive secondary with deferred selection, but no reallocation of teacher capacity from selective to comprehensive schools — reason: Leaves previously-depleted general secondaries under-resourced; within-school inequality persists. The bundled-supports assumption is violated.

- REJECTED: Comprehensive reform presented as compatible with maintainer pluralism (state, church, foundation) under existing contracts without renegotiation — reason: Ignores that maintainers' legal contracts guarantee the right to selective admission (documented constraint). Framing this as compatible with S3 without addressing maintainer negotiations is false consensus. S3 explicitly requires "negotiated deferred-selection compliance"; omitting this is consensus laundering.

- REJECTED: Track abolition phased *after* teacher retraining completes, rather than front-loaded during retirement window — reason: Extends transition to 20+ years, increasing reversal risk and letting organised opposition mobilise across multiple cycles. The scenario's strength is locking-in retraining investment before abolition is formally legislated, creating structural path-dependence.

## S4 — Keep the structure, compensate general schools (Portuguese-style package)

- CHOSEN: Curriculum standards alignment, TEIP-style targeted intensive support for underperforming schools, school clustering, and grade-retention alternatives — all preserving 6/8-year legal forms.

- REJECTED: Targeted support without curriculum standards coherence — reason: Remedial funding alone does not address the *mechanism* of stratification (examination-based gating and resource concentration in selective schools). Standards coherence is required to make the "floor-raising" pathway work; support without standards is redistribution without signal reform.

- REJECTED: S4 framed as a "wait-and-see" pilot before considering S3 or S2 — reason: Conflates S4 (a standalone policy) with an evaluation gate for structural reform. If S4 is presented as a precondition to structural change, it becomes a delay tactic and signals weak commitment to testing alternatives. S4 must stand alone as a plausible terminal policy, not a pretext.

- REJECTED: Targeted support bundled with admission reform (S1-style flattened criteria) without acknowledging the contradiction — reason: S4's stated design goal is to *retain* structure; mixing in admission-rule changes (S1) obscures the causal claim. If both are needed, that is a signal that S4 alone is insufficient (see uncertainties: peer-effect loss cannot be quantified). Framing this bundle as S4 is category confusion.

- REJECTED: TEIP-style support scaled nationally without prior pilot data on Hungarian receptiveness — reason: Assumes transfer from Portugal without testing; Portugal's fiscal and governance context differ. The scenario assumes this will work; presenting it as certain rather than conditional on evidence is premature. Pilot phase is implicitly required (noted in implementation steps), so framing as immediate national roll-out overstates confidence.

- REJECTED: Retention-alternative programmes presented without explicit tracking of displacement effects — reason: "Support for at-risk pupils" may lower grade retention but could shift failure to later stages (age 14, age 16) rather than prevent it. Without measuring where pupils flow after support, the equity effect is unmonitored. This is a methodological flaw, not just uncertainty.

## Cross-scenario rejected framings (meta-level)

- REJECTED: Any scenario framed as "compatible with enacted law without amendment" — reason: All four scenarios involve material changes to selection timing, admission rules, or funding. Presenting any as administrative-only violates transparency on political cost. S1 and S4 are *less* disruptive legislatively, but not cost-free.

- REJECTED: Any scenario claiming to reduce SES skew without measurement plan — reason: Uncertainties preserve signal; claiming success requires defined metrics. All scenarios note SES-gradient monitoring as implicit; none should be presented as finished without a baseline and measurement protocol.

---

## Summary of rejection patterns

**S1 rejections** centre on conflating mechanism (diagnostic scoring) with scope (choice/catchment), and on assuming that administrative redesign survives without legislative backing.

**S2 rejections** cluster around omitting bundled retraining and allowing place reduction to displace rather than eliminate tracking.

**S3 rejections** focus on curriculum/support unbundling from selection abolition, and on false compatibility claims with maintainer pluralism.

**S4 rejections** flag the risk of presenting support-only approaches as terminal policies when the underlying stratification mechanism (selection timing, choice, catchment) remains unchanged, and of framing TEIP transfer as certain rather than conditional.

All rejections preserve the uncertainty discipline: rejected framings are not impossible, but they violate the evidence or assumption rules, or hide design contradictions that should remain visible.
