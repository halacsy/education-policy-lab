# Critique: evidence_checker

## S3.mechanism
Objection: The tag [evidence: strong] on "Later between-school selection weakens the SES-performance link, per cross-country tracking evidence" overstates what the source supports. Cross-country tracking studies (e.g., PISA-based Hanushek-Woessmann) establish a *correlation* between later tracking and weaker SES gradients; they do not cleanly identify the causal effect of moving selection age, because tracking age is confounded with other system features. A "strong" tag implies causal identification that this literature does not deliver. The scenario's own evidence_status field already concedes the topic is "contested" — so labelling this specific mechanism "strong" is internally inconsistent with the scenario's own grading.

## S4.expected_benefits
Objection: "Politically inexpensive; implementable under any government [evidence: strong]" mislabels a political prediction as strong empirical evidence. There is no body of evidence that grades a claim about future cross-government implementability as "strong"; this is a feasibility judgement, not an evidenced finding. The scenario's own uncertainties field contradicts the "strong" tag by listing "Durability of the budget line across fiscal cycles" as open and the political_risks field flags "compensation lines are the first cut in fiscal consolidation." A claim that is simultaneously flagged as budget-vulnerable cannot be graded strong for durable implementability.

## S4.mechanism
Objection: "Targeted resources and retention reduction improved outcomes in Portugal without changing selection age [evidence: moderate]" borrows Portugal's evidence for a system whose selection age it does not match. Portugal does not select between schools at age 10/12; the moderate tag rests on a source demonstrating gains in a *comprehensive* system, which is precisely the transfer the scenario's own uncertainties field flags as unproven ("no country has demonstrated it at Hungary's selection age"). The evidence supports the components in Portugal's context but does not support the moderate tag as applied to offsetting early selection — this is a case of evidence tagged stronger than the source warrants for the specific claim made.

## S2.expected_benefits
Objection: "Steadily falling early-sorting share of each cohort (measurable annually) [evidence: strong]" tags a definitional consequence as if it were empirical evidence. That capping entry places lowers the sorted share is true by construction (it is what a cap does), not a finding supported by an evidentiary source. Labelling a mechanical identity "strong evidence" conflates logical necessity with empirical support and inflates the apparent evidential backing of the scenario; it should be marked as definitional/mechanical, not evidenced.
