# Critique: coherence_checker
## S1.mechanism
Objection: The mechanism states that reforming admission criteria "could reduce SES concentration while preserving legal forms [evidence: contested]". However, the "Evidence status" for S1 states that "the effect of admission-rule changes on composition is contested and not directly quantified for Hungary." This creates a contradiction: the mechanism claims a potential effect, but the evidence status explicitly states that this effect is contested and unquantified, making the claim in the mechanism unsupported by the provided evidence status.

## S2.assumptions
Objection: The assumption "Staged reform reduces reversal risk relative to abrupt change (untested — may merely extend conflict). [assumption]" directly contradicts the uncertainty "Whether staged reform reduces reversal risk or merely extends the conflict is untested." An assumption should be a premise taken as true for the scenario's logic, but here it's presented as an untested proposition, which is then explicitly listed as an uncertainty. This indicates a lack of coherence in the scenario's foundational logic.

## S3.expected_benefits
Objection: The expected benefit "Consistent reduction of the SES-score gradient, which in Hungary is elevated at 19–25% vs 12–14% OECD average [evidence: strong]" is too strong given the "Evidence status" and "Uncertainties" for S3. The evidence status states that "the Hungary-specific magnitude and the bundled-supports requirement introduce substantial conditionality and unquantified risk," and an uncertainty explicitly states, "No curated source directly quantifies the SES-gradient reduction Hungary would achieve; international elasticities may not transfer." Claiming a "consistent reduction" with "strong" evidence for a Hungary-specific magnitude is unsupported when the scenario itself admits this is unquantified and conditional.

## S4.equity_impact
Objection: The equity impact states that this scenario "mitigates rather than reverses stratification and does not address the loss of political voice/resources that follows departed top students." However, the "Expected benefits" section claims "Targeted support narrows the skill gap in depleted non-selective schools [evidence: moderate]". While mitigation is acknowledged, the equity impact does not fully reconcile with the stated benefit of narrowing the skill gap, which could be interpreted as a more direct reversal of stratification effects for the non-selected. The statement in equity impact is too definitive about not addressing the "loss of political voice/resources" without considering how a narrowed skill gap might indirectly influence these factors over time.
