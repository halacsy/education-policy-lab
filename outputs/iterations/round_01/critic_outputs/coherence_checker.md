# Critique: coherence_checker

## S2.evidence_status
Objection: The evidence_status labels the field "moderate" while the mechanism tag for the sorting-reduction claim is "strong" and the expected_benefits tag for "steadily falling early-sorting share" is also "strong". If the mechanical sorting reduction is strong and that is the scenario's core causal claim, an overall "moderate" is internally inconsistent unless the moderate rating is driven entirely by the retention link — which the evidence_status text concedes ("the retention of demand in general schools is the unevidenced link"). The problem: the goal gives equal weight to "investing the freed capacity in strong programmes," so the scenario's stated success depends on the unevidenced link, not the strong one. The evidence_status should be "weak" for the composite goal, not "moderate."

## S3.mechanism
Objection: The mechanism relies on in-school differentiation substituting for between-school tracks "if teachers are trained for it" [evidence: contested], while assumptions separately require that "teacher capacity for differentiated instruction can be built at national scale within the transition window." These are the same load-bearing condition stated twice, but the synthesis's agreed finding ("Transition capacity binds any structural variant to a 6-12 year horizon" [strong]) and the expected_benefits horizon ("within a decade of full phase-in") leave no slack: if retraining consumes the full decade, the peer-composition and differentiation benefits cannot have materialized "within a decade." The timeline is internally over-subscribed.

## S3.equity_impact
Objection: The equity_impact claims "the largest expected equity gain of all scenarios," but the expected_benefits tags are only "moderate" (weaker SES gradient; better track choice), whereas S2's core benefit carries a "strong" tag and S4's political-feasibility benefit carries "strong." A superlative equity claim resting on moderate/contested evidence contradicts the scenario's own evidence_status ("Contested overall"). The claim of largest gain is unsupported at the stated evidence level — it should read as largest potential gain, conditional on the contested peer-effect asymmetry holding.

## S4.expected_benefits
Objection: The expected_benefits tags "Politically inexpensive; implementable under any government" as [evidence: strong], but the political_risks field states "compensation lines are the first cut in fiscal consolidation" and uncertainties flags "Durability of the budget line across fiscal cycles." A high-recurring-cost package (cost_categories rates targeted funding "high" and premia "medium-high") that is the first thing cut cannot simultaneously be strongly evidenced as durably "implementable under any government." The "strong" tag conflates initial political cheapness with sustained implementability, which the scenario's own risk fields contradict.
