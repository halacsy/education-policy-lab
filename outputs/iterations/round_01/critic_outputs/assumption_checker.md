# Critique: assumption_checker

## S3.evidence_status
Objection: The status is labeled "contested" but the mechanism relies on two separately-rated claims that the evidence tag conflates. The "later selection weakens SES-performance link" is tagged [strong], while "asymmetric peer effects" is tagged [contested]. The scenario's headline equity claim (largest gain of all scenarios) depends on the asymmetric-peer-effects link, which is the contested one — not the strong tracking evidence. Labeling the overall status "contested" understates that the *equity-magnitude* claim rests entirely on the disputed link, while the *direction* claim rests on the strong one. The evidence record supports later selection reducing the SES gradient; it does not support the "largest expected equity gain" magnitude claim, which is asserted beyond what the tags carry.

## S2.assumptions
Objection: The assumption "Caps are enforceable across state, church and private maintainers" contradicts the scenario's own political_risks and the synthesis. Political_risks states "Church maintainers may litigate or negotiate exemptions," and S3's record flags "non-state maintainer exemption fights." An assumption that is directly negated by the scenario's own risk section is not a background condition but the core unresolved question. The scenario treats enforceability as a premise while simultaneously predicting it will be contested — the mechanism's "mechanically lowering early sorting" [strong] only holds if caps bind, which the record does not support for non-state maintainers.

## S4.expected_benefits
Objection: "Politically inexpensive; implementable under any government [evidence: strong]" is unsupported at the strong tag. The uncertainties section itself states "no country has demonstrated it at Hungary's selection age" and political_risks flags "budget vulnerability: compensation lines are the first cut in fiscal consolidation." A benefit tagged [strong] that is contradicted by the scenario's own budget-vulnerability risk cannot be strong — the Portuguese precedent supports that such packages are *cheaper than structural reform*, not that they are durable "under any government." The tag conflates low up-front political cost with durability, which the record explicitly disputes.

## S1.assumptions
Objection: The assumption "A workable SES indicator exists at application time" is asserted without evidence and is the linchpin of the entire mechanism (SES-aware admission rules, district quotas, weighted lottery above a threshold all require it). The scenario provides no evidence that Hungary has an administrative SES indicator usable at age-10 application. If no such indicator exists at application time, the core mechanism is inoperable, yet this is listed as a background assumption rather than flagged as an unverified precondition. (speculative — depends on whether Hungarian administrative data supports individual-level SES flagging at application.)
