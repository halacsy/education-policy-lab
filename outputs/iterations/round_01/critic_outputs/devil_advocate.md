# Critique: devil_advocate

## S3.mechanism
Objection: The mechanism tags "later selection weakens SES-performance link" as [evidence: strong] but rests the whole scenario on in-school differentiation "substituting" for between-school tracks, which it tags [evidence: contested]. The load-bearing claim is not the timing of selection but that Hungarian teachers can deliver quality differentiation at national scale — the assumptions concede this is unbuilt. If differentiation fails, the common school reproduces within-school tracking with worse resourcing, and the "strong" tracking evidence does not transfer. The scenario cannot inherit the strength of the cross-country tracking result while its own delivery precondition is contested.

## S2.mechanism
Objection: The scenario's collapse point is the claim that advanced-programme funding "retains ambitious families in the common track" — which the evidence_status itself calls "the unevidenced link." Yet the equity case depends on it entirely: if funding does not retain those families, the caps simply push demand to non-state maintainers (a risk S2 lists under assumptions but never rebuts), making residual selective places "even more SES-skewed" by the scenario's own admission. The mechanism asserts retention as a step with no evidence tag at all, which is the strongest form of the flaw, not the easiest.

## S4.expected_benefits
Objection: The benefit "improved outcomes within 5 years (Portuguese trajectory)" is tagged [evidence: moderate], but the uncertainties field concedes "no country has demonstrated it at Hungary's selection age." Portugal did not select at 10. Transplanting the Portuguese trajectory therefore supports only the weaker claim that compensation improves disadvantaged-school outcomes in general — not that it offsets *early sorting* effects, which is the goal. The tag overstates what the source supports: the Portuguese evidence is off-target for the specific mechanism (offsetting age-10 selection), so it should be [evidence: weak] for this goal.

## S1.assumptions
Objection: The assumption "a workable SES indicator exists at application time" is load-bearing for the entire admission-rule mechanism (weighted lottery above a threshold, district quotas), yet it is asserted without evidence and is precisely the point most likely to fail. Hungary lacks a validated pupil-level SES measure usable at age-10 application; proxies (address, parental declaration) are gameable by exactly the middle-class families the reform targets. If the indicator is manipulable, the SES-aware rules relabel rather than reduce sorting — which the equity_impact field admits as a failure mode but does not connect to this assumption. (speculative on the specific unavailability of a Hungarian indicator; the gaming vulnerability of proxies is not speculative.)
