# Critique: political_risk_checker

## S1.political_risks
Objection: The listed risks omit the most consequential reversal mechanism this scenario itself creates. S1 changes admission rules by decree ("amend admission decree") — a lower legal instrument that a successor administration can reverse with equal ease and no legislative friction. The political_risks section flags parental opposition and maintainer exemption but says nothing about the entrenchment weakness of decree-based SES weighting. Given that S2 and S3 both explicitly name reversal risk, its absence here is a substantive gap, not an oversight of a minor point: a reform that can be quietly unwound by the next education minister has no durability design at all.

## S2.political_risks
Objection: The objection is understatement of the reversal risk relative to the mechanism. The scenario names "a successor government can freeze or reverse the trajectory cheaply (Polish precedent)" but the mechanism relies on a "published decade-long trajectory" with only a "year-5 mid-course evaluation" as entrenchment. There is no design feature (statutory supermajority lock, cross-party pact, or irreversible capacity reallocation) that raises the reversal cost. Naming the Polish precedent while proposing no entrenchment mechanism to counter it means the risk is identified but left wholly unmitigated — the scenario should either state that reversal is accepted or specify a lock-in device.

## S3.political_risks
Objection: The scenario relies on a "Government + opposition (pact attempt)" in implementation_steps as its answer to reversal, yet political_risks treats "Polish-style reversal after government change" as a live threat without reconciling the two. Either the cross-party pact is a credible entrenchment device (in which case the reversal risk should be scored lower and the pact's failure conditions specified) or it is not (in which case the pact step is decorative). The current text asserts both the mitigation and the unmitigated risk without connecting them — the reader cannot tell whether reversal is designed against or merely hoped against.

## S4.political_risks
Objection: The scenario understates the opposition it will generate by framing itself as "politically inexpensive; implementable under any government." A high recurring targeted-funding formula plus teacher premia in disadvantaged schools creates a concentrated, mobilised losing coalition — non-disadvantaged schools and their teachers who receive no premium and see resources redirected. The political_risks section captures budget vulnerability and legitimisation risk but misses this distributional opposition, which is precisely the risk that materialises when premia are introduced (comparator teacher-pay reforms routinely trigger it). Claiming low political capital cost while omitting the redistribution backlash is an understated-opposition flaw.
