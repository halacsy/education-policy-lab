# Policy scenarios

## S1 — Admission reform within the current structure
**Goal**
Retain 6- and 8-year gimnázium tracks but rebalance who gets in, by reforming the entry examination and attaching regional equity quotas, while regulating non-state maintainer admissions to limit socio-demographic sorting.

**Mechanism**
- Replacing/redesigning the central written admission exam with a less preparation-sensitive, age-appropriate instrument reduces the coachability advantage of high-SES families [evidence: weak — no registry source measures the coachability of a redesigned Hungarian instrument; inferred from admission-statistics skew, evidence: strong]
- Regional equity quotas reserve selective places for high-ability low-SES applicants, shifting composition among applicants [evidence: moderate — KSH/Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics show current high-SES skew this reweights]
- Extending equity-based admission rules to church/foundation maintainers via renegotiated maintainer agreements prevents demand migrating off-balance-sheet [evidence: moderate — governance structure of Nkt.; maintainer plurality documented, evidence: strong, but consent terms unknown]

**Evidence status**
moderate — admission skew and structural design are strongly evidenced, but the effect of any specific instrument redesign or quota on sorting is not measured in the curated registry.

**Assumptions**
- A workable SES indicator exists at application time — in the Hungarian administrative context this would likely be address-based/settlement-deprivation or benefit-receipt proxy, since no student-level SES register usable at application age currently exists [assumption; unverified for Hungary]
- Church and foundation maintainers can be brought under uniform equity-based admission rules through agreement renegotiation or subsidy conditionality [assumption — maintainer consent terms not in curated registry]
- Reweighting who is selected among applicants also changes who applies; without outreach, rural and low-SES non-applicants remain outside the pool [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- More balanced socio-economic intake into selective tracks among applicants over successive admission cycles [evidence: moderate — mechanically follows from quota reweighting of the documented high-SES skew; timeline not evidenced in registry]
- Reduced test-preparation arms-race pressure on age-10 children IF the redesigned instrument is genuinely less coachable [evidence: weak — coachability reduction is model knowledge, not measured; and see political_risks, informal selection may reappear]
- Gains, where they occur, accrue to high-ability low-SES pupils who under current rules are screened out [evidence: weak — value-added of selective tracks is modest per Horn 2013, evidence: moderate, so composition change matters more than achievement change]

**Equity impact**
Improves equity of access to existing selective tracks IF a working SES indicator can be built and IF outreach broadens the applicant pool; gains are conditional on both, either of which failing would leave the composition change narrow. Does not address the peer-depletion of general schools that remain, since the tracks persist.

**Cost categories**
- One-off: assessment redesign, requiring psychometric development and validation lead time (medium — the 'low' framing of earlier drafts understates the agency lead time)
- Recurring: routine admission administration under new rules (low)
- Recurring: compliance-audit and monitoring mechanism to enforce quotas across state and non-state maintainers (medium — omitted in earlier drafts, flagged as needed by uncertainties)

**Implementation steps**
- Assessment agency — design, pilot and psychometrically validate the new instrument in volunteer districts (multi-year lead time before any live use)
- Legislator — amend Nkt. to authorise regional equity quotas in gimnázium admission
- Ministry — renegotiate maintainer agreements with churches/foundations to extend equity rules, using subsidy conditionality
- Oktatási Hivatal — build a compliance-audit function to monitor quota adherence

**Political risks**
- Elite-school and middle-class parent opposition, who view places as a mobility/insurance asset [evidence: moderate — press/stakeholder analyses]
- Church/private maintainer exemption claims and possible refusal of uniform quotas
- Organised private tutoring / test-prep industry as an unaddressed opposition actor with a direct commercial interest in the existing exam
- Scarcer/reformed selection channels may invite informal selection and off-exam sorting, partly offsetting the intended fairness gain

**Uncertainties**
- Whether a workable, non-gameable SES indicator can be constructed at application time in Hungary [confidence: low | would be reduced by: an administrative-data feasibility study cross-referencing Oktatási Hivatal registry with family tax/benefit records]
- Willingness of church maintainers to accept equity quotas under subsidy conditionality [confidence: medium | would be reduced by: surveys and interviews with maintainer representatives and Ministry officials]
- Whether the redesigned instrument is genuinely less coachable [confidence: low | would be reduced by: compliance audit design and coachability testing in the pilot]

## S2 — Gradual phase-down of 6/8-year entry places
**Goal**
Steadily reduce the number of early-entry (age 10/12) selective places on a published multi-year trajectory, shifting cohorts toward the common track, while protecting already-enrolled pupils.

**Mechanism**
- A declining statutory cap on entry places reduces the share of each cohort sorted before lower-secondary completion [evidence: moderate — structural design of the tracking mechanism is documented; effect of a cap specifically is inferred, not measured]
- Later effective selection age attenuates the inequality-amplifying effect of early tracking [evidence: moderate — timing-sensitivity literature, Chmielewski 2014; Brunello & Checchi 2007]
- Advanced-programme funding in general schools is intended to retain ambitious families in the common track [evidence: weak — this retention link is unevidenced; middle-class flight risk cuts the other way, evidence: moderate — Finnish reform interpretation]

**Evidence status**
moderate — the timing–equity relationship is well-supported internationally, but the retention mechanism and the cap-specific effect on sorting are weakly evidenced.

**Assumptions**
- General schools can absorb and challenge high-achievers if funded for it [assumption — this is the unevidenced link; expected_benefits depending on it inherit that weakness]
- The cap trajectory can be applied across state, church and foundation maintainers, not only state schools [assumption — maintainer autonomy limits uniform application]
- Families do not simply migrate to uncapped non-state selective places as state caps bite [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Falling early-sorting share of each cohort as caps bite, measurable annually via enrolment data [evidence: moderate — this is the mechanical direct effect of the cap on places; note no registry source measures the downstream sorting effect, so tagged moderate not strong]
- Stronger peer composition in urban general-school upper grades [evidence: weak — this is downstream of the unevidenced retention/absorption link and of the contested asymmetric-peer-effect claim; not moderate]
- Reduced inequality amplification over a decade if selection effectively moves later [evidence: moderate — timing-sensitivity literature; Hungary-specific causality not documented]

**Equity impact**
Equity gain depends on whether displaced demand stays in the common track or migrates to non-state selective places. Residual selective places become mechanically more SES-skewed absent S1-type admission reform (fewer seats, same high-SES demand, unchanged rules) [near-certain, mechanical — distinct from the uncertain absorption benefit]. Transition-cohort harm is likely: as caps tighten, better-informed higher-SES families capture the shrinking remaining places first.

**Cost categories**
- Recurring: advanced-programme funding in general schools (formula-based) — rises during the transition as caps bite, then approaches a lower steady state; earlier single-value framing conflated transition and steady-state cost (medium, time-varying)
- One-off: administrative redesign of place-allocation under a shifting cap (low-medium)
- Recurring: running two parallel allocation regimes during felmenő rendszer protection of enrolled cohorts (medium, temporary)

**Implementation steps**
- Legislator — enact the cap trajectory; note this requires felmenő rendszer guarantees implemented separately per maintainer type, not a single act, since church-maintained places need agreement renegotiation
- Ministry — negotiate parallel cap commitments with church/foundation maintainers to prevent migration
- KK/tankerület — phase in advanced-programme funding in general schools ahead of each cap step
- Legislator — attach an entrenchment mechanism (e.g., supermajority or multi-year statutory lock) to raise the cost of reversal

**Political risks**
- A successor government can freeze or reverse the trajectory cheaply (Polish precedent) [evidence: moderate — OECD; the entrenchment step above is the only described counter]
- Middle-class flight to non-state or private selective schools if general-school quality guarantees are not credible [evidence: moderate — Finnish reform interpretation]
- Maintainer resistance to parallel caps

**Uncertainties**
- Whether advanced-programme funding actually retains ambitious families in the common track [confidence: low | would be reduced by: Hungarian parent survey data and school-choice simulation]
- Whether displaced demand migrates to non-state selective places [confidence: medium | would be reduced by: longitudinal school-composition and student-flow tracking during phase-down]
- Magnitude of the cap's downstream effect on the SES gradient [confidence: low | would be reduced by: staggered-by-region difference-in-differences evaluation]

## S3 — Comprehensive school to age 14 (structural reform)
**Goal**
Abolish early selection by moving tracking to age 14, bundling the structural change with teacher-quality and diagnostic-support investment, on the Finnish/Polish model.

**Mechanism**
- Postponing selection to age 14 reduces the effect of family background on achievement [evidence: moderate — Pekkarinen et al. 2009 (Finland); Jakubowski et al. 2016 (Poland)]
- Delayed tracking does not lower mean performance and can raise it [evidence: moderate — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006; Polish PISA gains]
- Keeping high-achievers in the common track to age 14 improves peer composition for lower-SES pupils via asymmetric peer effects [evidence: contested — the peer-effect direction and magnitude are disputed; Hungarian negative-peer-effect evidence is weak]

**Evidence status**
moderate — the delayed-tracking/equity relationship is well-supported cross-nationally, but the core asymmetric-peer-effect channel is contested and Hungarian-specific causality is undocumented.

**Assumptions**
- Sorting does not simply migrate to school choice between general schools and to non-state maintainers — this is a load-bearing assumption that S3's own equity_impact identifies as the central risk; it is asserted, not evidenced, and if false the equity case collapses [assumption — directly in tension with the equity_impact field; unsupported by any registry source]
- General schools can handle high student heterogeneity given teacher-quality and diagnostic-support investment [assumption — Finnish bundling; Hungarian teacher shortage cuts against it]
- Teacher supply, especially in maths/science, can staff a fully comprehensive lower-secondary system [assumption — contradicted by documented shortage, evidence: strong]

**Expected benefits**
- Weaker SES-outcome gradient within a decade [evidence: moderate for the cross-national delayed-tracking result, but load-bearing on the asymmetric-peer channel tagged contested in the mechanism — the benefit is only as strong as its weakest required link]
- No mean-performance penalty, possibly a gain [evidence: moderate — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006; Polish PISA trajectory]
- Later, better-informed track choice at 14 reduces misallocation of talent [evidence: weak — no registry source directly supports this specific claim; it is an inference from the delayed-selection literature]
- Relieves demographic pressure on rural general-school networks by not draining top pupils early [evidence: moderate — demography/school-network analyses]

**Equity impact**
Largest expected aggregate equity gain of the four scenarios, but also the largest risk: middle-class exit to non-state schools. Crucially, if that exit materialises it does not harm the exiting families — the downside concentrates on the lower-SES pupils left in a now-avoided general track, so within the disadvantaged population the least mobile absorb the loss. Distributional equity within the disadvantaged group is therefore not guaranteed by the aggregate gain.

**Cost categories**
- One-off: nationwide teacher retraining in differentiated instruction (high — no per-teacher or per-cohort unit cost available in curated sources)
- One-off: network and building reconfiguration — largely bundled with demographic consolidation that occurs regardless of S3, so the incremental cost attributable to S3 alone is not separately estimated (uncertain)
- Recurring: standardised teacher-training pipeline and universal diagnostic support, on the Finnish model (high, ongoing — this recurring line was missing from earlier drafts)

**Implementation steps**
- Legislator — amend Nkt. to remove early-entry forms with multi-year pupil protections
- Ministry + universities — pilot the comprehensive model in 2-3 districts FIRST
- Ministry + universities — sequence differentiation retraining to follow pilot lessons, scaling nationally only after pilot evaluation (national-scale retraining cannot run concurrently with a pilot given the teacher shortage)
- Cross-party negotiators — attempt a multi-party pact before legislation; if the pact fails, fall back to S2 phase-down rather than an unanchored top-down mandate

**Political risks**
- Highest-intensity opposition of all four scenarios from urban middle-class parents and prestige schools [evidence: moderate — press/stakeholder analyses]
- Polish-style reversal after government change destroys sunk transition costs [evidence: moderate — OECD]; the pre-legislation pact is the primary defence, with S2 phase-down as the described fallback if it fails
- Migration of selective demand to non-state maintainers, which the state cannot uniformly mandate

**Uncertainties**
- Whether Hungary's high SES gradient is caused or merely reinforced by early selection [confidence: medium | would be reduced by: staggered-by-region abolition with difference-in-differences analysis]
- Whether sorting migrates to non-state schools rather than being eliminated [confidence: medium | would be reduced by: longitudinal school-composition tracking post-reform in comparable countries]
- Whether teacher supply can staff comprehensive lower-secondary without degrading quality [confidence: low | would be reduced by: institutional capacity and teacher-workforce audit]
- Relative weight of postponed tracking vs accompanying teacher reforms in producing equity gains [confidence: low | would be reduced by: comparative natural experiments isolating the two]

## S4 — Keep the structure, compensate general schools (Portuguese-style package)
**Goal**
Retain the 6/8-year structure unchanged and neutralise its equity effects through non-structural reforms: rigorous curriculum standards, school clustering, and TEIP-style targeted funding for disadvantaged general schools.

**Mechanism**
- Targeted resource packages to lagging general schools raise their outcomes without structural change [evidence: moderate — Portuguese TEIP trajectory; Crato 2020]
- Rigorous national curriculum standards and reduced grade retention lift system-wide attainment [evidence: moderate — Portuguese PISA trend 2006-2015]
- Compensation offsets, rather than merely masks, the effects of early sorting [evidence: weak — no country has demonstrated this at Hungary's selection age; directly contested by the scenario's own uncertainties]

**Evidence status**
moderate — the Portuguese non-structural improvement is reasonably evidenced, but whether compensation can offset (not just mask) sorting at Hungary's age-10/12 selection is undemonstrated, which is the single link determining whether the goal is achievable.

**Assumptions**
- Compensation can meaningfully offset, not just mask, the effects of early sorting [assumption — directly contradicted by S4's own uncertainties, which state no country has demonstrated this at Hungary's selection age; retained as explicitly unresolved]
- SES-based targeting formulas actually reach the disadvantaged majority where they are, rather than misfiring or stigmatising [assumption]
- The Portuguese TEIP effect size transfers to the Hungarian context despite different selection age and teacher-supply conditions [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Improved outcomes in disadvantaged general schools within roughly 5 years [evidence: weak — the Portuguese trajectory is moderate evidence for Portugal, but importing its effect size to Hungary at an earlier selection age is not evidenced]
- System-wide attainment lift from standards and reduced retention [evidence: moderate — Portuguese PISA trend]
- Avoids the disruptive transition costs of structural reform [evidence: moderate — transition costs of structural change are documented; this package incurs none of them]
- Politically less confrontational than S2/S3 in the short term, since it does not touch the tracks [evidence: weak — political feasibility is not graded in the curated registry, and this benefit is contradicted by the scenario's own budget-vulnerability risk]

**Equity impact**
Narrows the SES gradient partially at best, because the sorting mechanism itself is left intact. Carries a dynamic equity cost flagged in political_risks: sustained compensation risks legitimising early selection permanently, so if compensation only masks rather than offsets sorting, the package could entrench the very inequality it targets. Targeting-formula misfire could also leave the least-visible disadvantaged pupils unreached.

**Cost categories**
- Recurring: TEIP-style targeted funding for disadvantaged general schools (high; no numeric range available in curated sources, but this is the largest and most budget-exposed line)
- Recurring: curriculum-standards development, assessment, and retention-reduction programmes (medium-high; no numeric range in curated sources)
- Political capital: medium — not 'low', because the recurring premia/funding lines are fiscally exposed and require teacher-salary premia for disadvantaged schools, which the implementation steps themselves demand

**Implementation steps**
- Legislator/Ministry — establish a TEIP-style targeted-funding programme with protected multi-year budget lines
- Ministry — introduce salary premia and staffing incentives for disadvantaged schools
- Ministry — roll out rigorous national curriculum standards and grade-retention reduction
- KK — implement school clustering to share administrative and pedagogical capacity

**Political risks**
- Budget vulnerability: compensation lines are the first cut in fiscal consolidation, making the package fragile across fiscal cycles [evidence: moderate — this directly qualifies any claim of political inexpensiveness]
- Legitimising early selection permanently: sustained compensation may entrench the sorting structure rather than dissolve it
- Teacher shortage undermines both standards rollout and premia-based staffing of disadvantaged schools [evidence: strong — documented systemic shortage]

**Uncertainties**
- Whether compensation can offset rather than merely mask early-sorting effects at Hungary's selection age [confidence: low | would be reduced by: a longitudinal evaluation of TEIP-style intervention against sorting outcomes at age 10/12]
- Whether Hungarian teacher shortages permit implementing Portuguese-style standards and targeted support [confidence: medium (source: high that shortage exists; medium that it blocks implementation) | would be reduced by: comparative teacher-supply studies, Hungary vs historical Portugal]
- Whether political consensus can sustain long-term targeted funding without reversal [confidence: medium | would be reduced by: analysis of Hungarian budgetary commitments and legislative continuity]
