# Policy scenarios

## S1 — Reform admissions and quotas within the existing 6-/8-year gimnázium system
**Goal**
Reduce the SES-skew of who enters the existing 6-/8-year selective tracks, without changing the tracks' existence, age of entry, or structural role.

**Mechanism**
- Replacing or supplementing the coachable, prep-course-sensitive central written exam with a less-preparation-sensitive assessment component reduces the advantage that paid tutoring confers on high-SES applicants [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013 on selection vs. value-added; model knowledge on test-prep effects]
- Introducing SES-weighted quotas or bonus points at the point of admission mechanically raises the share of low-SES entrants among those who apply [evidence: strong — direct arithmetic effect of quota design, contingent on application-pool composition]
- Regulating non-state (church/foundation) maintainer admissions in parallel prevents demand from migrating wholesale to the unregulated non-state selective sector [evidence: weak — no Hungarian precedent for enforced uniform admissions across maintainer types; hungarian_education_system interpretation only]

**Evidence status**
Moderate overall — the arithmetic effect of quotas on intake composition is well-evidenced, but the assessment-redesign and cross-maintainer regulation components rest on weak or absent Hungarian evidence.

**Assumptions**
- A workable SES indicator exists at application time [evidence: contested — Hungary has no student-level administrative SES register usable at the point of a school application; the closest proxies (hátrányos/halmozottan hátrányos helyzet status, address-based catchment) are coarse, self- or institution-reported, and were designed for different administrative purposes, so their validity as an admissions criterion is unverified]
- Church and private maintainers can be brought under the same admissions constraints as state schools, or their share is small enough that leakage does not offset state-side gains [assumption]
- Parents and prep-course providers do not fully adapt their strategies to the new assessment format within the first admission cycles [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- More balanced intake into selective tracks, though the specific '2-3 admission cycles' timeline is a plausibility estimate, not a measured effect — no registry source tracks intake SES composition under a quota/assessment reform of this kind in Hungary [evidence: weak]
- Reduced returns to paid test preparation for admission, to the extent the new assessment is genuinely less coachable [evidence: weak — assessment-design literature is suggestive, not Hungary-specific]
- Preserves existing school identities, staff, and buildings, avoiding transition costs associated with structural reform [evidence: strong — mechanically true by construction, since no structural change occurs]

**Equity impact**
Gains are conditional on two unresolved premises: that a workable SES indicator exists at application time, and that low-SES rural families apply into the reformed process at similar rates to how they apply today — the mechanism only reweights who is selected among applicants, it does not address who applies. If rural low-SES families remain under-represented in the applicant pool (due to distance, information, or self-exclusion), quota-based reweighting will show little effect regardless of the indicator's quality. Absent parallel non-state regulation, higher-SES families who lose state-track access may exit to unregulated church/foundation selective tracks, muting the measured equity gain in state statistics while leaving actual segregation unchanged.

**Cost categories**
- One-off: assessment redesign and psychometric validation, including piloting (medium-high — meaningfully more than routine administration, since building and validating a less-coachable, age-appropriate instrument requires multi-year psychometric work, not a single-cycle pilot)
- One-off: legal and negotiation costs of extending admission constraints to church/foundation maintainers (medium)
- Recurring: compliance-audit and monitoring mechanism to verify quota adherence and detect informal circumvention (medium — omitted from the original cost list despite being flagged as necessary in the uncertainties below)
- Recurring: routine admission administration (low, marginal cost over current process)

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education / Oktatási Hivatal — commission psychometric design and validation of a lower-coachability assessment (timeline: year 1-3)
- Ministry of Education — pilot the new assessment and quota design in volunteer districts alongside the existing exam, running both in parallel to compare intake composition (timeline: year 2-4)
- Legislator — amend the public education act to set SES-weighted quota parameters and define the eligible indicator (timeline: year 2-3)
- Ministry / Klebelsberg Centre — negotiate parallel admission constraints with church and foundation maintainer associations (timeline: year 2-4, running concurrently with piloting)
- Oktatási Hivatal — stand up a compliance-audit unit to monitor quota adherence and informal circumvention before national rollout (timeline: year 3-4)
- Ministry — national rollout of the revised assessment and quotas across state-maintained tracks (timeline: year 4-5)

**Political risks**
- Elite-school and middle-class parent opposition to any perceived dilution of merit-based selection [evidence: moderate — political_feasibility expert, domestic press/stakeholder analyses 1990s-2010s]
- Church/private maintainer resistance to external admission constraints, including exemption claims on religious-autonomy grounds [evidence: moderate — legal_and_governance expert interpretation]
- Private tutoring and test-prep industry has a direct commercial stake in the current exam format and can mobilize against a redesign that reduces the returns to paid preparation [evidence: weak — no direct study of this actor's mobilization capacity in Hungary, but its scale and dependence on the current system make it a plausible organized opponent]

**Uncertainties**
- Whether a workable SES indicator can be constructed and administered at application time [confidence: low | would be reduced by: Oktatási Hivatal student registry data cross-referenced with family tax/benefit records, and a technical feasibility study on address- or status-based proxies]
- The willingness of church maintainers to accept uniform equity-based quotas under threat of subsidy withdrawal [confidence: medium | would be reduced by: surveys and qualitative interviews with church maintainer representatives and Ministry officials]
- Whether low-SES rural application rates rise enough for quota reweighting to matter in practice [confidence: low | would be reduced by: a baseline study of application (not just admission) rates by SES and settlement type]

## S2 — Gradually phase down 6-/8-year entry places while ramping general-school capacity (Polish-trajectory-inspired glide path)
**Goal**
Shrink the selective-entry share of each cohort year by year on a pre-announced statutory trajectory, shifting the marginal student into general schools, while protecting currently enrolled pupils.

**Mechanism**
- A pre-announced, legislated annual cap trajectory on 6-/8-year entry places mechanically reduces the selective-entry share of each cohort as caps bind [evidence: strong — direct arithmetic consequence of a binding quota, independent of behavioral response]
- Advanced-programme funding in general schools retains ambitious families in the common track as selective places shrink [evidence: weak — no Hungarian evidence on whether funded in-school advanced programmes are a credible substitute for a separate selective institution in parental decision-making]
- General schools can absorb and challenge high-achieving pupils if funded and staffed for it [evidence: contested — this is 'the unevidenced link' per the underlying analysis: no Hungarian study demonstrates general-school capacity to replicate the value-added or peer environment of selective tracks, and it is the single mechanism on which the scenario's peer-composition benefit depends]

**Evidence status**
Moderate-to-weak overall — the cap trajectory's mechanical effect on selective-entry share is close to certain, but every downstream equity and peer-quality benefit depends on the contested general-school-absorption link, which has no direct Hungarian evidence.

**Assumptions**
- General schools can absorb and challenge high-ability students if adequately funded [evidence: contested — see mechanism above; expected_benefits below are downstream of this same unresolved claim, not independent confirmation of it]
- Church and private maintainers can be bound to an equivalent cap trajectory, or their uncapped share is small enough not to absorb the displaced demand [assumption]
- A successor government will not freeze or reverse the cap trajectory before it reaches its target (see political_risks) [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Falling early-sorting share of each cohort, measurable annually as a direct statistical consequence of the binding cap [evidence: strong for the mechanical share reduction; evidence: weak for any claim that this reduction translates into reduced segregation, since no registry source measures whether displaced demand moves to non-state selective tracks instead]
- Stronger peer composition in urban general-school upper grades, contingent entirely on the contested general-school-absorption mechanism and the weak retention-via-funding claim above — this is not an independent benefit but a restatement of those assumptions holding [evidence: weak]
- Transition-cohort protection via felmenő rendszer avoids disrupting pupils already admitted under the old rules [evidence: strong — legal principle is well-established in Hungarian education law, per legal_and_governance]

**Equity impact**
Residual selective places become more SES-skewed as caps tighten, since a near-certain mechanical effect (fewer seats, unchanged high-SES demand, no change to the admission rule itself) concentrates remaining places among the most prepared applicants — this is a near-certain effect, distinct from and more confident than the contested peer-composition benefit above. During the transition, incumbent higher-SES families — who have more information and preparation resources — are best placed to secure the shrinking number of remaining places each year, so early transition cohorts may see equity effects opposite to the scenario's stated goal before the trajectory matures. Absent S1-type admission-rule reform running in parallel, capping alone does not change who wins the remaining seats, only how many seats exist.

**Cost categories**
- Recurring: advanced-programme funding in general schools, phased to rise as caps bind — early years (year 1-3) low-medium as few pupils are affected; later years (year 7-10) medium-high as the bulk of the cohort shifts into general-school advanced tracks (formula-based, no current numeric estimate exists)
- One-off: legal and administrative cost of the felmenő rendszer transition guarantee across state, church, and private maintainers (medium)
- One-off: negotiation and legal cost of extending the cap trajectory to church/private maintainers, or of designing compensating regulation if they remain uncapped (medium-high)

**Implementation steps**
- Legislator — enact the cap trajectory in the public education act, with felmenő rendszer guarantees for already-enrolled pupils (timeline: year 1)
- Ministry / Klebelsberg Centre — negotiate an equivalent or compensating cap arrangement with church and private maintainers separately from the state-school legislative act, since a single statutory act cannot bind independent maintainers by fiat (timeline: year 1-3)
- Ministry — design and fund advanced-programme tracks in general schools, phased in ahead of each year's cap reduction (timeline: year 2-10, rising in stages)
- Ministry — build an entrenchment mechanism (e.g., a supermajority or multi-year notice requirement to alter the trajectory) to raise the cost of reversal by a successor government (timeline: year 1)
- Oktatási Hivatal — monitor annual sorting share and non-state enrollment shifts to detect demand migration to uncapped maintainers (timeline: year 2 onward, annual)

**Political risks**
- A successor government can freeze or reverse the trajectory cheaply if no entrenchment mechanism exists in law — the Polish precedent shows structurally similar reforms reversed after a government change [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD on Polish reversal]. This scenario's implementation_steps now include an explicit entrenchment step, but its legal robustness against a determined future legislature is untested [evidence: weak]
- Urban middle-class and elite-school parent resistance to any narrowing of selective-place access [evidence: moderate — political_feasibility expert]
- Church/private maintainer resistance to binding cap agreements, with potential to absorb displaced demand rather than cooperate [evidence: moderate — legal_and_governance and hungarian_education_system interpretation]

**Uncertainties**
- Whether general schools can, in practice, absorb and challenge high-achievers given current teacher shortages [confidence: low | would be reduced by: pilot evaluations of funded advanced-programme tracks in general schools with matched comparison schools]
- Whether displaced selective demand migrates to non-state maintainers rather than genuinely entering the common track [confidence: medium | would be reduced by: annual Oktatási Hivatal enrollment tracking disaggregated by maintainer type]
- Whether a statutory entrenchment mechanism actually deters reversal, given the Polish precedent involved formal legislative repeal despite similar institutional design [confidence: low | would be reduced by: comparative legal analysis of entrenchment clauses and their reversal history across CEE education reforms]

## S3 — Structural reform to a comprehensive lower-secondary school to age 14, abolishing 6-/8-year gimnázium entry points
**Goal**
Eliminate early academic tracking entirely by moving the age of first selection from 10/12 to 14, converting 6-/8-year gimnaziums into upper-secondary-only institutions.

**Mechanism**
- Removing the age-10/12 selection point delays the point at which academic trajectories diverge, directly reducing the number of years over which SES-linked sorting can compound before age 14 [evidence: strong — mechanical consequence of removing an earlier selection point; Hanushek & Woessmann 2006 cross-country evidence that earlier tracking increases inequality without raising mean performance]
- Asymmetric peer effects — mixing high- and low-achieving pupils benefits lower achievers more than it costs higher achievers — would generate a narrower outcome gradient in comprehensive classrooms [evidence: contested — this is the single mechanism on which the scenario's equity benefit rests, and it is not confirmed by Hungarian data; cross-country peer-effect estimates are heterogeneous and context-dependent]
- Sorting does not simply migrate to school choice between general schools or to non-state maintainers once the formal selective track is removed [evidence: contested — the scenario's own equity_impact below identifies exactly this migration as its largest risk, and Kertesi & Kezdi 2013 / Berényi-Berkovits-Erőss school-choice studies find that free choice plus catchment effects already produce strong between-school sorting even where formal tracks are absent; this is a load-bearing but empirically unresolved claim]

**Evidence status**
Mixed — the mechanical claim that removing an early selection point compresses the sorting window is well-evidenced cross-nationally, but the two mechanisms that determine whether equity actually improves in the Hungarian context (asymmetric peer effects; non-migration of sorting to choice/non-state schools) are both contested and unconfirmed.

**Assumptions**
- Sorting does not simply migrate to school choice between general schools and to non-state maintainers [evidence: contested — see mechanism above; this is the load-bearing assumption on which S3's entire equity case rests, and the scenario's own equity_impact field concedes this migration is the 'largest risk,' so the assumption and the risk assessment are in direct tension rather than the assumption being a settled premise]
- A comprehensive lower-secondary school can achieve the teacher quality and support-system standardization that made the Finnish reform's equity gains possible, rather than just removing the tracking point in isolation [evidence: moderate — Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009 show Finnish equity gains were bundled with teacher-training and special-education reform, not tracking removal alone]
- Rural and semi-urban school networks can be reconfigured to serve a comprehensive model without excessive commuting burden on pupils aged 10-14 [assumption — school_network_planning flags 30-45 minute commuting tolerance as a binding constraint]

**Expected benefits**
- Weaker SES-outcome gradient within a decade, contingent entirely on the contested asymmetric-peer-effects mechanism holding in the Hungarian context — this is not an independently evidenced benefit but a restatement of that mechanism's claim [evidence: contested]
- Later, better-informed track choice at 14 reduces misallocation of talent between academic and vocational paths — no registry source in this analysis directly measures this outcome; the closest comparators (Poland, Finland) report PISA score and equity effects, not talent-misallocation measures specifically [evidence: weak]
- Removal of the age-10/12 admission exam eliminates that specific high-stakes stress point for 10-year-olds, though this may be offset if informal selection mechanisms (tutoring for the age-14 transition, or non-state entry exams) emerge in its place, as flagged in political_risks below [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
The largest expected gain and the largest risk are usually stated only in aggregate terms, but distributional equity within the disadvantaged population also matters: if the chief risk — middle-class flight to non-state or choice-sorted general schools — materializes, the families who cannot exit (lowest-SES, least mobile, most information-poor) are left concentrated together in the least-resourced remaining schools, a worse local peer environment than before reform for exactly the group the reform aims to help. Middle-class exit itself does not harm the exiting families; it concentrates downside risk onto those who stay. Whether the reform helps or harms the most disadvantaged sub-group therefore depends on the unresolved migration question in the assumptions above, not merely on the aggregate SES-gradient statistic.

**Cost categories**
- One-off: nationwide teacher retraining for differentiated instruction in comprehensive classrooms (high — no numeric range or per-cohort/per-district unit cost currently available)
- One-off: network and building reconfiguration to convert 6-/8-year entry points into upper-secondary-only institutions — this cost is bundled with demographic consolidation that would occur regardless of this policy (per the demography/school_network_planning experts), so the cost attributable to S3 alone, net of consolidation that happens anyway, is not separately identified here and should not be read as the full reconfiguration bill (high, largely non-incremental)
- Recurring: standardized special-education and diagnostic support to replicate the Finnish bundling condition — omitted from the original cost list despite being an assumption the scenario depends on; no estimate currently exists (medium-high, ongoing)

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry / university teacher-training institutes — pilot differentiation retraining with a first cohort of trainers in 2-3 volunteer districts before any wider rollout (timeline: year 1-3)
- Ministry — pilot the comprehensive model itself in the same 2-3 districts, sequenced after (not concurrent with) the initial retraining cohort, to avoid running an untested model with untrained staff (timeline: year 3-5)
- Legislator — attempt a cross-party pact anchoring the reform against reversal, given the Polish precedent of full reversal after a government change (timeline: year 1-2, prior to legislation)
- Ministry — scale differentiation retraining nationally only after pilot-district evaluation confirms feasibility (timeline: year 5-8)
- Legislator — national legislative conversion of 6-/8-year entry points to upper-secondary-only, with felmenő rendszer guarantees (timeline: year 6-8)

**Political risks**
- Highest-intensity opposition of all four scenarios, combining urban middle-class, elite-school, and church/private maintainer resistance [evidence: moderate — political_feasibility and legal_and_governance experts]
- No described fallback exists if the cross-party pact attempt fails before legislation — the scenario currently proceeds to pilot and scale-up regardless, leaving no contingency plan comparable to, e.g., restricting rollout to consenting regions or maintainers if a national pact cannot be secured [evidence: weak — this is a structural gap in the plan rather than an evidenced risk]
- Polish-style reversal after a change of government could destroy sunk transition costs (teacher retraining, piloted infrastructure) before the reform reaches its target state [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD on the 2016-2019 Polish reversal]

**Uncertainties**
- Whether sorting migrates to school choice and non-state maintainers rather than being eliminated [confidence: low | would be reduced by: a natural-experiment or comparative study of school-choice sorting intensity in districts with versus without early formal tracks, ideally using Hungarian non-state enrollment data]
- The precise causal weight of postponed tracking itself versus the accompanying teacher and support-system reforms in producing Finland's equity outcomes [confidence: low | would be reduced by: comparative natural experiments isolating tracking timing from teacher-training and special-education reform]
- Whether a fallback exists — or should be designed — if the cross-party pact fails [confidence: low | would be reduced by: explicit contingency planning and precedent review of phased/regional rollout models used elsewhere]

## S4 — Retain the 6-/8-year gimnázium structure and deploy a Portuguese-style compensation package for general schools
**Goal**
Neutralize the socio-economic inequities of early selection without altering the selection age or track structure, via targeted funding, curriculum standards, and school clustering for disadvantaged general schools.

**Mechanism**
- Targeted funding for disadvantaged schools (TEIP-style) raises resourcing and support intensity where SES-linked need is highest [evidence: moderate — Portugal's PISA gains 2006-2015 coincided with TEIP and related non-structural reforms, though causal attribution to TEIP specifically versus the full reform bundle is not isolated in the source studies]
- Rigorous, standardized national curriculum expectations reduce variance in instructional quality across schools regardless of track [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020, again as part of a bundled reform, not isolated]
- School clustering improves administrative capacity and resource-sharing for smaller or disadvantaged schools without requiring a change to the selection structure [evidence: weak — Portuguese clustering is documented as part of the reform bundle, but its independent contribution is not separately evidenced]
- Compensation can meaningfully offset, not just mask, the effects of early sorting on disadvantaged pupils in general schools [evidence: contested — this is the load-bearing assumption of the entire scenario, and it directly conflicts with this scenario's own uncertainties below, which state that no country in the source evidence has demonstrated compensation offsetting selection effects at Hungary's selection age of 10-12; Portugal's comparator did not have an early selection point of this kind to compensate for]

**Evidence status**
Moderate on the individual Portuguese-style interventions in their original context, but weak-to-contested on the central transfer claim that these interventions can compensate for the specific effects of age-10/12 selection, since Portugal's own reform did not have to compensate for a selection point this early.

**Assumptions**
- Compensation can meaningfully offset, not just mask, the effects of early sorting [evidence: contested — see mechanism above; this assumption is in direct tension with the scenario's own uncertainties field, which concedes no country has demonstrated this at Hungary's selection age]
- Non-structural interventions are politically easier to implement and sustain than structural changes to tracking age [assumption — plausible but untested against Hungary's specific fiscal-cycle volatility, see political_risks below]
- The mechanisms of Portugal's TEIP and curriculum standards are transferable to the Hungarian socio-economic and institutional context, despite Hungary's more acute teacher shortage [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Improved outcomes in disadvantaged general schools within roughly five years, based on the Portuguese trajectory — the specific timeline and effect size are imported from a different country context with a different starting selection structure, so the transfer to Hungary is a plausibility claim, not a measured prediction [evidence: moderate for the Portuguese case itself; evidence: weak for its transfer to Hungary]
- Politically inexpensive relative to structural reform in the sense that it requires no change to admission rules, track structure, or selection age, avoiding the specific opposition documented against S2/S3 [evidence: moderate for the narrower claim of 'less structural opposition than S2/S3'; the broader claim that it is 'implementable under any government' is not supported at strong confidence, since this scenario's own political_risks field identifies compensation budgets as fiscally vulnerable to reversal in a way that structural legal changes are not — a government can defund a compensation line far more cheaply than it can re-legislate a structural reform, so 'inexpensive to enact' does not imply 'durable once enacted' [evidence: weak for the durability claim]
- Avoids the transition costs of structural reorganization (retraining for a new grade structure, building reconfiguration, network redesign) that S2 and S3 incur [evidence: strong — mechanically true since no structural change occurs]

**Equity impact**
Compensation narrows the SES gradient at best partially, since it treats a symptom (under-resourced general schools) rather than the mechanism producing the gradient (removal of high-achieving peers and the sorting process itself). This also carries a dynamic equity cost not captured by the static gradient measure: by making the current structure more tolerable, compensation risks legitimising early selection permanently, foreclosing future structural reform options and locking in whatever residual segregation the compensation package does not reach. If compensation funding is later cut in a fiscal downturn — a risk this scenario's own political_risks section identifies as live — the structure remains in place with none of its inequities addressed, which is a strictly worse equilibrium than the pre-reform baseline for the same fiscal exposure as S2's recurring costs.

**Cost categories**
- Recurring: TEIP-style targeted funding for disadvantaged general schools — no numeric range or share-of-education-budget estimate currently exists; given that the scenario's own political-risk section flags this line as fiscally exposed, a range should be established relative to total general-school operating budget, tentatively high (double-digit percentage uplift for targeted schools based on the Portuguese TEIP model, unverified for Hungary)
- Recurring: curriculum standardization and associated teacher support/training — medium-high, ongoing, similarly without a current numeric estimate; Hungary's teacher shortage may raise this above the Portuguese comparator's cost given need for more external training capacity
- One-off: school clustering administrative reorganization (medium)

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry — design targeted funding formula (TEIP-equivalent) and identify eligible disadvantaged schools (timeline: year 1)
- Ministry — develop and legislate rigorous national curriculum standards (timeline: year 1-2)
- Klebelsberg Centre — implement school clustering for smaller/disadvantaged schools where feasible (timeline: year 1-3)
- Ministry — introduce funding premia and support packages for disadvantaged-school teachers, including recruitment incentives given the existing teacher shortage (timeline: year 2-4)
- Ministry / Ministry of Finance — establish a protected or multi-year budget line for the compensation package to reduce first-cut vulnerability in fiscal consolidations (timeline: year 1, revisited each budget cycle)

**Political risks**
- Budget vulnerability: compensation lines are the first cut in fiscal consolidation, since they lack the legal permanence of structural changes to the public education act — this directly limits the durability of the 'politically inexpensive' benefit claimed above, and is not fully mitigated by the multi-year budget line proposed in implementation_steps, whose legal robustness against a determined future budget process is untested [evidence: weak on the protection mechanism's effectiveness]
- Legitimising early selection permanently: by making the current structure fiscally and politically tolerable, this scenario may foreclose future appetite for structural reform even if compensation later proves insufficient [evidence: weak — this is an interpretive risk, not a measured one]
- Teacher shortage constrains the credibility of premia and support-package promises, since money alone cannot manufacture qualified teachers in a shortage market [evidence: strong — OECD Education at a Glance; PSZ/PDSZ surveys on Hungarian teacher shortage]

**Uncertainties**
- Whether compensation can offset selection effects at Hungary's selection age of 10-12, given no country in the reviewed evidence has demonstrated this at so early an age [confidence: low | would be reduced by: a Hungarian pilot with matched comparison schools tracking SES-gradient change under full TEIP-style funding versus control]
- Whether Hungarian political consensus can sustain long-term funding for targeted programmes without reversal across fiscal cycles [confidence: medium | would be reduced by: analysis of Hungarian budgetary commitments and legislative continuity in education policy across at least one full electoral cycle]
- The precise impact of Hungary's teacher shortage on the viability of Portuguese-style curriculum and support standards [confidence: high | would be reduced by: comparative studies on teacher supply and qualifications in Hungarian disadvantaged schools versus historical Portuguese levels]
