## Overview

The expert record converges on a shared diagnosis but diverges sharply on prescription. There is strong evidence that Hungary's 6- and 8-year gimnázium tracks select roughly 8–12% of a cohort at ages 10–12, that entrants skew heavily toward high-SES urban families, and that Hungary's socio-economic gradient in PISA (~19–25%) is among the worst in the OECD. Cross-national evidence links early between-school tracking to higher inequality without mean-performance gains. Against this, experts split on whether the correct response is structural (phase out early tracks) or non-structural (retain tracks, attack sorting by other means), and on whether reform is feasible at all given maintainer plurality, teacher shortages, transition costs, and political durability risks. Demographic contraction runs underneath the whole debate as an independent driver of school-network restructuring.

## Disagreement map

- **international_comparison, finnish_reform, polish_reform, equity_and_social_mobility, demography, school_network_planning**: Hungary should phase out early selection (ages 10/12) in favour of delayed/within-school differentiation. Why: cross-national and Finnish/Polish evidence predicts reduced SES-inequality without mean-performance loss, and early sorting yields little value-added while concentrating demographic and equity vulnerability on the general primary network.
- **hungarian_education_system**: Retain the 6/8-year tracks but align entry quotas with regional equity targets and regulate non-state admissions. (minority) Why: banning selective tracks only within state schools will push selective demand into the autonomous church/foundation sector, shifting rather than eliminating sorting.
- **portuguese_reform**: Retain the tracks and neutralise inequity via non-structural reforms (TEIP-style targeted funding, rigorous curriculum standards). (minority) Why: Portugal improved PISA outcomes and equity without structural reorganisation, and non-structural reforms are politically easier to sustain.
- **education_finance**: Retain the tracks because structural consolidation yields no significant steady-state savings while front-loading transition costs. (minority) Why: per-pupil costs are flat across secondary tracks and national wage grids mean transfers do not cut wage bills; retraining and facility re-adaptation dominate budgets.
- **implementation_planning**: Retain the tracks in the medium term, delaying integration until teacher supply stabilises. (minority) Why: acute shortages of specialised (maths/science) teachers and fragile administrative capacity mean the transition would collapse educational delivery in disadvantaged regions.
- **political_feasibility**: Do not abolish abruptly; gradually phase out by disincentivising selective admissions while upgrading primary-school funding. Why: urban middle-class beneficiaries have vetoed past attempts, and the Polish precedent shows even successful reforms are vulnerable to political reversal.
- **finnish_reform vs. equity_and_social_mobility/international_comparison (on conditionality)**: finnish_reform holds that phase-out yields equity gains only if bundled with standardised teacher training and universal diagnostic support; otherwise within-class variance rises and middle-class flight follows. Why: Finnish equity outcomes cannot be isolated from teacher-quality and early-support bundling. The pro-reform equity/comparison experts do not attach this precondition.
- **legal_and_governance**: The structural question (form existence) must be separated from the selection-timing question, and no change can be imposed uniformly on church/foundation maintainers without renegotiating maintainer agreements. Why: the 6/8-year form is statutorily entrenched in the Nkt. and maintainer plurality precludes a uniform mandate; curated sources are silent on Hungarian pedagogical/equity outcomes.

## What the experts agree on

- The 6- and 8-year gimnázium tracks select ~8–12% of a cohort at ages 10/12, with entrants strongly skewed toward high-SES urban families. [evidence: strong]
- Hungary's SES gradient in PISA (~19–25%) is among the highest in the OECD, well above the ~12–14% average. [evidence: strong]
- Since 2013 most state schools run under tankerület/Klebelsberg centres while churches and foundations maintain a growing, autonomous share. [evidence: strong]
- Demographic contraction (births falling from ~125,000 to ~85,000–95,000) forces school-network consolidation in the 2020s–2030s regardless of selection policy. [evidence: strong]
- Cross-national evidence links earlier between-school tracking to higher inequality without raising mean performance. [evidence: strong for the cross-national relationship; Hungary-specific causality remains uncertain]
- Reform (if pursued) faces significant transition costs, teacher-supply constraints, and political-durability risks — none of the pro-reform experts dispute these frictions exist.

## Minority positions

- **hungarian_education_system (minority)**: Retain 6/8-year tracks with equity-aligned quotas plus regulation of non-state admissions, rather than phase-out. Rationale: phase-out confined to state schools displaces selective demand into the autonomous church/foundation sector, shifting sorting rather than removing it. This directly contradicts the pro-reform assumption (S2) that non-state maintainers can be restricted from absorbing phased-out selective capacity — a tension the record does not resolve.
- **portuguese_reform (minority)**: Retain tracks; pursue non-structural equity interventions (TEIP-style targeted funding, curriculum standards). Rationale: Portugal raised PISA outcomes and equity without structural reorganisation, and non-structural reforms are easier to implement and sustain politically.
- **education_finance (minority)**: Retain tracks on fiscal grounds. Rationale: steady-state per-pupil costs are flat across tracks and national wage grids neutralise transfer savings; transition costs (retraining, facilities) dominate with no offsetting steady-state gain.
- **implementation_planning (minority)**: Retain tracks in the medium term until teacher supply stabilises. Rationale: specialised-teacher shortages and fragile administrative capacity would cause the transition to collapse educational delivery in disadvantaged regions.
- **finnish_reform (conditional minority on preconditions)**: Even among reform supporters, phase-out is endorsed only if bundled with standardised teacher training and universal diagnostic support; unbundled, it risks rising within-class variance and middle-class flight. Rationale: Finnish equity gains cannot be isolated from their teacher-quality and early-support bundling.
- **legal_and_governance (minority framing)**: The structural and selection-timing questions must be treated separately, and no uniform mandate is possible without renegotiating church/foundation maintainer agreements. Rationale: statutory entrenchment in the Nkt. plus maintainer autonomy preclude a uniform reform, and curated sources are silent on Hungarian outcome evidence.
