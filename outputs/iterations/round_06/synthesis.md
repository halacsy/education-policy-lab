## Overview

Twelve experts examine whether Hungary should retain, modify, or phase out its 6- and 8-year gimnazium tracks, which select ~8-12% of a cohort at ages 10/12 via central exam and skew strongly toward high-SES, urban families [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal]. There is no consensus on the central policy question. A majority reads comparative and Hungary-specific evidence as supporting phase-out or delay of early selection; a substantial minority argues for retaining the structure and pursuing non-structural or delayed remedies instead. Several sub-disagreements — on pace, on whether non-state maintainers can be regulated, on causal certainty, and on how much of the structure to remove — cut across this main split and are preserved below rather than resolved.

## Disagreement map

- **finnish_reform, polish_reform, equity_and_social_mobility, demography, political_feasibility, international_comparison**: Hungary should phase out or substantially delay early academic selection (ages 10/12). Why: cross-country evidence that earlier tracking raises inequality without raising mean performance (Hanushek & Woessmann 2006 [moderate/strong]), the Finnish and Polish delayed-tracking precedents (Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009; Jakubowski et al. 2016 [moderate]), Hungary's OECD-leading SES-performance gradient (OECD PISA [strong]), and demographic cohort shrinkage (KSH [strong]) are together read as evidence that structure itself, not just funding, drives sorting and network strain.

- **portuguese_reform, education_finance, implementation_planning, hungarian_education_system (minority)**: Hungary should retain the 6-/8-year gimnazium structure and pursue non-structural remedies instead (targeted funding/TEIP-style support, curriculum standards, admissions/quota regulation). Why: Portugal raised PISA outcomes 2006-2015 without changing selection age (OECD; Crato 2020 [moderate]); per-pupil operating costs are flat across tracks so reform saves little while transition costs (retraining, facility reuse) dominate [weak/strong — Budget chapters, model knowledge]; a systemic teacher shortage means administrative capacity cannot absorb a structural reorganization without degrading delivery in disadvantaged regions [strong — OECD, PSZ/PDSZ]; and simply banning tracks in state schools may not even achieve the equity goal (see below).

- **school_network_planning (minority variant within the reform camp)**: Only the 8-year gimnazium should be phased out; 6-year programs should be restricted to, not eliminated from, regional urban hubs — a narrower cut than the full phase-out proposed by equity_and_social_mobility, finnish_reform, and polish_reform. Why: small-school diseconomies and low commuting tolerance for 10-12 year-olds (≤30-45 min one-way) [moderate] argue for consolidating around urban hubs rather than eliminating the earliest track everywhere.

- **hungarian_education_system** vs **school_network_planning (unresolved contradiction, carried forward from prior rounds)**: hungarian_education_system's position depends on the assumption that non-state (church/foundation) school admissions can be regulated to prevent selective demand simply migrating out of the state sector. school_network_planning's own assumption states the opposite — that "church and private maintainers will continue to prioritize selective academic tracks to attract students" and can "absorb selective tracks and bypass national consolidation plans" — a view legal_and_governance's finding on maintainer plurality also supports. Why: non-state maintainers operate under separate legal/regulatory incentives and hold a growing, decentralized share of schools [strong — Public education act amendments; maintainer statistics], so whether the state actually has leverage to bind them is disputed rather than settled by either expert.

- **finnish_reform, polish_reform, equity_and_social_mobility** vs **international_comparison (minority caveat)**: The former treat delayed tracking as having real, if partly uncertain, causal equity value based on the Finnish and Polish trajectories. international_comparison explicitly cautions that the observed cross-country correlation between early selection and Hungary's high SES-gradient "does not by itself prove a causal link at the country level," and separately rates transferability of cross-country estimates to Hungary's institutional mix as only medium confidence.

- **polish_reform, political_feasibility** vs **implementation_planning (minority)**: polish_reform and political_feasibility argue reform is feasible if paced gradually and anchored in multi-party pacts and compensatory funding to avoid a Polish-style reversal. implementation_planning argues gradualism is not enough — reform should be delayed outright in the medium term until teacher supply stabilizes, because even a phased transition would compete for an already-scarce, aging teacher workforce concentrated in math/science and marginalized regions [strong — OECD, PSZ/PDSZ surveys].

- **education_finance** vs **demography, school_network_planning**: education_finance frames the reform question fiscally — steady-state savings are minimal so the status quo should be kept. demography and school_network_planning argue the fiscal savings framing is beside the point, since consolidation is being forced by birth-rate collapse (125k → 85-95k annual births [strong — KSH]) independent of any tracking decision — the two camps are answering different questions rather than directly refuting each other, and neither side reconciles this.

## What the experts agree on

- The 6-/8-year gimnazium tracks select roughly 8-12% of a cohort at ages 10/12 via central written exam, and entrants are strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics].
- Hungary's share of PISA reading/maths variance explained by socio-economic status (~19-25%) is among the highest in the OECD (~12-14% average) [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2012-2022 country notes].
- Since 2013 most state schools are run by centralized tankerület district centres, while churches and foundations maintain a growing, independently-governed share of schools, which complicates any uniform structural mandate regardless of which policy direction is chosen [evidence: strong — Public education act amendments; maintainer statistics] (hungarian_education_system, legal_and_governance, school_network_planning).
- Annual births fell from ~125,000 in the early 1990s to ~85,000-95,000 recently, forcing some form of school-network consolidation decision in the 2020s-2030s regardless of selection policy [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks] (demography, school_network_planning).
- Any structural change to the 6-/8-year system requires statutory amendment and a multi-year transition rather than an administrative adjustment, and the transition itself (retraining, facility reuse, notice periods for enrolled pupils) is where most of the cost and friction lies [evidence: strong/weak — Nkt. analysis; Budget chapters] (legal_and_governance, education_finance, implementation_planning).

## Minority positions

- **portuguese_reform**: Retain the gimnazium structure entirely; pursue TEIP-style targeted funding and rigorous curriculum standards instead. Rationale: Portugal achieved substantial PISA gains between 2006-2015 without altering its selection age, suggesting the structural debate may be a distraction from more transferable classroom- and funding-level interventions [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020].
- **education_finance**: Retain the system because transition costs (teacher retraining, facility re-adaptation) outweigh marginal steady-state operational savings from consolidation [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates].
- **implementation_planning**: Retain in the medium term and delay any structural integration until teacher supply stabilizes; the existing shortage of math/science teachers, especially in marginalized regions, means the administrative burden of reform would collapse delivery quality [evidence: strong — OECD Education at a Glance; PSZ/PDSZ surveys].
- **hungarian_education_system**: Retain the tracks but align entry requirements/quotas with regional equity targets while regulating non-state admissions — a hybrid minority position premised on the (disputed) assumption that non-state maintainer admissions can be constrained; without that constraint the expert's own equity goal is undermined by displacement to church/foundation schools [assumption, contested by school_network_planning].
- **school_network_planning**: A narrower structural cut than the reform majority — abolish only the 8-year track, restrict (not eliminate) 6-year programs to urban hubs — grounded in small-school diseconomies and commuting-tolerance limits rather than a full-abolition equity argument [evidence: moderate — model knowledge / OECD school network studies; transport planning guidelines].
- **international_comparison**: Holds a narrower epistemic minority position within the reform-supporting camp itself — endorses later selection as the "international default" but explicitly withholds causal certainty, noting the SES-gradient/early-selection correlation "does not by itself prove a causal link," a hedge not present in finnish_reform's or polish_reform's framing of the same evidence.
