## S1 — Reform admissions and quotas within the existing 6-/8-year gimnázium system
- CHOSEN: Reform admissions and quotas within the existing 6-/8-year gimnázium system — combine a less-coachable assessment with SES-weighted quotas/bonus points, plus parallel regulation of non-state maintainer admissions, leaving track structure and entry age unchanged.
- REJECTED: Test-format redesign alone, without SES-weighted quotas — reason: quota design has a strong, near-mechanical arithmetic effect on intake composition, whereas assessment redesign alone rests on weak, non-Hungarian-specific evidence for shifting SES composition.
- REJECTED: Blind lottery among qualified applicants instead of weighted quotas — reason: removes the graduated SES-weighting lever entirely and was judged to trigger stronger elite-parent and legitimacy opposition than a quota-based approach, without added equity evidence to justify the tradeoff.
- REJECTED: Cost-side compensation (subsidizing prep-course access for low-SES applicants) instead of admission-rule reform — reason: this addresses only the cost of preparation, not the selection rule itself, and duplicates the compensation logic already assigned to S4, so was kept out of S1 to preserve distinct scenario logics.

## S2 — Gradually phase down 6-/8-year entry places while ramping general-school capacity (Polish-trajectory-inspired glide path)
- CHOSEN: A pre-announced, legislated annual cap trajectory phasing down 6-/8-year entry places over roughly a decade, with felmenő rendszer protection for enrolled pupils and phased advanced-programme funding in general schools.
- REJECTED: Immediate abolition of new-entry cohorts (hard stop rather than glide path) — reason: offers no felmenő rendszer transition protection and was judged to carry materially higher legal and political-reversal risk than a pre-announced statutory trajectory.
- REJECTED: Capping state-school entry places only, leaving church/private maintainers uncapped — reason: modeled as high risk that displaced demand migrates wholesale to the unregulated non-state sector, undermining the mechanical sorting-share reduction that is this scenario's best-evidenced effect.
- REJECTED: Voluntary, non-statutory reduction negotiated maintainer-by-maintainer — reason: lacks the near-certain mechanical effect of a binding legislated cap and depends on unevidenced voluntary compliance rather than statutory guarantee.

## S3 — Structural reform to a comprehensive lower-secondary school to age 14, abolishing 6-/8-year gimnázium entry points
- CHOSEN: Sequenced national legislative conversion of 6-/8-year gimnázium entry points to upper-secondary-only institutions, moving first selection to age 14, preceded by pilot districts and a cross-party pact attempt.
- REJECTED: Immediate nationwide conversion without a pilot phase — reason: feasibility_checker concerns about running an untested comprehensive model with untrained staff at national scale, with no evidence base for differentiation retraining at that scale absent prior piloting.
- REJECTED: Permanent regional/opt-in comprehensive model rather than an eventual national endpoint — reason: would leave the sorting-migration risk (to choice-sorted or non-state schools) untested at national scale and could shift segregation via inter-regional mobility rather than resolve it.
- REJECTED: Proceeding to legislation without attempting a cross-party pact — reason: the Polish reversal precedent indicates a structural reform of this scale is highly vulnerable to a change of government without some anchoring political mechanism.

## S4 — Retain the 6-/8-year gimnázium structure and deploy a Portuguese-style compensation package for general schools
- CHOSEN: Retain the 6-/8-year gimnázium structure and selection age; deploy TEIP-style targeted funding, standardized curriculum expectations, and school clustering for disadvantaged general schools, backed by a protected multi-year budget line.
- REJECTED: Funding the compensation package from a discretionary annual budget line rather than a protected multi-year line — reason: political_risks identifies compensation lines as the first cut in fiscal consolidation, so a purely discretionary line was judged too exposed to reversal to serve the scenario's stated durability goal.
- REJECTED: Hybridizing compensation with partial admission-rule reform (merging with S1) — reason: kept structurally distinct from S1 so the tradeoff between non-structural compensation and structural admission reform remains visible as genuine disagreement rather than being merged away.
- REJECTED: Untargeted, across-the-board general-school funding increase instead of SES-targeted TEIP-style allocation — reason: dilutes resources away from the highest-need schools and has weaker evidence for narrowing the SES gradient than the targeted Portuguese comparator.
