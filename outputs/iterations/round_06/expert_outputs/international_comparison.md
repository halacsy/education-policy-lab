# Expert analysis: international_comparison

## Findings (evidence)

- **Tracking and inequality relationship**: Cross-country difference-in-differences evidence shows that earlier between-school tracking increases inequality of outcomes without raising mean performance. [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006, Economic Journal]

- **Hungary's socio-economic gradient**: The share of reading/maths score variance explained by socio-economic status in Hungary is among the highest in the OECD (~19–25% across PISA cycles 2012–2022 vs ~12–14% OECD average). [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2012–2022 country notes]

- **Age-based tracking mechanism**: The 6- and 8-year secondary grammar school system in Hungary constitutes early academic selection (entry typically at ages 10–12), operating as a between-school tracking mechanism that sorts students before lower-secondary completion. [evidence: moderate — structural design of Hungarian secondary system; confirmed in OECD Education at a Glance country profiles]

- **Timing sensitivity**: International evidence suggests selection effects on inequality are most pronounced when tracking occurs before age 13; later tracking shows attenuated equity impacts. [evidence: moderate — meta-analysis patterns in Chmielewski 2014 and Brunello & Checchi 2007 literature]

## Interpretation

The convergence of these findings suggests Hungary faces a direct policy dilemma: the 6- and 8-year grammar school system operates as early tracking precisely at an age when international evidence predicts maximal inequality amplification. Hungary's already-elevated socio-economic gradient (nearly 2× OECD average) indicates that current institutions are not insulating opportunity from family background. Earlier selection is unlikely to improve this pattern and cross-national evidence predicts it will worsen it.

The absence of a mean performance penalty for delaying or reducing tracking suggests Hungary need not trade equity for excellence—the efficiency case for early selection is weak.

## Assumptions

- Hungary's institutional context (curriculum structure, teacher supply, school autonomy) is sufficiently similar to OECD comparison countries that tracking–equity relationships transfer. [assumption]
- The socio-economic gradient reflects tracking effects rather than (or in addition to) other institutional features (e.g., residential segregation, parental choice mechanisms). [assumption]
- PISA's coverage of the school-age population (exclusion of early dropouts) does not mask larger selection effects operating before age 15. [assumption]

## Position

Hungary should phase down early academic selection (6- and 8-year grammar school entry) in favour of delayed or within-school differentiation, because international evidence predicts this will reduce socio-economic inequality without sacrificing mean outcomes, and Hungary's current equity gradient is among the worst in the OECD.

## Uncertainties

- **Hungarian-specific mechanism**: It is unclear whether Hungary's high SES gradient is primarily *caused* by early selection or merely *reinforced* by it; causal attribution would be reduced by: a quasi-experimental policy change (staggered abolition of early selection by region or cohort) with matching difference-in-differences analysis.
  [confidence: medium — the cross-national relationship is strong, but Hungary-specific causality is not documented]

- **Implementation feasibility**: Evidence on successful transition costs, teacher retraining needs, and political economy of reform abolition in Hungary is absent from curated sources; would be reduced by: institutional capacity audit and comparative case studies (e.g., Germany's Länder reforms, Austria's recent tracking delays).
  [confidence: low — this is a transfer problem, not an evidence gap in principle]

- **Interaction with parental choice**: If selection is abolished but private grammar schools expand, sorting may persist off-balance-sheet; would be reduced by: longitudinal tracking of school composition and student flows post-reform in comparable countries.
  [confidence: medium — observed in some European contexts but Hungarian-specific prevalence unknown]
