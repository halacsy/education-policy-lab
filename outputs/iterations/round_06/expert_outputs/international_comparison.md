# Expert analysis: international_comparison

## Findings (evidence)

- **Early tracking increases inequality without raising mean achievement.** Cross-country difference-in-differences designs show that systems with between-school tracking initiated before age 10–11 produce larger socio-economic gradients in attainment without gains in average performance. [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006, *Economic Journal*]

- **Hungary exhibits exceptionally high socio-economic stratification in PISA.** The share of reading and mathematics score variance attributable to socio-economic status in Hungary ranges 19–25% across PISA cycles 2012–2022, compared to 12–14% OECD average. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA country notes 2012–2022]

- **6- and 8-year gimnazium entry (age 10–12) aligns with critical juncture for tracking effects.** International evidence identifies ages 10–11 as a threshold where between-school selection begins to stratify long-term outcomes. The Hungarian system's two entry windows (grades 5 and 7) fall within this sensitive range. [evidence: moderate — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006; OECD equity reviews]

- **Equity–efficiency trade-off is context-dependent.** Some high-performing systems (e.g. Singapore, South Korea) maintain early streaming with high absolute performance via intensive compensatory resourcing; others (Finland, Estonia) delay selection and achieve both equity and high mean performance. [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA structural reviews]

## Interpretation

Hungary's combination of early selection and above-average socio-economic stratification suggests that the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system is operating in a high-risk regime for equity. The international evidence does not support the claim that early academic selection raises average achievement; rather, it concentrates gains among advantaged cohorts. Hungary's PISA inequality gradient (~2× OECD average) implies that selection mechanics are amplifying pre-existing family background effects rather than identifying latent talent across socio-economic strata.

The transferability question hinges on Hungary's institutional capacity. If Hungary cannot replicate Singapore's or South Korea's fiscal and pedagogical intensity in lower-tracked pathways, delaying or loosening selection criteria would likely reduce inequality at minimal or zero cost to mean outcomes.

## Assumptions

- Selection at age 10–12 in Hungary operates under similar information asymmetries and parental behaviour as in OECD baseline studies. [assumption]
- The PISA socio-economic index captures relevant dimensions of family advantage in the Hungarian context. [assumption]
- No major confounders (e.g. curriculum intensity, teacher quality sorting) are unmeasured in the cross-country designs cited. [assumption]

## Position

Hungary should postpone between-school academic selection beyond age 14 and broaden entry criteria to reduce socio-economic sorting, because early tracking demonstrably increases inequality without raising mean performance in comparable systems.

## Uncertainties

- **Causal effect size for Hungary specifically.** The Hanushek & Woessmann estimate applies to pooled OECD; Hungary's structural context (teacher supply, regional disparities, parental demand for selection) may modify the effect. [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: Hungary-specific quasi-experimental design or RCT of delayed selection]

- **Political feasibility and parental acceptance.** No evidence is available on whether the Hungarian middle class would tolerate postponement of selection without parallel institutional reform (e.g. quality assurance in non-selective pathways). [confidence: low — would be reduced by: qualitative survey or policy simulation]

- **Alternative mechanisms for talent identification.** The analysis does not address whether later, non-academic selection (e.g. age 15–16, by demonstrated achievement) could preserve equity gains while retaining specialized pathways. [confidence: low — would be reduced by: piloted variants or comparative case studies]
