# Expert analysis: school_network_planning

## Findings (evidence)
- Hungary's annual births declined from approximately 125,000 in the early 1990s to about 85,000–95,000, forcing school-network consolidation decisions in the 2020s–2030s due to cohort shrinkage [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks].
- Since 2013, most state schools have been managed by centralized state school district centres (*tankerület*), while churches and foundations maintain a growing share of schools [evidence: strong — Public education act amendments; maintainer statistics].
- Small school size increases per-pupil operating costs and limits curriculum breadth, making schools with fewer than 20 students per grade economically inefficient [evidence: moderate — Model knowledge / OECD school network studies].
- Commuting tolerance for students aged 10–12 (the age of entry for 8-year gimnaziums) is lower than for older students, typically capping acceptable one-way travel times at 30–45 minutes [evidence: moderate — Model knowledge / transport planning guidelines].

## Interpretation
Cohort shrinkage makes the maintenance of parallel, highly selective early-track programs (starting at ages 10 or 12) increasingly inefficient in rural and semi-urban areas. Selective institutions drain high-achieving pupils from standard local schools, reducing those schools' student bodies below critical mass and driving up per-pupil costs. Because non-state maintainers (churches/foundations) hold a growing, decentralized share of schools, any state-mandated closure of early-selection programs will likely lead to structural fragmentation, as these independent maintainers can absorb selective tracks and bypass national consolidation plans.

## Assumptions
- School consolidation plans prioritize minimizing public fiscal expenditure over local community preservation [assumption].
- Student commuting tolerances will not significantly expand through infrastructure investments [assumption].
- Church and private maintainers will continue to prioritize selective academic tracks to attract students [assumption].

## Position
Hungary should phase out 8-year grammar schools and restrict 6-year programs to regional urban hubs to prevent demographic collapse from hollower rural school networks.

## Uncertainties
- The exact proportion of 6- and 8-year gimnazium seats currently operated by non-state (church and foundation) maintainers in rural districts (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: registry data on school maintainers by settlement type).
- The maximum distance rural families are willing to transport 10-year-olds to access selective education before choosing local alternatives (confidence: low; would be reduced by: national travel-to-school mobility surveys).
