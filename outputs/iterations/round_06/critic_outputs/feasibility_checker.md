# Critique: feasibility_checker
## S1.implementation_steps
Objection: The timeline for "negotiate parallel admission constraints with church and foundation maintainer associations (timeline: year 2-4)" is highly optimistic and likely infeasible. The scenario's own `Mechanism` and `Assumptions` sections flag that there is "no Hungarian precedent for enforced uniform admissions across maintainer types" and that the ability to bring church and private maintainers under the same constraints is an `assumption` that is `contested`. Given the strong legal autonomy and growing share of non-state maintainers, negotiating such a fundamental change in admissions policy, which directly impacts their institutional identity and selection freedom, is likely to be a multi-year, contentious process, potentially requiring legislative changes or significant concessions that extend beyond a 2-year negotiation window. It's more likely to be a continuous, iterative process rather than a discrete negotiation with a clear end date within this timeframe.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Extend the timeline for negotiations to year 2-6 or year 2-8, acknowledging the complexity and lack of precedent. Add a contingency step for what happens if negotiations fail or stall, such as restricting rollout to state schools initially.

## S2.implementation_steps
Objection: The step "Ministry — build an entrenchment mechanism (e.g., a supermajority or multi-year notice requirement to alter the trajectory) to raise the cost of reversal by a successor government (timeline: year 1)" is presented as a discrete, achievable step within one year, but the scenario's `Political risks` and `Uncertainties` sections explicitly state that the "legal robustness against a determined future legislature is untested [evidence: weak]" and question "Whether a statutory entrenchment mechanism actually deters reversal, given the Polish precedent involved formal legislative repeal despite similar institutional design [confidence: low]". Building such a mechanism, especially one that is legally robust against a determined future government, is a complex legal and political challenge that cannot be guaranteed within a single year, nor is its effectiveness certain.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Revise the timeline to year 1-3 for designing and legislating the entrenchment mechanism, acknowledging its complexity and the need for robust legal drafting. Add a step to conduct a comparative legal analysis of entrenchment clauses and their reversal history to inform the design, as suggested in the `Uncertainties` section.

## S3.implementation_steps
Objection: The sequencing of "Ministry — pilot the comprehensive model itself in the same 2-3 districts, sequenced after (not concurrent with) the initial retraining cohort, to avoid running an untested model with untrained staff (timeline: year 3-5)" is too compressed. The initial retraining cohort is planned for year 1-3. Piloting the comprehensive model *after* this initial retraining, within the timeframe of year 3-5, leaves very little time for the initial cohort of trainers to actually train a sufficient number of teachers in the pilot districts before the model is implemented. Differentiated instruction is a complex pedagogical shift, and effective retraining and subsequent implementation would require more time for teachers to internalize and apply new methods before a full pilot. This timeline risks running the pilot with inadequately prepared staff, undermining the pilot's validity.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Extend the timeline for the pilot of the comprehensive model to year 4-7, allowing for a more realistic period for teacher training and preparation following the initial trainer cohort.

## S4.implementation_steps
Objection: The step "Ministry / Ministry of Finance — establish a protected or multi-year budget line for the compensation package to reduce first-cut vulnerability in fiscal consolidations (timeline: year 1, revisited each budget cycle)" is highly optimistic regarding its feasibility and effectiveness within a single year, especially given the `Political risks` section explicitly stating "Budget vulnerability: compensation lines are the first cut in fiscal consolidation... not fully mitigated by the multi-year budget line proposed... whose legal robustness against a determined future budget process is untested [evidence: weak]". Establishing a truly "protected" budget line that is immune to future fiscal consolidations is extremely difficult, if not impossible, in a single year, particularly in a context of "Hungary's specific fiscal-cycle volatility" (as noted in `Assumptions`). This step overstates the ease and certainty of securing such a budget line.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Revise the timeline to year 1-3 for establishing and attempting to protect the budget line, acknowledging the ongoing political and fiscal challenges. Add a step to conduct a detailed analysis of past budgetary commitments and their resilience to fiscal cuts in Hungary to inform the design of the protection mechanism, as suggested in the `Uncertainties` section.
