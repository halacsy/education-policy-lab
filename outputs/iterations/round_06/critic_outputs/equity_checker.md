# Critique: equity_checker
## S1.equity_impact
Objection: The scenario states that "Gains are conditional on two unresolved premises: that a workable SES indicator exists at application time, and that low-SES rural families apply into the reformed process at similar rates to how they apply today." This is a critical flaw because the scenario's core mechanism (quotas/bonus points) relies entirely on these premises. If low-SES rural families remain under-represented in the applicant pool, or if the SES indicator is unworkable, the mechanism will not achieve its stated equity goal, rendering the entire scenario ineffective for the most disadvantaged groups it aims to help. The scenario acknowledges this but does not offer a concrete plan to address it, making the equity gains highly speculative.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add a concrete implementation step or a dedicated mechanism to address the issue of low-SES rural application rates, such as targeted outreach programs, information campaigns, or financial support for application processes. Also, propose a pilot study specifically to test the validity and usability of potential SES indicators in a real-world application setting.

## S2.equity_impact
Objection: The scenario states that "Residual selective places become more SES-skewed as caps tighten... incumbent higher-SES families... are best placed to secure the shrinking number of remaining places each year, so early transition cohorts may see equity effects opposite to the scenario's stated goal before the trajectory matures." This is a severe equity risk that is acknowledged but not mitigated. The scenario's mechanism of simply reducing places, without parallel reform of the admissions process (as in S1), will exacerbate existing inequities for a significant period, potentially undermining public support for the reform and harming the very groups it aims to eventually help.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Integrate elements of S1's admission reform (e.g., less coachable assessments, SES-weighted bonus points) into S2's implementation steps, specifically for the shrinking number of selective places. This would help mitigate the increased SES-skew during the transition period and ensure that the remaining places are allocated more equitably.

## S3.equity_impact
Objection: The scenario identifies "middle-class flight to non-state or choice-sorted general schools" as the "largest risk," which would leave "the families who cannot exit (lowest-SES, least mobile, most information-poor) concentrated together in the least-resourced remaining schools, a worse local peer environment than before reform for exactly the group the reform aims to help." This is a critical, unmitigated equity-negative outcome. The scenario's core equity benefit (narrower outcome gradient) is entirely dependent on this not happening, yet the scenario's own assessment suggests it is the most likely outcome for the most vulnerable. The scenario does not propose any concrete mechanism to prevent or counteract this flight, making its equity claims highly uncertain and potentially negative.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add a dedicated mechanism or implementation step to prevent or mitigate middle-class flight. This could include: (1) strong regulatory measures for non-state schools to prevent them from becoming new selective tracks, (2) significant investment in all general schools to make them attractive to a diverse student body, including advanced programs and high-quality teachers, and (3) a robust monitoring system to track enrollment shifts and trigger corrective actions if flight occurs.

## S4.equity_impact
Objection: The scenario states that "by making the current structure more tolerable, compensation risks legitimising early selection permanently, foreclosing future structural reform options and locking in whatever residual segregation the compensation package does not reach." This is a significant, unmitigated long-term equity risk. While the scenario aims to improve outcomes in disadvantaged schools, it explicitly acknowledges that it does not address the root cause of inequity (early sorting). By making the status quo more palatable, it could entrench a system that continues to disadvantage certain groups, especially if funding is later cut, leading to a worse equilibrium.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: The scenario should include a mechanism to periodically re-evaluate the effectiveness of the compensation package in achieving genuine equity, beyond just PISA scores, and to explicitly link this evaluation to a commitment to revisit structural reforms if the compensation proves insufficient or unsustainable. This would prevent the "legitimising early selection permanently" risk by maintaining an open pathway for future structural change.
