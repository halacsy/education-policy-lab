# Policy brief — early selection and the 6/8-year gimnázium

This brief does not decide what Hungary should do with early academic selection and the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system — it accelerates the thinking and public debate that must precede that decision. It separates what we know from what we merely consider likely, maps where experts disagree and what we don't yet know, lays out the real alternatives and their prices, names what research or real deliberation could resolve, and flags which debates are red herrings. Every important claim carries an evidence status and a [fact]/[estimate]/[assumption]/[value] tag. It is decision support, not a decision.

## What we know
- In PISA, the share of reading/maths score variance explained by socio-economic status is among the highest in the OECD for Hungary (~19-25% across cycles vs ~12-14% OECD average). [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Cross-country difference-in-differences evidence indicates earlier between-school tracking increases inequality of outcomes without raising mean performance. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Roughly 8-12% of a cohort enters 6- or 8-year gimnazium tracks, selected at age 10 or 12 largely via the central written admission examination; entrants are strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Poland's 1999 reform postponed tracking to age 15 (gimnazjum); PISA scores rose substantially by 2012, with analyses attributing part of the gain to delayed selection. The reform was reversed in 2016-2019 for political reasons. [fact] (evidence: moderate)
- Annual births fell from ~125k (early 1990s) to ~85-95k; cohort shrinkage forces school-network consolidation decisions in the 2020s-2030s regardless of selection policy. [fact] (evidence: strong)

## What we consider likely
- The Hungarian SES gradient is consistent with the causal tracking evidence, but country-level causality is an inference, not a measured fact. [estimate]
- The gimnázium tracks' raw advantage is mostly selection effect; their causal value added is modest. [estimate, based on moderate evidence]

## Where experts disagree
### Structural vs. non-structural reform
- **equity_and_social_mobility, polish_reform, international_comparison**: Delayed selection (structural change) is necessary; compensation alone cannot offset sorting this early. [value] Why: Cross-country tracking evidence and the Hungarian SES gradient.
- **portuguese_reform, political_feasibility** (minority): A non-structural package (standards, targeted support) is preferable: comparable gains, far lower political risk. [value] Why: Portugal's improvement without changing selection age; Poland's reversal.
### Speed of any phase-out
- **equity_and_social_mobility** (minority): Cap new entries immediately; every delayed cohort pays the equity cost. [value] Why: Each admission cycle sorts ~10% of a cohort irreversibly.
- **implementation_planning, school_network_planning, legal_and_governance**: A sub-6-year transition is operationally and legally infeasible; forcing it guarantees chaotic failure. [value] Why: Teacher supply, felmenő rendszer guarantees, network re-planning lead times.
### What the gimnazium forms actually produce
- **equity_and_social_mobility, international_comparison**: Mostly selection effect; modest causal value added does not justify the system-wide equity cost. [value] Why: Hungarian value-added studies.
- **political_feasibility, hungarian_education_system** (minority): Even if gains are partly positional, the forms answer a real demand for demanding academic tracks; suppressing them shifts demand to private exit. [value] Why: Parental attachment evidence; growth of non-state maintainers.
### Bundling with network consolidation
- **demography, school_network_planning**: Bundle selection reform with the demography-forced network redesign to share transition costs. [value] Why: Cohort shrinkage forces consolidation regardless.
- **political_feasibility** (minority): Bundling couples the reform to unpopular school closures and doubles the opposition coalition. [value] Why: Closure politics historically dominates local media cycles.

## What we don't know
- Whether institutional capacity for a structural reform can be built within a decade is unresolved. [assumption]
- Whether published intake data would stay accurate and politically survivable is untested. [assumption]
- S1: Behavioural response of elite schools (informal selection channels). [assumption]
- S2: Whether general-school programmes actually retain high-achieving families. [assumption]

## What could be done
- **S1 — Admission reform within the current structure**: Reduce the SES bias of entry into 6- and 8-year gimnazium tracks without changing the structure, by reforming the admission examination and admission rules.
- **S2 — Gradual phase-down of 6/8-year entry places**: Shrink early-selective intake over a decade by capping and gradually reducing entry places, while investing the freed capacity in strong programmes inside general schools.
- **S3 — Comprehensive school to age 14 (structural reform)**: End between-school academic selection before age 14 by phasing out 6- and 8-year gimnazium entry, moving first selection to the end of grade 8, with differentiated teaching inside a common school.
- **S4 — Keep the structure, compensate the general schools**: Accept early selection as politically fixed and offset its equity costs by a Portuguese-style package: targeted funding, teacher incentives and support programmes for general schools serving disadvantaged pupils.

## What each option costs
- **S1**: Directly targets the entry gate, so gains accrue to high-ability low-SES pupils; does nothing for the majority who remain in general schools, and may relabel rather than reduce sorting if compliance is weak. [value] — One-off: assessment redesign and piloting (low, central budget) [estimate] — Elite-school and middle-class parent opposition to lottery elements; framed as 'punishing talent'. [estimate]
- **S2**: Benefits the non-selected majority through improved peer composition and reinvested capacity; the residual selective places become scarcer and, without S1-type admission reform, even more SES-skewed. [value] — Recurring: advanced-programme funding in general schools (medium, formula-based) [estimate] — A successor government can freeze or reverse the trajectory cheaply (Polish precedent). [estimate]
- **S3**: The largest expected equity gain of all scenarios — and the largest risk: if middle-class families exit to non-state schools, segregation could worsen relative to baseline. [value] — One-off: nationwide teacher retraining for differentiation (high) [estimate] — Highest-intensity opposition of all scenarios: mobilised parents, elite schools, parts of the teaching profession. [estimate]
- **S4**: Helps the disadvantaged majority where they actually are; leaves the sorting mechanism and its signalling effects intact, so the SES gradient narrows at best partially. [value] — Recurring: targeted school funding formula (high — this is the package) [estimate] — Budget vulnerability: compensation lines are the first cut in fiscal consolidation. [estimate]

## What research could resolve
- Do not choose between scenarios yet: commission the S1 admission pilot and the S4 compensation pilot immediately — they are cheap, reversible, and their results discriminate between the structural and non-structural paths.
- Treat S3 (comprehensive school to 14) as the reference structural option, but gate any legislation on the pilot evaluations, a teacher-capacity audit, and a cross-party feasibility test.
- Whatever path is chosen, publish annual intake-composition data by track and maintainer: every scenario's evaluation depends on it and it costs almost nothing.

## What people must decide
- How should equity gains be weighed against risks to high-achiever outcomes and against parental choice? This weighting determines the S3-vs-S4 ranking and is a values question the system must not answer. [value]
- Is a Polish-style reversal risk acceptable if the expected equity gain is large? Only an accountable decision-maker can accept this risk. [value]
- Is early selection genuinely politically immovable, or is that assumption itself a choice? The devil_advocate flagged this as a foreclosing premise. [value]
- Hungarian 'egységes alapiskola' carries historical connotations (1970s-80s reform debates) that 'comprehensive school' lacks; a native policy editor should confirm register in public-facing texts. [value]
