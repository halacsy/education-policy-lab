# Policy brief — The Future of Rural Primary Schools

Hungary's rural primary schools face a demographic and structural crisis: collapsing birth rates, over 1,200 schoolless settlements, and acute rural teacher shortages are colliding with a governance system that gives municipalities and parents little formal say over closure. Twelve expert analyses and a wide range of stakeholder voices agree the status quo is unsustainable in many villages within 5-10 years, but disagree sharply on whether the answer is retention, restructuring, networked models, consolidation, or a transparency framework — and on whether keeping small schools open helps or harms equity. This brief lays out five policy scenarios, what is known and unknown about each, where experts and stakeholders diverge, and the value choices that remain for decision-makers to make explicitly rather than by default.

## Scenario key (full detail: scenarios.en.md)
- S1 — S1 — Status quo plus incremental adaptation: keep the village school open via multigrade classes and gradual phase-out, without a national framework.
- S2 — S2 — Structural grade reorganization: lower grades stay in the village, upper grades centralize into a town-based school (Polish gimnazjum-style template).
- S3 — S3 — Networked retention: inter-municipal clustering, association/church-run schools, or multi-service hubs to keep village schools viable.
- S4 — S4 — Consolidation with mitigation: closure as the default, paired with statutory transport guarantees and TEIP-style targeted support.
- S5 — S5 — National systemic framework: transparent viability thresholds, a closure/outcome registry, and standardized teacher training to inform (not dictate) local decisions.

## What we know
- Hungary's rural primary schools face a structural demographic crisis — collapsing birth rates, shrinking cohorts, and accelerating rural population decline — that is not cyclical and will intensify over the coming decade. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Rural and disadvantaged-area schools face disproportionately severe teacher shortages, with older and less qualified staff concentrated there relative to urban or advantaged schools. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Cross-national quasi-experimental evidence (Denmark, Finland, China) shows school closures are linked to — and in several designs causally linked to — local population decline and/or reduced student achievement, with effects sometimes emerging only years after closure. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- No authoritative statutory minimum enrollment threshold exists in Hungarian law for launching a single-grade class; the widely-cited ~14-pupil figure is unconfirmed community knowledge, not legal text. [fact] (evidence: weak)
- Hungarian law currently gives parents only 'interest injury' (érdeksérelem), not enforceable 'legal injury' (jogsérelem) standing, to challenge a school-closure decision; closure remains the maintainer's exclusive statutory prerogative under Nkt. §102(2). [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Portugal's rigid 2005-2007 enrollment-threshold closures (1,615 schools) showed no correlation between concentrating students in larger 'school centers' and improved educational success over a 2005-2021 longitudinal study. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Portugal's TEIP targeted-support programme reduced dropout and failure rates in targeted schools over multiple cycles, yet the same evaluation body found it failed over 15 years to close the overall performance gap with non-TEIP schools. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Poland's 1999 gimnazjum structural reform, which centralized upper grades into town-based institutions, was later reversed (2017-2019) without clear evidence of net academic benefit. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Poland's post-1990 association/NGO-run 'social schools' rescued or established roughly 500-600 rural schools, but even under such management 269 of 1,076 rural primary schools in Małopolska fell below the ~70-student sustainability threshold. [fact] (evidence: moderate)
- Hungary's post-2013 centralization of school maintenance into tankerületi központok removed municipal maintainer authority over most rural primary schools, a governance barrier to inter-municipal or association-run network models that was not present in the Polish or French comparators when they adopted such models. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Multigrade classroom pedagogy, when paired with adequate teacher training and institutional support, can deliver high-standard education and reduce regional inequalities, per a 2025 international systematic review of 40 studies. [fact] (evidence: moderate)
- Nearly 300 'ghetto schools' (over 50% Roma pupils) exist in Hungary, three-quarters of them in villages — showing rural small schools, not urban ones, are the primary site of extreme segregation. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- School transport is a major, often underfunded cost and logistical burden in rural school-network decisions; rural per-student bus costs run roughly 40% higher than urban, and commute-time increases beyond 30 minutes are independently linked to worse academic, social, and wellbeing outcomes. [fact] (evidence: moderate)
- Implementation of school-network reduction in Hungary is currently proceeding via gradual 'phase-out' (halting new first-grade enrollment, letting cohorts finish) rather than abrupt closure, partly to avoid the civic backlash seen in the mid-2000s consolidation wave. [fact] (evidence: moderate)

## What we consider likely
- Without systemic teacher-training reform, multigrade classroom viability in Hungary will likely erode further as the rural teaching workforce ages and unqualified-teacher rates in disadvantaged rural schools remain 2-3x the national average. [estimate]
- A rural per-pupil funding-weight multiplier analogous to Poland's could partially offset the structural cost diseconomy of small schools, but is unlikely by itself to prevent the poorest, lowest-capacity municipalities from being excluded from networked models without dedicated capacity-building support. [estimate]
- Displacement to a consolidated center school will likely not, on its own, move most affected pupils to demonstrably higher-quality schools, based on the international pattern documented across the dossier's comparator cases. [estimate]
- An advisory, non-binding national registry and threshold framework is likely to be selectively invoked to legitimize closure decisions already made on fiscal or administrative grounds, absent independent audit and binding force. [estimate]
- Long-run population-decline effects of closures, which in Danish/Finnish evidence emerge significantly only after 5-6 years, are likely to create an accountability mismatch in Hungary, where decision-makers approving closures will not bear the visible consequences within their own tenure. [estimate]

## Where experts disagree
### Does keeping small rural schools open reduce or worsen educational inequity?
- **hungarian_education_system, demography, school_network_planning, polish_reform, portuguese_reform, finnish_reform**: Small rural schools serve an irreplaceable equity and community-anchoring function; closure accelerates depopulation and disproportionately harms disadvantaged, commuting, and Roma pupils who lack alternatives. [value] Why: Supported by cross-national quasi-experimental evidence (Denmark, Finland, China, Portugal) showing closures causally depress local population and achievement, and by Hungarian demographic data showing disadvantage concentrating in schoolless settlements.
- **equity_and_social_mobility** (minority): Keeping small village schools open, absent regulation of school-choice exit (white flight to church/urban schools), can itself deepen segregation by concentrating disadvantaged and Roma pupils in the schools left behind ('ghetto schools'); closure is not automatically the equity-harming option. [value] Why: Nearly 300 'ghetto schools' (over 50% Roma pupils) exist in Hungary, three-quarters in villages, showing rural small schools — not urban ones — are the primary site of extreme segregation; church-school expansion draws non-Roma pupils away, deepening residual concentration.
### Is school closure justified primarily by demographic/fiscal necessity, or is it a policy choice that outpaces genuine need?
- **school_network_planning, demography, finnish_reform**: Demographic collapse makes phase-out closures economically and demographically inevitable in many villages within 5-10 years, regardless of governance design. [value] Why: Specific micro-regions already show birth cohorts too small to sustain even multi-grade classes; Finland's own demographic collapse is driving comparable closures despite a historically protective small-school culture.
- **polish_reform, education_finance, legal_and_governance** (minority): Closure often outpaces demographic necessity because of policy design (funding-formula incentives, governance centralization, weak procedural oversight), not enrollment decline alone; the financial case for closure is frequently weak once full costs are counted. [value] Why: Poland's school network contracted 17% while pupils fell only 3%; Hungarian per-pupil cost disadvantages are structural but transport, capital, and lagged demographic costs typically exceed salary savings from consolidation.
### Should closure decision-making be centralized (state/district level) or decentralized (municipal/local level)?
- **hungarian_education_system, legal_and_governance, political_feasibility**: Centralized tankerületi központ authority since 2013/2017 has stripped municipalities and parents of decisive influence over closure, reducing local responsiveness; decentralization (or at minimum binding local consultation) is needed to protect territorial access rights. [value] Why: Under Nkt. §102(2), closure is exclusively the maintainer's prerogative; communities suffer only an 'interest injury,' not a legal injury, with no formal appeal.
- **education_finance** (minority): Returning full financial autonomy/maintainer responsibility to individual municipalities could impose higher administrative unit costs than the current centralized model, and poorer municipalities explicitly cannot self-finance operating costs. [value] Why: A 2026 mayoral survey found wealthier municipalities welcomed returned responsibility while poorer settlements cited inability to self-finance since the 2012 state takeover.
### Are multigrade/small-school pedagogical alternatives a genuinely viable substitute for consolidation, or a stopgap of limited scalability?
- **international_comparison, educational_psychology, finnish_reform**: Multigrade classrooms, operated to minimum quality standards with adequate teacher training and institutional support, can deliver high-standard education and reduce regional inequalities, offering a genuine evidence-backed alternative to closure. [value] Why: A 2025 systematic review of 40 international studies found multigrade classrooms viable under quality conditions; small schools show no consistent achievement disadvantage but significantly better self-esteem, safety, and school climate.
- **implementation_planning, school_network_planning** (minority): The 'traveling teacher' model and multigrade expansion are operationally unsustainable at scale in the current Hungarian context, given acute teacher shortages and a four-year training pipeline lag; alternatives are stopgaps, not durable substitutes. [value] Why: Trade-union officials and school directors report the traveling-teacher model as operationally unsustainable and disliked; specific micro-regions already face birth cohorts too small for single-grade viability even under multi-grade clustering rules.
### Is the church-maintainer transfer pathway (fenntartóváltás) a legitimate rescue mechanism or a problematic privatization/asset-loss dynamic?
- **legal_and_governance, polish_reform**: Maintainer-change to church, private, or NGO status is a legally permitted pathway that has kept schools open, representing institutional adaptation rather than pure loss. [value] Why: Documented survival cases exist; Hungarian law requires majority parental support for such transfers, providing at least a nominal safeguard.
- **hungarian_education_system** (minority): The 2023 property-transfer law allowing churches to claim state/municipal buildings free of charge, with no local discretion, represents a substantial fiscal and asset transfer from municipalities, raising equity and governance concerns distinct from mere school survival. [value] Why: Municipalities cite substantial documented asset losses with no local discretion in the process, and church schools receive an additional ~50% operational-budget supplement, shifting public resources toward non-state providers.

## What we don't know
- Whether multigrade pedagogy in Hungarian rural schools can achieve outcomes comparable to international evidence, given local teacher-training curricula and resource availability — no Hungarian pilot with matched comparison groups yet exists. [fact]
- No Hungary-specific quasi-experimental (difference-in-differences or equivalent causal) study exists isolating the effect of rural school closure on local population decline or student achievement; all causal claims for Hungary rely on extrapolation from other countries. [fact]
- Whether an advisory, non-binding national registry and threshold framework would be used neutrally by tankerületi központok or selectively invoked to justify pre-determined closure decisions is not yet tested in the Hungarian context. [assumption]
- Whether Hungary's poorest, lowest-capacity municipalities would be systematically excluded from networked/association-run models due to administrative burden is an open empirical question; no Hungarian pilot data yet exists. [assumption]
- The actual cost and safety adequacy of scaled rural transport for daily upper-primary commuting (S2) is unquantified at the national level; no national household-level transport-cost and safety audit currently exists. [fact]
- Whether Hungary's central government would legally permit and sustain a devolved association-run/network school category, given the post-2013 centralization trend and demonstrated coercive reversal capacity (2023 building-transfer law), is unknown. [assumption]
- Whether TEIP-style targeted support, if implemented in the Hungarian consolidated-school context, would replicate Portugal's partial dropout/failure reduction or its persistent 15-year performance-gap failure, is untested. [assumption]
- The magnitude and timing of long-run community population decline following Hungarian rural school closures is unmeasured; Danish/Finnish effect sizes may not transfer directly to the Hungarian context. [fact]

## What could be done
- **S1 — Status quo plus incremental adaptation**: Keep the village school open through multigrade classrooms and gradual felmenő rendszer phase-out, relying on local negotiation rather than a national framework. This preserves community continuity and avoids abrupt-closure conflict, but leaves multigrade quality dependent on a currently unfunded teacher-training reform and an aging, undersupplied rural teaching workforce.
- **S2 — Structural grade reorganization**: Keep lower grades in the village, centralize upper grades into a town-based ISCED2 school following the Polish structural-reform template. Reduces fixed-cost diseconomy for upper-grade subject teaching, but imposes daily commutes on 9-10-year-olds and relies on a Polish precedent that was itself later reversed without clear net benefit.
- **S3 — Networked retention**: Retain village schools through inter-municipal pedagogical networks, association/church-run publicly-subsidized schools, or multi-service hubs, drawing on Polish and French precedents. Offers a genuine local-retention alternative but faces a documented Hungarian legal barrier (post-2013 centralization) and a real risk of excluding the poorest, lowest-capacity municipalities without dedicated support.
- **S4 — Consolidation with mitigation**: Close low-enrollment village schools by default, reassigning pupils to a consolidated center school, paired with statutory transport guarantees and TEIP-style targeted support. Addresses structural cost diseconomy directly, but its own cited compensatory mechanism (TEIP) is documented as only partially effective, and Hungarian parents currently lack binding legal appeal rights against closure.
- **S5 — National systemic framework**: Establish a national framework of transparent minimum-viability thresholds, cost-visibility rules, teacher-training standardization, and a closure/outcome registry to inform local decisions across any of S1-S4. Fills a documented evidentiary gap, but its own evidence status is weak on whether an advisory (non-binding) version actually changes closure outcomes rather than being selectively invoked.

## What each option costs
- **S1**: S1 entrenches — rather than resolves — Hungary's dominant rural equity problem: multigrade classrooms taught by unqualified or overstretched teachers risk delivering lower-quality education precisely to the disadvantaged pupils who cannot exit via school choice, while higher-SES families exit to church or urban schools, concentrating disadvantage in the villages this scenario aims to protect. [value]
- **S1**: Recurring costs include multigrade teacher-training and in-service upskilling (years 1-4 then ongoing), capital-maintenance backlog and stranded-asset risk from EU-financed retrofits, and teacher-retention incentives currently funded at only a small fraction of documented need. [estimate]
- **S2**: S2 shifts the travel burden onto upper-primary pupils who are disproportionately from lower-income and Roma families already facing much higher early-school-leaving rates; without a statutory transport guarantee, the commute burden falls hardest on families least able to absorb it, risking a widened rather than narrowed dropout gap. [value]
- **S2**: One-off capital investment for ISCED2 receiving-site capacity is major and currently unestimated; recurring rural transport costs run roughly 40% above urban levels, with a single precedent route costing ~35 million HUF/year for ~50 students — teachers displaced from village schools also bear severance/redeployment costs, currently unbudgeted. [estimate]
- **S3**: Administrative-capacity burdens (grant applications, compliance, governance boards) may systematically exclude precisely the villages most in need, since fiscally exhausted municipalities already lack capacity even under the current centralized system; without targeted support, S3 risks a two-tier outcome — better-resourced villages network successfully, the most disadvantaged do not. [value]
- **S3**: One-off legal/regulatory design cost to create a Hungarian association-run school category is currently unquantified since no such category exists; recurring costs include a rural per-pupil funding-weight subsidy (not yet designed or costed) and dedicated capacity-building support for the poorest municipalities to prevent exclusion. [estimate]
- **S4**: Closure-as-default disproportionately affects lower-income and Roma families who cannot easily relocate or absorb commute burdens; the scenario's own cited evidence shows TEIP reduced dropout/failure yet failed over 15 years to close the underlying performance gap, meaning closure paired with mitigation still likely produces a net equity cost for the most disadvantaged families relative to well-resourced retention alternatives. [value]
- **S4**: Recurring statutory transport costs run ~40% above urban rates; TEIP-style support and destination-school capacity add further recurring costs; Portugal's experience shows ~20% of closed school buildings remain vacant absent a repurposing mandate, and long-run community fiscal effects (tax-base shrinkage, property-value decline) are largely unmonetized in current Hungarian budget analyses. [estimate]
- **S5**: A non-binding, advisory registry and threshold system, absent enforcement teeth, risks being used selectively to legitimize pre-determined closure decisions rather than genuinely informing them, since Hungarian law already treats closure as the maintainer's exclusive discretionary prerogative — transparency without binding force or Roma/poverty-tier disaggregation risks producing transparency without accountability. [value]
- **S5**: One-off registry-design and data-infrastructure build cost (including GDPR-equivalent compliance) is unquantified but comparable Hungarian data-transparency projects have taken multiple years; fiscal responsibility for ongoing registry funding is unclear across government tiers, risking underfunding or discontinuation absent an explicit budget line. [estimate]

## What research could resolve
- A Hungary-specific pilot with matched-comparison villages tracking achievement, attendance, and dropout before and after multigrade adoption or upper-grade centralization would resolve whether these models produce comparable or net-positive outcomes in the Hungarian context, rather than relying on extrapolation from Poland, Portugal, Denmark, or Finland.
- A national household-level transport-cost and safety audit of routes serving villages under 2,000 population, cross-referenced with cancelled-route case data, would quantify the actual cost and safety adequacy of any consolidation or centralization model at national scale.
- Post-implementation tracking of tankerület closure decisions against registry data and thresholds over 3-5 years, with independent audit of decision rationales, would resolve whether a national transparency framework changes actual outcomes or is selectively invoked.
- Pilot programme evaluation disaggregating uptake and outcome by municipal fiscal-capacity tier, with qualitative interviews on administrative-burden barriers, would show whether networked/association-run models systematically exclude the poorest municipalities.
- A Hungary-specific difference-in-differences study comparing closure and non-closure villages over a 10+ year window, tracking population, migration, and housing-price trajectories, would establish whether Danish/Finnish closure-depopulation effect sizes transfer to the Hungarian context.
- A formal legal-feasibility and timeline assessment by the data-protection authority and KSH, published before registry-design finalization, would clarify whether disaggregated closure/outcome data collection is achievable within a realistic timeframe and legal framework.

## What people must decide
- Whether the primary value priority is preserving local community continuity and access (favoring S1/S3) or achieving structural cost-efficiency and concentrated resource quality (favoring S2/S4) — these are not reconcilable through evidence alone, since both goals are legitimate and the trade-off between them is a political choice. [value]
- Whether Hungary is willing to legislate binding legal appeal rights for parents (replacing the current 'interest injury' standard) before any closure proceeds, accepting the political and administrative cost of slower, contestable decision-making in exchange for procedural fairness. [value]
- Whether a national registry and threshold framework should be made legally binding on maintainer discretion, or remain advisory — a decision that determines whether central government is willing to cede some final closure authority to an external, evidence-based standard. [value]
- Whether public funds should be committed to a sustained, multi-year rural teacher-training and retention reform before or concurrently with any structural school-network change, given that no scenario's success is independent of teacher supply. [value]
- Whether to accept the equity trade-off documented across multiple scenarios — that both retention (S1/S3, risking segregation entrenchment) and closure (S2/S4, risking disproportionate harm to Roma and low-income families) carry genuine equity costs, meaning no option is equity-neutral and a conscious choice about which harm is more tolerable is unavoidable. [value]

## What to verify with real stakeholders
- A1 (Multigrade teaching is a demanding, specialized skill, and support for keeping village schools open via multigrade classes should be conditional on mandatory, funded multigrade-pedagogy training and workload/pay adjustment, not assumed to happen automatically.): policy_design_fixable — The dossier itself identifies this gap (no funded systemic training reform accompanies phase-out); a design fix — mandatory, funded multigrade training as a recurring budget line paired with workload/pay adjustment before phase-out proceeds — directly addresses the concern without requiring new evidence or an irreducible value trade-off.
- A2 (Multigrade classes and phased enrollment respect subsidiarity and allow gradual local adaptation, preserving the village school as an anchor of community continuity.): value_conflict — This is a genuine value position (subsidiarity, community continuity) weighed against teaching-quality risk under an unresourced multigrade model; whether gradual local adaptation is preferable to decisive restructuring is a value choice about what a school is for, not a factual question evidence alone resolves.
- A3 (Without a national framework, state tankerület offices could quietly favour their own schools' survival over church-maintained schools in the same catchment, denying them equivalent institutional autonomy or transition support.): needs_more_info — The dossier itself labels this pattern 'contested and largely unknown' — no audited catchment-level case comparisons exist showing systematic favoritism. This is not yet decidable from available evidence, though the concern is legitimate and worth monitoring; published, non-discretionary viability criteria applied equally across maintainer types would be a reasonable policy-design response once the pattern is verified or disproven.
- A4 (Forcing upper-primary pupils into daily commutes to centralized ISCED2 sites risks child safety and educational continuity; this should not proceed without a legally binding, funded transport guarantee with verified route safety established before any transfer.): policy_design_fixable — A design change — sequencing so that a legislated, funded, independently verified transport guarantee is operational before any pupil transfer, rather than concurrently — directly addresses the documented harm (30-minute-plus commute correlation with lower achievement, documented route cancellations) without requiring new evidence about whether the harm exists.
- A5 (Consolidation should not proceed without binding, pre-negotiated redeployment terms guaranteeing severance, retraining, housing/transport support, and a specialization-wage supplement for relocated teachers.): policy_design_fixable — The dossier already documents that redeployment/severance costs are one-off and currently unbudgeted; making binding redeployment terms, statutory wage supplements, and union consultation a precondition of consolidation is a concrete design fix, not a value dispute — Poland's own reversed reform underscores the cost of skipping this sequencing.
- A6 (Centralizing upper grades without a pre-funded, legally guaranteed transport scheme would widen the already documented dropout gap between Roma and non-Roma pupils.): policy_design_fixable — The existing dropout gap and unsubsidized transport precarity are documented facts; the projected widening under S2 without guarantees is a plausible but unmeasured causal claim, addressable by the same design fix as A4 — a pre-funded, statutory transport guarantee verified operational before transfer, ideally paired with disaggregated monitoring by Roma/non-Roma composition.
- A7 (S2's ISCED2 consolidation model is built on a Polish template that Poland itself later reversed without demonstrated net academic benefit, undermining the evidentiary basis for structural centralization.): evidence_answerable — Whether the Polish reversal is causally attributable to academic failure versus political factors, and whether Polish conditions transfer to Hungary, is precisely the kind of question a Hungary-specific matched-comparison pilot could meaningfully clarify — this is not an irreducible value conflict but an open empirical question with a feasible research path.
- A8 (Consolidation could improve access to qualified STEM/ICT specialists, but only if receiving ISCED2 sites are pre-certified for digital infrastructure and qualified-teacher capacity before any transfer.): policy_design_fixable — A pre-certification requirement (verified digital-infrastructure and ICT-teacher capacity audit before transfer) is a straightforward design condition that can be attached to any consolidation implementation plan, converting an unverified assumption into a testable precondition.
- A9 (Network and association-run school models are a legitimate extension of Hungary's existing plural-maintainer legal framework, restoring local, church, and community responsibility for education.): value_conflict — Whether restoring local/church/association authority over schools should take priority over centralized state oversight is a genuine subsidiarity-versus-centralization value question; the dossier confirms plural maintainership is a legal fact, but whether it *should* be expanded is a political and value choice, not something evidence alone settles.
- A10 (Without ring-fenced capacity-building funding targeting the poorest municipalities, network/association models risk a two-tier system that excludes exactly the lowest-capacity, often Roma-concentrated villages the reform claims to help.): policy_design_fixable — The Małopolska evidence documents this exclusion risk under comparable conditions; a design fix — statutory, ring-fenced capacity-building funds pre-allocated to the lowest-capacity municipalities, with uptake monitored by poverty tier — directly targets the mechanism of exclusion rather than requiring a value trade-off.
- A11 (Governance experimentation should not shift administrative burden or employment uncertainty onto already-stretched teaching staff or the poorest municipalities' capacity, so pilots need explicit employment-continuity guarantees and dedicated administrative-capacity funding.): policy_design_fixable — This is a concrete implementation-design request (employment-continuity guarantees, dedicated funding for administrative burden) attachable to any S3 pilot design; it does not require new evidence to resolve, only a design commitment before pilots launch.
- A12 (Hungary's post-2013 centralization removed municipal authority and is a documented legal barrier that S3 must explicitly overcome through parliamentary legislation, not assume away.): policy_design_fixable — The legal barrier is a documented fact, and the fix — explicit legislative restoration of municipal/association authority as a precondition of S3 implementation — is a concrete, though politically demanding, design step rather than an unresolvable conflict; feasibility and timeline remain open questions but the mechanism to address it is clear.
- A13 (The scenario's own evidence labels Portugal's TEIP-style compensatory mechanism 'contested': it reduced dropout but failed over 15 years to close the underlying performance gap, so relying on it to offset closure harm is an unverified promise, not a confirmed safeguard.): irreducible_tradeoff — This is a genuine irreducible trade-off: proceeding with consolidation now trades a documented structural cost-diseconomy for a compensatory mechanism the dossier's own evidence confirms is only partially effective — no policy design fully closes this gap, since even a well-implemented TEIP-equivalent has a 15-year track record of partial, not complete, success. The honest answer is that closure-with-mitigation accepts a residual, unresolved harm in exchange for cost savings.
- A14 (Hungarian parents currently hold only non-binding 'interest injury' standing, not enforceable legal injury, to challenge a school-closure decision, treating families as having no real legal recourse against a maintainer's unilateral prerogative.): policy_design_fixable — Legal feasibility analysis by education-law scholars of amending Nkt. §102(2) to create an enforceable parental appeal right is explicitly identified as a research/legal path forward; this is a legislative design gap with a known remedy pathway (comparative reference to Poland's binding closure-review procedures exists), not an irreducible conflict, though the political will to act is a separate, harder question.
- A15 (Closure should not proceed without pre-verified, independently audited destination-school quality, a legislated and pre-funded transport guarantee, and binding parental/union appeal rights established before, not concurrently with, any closure decision.): policy_design_fixable — This convergent, seven-cluster demand is a coherent sequencing and design requirement — pre-closure independent quality audits, pre-funded transport, and binding appeal rights — that can be written into implementation steps as non-negotiable preconditions; it is answerable by policy design even though whether such preconditions change outcomes in practice remains an open empirical question worth monitoring.
- A16 (Displaced pupils internationally most often do not move to demonstrably stronger schools, and Portugal's 2005-2007 closures found no link between larger 'school centers' and better outcomes, undermining the core assumption that consolidation improves quality.): evidence_answerable — Whether Hungarian consolidation would replicate this international pattern or differ is directly testable via a pre-closure independent quality audit of destination schools and post-transfer outcome tracking — this is an empirical question with a clear, feasible research design already named in the dossier's uncertainty list.
- A17 (Church maintainers already have a statutory 2023 building-claim mechanism for public education buildings, yet the scenario's mandatory repurposing-plan step names only the local municipality as responsible, omitting church and other plural maintainers as eligible partners.): policy_design_fixable — This is a straightforward implementation-step gap: explicitly including church and other plural maintainers as eligible partners in the repurposing-plan process is a concrete design amendment that integrates the existing statutory claim mechanism rather than leaving it as a parallel, potentially conflicting track.
- A18 (Multigrade phase-out lets government offices manage rural school decline gradually and without a single dramatic closure decision, matching a governing preference for incremental, low-conflict reform.): not_decision_relevant — Whether phase-out is politically more convenient than abrupt closure is a governance-tactics observation about implementation style, not a claim that would change the underlying choice among S1-S5; it consumes attention on political framing rather than bearing on which scenario's substantive trade-offs are preferable.
- A19 (A governing majority that adopted incremental phase-out language specifically to avoid mobilization dynamics would reject naming closure as the explicit default policy, since political exposure would not be matched by full substantive justification.): not_decision_relevant — This is a political-communication observation about framing and language choice, not a substantive claim about which scenario best serves pupils or communities; it would not change the underlying trade-off analysis between S1-S5, only how a chosen policy is publicly described.
- A20 (A national registry and transparent viability thresholds fill a currently named evidentiary gap, but the framework must be made legally binding, with independent audit, to avoid becoming a tool for legitimizing pre-decided closures.): policy_design_fixable — Bindingness plus independent audit is a concrete, specifiable design choice (as opposed to an advisory-only framework); while the dossier notes even binding frameworks (Poland) show enforcement variation, the design fix directly targets the named risk mechanism and is implementable through legislation and an oversight body.
- A21 (The registry's disaggregation is currently specified only by settlement, Roma/non-Roma composition, and poverty tier; without adding maintainer type and digital-infrastructure/ICT-teacher-qualification indicators, church networks, digital-sector partners, and equity observers cannot verify equitable treatment.): needs_more_info — Whether adding maintainer-type and digital-infrastructure indicators is technically and legally feasible without breaching data-protection constraints (especially small-settlement re-identification risk) is not yet resolved in the dossier; a formal data-protection feasibility assessment is needed before this design change can be committed to.
- A22 (An advisory, non-binding registry that informs rather than dictates matches a governing preference for evidence infrastructure that improves consultation quality without ceding final closure-decision authority to a legally binding external standard.): value_conflict — Whether central/local maintainers should retain full discretion or cede some authority to a binding external standard is precisely the value tension named across A20/A21/A22 — administrative flexibility versus binding accountability — and is a legitimate, unresolved political choice about how much authority a maintainer should retain, not a factual dispute evidence can settle.

## Where the red herrings are
- A18: Whether phase-out language is politically more convenient than naming closure explicitly consumes significant attention as a framing debate but would not change which of S1-S5's substantive trade-offs (equity, cost, transport, teacher supply) is preferable — the underlying scenario choice remains the same regardless of what the policy is called.
- A19: Debating whether a governing majority would explicitly name closure as a 'default' policy is a communications-strategy question that consumes attention but does not alter the substantive equity, cost, or teacher-supply trade-offs that determine which scenario best serves affected communities.

- (implementation_planning, school_network_planning) The 'traveling teacher' model and multigrade expansion are operationally unsustainable at scale in the current Hungarian context, given acute teacher shortages and a four-year training pipeline lag; alternatives are stopgaps, not durable substitutes. — Trade-union officials and school directors report the traveling-teacher model as operationally unsustainable and disliked; specific micro-regions already face birth cohorts too small for single-grade viability even under multi-grade clustering rules.
- (hungarian_education_system) The 2023 property-transfer law allowing churches to claim state/municipal buildings free of charge, with no local discretion, represents a substantial fiscal and asset transfer from municipalities, raising equity and governance concerns distinct from mere school survival. — Municipalities cite substantial documented asset losses with no local discretion in the process, and church schools receive an additional ~50% operational-budget supplement (~5 billion HUF annual system-wide cost), shifting public resources toward non-state providers.
