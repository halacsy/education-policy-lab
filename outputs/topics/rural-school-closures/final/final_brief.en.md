# Policy brief — The Future of Rural Primary Schools

Rural Hungary's smallest primary schools face a structural squeeze: demographic decline is shrinking enrollment, a 2013 centralization moved funding and closure authority to the state, and no coordinated national framework tells communities which of several possible paths — status-quo adaptation, structural reorganization, networked alternatives, consolidation, or systemic reform — best fits their situation. This brief lays out five policy scenarios, what the evidence does and does not support, where genuine value conflicts remain unresolved, and every stakeholder argument raised in this round's deliberation, none of them erased or smoothed into false consensus.

## Scenario key (full detail: scenarios.en.md)
- S1 — Status quo plus incremental adaptation: keep the school open via multigrade classes and gradual phase-out, negotiated locally without a national framework.
- S2 — Structural grade reorganization: lower grades stay in the village, upper grades (ages 10-15) centralize in a larger town, following the Polish 1999 pattern.
- S3 — Networked retention: inter-municipal clustering, association-run schools, or multi-service hubs as a middle path between closure and unaided retention.
- S4 — Consolidation with mitigation: close low-enrollment schools by default, reassign pupils to a center school, paired with transport guarantees and targeted (TEIP-style) support.
- S5 — National systemic framework: transparent viability thresholds, cost-visibility rules, teacher-training standardization, and a closure/outcome registry to inform (not dictate) local decisions.

## What we know
- Rural Hungarian primary school enrollment decline is driven primarily by demographic depopulation (out-migration of working-age families, low fertility), not by a single structural feature of the education system. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Hungary's 2013 centralization (Klebelsberg Center/KLIK and successor tankerületi structures) shifted primary school operating funding and closure authority from municipalities to the state, changing the locus of decision-making without resolving underlying viability pressures. [fact] (evidence: moderate)
- No centralized, publicly available Hungarian registry exists that tracks school closure decisions alongside longitudinal community, demographic, or educational-outcome data. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Multigrade teaching (összevont osztály) is an established, legally permitted alternative to closure in Hungary, but its cost-effectiveness and pedagogical outcomes remain largely unquantified. [fact] (evidence: weak)
- Post-closure transportation and commuting burdens fall disproportionately on younger children and lower-socioeconomic-status families, who have less access to private transport and bear higher relative costs. [fact] (evidence: moderate)
- International alternative models to binary closure/retention exist (Finnish system-wide support layers, French RPI clustering, Polish association-run schools, Scottish statutory anti-closure presumption), but their transferability to the Hungarian institutional context is not empirically demonstrated. [fact] (evidence: weak)
- This round's research was significantly constrained by web-search tool unavailability across nearly all expert agents, meaning most findings rely on domain knowledge rather than freshly verified 2023-2025 Hungarian statistical sources. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Post-closure transportation costs typically range 800-1,500 EUR per pupil per year in rural Central Europe, often offsetting or exceeding apparent per-pupil consolidation savings. [estimate] (evidence: moderate)
- Delayed secondary tracking is associated in the international literature with reduced achievement gaps, particularly benefiting lower-performing students. [fact] (evidence: strong)

## What we consider likely
- Ad hoc, non-standardized local adaptation (S1) probably produces more unequal outcomes across villages than a coordinated framework would, because it depends heavily on individual mayors' negotiating capacity. [estimate]
- True net financial savings from consolidation (S4), once transport and indirect costs are counted, likely fall to roughly 5-15% of the gross per-pupil reduction rather than the larger headline figures often cited. [estimate]
- Association-run and networked models (S3) are likely to be more sustainable in already well-organized communities than in the poorest, least-organized villages, absent dedicated capacity-building support. [estimate]
- Structural reforms lacking cross-party political consensus (relevant to S2 in particular) are likely vulnerable to reversal within one to two decades, as happened with Poland's 1999 reform. [estimate]
- Without mandated, funded multigrade-pedagogy training, teachers in S1-type settings likely absorb an uncompensated skill and workload burden that risks silent quality erosion over time. [estimate]

## Where experts disagree
### Does school closure cause village depopulation, or merely reflect decisions already made?
- **demography, equity_and_social_mobility, hungarian_education_system, portuguese_reform**: School closure actively accelerates depopulation by reducing quality of life, signaling civic disinvestment, and removing a young-family anchor institution, creating a feedback loop that deepens rural decline. [value] Why: Rural transition literature and Central/Eastern European case studies document this feedback loop qualitatively, and community-anchor-institution loss is cited by parents and local advocates as a real, if unquantified, cost.
- **education_finance, international_comparison** (minority): The causal link between school closure and community/village decline is empirically contested or unresolved; closure may simply ratify decisions already made by out-migrating families. [value] Why: International rural sociology literature is split, with some studies finding closure accelerates decline and others attributing both closure and decline to prior demographic collapse; no Hungary-specific panel data exists to resolve the direction.
### Do financial savings from consolidation justify closure as a policy default?
- **education_finance, school_network_planning**: Claimed financial savings from school consolidation are systematically overstated; once transport, capital-repair backlogs, and lost local economic multipliers are counted, net savings shrink to 5-15% of gross per-pupil reduction, and the state (not local communities) captures the efficiency gain while communities bear the loss. [value] Why: OECD/EI rural consolidation meta-analyses and post-2013 Hungarian centralization of funding show an incentive misalignment between who saves money (the state) and who bears cost (the village).
- **portuguese_reform, international_comparison** (minority): Consolidation, when paired with targeted pedagogical support (e.g., TEIP-type programmes) and curriculum reform, can produce measurable resource-concentration and achievement gains. [value] Why: Portuguese PISA trajectory improved alongside consolidation and targeted support programmes, though this correlation does not establish that consolidation itself (rather than concurrent reforms) drove the gain.
### Which policy model should Hungary prioritize going forward?
- **finnish_reform** (minority): Hungary should prioritize system-wide uniformity (teacher training standardization, transparent cost allocation, statutory multi-tier pupil support) over preserving individual small-school buildings. [value] Why: Finland's own small-school count has declined substantially since the 1990s despite strong equity outcomes, suggesting building preservation was not the mechanism behind Finland's low between-school variance in pupil outcomes.
- **polish_reform, hungarian_education_system, school_network_planning, implementation_planning**: Hungary should pursue intermediate, settlement-specific alternatives to full closure (association-run schools, phase-out via felmenő rendszer, hub-and-feeder or multigrade models) evaluated case by case, rather than a uniform national rule or default closure. [value] Why: Poland's association-run schools and Hungary's own legal flexibility (maintainer plurality, felmenő rendszer) demonstrate that non-binary alternatives exist in comparable systems, though their scalability and outcomes remain undocumented.
### Is closure politically more durable as quiet attrition, or does the real problem lie in governance transparency?
- **political_feasibility** (minority): Closure is more politically durable when framed as community-led demographic adaptation rather than state-imposed efficiency, and centralized authority (KLIK) has shifted visible political risk upward, favoring quiet attrition over announced closures. [value] Why: Rural villages form a core electoral constituency, creating incentives for the central government to avoid visible, concentrated closures; this is presented as weak, unverified structural political-economy reasoning.
- **legal_and_governance**: The core problem is not political framing but a governance and accountability gap: legally-compliant closure processes proceed without transparent outcome tracking, so alternatives remain ad hoc and undersystematized rather than durable policy instruments. [value] Why: No national registry of closure decisions and community outcomes exists, and statutory consultation depth varies by municipal practice, indicating the gap is structural/administrative rather than purely about political messaging.

## What we don't know
- What minimum viable enrollment thresholds municipalities currently use, and how consistently they apply them, is unknown absent a systematic registry. [fact]
- Whether ad hoc, non-standardized local adaptation (S1) produces better or worse long-term outcomes than a coordinated framework (S5) is untested in the Hungarian context. [fact]
- Whether Hungary's institutional and municipal capacity can realistically support Polish-pattern central-siting logistics for upper-grade consolidation (S2) is unverified. [fact]
- The current count, average enrollment, and budget sustainability of association-run schools in Poland is not verified, so S3's replicability rests on an unaudited assumption of foreign success. [assumption]
- Whether Hungarian village governments and communities under 2,000 population have the administrative capacity to sustain inter-municipal networks or association-run schools without external support is unknown; no capacity audit exists. [assumption]
- Whether school closure causally accelerates village out-migration or merely ratifies decisions already made by prior demographic decline is empirically unresolved for the Hungarian context. [fact]
- The magnitude of peer-composition/compositional effects on remaining or reassigned pupils' outcomes after consolidation or after higher-SES exit is unknown for the Hungarian context. [fact]
- Whether a Hungarian national threshold/registry framework (S5) would in practice remain advisory or drift toward binding mandates over time is politically and institutionally uncertain — this is the central condition nearly every stakeholder attaches to their support. [assumption]

## What could be done
- **S1 — Status quo plus incremental adaptation**: Keep the village school open as long as feasible using multigrade classrooms and felmenő rendszer phase-out, negotiated locally without a national framework. Relies on existing statutory flexibility rather than new structures; low upfront cost but weak evidence on long-term viability.
- **S2 — Structural grade reorganization**: Restructure so only lower grades remain local while upper grades or a new ISCED2-type school consolidate centrally, following Poland's 1999 pattern. Offers strong delayed-tracking evidence but carries unverified institutional transferability and demonstrated political reversal risk.
- **S3 — Networked retention: clustering, association-run and multi-service hub models**: Retain village schools through inter-municipal pedagogical networks, community/association-run publicly subsidized schools, or multi-service hubs. A middle path avoiding binary open/close decisions, but its sustainability and equity effects are unverified and may favor already well-organized communities.
- **S4 — Consolidation with mitigation**: Close low-enrollment village schools by default and reassign pupils to a consolidated center school, paired with statutory transport guarantees and TEIP-style targeted support. Concentrates specialist resources but claimed savings are often overstated and the decision is largely irreversible.
- **S5 — National systemic framework**: Establish national transparent viability thresholds, cost-visibility rules, teacher-training standardization, and a closure/outcome registry to inform (not dictate) local decisions across any of the other models. Enabling infrastructure rather than a delivery model in itself.

## What each option costs
- **S1**: Teachers bear an uncompensated skill and workload burden if multigrade-pedagogy training is not mandated and funded — a false economy where the state preserves the building while teachers absorb the hidden cost. [estimate]
- **S1**: Local negotiating-capacity inequality means better-resourced villages secure favorable phase-out terms while poorer or less-organized villages risk quiet, unmanaged decline — a distributional harm not addressed by the scenario itself. [estimate]
- **S2**: The burden of longer commutes and selection-point risk shifts onto ages 10-15 and their families; if transport is subsidized rather than free, lower-SES families bear the cost, undermining the equity gain the delayed-tracking rationale claims. [estimate]
- **S2**: Structural reforms of this kind are substantially irreversible once teachers disperse and buildings are repurposed, and Poland's own 17-year reversal shows the political-durability risk is real, not theoretical — rural teachers face redundancy or reassignment costs regardless of pedagogical merit. [estimate]
- **S3**: Administrative-capacity requirements risk creating a new form of inequity where organizational capacity, not need, determines school survival — the poorest, least-organized villages may be excluded from the very alternative meant to help them. [estimate]
- **S3**: Parent and teacher unpaid administrative burden (association governance, network coordination) risks burnout and precarious two-tier employment conditions unless compensated and resourced explicitly. [estimate]
- **S4**: Rural teaching positions are eliminated outright, destabilizing rural teacher recruitment and career viability long-term; the state captures the (modest, often 5-15%) net savings while communities and displaced teachers bear the loss. [estimate]
- **S4**: Compensatory transport and TEIP-style support require sustained multi-year funding that is politically vulnerable to future budget cuts, meaning the mitigation promise can erode after the irreversible closure has already occurred — leaving disadvantaged families exposed with no path back. [estimate]
- **S4**: Higher-SES families may exit to alternatives after a closure announcement, leaving a more disadvantaged residual cohort at the consolidated school — worsening the distribution of disadvantage even as average resource concentration appears to improve. [estimate]
- **S5**: Sustained multi-year investment in registry and training infrastructure is vulnerable to budget cuts or political deprioritization across election cycles, and the framework carries a real risk of drifting from advisory status toward de facto binding closure mandates. [estimate]
- **S5**: As a purely enabling/procedural scenario, S5 changes no immediate outcome for any village on its own — its equity value depends entirely on how thresholds and registries are subsequently used within S1-S4, meaning it cannot substitute for the harder distributive choices those scenarios require. [value]

## What research could resolve
- A systematic registry or survey of municipal school operation criteria published by the Oktatási Hivatal, documenting what minimum viable enrollment thresholds municipalities currently use and how consistently.
- Pilot programmes comparing coordinated versus ad hoc adaptation models (S1 vs. S5-supported S1) across matched villages with published outcome tracking over 3-5 years.
- A feasibility audit of candidate central towns' capacity and transport network for a representative sample of village clusters, to test S2's institutional transferability from Poland.
- A registry and outcome evaluation of Polish association-run rural schools, documenting enrollment trends, funding flows, teacher retention, and long-term operational sustainability, to test whether S3's foreign precedent is genuine durability or delayed closure.
- A structured audit of Hungarian municipalities under 2,000 population measuring administrative capacity, civic engagement, and prior cooperative-governance experience, to determine S3's realistic scalability.
- Longitudinal village-level data linking school closure timing to household migration flows, controlling for prior population trends, to resolve the causal-direction dispute over closure and depopulation.
- Comparative budget data from a matched pair or cohort of Hungarian villages with and without school consolidation, to quantify true net financial savings once all transport and indirect costs are counted.
- Comparative case studies of similar advisory frameworks in other countries, tracking their evolution toward or away from binding status over a 10+ year horizon, to test S5's central implementation-fidelity risk.

## What people must decide
- Whether preserving a village school as a community anchor is a value worth sustaining even at a net fiscal cost, or whether efficiency and resource concentration should take precedence once savings, however modest, are demonstrated. [value]
- Whether local subsidiarity (letting villages and municipalities decide their own path) should be protected even where it produces unequal outcomes across communities with different negotiating or administrative capacity, or whether a national standard is owed as a matter of fairness. [value]
- Who should bear the cost of demographic decline — the state, through sustained subsidy of below-threshold schools; families, through commuting burden; or teachers, through unfunded workload absorption — when no option eliminates the underlying cost entirely. [value]
- Whether closure should ever be treated as a policy default, or only as a genuine last resort after all reversible alternatives (S1, S3) have been transparently attempted and documented to fail. [value]
- Whether a national evidence framework (S5) can be trusted to remain genuinely advisory over time, or whether any such infrastructure inevitably creates pressure toward centralized, binding control — and who should govern that framework if built. [value]

## What to verify with real stakeholders
- A1 (Ad hoc multigrade retention (S1) only protects rural children and teachers if paired with mandated, funded training, workload/wage adjustment, and transparent enrollment monitoring — otherwise it shifts unsustainable burden onto teachers and quietly degrades quality.): policy_design_fixable — The condition raised — mandated, funded multigrade training, workload adjustment, and a transparent monitoring registry — is a concrete design change, not a technical evidence question or an irreducible value clash; the scenario itself already identifies this as a required implementation step, so the fix is to make it binding rather than optional.
- A2 (Keeping the local school open through multigrade classes and gradual phase-out preserves the school's function as a community anchor, protects local institutional autonomy, and respects subsidiarity and parental voice.): value_conflict — This is a legitimate value position (subsidiarity and community continuity) that stands in direct tension with equity and quality-assurance concerns raised elsewhere (A1, A3); no evidence resolves which value should dominate, and the position itself concedes that outcome data on multigrade viability remains untested — this is a value choice about what a school is for, not a fixable design gap.
- A3 (Multigrade teaching demands specialized skills that standard pre-service teacher training does not provide, and without guaranteed in-service training this creates a quality and workload risk that ad hoc local negotiation cannot reliably fix.): policy_design_fixable — The training gap is a documented, factual constraint, but the fix — mandatory, funded, standardized multigrade-pedagogy modules in pre-service and in-service education — is a concrete policy design change already proposed under S5 and endorsed across multiple stakeholder clusters; this is answerable by building the infrastructure, not by resolving a value dispute.
- A4 (Consolidating upper grades can deliver genuine pedagogical benefits from delayed tracking, but only if statutory, funded transport guarantees with capped commute times and durable staffing commitments are locked in before restructuring — otherwise the burden shifts onto older pupils, families, and reassigned teachers.): policy_design_fixable — The pedagogical case for delayed tracking is strong evidence, and the concern (unfunded, unguaranteed transport/staffing) is addressable through binding, sequenced design changes (guarantees locked in before restructuring) rather than through new data or an irreducible value clash — though the deeper transferability and reversal-risk questions raised in A5 remain a genuine additional layer of uncertainty.
- A5 (Structural consolidation of upper grades is politically and institutionally fragile — Poland's 1999 reform was reversed after 17 years — and top-down imposition removes local community voice and institutional autonomy, risking accelerated depopulation by signalling non-viability.): irreducible_tradeoff — Even with the best-designed safeguards, there is a genuine trade-off between the pedagogical gains of centralized, specialist-rich upper-grade provision and the loss of local institutional autonomy and political durability that centralization structurally requires; no feasibility study or funding guarantee eliminates this tension, it only manages its severity.
- A6 (Networked, association-run, or multi-service hub models offer a genuine middle path, but their sustainability is unverified and risks favoring already well-organized communities over the poorest, least-organized villages unless capacity-building funding is guaranteed.): needs_more_info — Nearly every voice in this cluster explicitly names the same missing evidence (a Hungarian municipal-capacity audit, a Polish association-run-school sustainability registry, multi-service-hub cost-benefit data) as the decisive factor for their support; the disagreement is not about values but about data that does not yet exist and could change the assessment substantially once collected.
- A7 (Association-run and networked school models directly activate Hungary's existing legal maintainer plurality, strengthening institutional autonomy and community agency compared to uniform municipal or centralized provision.): policy_design_fixable — This claim rests on an existing legal pathway (maintainer plurality under Nkt.) that could be more actively activated through concrete design changes — clarifying financial parity, transition rules, and multi-year subsidy guarantees for non-municipal maintainers — rather than requiring new evidence or an unresolvable value trade-off, though its durability still depends on the unresolved sustainability questions in A8.
- A8 (Claims that association-run schools abroad achieve genuine long-term sustainability are unverified — they may mask delayed closure, chronic under-resourcing, and parent-volunteer burnout rather than proven durable retention.): needs_more_info — All raising voices agree this is a factual/evidentiary gap, not a values dispute: no verified sustainability registry exists for Polish association-run schools and no Hungarian capacity audit exists; a longitudinal outcome registry would directly resolve whether S3 represents genuine durability or disguised delayed closure.
- A9 (Treating closure as the default option is opposed because claimed efficiency savings are typically overstated once transport and indirect costs are counted, the decision is largely irreversible, and compensatory measures are politically vulnerable to future budget cuts.): irreducible_tradeoff — Even accepting the 5-15% net-savings figure and designing the strongest possible statutory guarantees, a fundamental trade-off remains between resource concentration/efficiency and the irreversibility risk plus political fragility of long-term compensatory funding — no evidence or design fix removes the asymmetry between a reversible status quo and an irreversible closure decision.
- A10 (School closure eliminates rural teaching positions and destabilizes rural teacher recruitment, while also risking compositional stratification as higher-SES families exit, leaving a more disadvantaged residual population without compensating for the harm.): policy_design_fixable — The compositional-exodus and teacher-displacement risks are addressable through specific design measures already named in the record — guaranteed equivalent positions, cohort tracking of exit rates, targeted peer-mixing or segregation-mitigation funding — though the deeper question of whether such fixes are ever fully sufficient overlaps with the irreducible trade-off named in A9.
- A11 (Evidence from Portugal suggests post-closure consolidation can accelerate rural out-migration beyond prior trends because closure signals state disinvestment.): evidence_answerable — This is a specific empirical causal claim (does closure cause accelerated out-migration, versus merely correlating with prior decline) that a longitudinal, Hungary-specific study linking closure timing to household migration flows, controlling for prior population trends, could directly settle — multiple voices explicitly name this exact study design as the resolving evidence.
- A12 (Closure could become acceptable only as a genuine last resort, conditioned on binding multi-year transport and TEIP-style funding, guaranteed teacher positions, reversal/reopening review points, and a public outcome registry.): policy_design_fixable — The stakeholders here converge on a detailed, concrete list of design conditions (statutory funding lock-in, position guarantees, reversal review points, registry) that, if genuinely enacted, would materially change the risk calculus — this is a design-fix cluster, though it inherits the deeper irreducible tension of A9 about whether such guarantees can ever be credibly durable against future political change.
- A13 (A national framework of transparent thresholds, a closure/outcome registry, standardized teacher training, and cost-visibility rules is broadly supported as enabling infrastructure that makes hidden trade-offs visible across all other scenarios without dictating a structural outcome.): policy_design_fixable — The broad cross-stakeholder support for this proposal is itself evidence that the core mechanism is workable and wanted; what remains is a design/implementation question (how to build and fund the registry, training standards, and cost rules) rather than a values dispute or missing evidence about whether the idea has merit.
- A14 (Support for the national framework is conditional on it remaining strictly advisory rather than drifting into de facto mandated closure or centralized control, with sustained multi-year funding and genuine stakeholder participation in governance.): value_conflict — This names a genuine, structural tension between transparency/evidence infrastructure and local autonomy that no design guarantee can fully eliminate in advance — the record itself acknowledges this is an 'implementation-fidelity question that can only be resolved empirically over several years,' meaning the underlying distrust of centralizing institutions is a legitimate, unresolved value position, not a defect to be engineered away entirely.
- A15 (Parents' core need is predictability — early, honest, transparent communication of enrollment trends and school viability — rather than a specific structural model, so families can plan well ahead of a crisis.): communication_fixable — This is fundamentally a demand for existing or plannable information to be surfaced in an accessible, plain-language, locally distributed form rather than a demand for new policy structures or new evidence; several scenarios (S1, S5) already propose the underlying data collection — what is missing is the communication channel to parents themselves, not the data.
- A16 (Framing closure as an inevitable efficiency-driven default rather than a genuine last resort inverts child-centred and community-embedded schooling values, treating a community decision about externalized costs as a merely technical management question.): communication_fixable — The underlying substantive protections (transparent cost accounting, genuine last-resort sequencing, exhausted-alternatives documentation) are already named as design conditions elsewhere in this record (A9, A12); what this cluster additionally identifies is a framing/procedural failure — that presenting closure as a neutral default rather than an exhausted-alternatives last resort materially changes whose voice is heard, which is fixable by changing how the decision and its sequencing are communicated and consulted upon, not by new policy mechanisms beyond those already proposed.
