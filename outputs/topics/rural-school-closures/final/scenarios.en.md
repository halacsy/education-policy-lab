# Policy scenarios

## S1 — Status quo plus incremental adaptation
**Goal**
Keep the existing municipal/state-maintained village school open as long as feasible, relying on multigrade classrooms, felmenő rendszer phase-out and ad hoc local negotiation without a national framework.

**Mechanism**
- Hungarian law already provides an administratively ready alternative to closure: multigrade (összevont) classrooms combining up to three (exceptionally four) consecutive grades, used in an estimated 200-300 schools nationally. [evidence: strong]
- OFI research concludes that rural small-school pupil outcomes are broadly comparable to larger village schools and only moderately below urban schools once family socioeconomic background is controlled for, undermining pure efficiency arguments for closure. [evidence: moderate]
- The felmenő rendszer (grade-by-grade phase-in mechanism) is applied in practice to school reorganization to protect currently-enrolled student cohorts from mid-course disruption, with documented 2025 cases implementing closure-related splits across multiple school years. [evidence: moderate]
- Small schools (under 150 pupils) comprise nearly one-third of municipal primary schools yet enroll fewer than 10% of all students, and researchers argue the cost differential alone does not constitute a serious system-wide efficiency problem. [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
moderate — Multigrade pedagogy and outcome-parity findings are moderately well documented, but no Hungarian data track long-term viability of ad hoc local negotiation without systemic support.

**Assumptions**
- Teacher supply for multigrade classrooms is adequate without systemic training reform, despite Finnish evidence that teacher-education programmes historically have not prepared educators for small-school pedagogy and Hungary's own implementation-planning findings that rural teacher vacancies are rising.
- Local decision-makers have comparable financial and demographic data to negotiate fair, transparent retention arrangements, though no national closure/outcome registry currently exists to support this.
- Ad hoc, case-by-case maintainer discretion (absent a bright-line statutory threshold) will continue to permit village schools to remain open rather than be forced to close by administrative pressure from tankerület budgets.

**Expected benefits**
- Preserves the school's documented community-hub and place-identity function without requiring new legal or institutional structures. [evidence: moderate]
- Avoids abrupt travel-burden increases for families, since children continue attending the local school rather than being reassigned under mandatory catchment rules. [evidence: weak]
- Requires minimal upfront legal or administrative change, since multigrade classrooms and felmenő rendszer mechanisms are already codified and operational. [evidence: strong]

**Equity impact**
Likely reduces short-term travel-burden inequity for lower-SES and Roma families who currently benefit from local schooling, but this rests on weak evidence and untested assumptions about which families remain versus exit under 'white flight' dynamics documented elsewhere in the Hungarian system; without transparent enrollment thresholds or transport-cost data, the scenario cannot guarantee that the poorest villages retain viable multigrade staffing rather than drifting into de facto under-resourced status.

**Cost categories**
- Recurring multigrade teacher-training and in-service pedagogy support costs
- Capital-maintenance and backlog costs of under-enrolled buildings (roof, heating, accessibility upgrades) often a binding driver of closure decisions if left unaddressed
- Opportunity cost of maintaining spare capacity relative to potential consolidated-site investment
- Local negotiation and administrative transaction costs (per-settlement, case-by-case maintainer discussions)

**Implementation steps**
- School leaders and teaching staff — Undertake extended in-service multigrade-pedagogy training with realistic multi-year lead time, recognizing that upskilling sufficient teachers for quality multigrade instruction requires longer preparation than a single cycle. (timeline: year 1-4, recurring)
- Tankerület (district center) and municipality — Negotiate transparent, documented retention arrangements including enrollment and cost data disclosure to parents and local council. (timeline: year 1-2, ongoing)
- Ministry/EMMI — Clarify whether pre-2010 independent expert assessment requirements before closure remain in force, to standardize procedural protections. (timeline: year 1)

**Political risks**
- Chronic under-budgeting of the district (tankerület) system creates persistent fiscal pressure to consolidate regardless of stated 'protect as long as there is demand' policy, risking quiet erosion rather than open confrontation.
- Absence of a bright-line statutory threshold leaves retention decisions to discretionary tankerület judgment, which is politically reversible year to year and vulnerable to administration change.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether teacher supply for multigrade classrooms can scale fast enough given that half of Hungarian teachers are 50+ and rural vacancy postings are rising ~25% year-on-year. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A district-level capacity audit of multigrade-qualified teacher supply and pipeline projections through 2030.)
- Whether ad hoc local negotiation without a national framework produces systematically fair outcomes across settlements, or advantages better-organized/wealthier villages. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A comparative study of retention-negotiation outcomes across settlements of varying administrative capacity and wealth.)

## S2 — Structural grade reorganization (lower grades stay, upper grades centralize)
**Goal**
Restructure the school system so only lower grades (1-4/1-6) remain in the village while upper grades or a new ISCED2-type school are consolidated in a larger town, following the Polish 1999 structural-reform pattern of shortened primary plus centrally sited later-stage schooling.

**Mechanism**
- Poland's 1999 educational reform (reducing primary school from 8 to 6 years and creating larger-catchment lower-secondary schools) was explicitly designed to improve rural education quality but inadvertently reduced primary school size and triggered rural school closures as a structural side effect. [evidence: moderate]
- Delayed secondary tracking is associated in some international comparative literature with reduced achievement gaps, though the strength and causal attribution of this association (versus other confounding system features) is disputed among comparative-education researchers. [evidence: contested]
- Very small rural schools cannot provide full-time employment to subject specialists, forcing teachers to commute between multiple schools; centralizing upper grades in a larger ISCED2-type site would allow full-time specialist staffing not achievable in scattered village schools. [evidence: moderate]
- Poland's gmina-level referendum mechanism and constitutional protection of local self-determination proved effective in preventing centrally imposed closures, offering a potential model for community consent in the centralization process. [evidence: weak]

**Evidence status**
contested — The Polish 1999 case shows a structural reform pattern and closure side-effect, but the achievement-gap benefit is contested in the literature, and no Hungarian pilot data exist to confirm transferability.

**Assumptions**
- Hungary's institutional and municipal governance context is sufficiently similar to Poland's 1999 conditions for the structural reform and its purported achievement-gap benefits to transfer, despite differences in centralization (Hungary's post-2013 tankerület system vs. Poland's gmina-level autonomy).
- Current commuting infrastructure and subsidized public transport can sustain daily trips for upper-primary-age children to centrally sited ISCED2 schools without unacceptable welfare cost, though documented informal parent-organized escort shifts suggest existing transport safeguards are already strained.
- Sufficient political will and cross-party consensus exist to entrench this structural reform against reversal, despite Poland's own 1999 reform later being reversed after 17 years and Hungary having no documented cross-party consensus mechanism for rural school reform.

**Expected benefits**
- Enables full-time specialist subject teaching at the centralized upper-grade site, addressing the documented Polish problem of rural teachers commuting between multiple part-time postings. [evidence: moderate]
- Preserves a local school presence for the youngest, most travel-vulnerable children (grades 1-4/1-6), partially mitigating the documented burden of early-morning long-distance travel for young pupils. [evidence: weak]
- May reduce achievement gaps associated with delayed tracking, per some international comparative literature, though this benefit's magnitude and causal attribution remain disputed. [evidence: contested]

**Equity impact**
Shifts travel burden onto upper-primary-age children and their families, who must commute daily to the centralized site; this burden falls disproportionately on lower-income and Roma families already documented to face 2-3 times higher rates of unqualified-teacher exposure and geographic concentration in disadvantaged rural settlements, and risks triggering 'white flight' exit to alternative schools by better-resourced families, leaving the centralized site more segregated; no compensatory transport-cost or targeted-support mechanism is built into this scenario's core design.

**Cost categories**
- Capital investment for new or expanded ISCED2-type centralized school facilities
- Statutory transport infrastructure and subsidized daily commuting costs for upper-grade pupils
- Severance, compensation, or redeployment costs for redundant teachers and support staff at village sites losing upper grades
- Temporary scale-up costs at central sites (modular classrooms, staffing surge) during transition period

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry/EMMI and tankerület centers — Designate centrally-sited ISCED2-type schools and conduct capital-investment planning with realistic multi-year procurement and construction timelines, not compressed into a single year. (timeline: year 1-5)
- Municipality and transport authority — Establish statutory transport routes and subsidized service with capacity and safety audits before pupil transfer begins. (timeline: year 2-4)
- District center (tankerület) and teacher unions — Negotiate redeployment, severance, or retraining terms for teachers affected by grade-level restructuring. (timeline: year 2-3)

**Political risks**
- Poland's 1999 reform was politically reversible and was in fact reversed after 17 years; without concrete cross-party entrenchment mechanisms or constitutional-level protection, Hungary's version faces similar reversal risk, especially given the documented absence of any cross-party consensus mechanism for rural school reform.
- Teacher redeployment under Act LII of 2023 (Status Law), which grants district centers authority to reassign teachers, may trigger the kind of mobilization seen in the Tanítanék movement if perceived as involuntary displacement without adequate consultation.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether the achievement-gap benefit attributed to delayed tracking actually materializes in the Hungarian context, given contested causal attribution in the international literature and no Hungarian pilot data. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A Hungarian pilot study comparing achievement gaps in villages that adopt centralized ISCED2 structuring versus those that retain full 1-8 local schools, controlling for socioeconomic background.)
- Whether current commuting infrastructure can sustain daily upper-grade pupil transport without unacceptable welfare cost, given documented informal parent-organized escort shifts arising from regional buses refusing unaccompanied minors. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A transport-safety and welfare audit of existing school-bus routes and capacity in candidate centralization regions, including unaccompanied-minor policy compliance.)

## S3 — Networked retention: clustering, association-run and multi-service hub models
**Goal**
Retain village primary schools through inter-municipal pedagogical networks (French RPI-style clustering), community/association-run publicly-subsidized schools (Polish model), or multi-service hubs combining school with other civic functions.

**Mechanism**
- Poland introduced a legal framework (gmina agreements with associations, foundations, or natural persons) allowing small schools (up to 70 students) to remain open under non-governmental management while retaining public funding and curriculum. [evidence: strong]
- Structural social capital decline in villages where primary schools closed (2000-2016) was significantly larger than in villages where schools remained open, as measured by a multiple-case study of four Polish villages. [evidence: moderate]
- Poland's 2024-2025 draft education law formalizes multi-service community hub functions for shrinking primary schools, including childcare, pre-school provision, and senior services, as an alternative to closure. [evidence: moderate]
- Grassroots and civic maintainer models exist as alternatives to closure in Hungary; in Sáta (Borsod), a local community organization took over school maintainership rather than allow the village to lose its school. [evidence: weak]
- Very small rural schools in Poland cannot provide full-time employment to subject specialists, forcing teachers to commute between multiple schools and take secondary employment; approximately one in five rural teachers holds a part-time job, indicating networked models do not automatically resolve staffing precarity. [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
weak — Polish legal-framework and social-capital evidence is moderate-to-strong, but no Hungarian pilot data exist and the Sáta case is a single weak-evidence example, so overall Hungarian transferability remains weak.

**Assumptions**
- Polish association-run, publicly-subsidized rural schools have achieved genuine operational sustainability, though neither the Polish nor Hungarian evidence base confirms this over the 10+ year horizon needed to validate the model, and teacher precarity (one in five part-time) persists as an unresolved separate problem.
- Hungary's constitutional and local self-government framework is sufficiently comparable to Poland's gmina structure to permit legal transfer of association-run school models, despite Hungary's post-2013 centralization of school maintenance under tankerület/KLIK removing local government authority that Polish gminas retain.
- Municipalities and civic organizations have or can rapidly build the administrative capacity to run multi-service hubs (childcare, senior services) alongside core educational functions, without this capacity requirement systematically disadvantaging the poorest, least-organized villages.

**Expected benefits**
- Preserves structural social capital that Polish multi-case evidence shows declines significantly more in villages that lose their school than in villages that retain it. [evidence: moderate]
- Retains public funding and curriculum standards under non-governmental management, avoiding a binary choice between full state operation and closure. [evidence: strong]
- Diversifies the school building's function into multi-service community hub roles, potentially increasing fiscal and civic utilization of underused rural infrastructure. [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
Retaining schools via networked or association-run models plausibly protects the poorest, most isolated villages from losing their only public institution, but the administrative-capacity burden of running association contracts, multi-service hubs, or inter-municipal networks risks systematically excluding exactly those poorest, least-organized villages that most need protection, unless the state provides dedicated capacity-building grants, model-contract templates, and legal/accounting support targeted at low-capacity municipalities; without such targeted support, this scenario could paradoxically widen the gap between well-organized and poorly-organized rural communities.

**Cost categories**
- Legal drafting and negotiation costs for establishing association-run or network-clustering agreements
- Ongoing administrative and accounting capacity for association-run schools and multi-service hubs
- Capacity-building grants and technical assistance targeted at low-administrative-capacity municipalities to prevent exclusion of the poorest villages
- Teacher salary and secondary-employment support to address commuting/part-time precarity documented in the Polish networked model

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry/EMMI and legal drafters — Establish or clarify a legal mechanism analogous to Poland's association-run school amendment, addressing barriers such as changing maintainer law and defining ministerial approval procedures, with realistic allowance for legislative negotiation and stakeholder consultation delays. (timeline: year 1-3)
- Municipalities and civic/association organizations — Form inter-municipal pedagogical networks or association-run maintainer bodies, with dedicated technical assistance for low-capacity settlements. (timeline: year 2-4)
- Ministry of Agriculture (Magyar Falu Program) and Ministry/EMMI — Coordinate parallel infrastructure funding channels to support multi-service hub conversion of school buildings. (timeline: year 2-5)

**Political risks**
- Association-run and civic-maintainer models risk being perceived as privatization by teacher unions and some political actors, and the state has previously demonstrated willingness to forcibly reverse maintainer status (as in the MET case) when operators fall out of political favor, raising the risk that the same coercive-reversal capacity could be applied to civic/association maintainers.
- Organized union resistance may intensify if networked models are seen as a mechanism to reduce state employment guarantees or bypass Act LII (Status Law) protections, requiring explicit political or legal safeguards for teacher employment continuity across network/association transitions.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether Hungary's post-2013 centralized tankerület/KLIK governance structure permits genuine legal transfer of Polish-style association-run school autonomy, given that Polish gminas retain local school-ownership authority that Hungarian municipalities lost. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A formal legal-comparative analysis by Hungarian and Polish education-law experts assessing transferability of the association-run maintainer mechanism under current Nkt. provisions.)
- Whether association-run and multi-service hub models can achieve genuine long-term operational sustainability and educational quality, or whether they merely delay closure while quality erodes, given that no rigorous quasi-experimental study exists comparing association schools to traditional public schools on outcomes over 10+ years. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A longitudinal quasi-experimental study (diff-in-diff or matched-pair) comparing association schools vs. traditional public or closed schools on student outcomes, teacher retention, and institutional vitality over 10+ years.)

## S4 — Consolidation with mitigation (closure as default, compensatory measures)
**Goal**
Close low-enrollment village schools and reassign pupils to a consolidated center school, paired with statutory transport guarantees, targeted support (TEIP-style), and transparent cost/consultation rules to offset travel and equity harms.

**Mechanism**
- Portugal's 2005/2006 school network reorganization paired the closure of 1,418 schools with construction of 800 larger, better-equipped 'centros escolares,' demonstrating that closure can be paired with matched receiving-site investment. [evidence: moderate]
- Post-closure transportation costs have been cited in Central European contexts in a range of roughly 800-1,500 EUR per pupil per year, though this precise figure has not been validated against Hungarian route lengths, occupancy rates, or regional terrain, and often offsets part of the projected consolidation savings. [evidence: weak]
- Portugal's TEIP compensatory programme reduced dropout and failure rates in disadvantaged school clusters but failed over 15 years to close the performance gap between TEIP and non-TEIP schools on national exams, indicating access/retention support alone does not guarantee learning-outcome equity. [evidence: strong]
- An Italian study exploiting a school-network contraction reform found that municipalities affected by school closures experienced significant population and income declines beyond pre-existing demographic trends, indicating a causal feedback loop that mitigation measures would need to specifically counteract. [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
contested — Portuguese and Italian evidence on closure-mitigation pairing is documented, but the precise Hungarian cost data are absent, and TEIP evidence shows compensatory measures do not resolve equity gaps, so overall net effect is contested.

**Assumptions**
- Current commuting infrastructure and subsidized public transport can sustain daily trips of 20+ km for primary-age children without unacceptable welfare cost, despite documented informal parent-organized escort shifts arising because regional buses often refuse unaccompanied minors.
- TEIP-style targeted support, once implemented in a consolidated center school, will meaningfully offset the equity harms of closure for displaced rural pupils, despite Portugal's own 15-year TEIP evidence showing persistent performance gaps.
- Transparent cost/consultation rules will be genuinely observed and enforced by tankerület authorities, rather than serving as a procedural formality, given that current judicial review of maintainer decisions is limited to legality of procedure and cannot override closure decisions on policy-merit grounds.

**Expected benefits**
- Enables investment concentration in fewer, better-equipped consolidated sites, following the Portuguese centros escolares pattern of matched receiving-site investment. [evidence: moderate]
- Statutory transport guarantees and targeted support provide a formal, legally enforceable floor of protection for displaced pupils, an improvement over ad hoc arrangements. [evidence: weak]
- May reduce per-pupil operating costs at the system level by concentrating specialist staff and facilities, though empirical audits of actual realized savings (net of transport and mitigation costs) are scarce. [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
Disproportionately burdens lower-SES and Roma families concentrated in peripheral rural settlements, who face longer travel distances, higher risk of dropout (Roma dropout already 58.7% vs. 9.3% non-Roma), and the documented risk that non-Roma/wealthier families exit consolidated hub schools via free school choice ('white flight'), reproducing segregation at the hub-school scale; without specific, enforceable thresholds (e.g., maximum travel time such as Portugal's Fundão 25-minute rule), disaggregated equity-outcome monitoring by ethnicity and income at the consolidated site, and binding (not merely advisory) transport-safety standards, the compensatory measures in this scenario risk remaining declarative rather than protective.

**Cost categories**
- Statutory transport service capital and recurring operating costs (vehicles, drivers, fuel, supervision), not yet validated against Hungarian route-specific data
- Capital investment for expanded consolidated 'centros escolares'-style receiving-site capacity
- TEIP-style targeted support staffing and programme costs at consolidated sites, recurring annually
- Indirect community-level costs (property-value decline, reduced local employer base) documented in comparative literature but not typically included in official budget accounting

**Implementation steps**
- Tankerület (district center) — Conduct and publish transparent cost/consultation disclosure prior to closure decision, including independent expert assessment where legally required, respecting the statutory May-deadline procedural window. (timeline: year 1, ahead of each May decision deadline)
- Municipality and transport authority — Establish statutory transport guarantees with route-specific safety and capacity audits before pupil reassignment, addressing the unaccompanied-minor gap documented in current informal coping mechanisms. (timeline: year 1-2, before reassignment takes effect)
- Ministry/EMMI and receiving-school leadership — Deploy TEIP-style targeted support staffing with disaggregated outcome monitoring by ethnicity and income at the consolidated site. (timeline: year 2-5, recurring)

**Political risks**
- Compensatory measures (transport guarantees, TEIP-style support) require sustained multi-year funding; without a specified binding funding-commitment mechanism (e.g., ring-fenced budget line, multi-year statutory allocation), these measures risk being cut in future budget cycles once the initial closure has already occurred and political attention has moved on.
- Closure decisions, even with consultation, may trigger community mobilization (as in the 2007 Erdőkürt 'Human Chain for Rural Schools' precedent), and limited judicial review (legality-only, not policy-merit) means affected communities have few formal channels to contest closure substance.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether post-closure transportation costs in the Hungarian context actually fall within the cited 800-1,500 EUR/pupil/year range, and whether the true net financial effect of consolidation (savings minus transport, mitigation, and indirect community costs) is positive or negative. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A Hungary-specific cost-benefit analysis of small-village school operation versus closure under 2-3 realistic consolidation scenarios, including transport, mitigation, and indirect community costs.)
- Whether TEIP-style targeted support, once implemented at Hungarian consolidated sites, would close or merely narrow the performance gap for displaced rural and Roma pupils, given Portugal's own 15-year TEIP evidence of persistent gaps despite dropout reduction. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A Hungarian pilot evaluation comparing learning outcomes of displaced rural/Roma pupils at consolidated sites with and without TEIP-style targeted support, tracked over 5-10 years.)

## S5 — National systemic framework: transparency, thresholds and evidence infrastructure
**Goal**
Establish a national framework of transparent minimum-viability thresholds, cost-visibility rules, teacher-training standardization, and a closure/outcome registry to inform (not dictate) local decisions across any of the above models.

**Mechanism**
- No bright-line statutory minimum enrollment threshold currently exists in Hungarian education law, unlike comparative models such as Ukraine's 45-student rule; closure decisions operate on a discretionary, case-by-case basis, creating unpredictability that a transparent national threshold framework would address. [evidence: moderate]
- Given that the curated source registry available for this topic contains no equivalent Hungarian closure/outcome dataset and multiple experts independently note the absence of systematic tracking, the claim that 'no current national registry of closure decisions and outcomes exists in Hungary' is a reasonable inference from an absence of evidence rather than a directly documented negative finding. [evidence: weak]
- Finnish rural village-school teachers report being unprepared for multigrade teaching during initial teacher education, despite multigrade instruction being their routine working environment, illustrating a training-standardization gap that a national framework's teacher-training standards component would target. [evidence: moderate]
- Historical Hungarian small-school subsidies were deemed 'economically fully justified' by a 2007 analysis, yet base normatívák often fell short of actual per-pupil costs, indicating that cost-visibility rules could correct a documented historical mismatch between subsidy design and real costs. [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
weak — The rationale for a national framework rests on documented gaps (no threshold, no registry, training mismatches, subsidy-cost mismatch) rather than direct evidence that such a framework would work in Hungary, since no comparable Hungarian pilot exists.

**Assumptions**
- Sufficient political will exists to fund and maintain a long-term evidence infrastructure (registry, thresholds) across changing government administrations, despite the documented absence of any cross-party consensus mechanism for rural school reform and the demonstrated political sensitivity that has stalled even existing leaked consolidation plans.
- A non-binding, advisory national framework (transparency and thresholds informing rather than dictating local decisions) will be respected as genuinely advisory by tankerület and ministry actors, rather than being used selectively to justify predetermined closure decisions.
- The registry and thresholds, once established, will capture and publish disaggregated equity data (by settlement size, ethnicity, income) sufficient to detect and prevent inequitable downstream application across any of S1-S4's models, rather than remaining an aggregate, equity-blind dataset.

**Expected benefits**
- Provides a transparent, evidence-based reference point for local decisions currently made on a discretionary, case-by-case basis, addressing a documented gap in Hungarian education law. [evidence: weak]
- Standardized teacher training for multigrade and small-school pedagogy could address the documented preparation gap seen in both Finnish and inferred Hungarian contexts. [evidence: moderate]
- A closure/outcome registry would, over time, enable retrospective evaluation of which models (S1-S4) perform best under which conditions, informing future policy—though this benefit accrues only after multi-year data accumulation and is not immediate. [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
As a procedural, enabling framework rather than a direct service-delivery model, S5's equity impact depends entirely on downstream use: if thresholds and the registry are designed from inception to require disaggregated reporting by settlement size, ethnicity, and income, and if cost-visibility rules explicitly surface transport and mitigation costs, the framework could materially improve equity oversight across S1-S4; but if it is implemented as an aggregate, ethnicity-blind system, it risks providing a veneer of evidence-based legitimacy to decisions that remain as inequitable as under the status quo, since transparency alone does not compel any particular equity-protective action.

**Cost categories**
- One-off design and legal-drafting costs for minimum-viability thresholds and cost-visibility rules
- One-off IT build and ongoing data-management costs for the closure/outcome registry, including data-protection compliance
- Recurring costs for standardized teacher-training module rollout and staffing of analytic/monitoring functions
- Ongoing funding-responsibility allocation across ministry, tankerület, and municipal levels, currently unspecified and requiring explicit division

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry/EMMI and independent education-research institutes — Design and publish transparent minimum-viability thresholds and cost-visibility rules, with realistic allowance for legal, data-protection, and stakeholder-consultation review before publication. (timeline: year 1-3)
- Ministry/EMMI and KSH (Central Statistical Office) — Build and populate a closure/outcome registry disaggregated by settlement size, ethnicity, and income, with data-protection and legal-basis clearance secured before data collection begins. (timeline: year 1-4)
- Teacher-training institutions and Ministry/EMMI — Roll out standardized multigrade and small-school pedagogy training modules into initial and in-service teacher education, sequenced with realistic curriculum-development and accreditation lead time. (timeline: year 2-5)

**Political risks**
- A non-binding, advisory registry and threshold framework may be politically instrumentalized: government or tankerület actors could selectively cite thresholds to justify predetermined closure decisions while ignoring them when politically inconvenient, absent explicit rules on how thresholds may or may not be used in decisions.
- Sustained multi-year funding for the registry, training rollout, and analytic staffing is vulnerable to budget-cycle discontinuity and administration change, given the documented absence of cross-party consensus mechanisms and the chronic under-budgeting pattern already observed in the tankerület/KLIK system.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether a genuinely non-binding, advisory framework can remain politically neutral in practice, or whether it will inevitably be used selectively to legitimize predetermined closure or retention decisions. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A process-tracing case study of how similar advisory frameworks (e.g., EU pay-conditionality benchmarks, prior Hungarian education indicators) have actually been used in real decisions over a multi-year period.)
- Whether the registry and thresholds, once operational, will in fact be designed to capture and publish disaggregated equity data (settlement size, ethnicity, income), or whether they will default to aggregate reporting that obscures unequal downstream impacts. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: An explicit technical-specification review, published for public comment, of the registry's planned data schema and disaggregation fields before system-build begins.)
