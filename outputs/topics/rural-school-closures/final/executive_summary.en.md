Hungary's village primary schools sit at the crossing point of a sharp demographic collapse (births down from 110,800 in 2015 to 77,600 in 2025), a severe rural teacher shortage, and a governance system that concentrated closure authority at state level after 2013. Twelve expert analyses and a ten-perspective stakeholder stress test agree on this empirical core but diverge sharply on what follows from it. Five policy scenarios frame the choice: **S1** (status quo plus incremental adaptation via multigrade classes and phased decline), **S2** (structural grade reorganization, centralizing upper grades on a Polish gimnazjum template later itself reversed), **S3** (networked retention through inter-municipal, church, or association-run clusters), **S4** (consolidation with mitigation — closure as default paired with transport guarantees and TEIP-style support), and **S5** (a national framework of transparent viability thresholds and a closure/outcome registry, advisory rather than binding).

The central, unresolved disagreement is whether closure/phase-out is economically and demographically inevitable — as demography and school-network-planning experts argue from cohort collapse data — or a policy choice with a weak net-savings case once transport, capital, and lagged demographic costs are counted, as a minority (education finance) insists. A second fault line concerns equity: does keeping small schools open protect disadvantaged pupils, or does uncontrolled higher-SES exit to church/urban schools entrench the segregation already visible in Hungary's nearly 300 'ghetto schools'? Experts also disagree on whether the dominant harm from closure is psychological/logistical (peer effects, commute burden) or community-structural (population and social-capital decline), and whether centralized state control helps or hinders survival, given that poorer municipalities cannot always self-finance even if authority were returned to them. This brief does not resolve these disagreements — they reflect genuine value conflicts, not gaps that more data alone will close.

No-regret moves now: fund and mandate multigrade teacher training wherever S1-type arrangements persist; commission a Hungary-specific quasi-experimental study of closure effects, since all current effect sizes are imported; and, before any consolidation proceeds, legislate binding, pre-funded transport guarantees and independently verified destination-school quality audits.
