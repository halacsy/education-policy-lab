Rural Hungary's smallest primary schools sit at the intersection of demographic decline and a 2013 centralization that moved funding and closure authority to the state, leaving no national framework to guide communities among five policy scenarios: **S1** (status quo plus incremental adaptation via multigrade classes and locally negotiated phase-out), **S2** (structural grade reorganization, lower grades local/upper grades centralized, following Poland's 1999 pattern), **S3** (networked retention through inter-municipal clustering, association-run schools, or multi-service hubs), **S4** (consolidation with mitigation — default closure paired with transport guarantees and TEIP-style targeted support), and **S5** (a national systemic framework of transparent viability thresholds, cost-visibility rules, and a closure/outcome registry, advisory rather than mandating any single path).

Experts agree that depopulation, not school structure, drives enrollment decline, and that no registry currently tracks closure decisions against community outcomes. But the central disagreement is left unresolved, not smoothed over: does school closure *cause* village depopulation by removing a community anchor and signaling disinvestment (demography, equity, hungarian_education_system, portuguese_reform), or does it merely *ratify* decisions families already made through out-migration, with the causal direction empirically undetermined (education_finance, international_comparison, minority)? A parallel dispute concerns consolidation's financial case: one camp argues claimed savings are systematically overstated once transport and indirect costs are counted, with the state capturing gains while villages bear losses (education_finance, school_network_planning); another (portuguese_reform, international_comparison, minority) points to Portugal's PISA gains as evidence that consolidation paired with targeted support can genuinely work. A third split concerns preferred direction: system-wide uniformity modeled on Finland (finnish_reform, minority) versus case-by-case intermediate alternatives (polish_reform, hungarian_education_system, school_network_planning). None of these tensions is resolved here, nor should it be.

**No-regret moves regardless of which framing prevails**: (1) mandate and fund multigrade-pedagogy training now, since teachers currently absorb this burden unpaid under S1; (2) build the national closure/outcome registry (core of S5) as enabling infrastructure compatible with any scenario; (3) commission the Hungary-specific longitudinal study linking closure timing to migration flows, which could resolve the causality dispute directly; (4) guarantee transport funding *before*, not after, any consolidation proceeds.
