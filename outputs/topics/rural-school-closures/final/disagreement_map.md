# Disagreement map (final)

Preserved per the Habermas-machine principle: mapped, not collapsed into consensus. Minority positions also appear in the final brief.

## Disagreement map
### Is rural school closure/phase-out economically/demographically inevitable, or a policy choice with a weak net-savings case?
- **demography, school_network_planning**: Given birth-rate collapse (–30% in a decade), reversing rural-urban migration, and specific micro-regions already below viable cohort size, phase-out closures of small-village schools are economically and demographically inevitable within 5–10 years absent radical service redesign. Why: Grounded in national fertility/enrollment trend data and settlement-level cohort counts (e.g., Bélapátfalva 55, Tab 67 births/year) showing structural, not cyclical, demand collapse that mechanically undercuts minimum class-launch thresholds.
- **education_finance** (minority): The financial case for closing rural primary schools is weak: transport, capital-construction, and lagged demographic costs (population decline, housing depreciation, social-capital loss) typically exceed the salary savings from consolidation, and these aggregate costs fall on families and communities rather than the education budget. Why: Grounded in cost-accounting evidence (Délegyháza bus-route case, Danish 7.6pp population-decline effect, international consolidation-savings-offset literature) showing that once transport and capital costs are counted, net savings are minimal or negative — an economic-mechanism argument distinct from the demographic-inevitability framing.

### Does keeping small rural schools open protect equity, or does it risk entrenching segregation ('ghetto schools')?
- **hungarian_education_system, demography, educational_psychology**: Rural primary schools serve an irreplaceable equity and community-anchoring function for disadvantaged and commuting pupils in depopulating villages; closure without alternative provision will concentrate harm among the poorest and most minority students. Why: Grounded in territorial-equity data (1,200+ schoolless settlements, higher parental-education gaps in small-settlement schools) and psychological evidence on commute-driven wellbeing harm and disadvantaged-pupil vulnerability to disruption — a protective/access-based mechanism.
- **equity_and_social_mobility** (minority): Without regulating school-choice and church-school exit, keeping small village schools open can paradoxically increase between-school segregation as higher-SES/non-Roma families leave, concentrating disadvantage in the schools left behind — nearly 300 'ghetto schools' (over 50% Roma) exist, three-quarters in villages. Why: Grounded in a distinct selection/exit mechanism (white flight, church-school competitive selection) rather than a resource-access mechanism: the harm arises from who leaves, not from the school's mere presence or absence.

### What is the primary mechanism of harm to children from closure/consolidation?
- **educational_psychology** (minority): The dominant harm channel is psychological and logistical: peer-reshuffling rank effects, persistent behavioral disruption, and commute burdens (over 30 minutes) independently degrade achievement, attendance, and wellbeing, concentrated among disadvantaged and minority pupils. Why: Based on peer-effects and rank-effect literature (Big-Fish-Little-Pond Effect meta-analysis, ordinal rank studies) and commute-time health/achievement studies — a distinct psychological/behavioral causal pathway, not an economic or demographic one.
- **demography, international_comparison, finnish_reform**: The dominant harm channel is community-structural: closure removes a social-infrastructure hub and accelerates self-reinforcing local population decline via housing-market and social-capital mechanisms that emerge years after closure. Why: Based on quasi-experimental population-effect studies (Danish 7.6pp decline, Finnish 518-closure DID study) that isolate a demographic/community-infrastructure causal pathway distinct from individual-level psychological mechanisms.

### Does centralized (state/tankerület) governance help or hinder rural school survival compared to local/decentralized control?
- **legal_and_governance, hungarian_education_system**: Post-2013 state-level tankerületi központ concentration of maintainer authority reduced municipal and parental agency over closure decisions, leaving communities with only discretionary, non-binding appeal channels ('interest injury' not legal injury) despite statutory safeguards existing on paper. Why: Grounded in statutory analysis (Nkt. §102(2), catchment-designation procedure) showing decision authority is legally exclusive to the maintainer, with communities lacking formal legal remedy — a procedural/governance-structure mechanism.
- **political_feasibility** (minority): Devolving maintainer responsibility back to municipalities is not straightforwardly beneficial: poorer rural municipalities explicitly cite inability to self-finance operating costs since the 2012 state takeover, meaning decentralization without ring-fenced co-financing could worsen, not improve, rural school durability. Why: Grounded in a 2026 mayoral survey showing fiscal-capacity divergence between wealthy and poor municipalities — a fiscal-capacity mechanism distinct from the legal-authority mechanism above; the concern is affordability, not who holds legal closure power.

### Are alternative models (multigrade, NGO/church maintainers, phase-out, exception pathways) a genuine solution or a stopgap that redistributes rather than resolves the underlying problem?
- **polish_reform, portuguese_reform, hungarian_education_system**: Alternative governance and pedagogical models (Polish NGO-run schools, rural funding weights, Portuguese case-by-case exception authorizations, multigrade classrooms with adequate investment) can meaningfully preserve rural school access and are evidence of closure not being inevitable. Why: Grounded in comparative policy evidence showing measurable institutional survival outcomes (500–600 Polish social schools rescued, 37 Portuguese exceptional-authorization schools) attributable to specific funding-formula and legal-procedure design choices.
- **education_finance, legal_and_governance** (minority): Maintainer-change to church/private providers and centralized district structures redistribute costs and authority (church supplements ~5 billion HUF/year; 2023 free asset-transfer law causing municipal losses) without resolving the structural per-pupil cost disadvantage of small schools. Why: Grounded in fiscal-transfer evidence (church supplementary funding costs, 2023 asset-transfer law, tankerület system preserving cost problem) — a distributional/fiscal-accounting mechanism distinct from the pedagogical-viability argument on the other side.
