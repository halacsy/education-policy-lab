## Disagreement map
### Direction of causality between school closure and village depopulation
- **demography, equity_and_social_mobility, hungarian_education_system, portuguese_reform**: School closure actively accelerates depopulation by reducing quality of life, signaling civic disinvestment, and removing a young-family anchor institution, creating a feedback loop that deepens rural decline. Why: Rural transition literature and Central/Eastern European case studies document this feedback loop qualitatively, and community-anchor-institution loss is cited by parents and local advocates as a real, if unquantified, cost.
- **education_finance, international_comparison** (minority): The causal link between school closure and community/village decline is empirically contested or unresolved; closure may simply ratify decisions already made by out-migrating families rather than cause further decline. Why: International rural sociology literature is split, with some studies finding closure accelerates decline and others attributing both closure and decline to prior demographic collapse; no Hungary-specific panel data exists to resolve the direction.

### Whether financial savings from consolidation justify closure
- **education_finance, school_network_planning**: Claimed financial savings from school consolidation are systematically overstated; once transport, capital-repair backlogs, and lost local economic multipliers are counted, net savings shrink to 5–15% of gross per-pupil reduction, and the state (not local communities) captures the efficiency gain while communities bear the loss. Why: OECD/EI rural consolidation meta-analyses and post-2013 Hungarian centralization of funding show an incentive misalignment between who saves money (the state) and who bears cost (the village).
- **portuguese_reform, international_comparison** (minority): Consolidation, when paired with targeted pedagogical support (e.g., TEIP-type programmes) and curriculum reform, can produce measurable resource-concentration and achievement gains (e.g., Portugal's PISA improvement 2000–2015), suggesting efficiency gains are real if properly designed and mitigated. Why: Portuguese PISA trajectory improved alongside consolidation and targeted support programmes, though the analysis notes this correlation does not establish that consolidation itself (rather than concurrent reforms) drove the gain.

### Preferred policy model for Hungary going forward
- **finnish_reform** (minority): Hungary should prioritize system-wide uniformity (teacher training standardization, transparent cost allocation, statutory multi-tier pupil support) over preserving individual small-school buildings; the lesson from Finland is systemic reform, not building preservation. Why: Finland's own small-school count has declined substantially since the 1990s despite strong equity outcomes, suggesting building preservation was not the mechanism behind Finland's low between-school variance in pupil outcomes.
- **polish_reform, hungarian_education_system, school_network_planning, implementation_planning**: Hungary should pursue intermediate, settlement-specific alternatives to full closure (association-run schools, phase-out via felmenő rendszer, hub-and-feeder or multigrade models) evaluated case by case against commuting-time limits and community value, rather than a uniform national rule or default closure. Why: Poland's association-run schools and Hungary's own legal flexibility (maintainer plurality, felmenő rendszer) demonstrate that non-binary alternatives exist in comparable systems, though their scalability and outcomes remain undocumented.

### Political framing and durability of closure policy
- **political_feasibility** (minority): Closure is more politically durable when framed as community-led demographic adaptation rather than state-imposed efficiency, and centralized authority (KLIK) has shifted visible political risk upward, favoring quiet attrition over announced closures. Why: Rural villages form a core electoral constituency, creating incentives for the central government to avoid visible, concentrated closures; this is presented as weak, unverified structural political-economy reasoning.
- **legal_and_governance**: The core problem is not political framing but a governance and accountability gap: legally-compliant closure processes proceed without transparent outcome tracking, so alternatives (maintainer change, phase-out) remain ad hoc and undersystematized rather than durable policy instruments. Why: No national registry of closure decisions and community outcomes exists, and statutory consultation depth varies by municipal practice, indicating the gap is structural/administrative rather than purely about political messaging.
