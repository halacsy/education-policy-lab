# Synthesis

## Overview
Ten expert analyses converge on a shared empirical core: Hungary's rural primary-school network is being squeezed by a demographic collapse (fertility down to roughly 1.3, births falling double-digit percentages year over year) that is compounding a decade-plus contraction already visible in enrollment (−17.3% since 2005/06) and site counts (−88 sites 2021–2024). Beyond this shared factual base, the experts diverge sharply on what follows from it. International-comparison, Finnish, Polish and Portuguese case studies show that closure/consolidation outcomes are highly context-dependent — sometimes accelerating depopulation and harming disadvantaged pupils, sometimes proving neutral — and that alternatives (multigrade teaching, association-run schools, building repurposing, targeted subsidies) can delay but rarely reverse the underlying demographic arithmetic. The equity_and_social_mobility analysis adds a genuine counter-current: keeping small schools open without desegregation policy can itself entrench segregation via selective non-Roma out-migration, meaning neither blanket closure nor blanket preservation is equity-neutral. Legal, governance, political-feasibility and implementation-planning experts describe a system with formal safeguards (expert-opinion requirements, felmenő rendszer, an 8-child statutory minimum, a new government's no-top-down-closure pledge) that coexists with acute operational fragility (teacher shortages, fragile church/municipal co-financing arrangements, unfunded transport promises, non-existent Hungary-specific causal evidence on closure effects). The result is a genuinely unsettled policy space rather than a resolved one: the record supports strong claims about the scale and direction of demographic pressure, but only moderate-to-weak, often contested claims about which interventions durably counteract it, and it explicitly flags where Hungary-specific causal evidence is simply missing.

## Disagreement map
### Should small rural primary schools be closed/consolidated, or preserved through multigrade and community-hub models?
- **finnish_reform, polish_reform, school_network_planning**: Rural primary schools should be preserved as long as feasible via multigrade teaching, building repurposing, and community-hub investment, because closure accelerates depopulation and international precedent (Finland, Poland) shows consolidation savings are often illusory once transport and social costs are counted. Why: Danish and Finnish studies show closures accelerate population decline and self-reinforcing depopulation cycles; Finland's own cost-savings from closure remain unproven despite up to 2× per-pupil cost differences; Poland's association-run 'citizens' schools' and split-grade models demonstrate viable delay mechanisms.
- **portuguese_reform, equity_and_social_mobility** (minority): Preservation of small schools without accompanying pedagogical investment, desegregation policy, or quality upgrading can itself cause harm — entrenching segregation (Hungary) or failing to close achievement gaps (Portugal) — so neither closure-first nor preservation-first is defensible without conditional safeguards. Why: Portugal's 15-year consolidation and TEIP experience shows clustering met efficiency targets but did not close PISA-measured achievement gaps or reverse decline; Hungarian evidence shows between-school segregation is driven by keeping small rural schools open while non-Roma families selectively exit, tipping schools into self-reinforcing segregation once Roma share passes ~50%.

### Is school closure a primary driver of rural depopulation, or mainly a consequence/coincident marker of it?
- **finnish_reform, education_finance, demography**: School closure functions as a causal driver of depopulation, not merely a lagging response to it — Danish and Italian quasi-experimental evidence shows closures cause measurable population and income decline, particularly in peripheral areas. Why: Sørensen et al. (2021) find a 7.6-point 10-year population-decline effect in Denmark with lock-in effects from year six; Di Cataldo & Romani (2024) use an instrumental-variable design in Italy to show closures cause, not merely correlate with, population and income loss.
- **international_comparison** (minority): The causal link between school closure and depopulation is contested rather than universal — multiple countries show mixed or null population effects, so closure's causal weight relative to pre-existing decline is not established generally. Why: Sørensen et al.'s own literature review cites Johnson (1978), Amcoff (2012), Barakat (2015) and Kroismayr (2019) showing mixed or null effects across contexts, explicitly flagging the causal link as contested rather than settled.

### Can Hungary's current government protection pledge ('as long as demand exists') durably prevent closures, or is it rhetorically fragile against fiscal and demographic pressure?
- **political_feasibility, legal_and_governance**: The new government's commitments (KLIK abolition, restored strike rights, no top-down closures) represent a genuine structural shift creating institutional space for rural-school protection, though durability depends on unproven fiscal follow-through. Why: Minister Lannert Judit has explicitly pledged no top-down closures and a transparent audit; KLIK abolition and restored strike rights formally shift power toward schools/municipalities and strengthen union leverage in future negotiations.
- **political_feasibility, hungarian_education_system** (minority): The protection pledge is vulnerable to demographic collapse and operational funding failure — the Gersekarát church-municipal model collapsed within a year despite a signed 5-year contract, and unresolved wage disputes (NOKS) threaten the staffing base multigrade teaching would require, while the union head calls consolidation 'almost certainly unavoidable.' Why: Documented collapse of the Gersekarát five-year contract after one year, plus the PDSZ NOKS wage dispute remaining unresolved as of mid-2026, are cited as concrete evidence that formal commitments face fiscal and operational fragility independent of political intent.

### Is multigrade teaching a genuinely viable substitute for closure, or a resource-intensive stopgap with unproven Hungarian feasibility?
- **international_comparison, finnish_reform, polish_reform**: Multigrade schooling is an internationally established, pedagogically defensible alternative to closure with negligible achievement differences when well implemented, and should be scaled with adequate teacher training as Hungary's primary retention strategy. Why: A Danish study found an achievement effect size of only −0.06 for multigrade vs single-grade instruction; the model is widely used across France, Britain, Netherlands, Scandinavia, Scotland and North America and confirmed prevalent in a 2025 40-study systematic review.
- **implementation_planning, portuguese_reform** (minority): Multigrade teaching is documented to cost more per pupil, requires teacher retraining and materials rarely available in Hungary, and its rollout is constrained by an acute, worsening teacher-supply crisis (halved pedagogical-degree applicants, 40-50% dropout) and an aging teacher workforce nearing mass retirement. Why: MDPI/UNESCO sources cited by implementation_planning confirm multigrade teaching costs more per pupil and requires training rarely available; Portugal's teacher-age data (over half of 134,000 teachers over 50, 4,000 retirements projected in 2025 alone) illustrates the structural staffing constraint on any rural model.

## What the experts agree on
- Hungary's fertility rate has fallen sharply (to roughly 1.3 in 2025) and primary-school enrollment has been contracting for two decades, with the demographic cohort squeeze now directly hitting primary level. [evidence: strong]
- The number of primary-school sites in Hungary has declined measurably in recent years (roughly 88 fewer sites between 2021 and 2024), and hundreds of settlements now have no public educational institution at all. [evidence: strong]
- Teacher shortages, and especially the prevalence of unqualified teachers, are markedly more severe in rural/disadvantaged schools than in urban ones, constraining any rural school model regardless of enrollment policy. [evidence: strong]
- School transport (school-bus) provision in Hungary is fragile and has been documented to be cancelled or curtailed with little notice, creating an unreliable foundation for any consolidation strategy that depends on commuting. [evidence: moderate]
- No causal, Hungary-specific study currently isolates the effect of small-village primary-school closure on pupil achievement, attendance, population retention, or wellbeing; the applicable evidence base is drawn mainly from Denmark, Finland, Poland, Portugal, the US, Chile, and China. [evidence: weak]
- International consolidation research shows mixed, context-dependent effects on learning outcomes rather than a uniform pattern, meaning the direction and magnitude of any effect cannot be assumed to transfer automatically to Hungary. [evidence: contested]
