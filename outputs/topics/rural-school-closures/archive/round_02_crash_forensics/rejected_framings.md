# Rejected framings

Candidate framings generated per scenario; one selected, the rest recorded here with the reason for rejection.

## S1
- CHOSEN: Status quo plus incremental adaptation with systematic monitoring and desegregation safeguards
- REJECTED: Pure status quo with no monitoring or adaptation measures — reason: Fails to address the documented risk of SES/ethnic sorting as non-Roma families selectively exit when Roma enrollment share rises; without desegregation measures or transparency infrastructure (registry, outcome tracking), the scenario would entrench between-school inequality while claiming preservation.
- REJECTED: Status quo relying solely on teacher supply market without systemic training reform — reason: Directly contradicted by the scenario's own implementation timeline, which mandates multi-year recurring in-service training; this framing ignores the national data showing 7,700 unfilled teaching posts and a halved graduate pipeline, making it operationally incoherent.
- REJECTED: Retention at any enrollment level without cost/benefit verification against alternatives — reason: Removes the fiscal sustainability anchor; absent transparent cost-sharing rules and mandatory comparison to consolidation alternatives (per S5 framework), local negotiation becomes purely political rather than evidence-informed, risking locked-in inefficiency.

## S2
- CHOSEN: Structural grade reorganization with cross-party entrenchment and Hungary-specific feasibility study
- REJECTED: Direct transfer of Poland's 1999 reform model without institutional verification — reason: Poland's own 1999 reform was reversed after 17 years; this framing treats the institutional and governance context as a given rather than testing whether Hungary's constitutional, funding, and labor-relations structures can support the same mechanism, which the scenario itself labels contested.
- REJECTED: Grade consolidation with achievement-gap closure as the primary benefit justification — reason: The mechanism label for this claim is explicitly marked [evidence: contested], not strong; relying on contested evidence as the primary justification undermines the scenario's credibility when both the literature and Poland's reversal demonstrate weaker and more variable effects than the framing implies.
- REJECTED: Upper-grade consolidation without statutory transport guarantee or infrastructure pre-verification — reason: Violates the equity accountability standard; commuting infrastructure is known to be unreliable (e.g., Hódmezővásárhely 2023 bus cancellations, the unbuilt 2018 national school-bus network), so consolidation without a legally binding transport floor and pre-implementation capacity audit would predictably harm lower-SES and disability-affected families.

## S3
- CHOSEN: Networked retention through association-run and multi-service hub models with capacity-building support for weak villages
- REJECTED: Association-run schools as a scaling solution for all rural retention without viability thresholds — reason: The scenario's own evidence concedes that even association-operated schools face structural viability limits and 'will have to disappear' below sustainable enrollment; this framing elides the hard boundary between flexible governance and demographic unsustainability, presenting a governance fix as a solution to a demographic problem.
- REJECTED: Multi-service hub model as proven and broadly transferable without pilot testing — reason: The scenario rates multi-service hubs [evidence: weak] based on legal permission in Poland, not demonstrated outcome success; this framing confuses regulatory eligibility with operational efficacy, risking expensive repurposing investments that may not sustain service viability.
- REJECTED: Inter-municipal networking without addressing the Gersekarát precedent of multi-stakeholder cost-sharing collapse — reason: The documented collapse of the Gersekarát diocese-plus-3-municipalities arrangement after one year despite a signed five-year contract is a concrete failure precedent; dismissing it as an outlier rather than using it to design binding multi-year funding structures leaves the scenario vulnerable to the same failure mode.

## S4
- CHOSEN: Consolidation with mandatory receiving-school quality verification and disaggregated equity-outcome monitoring
- REJECTED: Consolidation with compensatory measures (transport, TEIP) as aspirational commitments without enforcement thresholds — reason: The scenario acknowledges TEIP's documented partial-failure record on closing achievement gaps but provides no mandatory quality/integration verification step before reassignment; compensatory measures without pre-verified receiving-school conditions are aspirational, not operative, and predictably replicate international consolidation harms.
- REJECTED: Consolidation relying on multi-year transport and TEIP funding commitments without statutory protection — reason: The scenario identifies that municipalities and the central budget must sustain commitments across administrations, but documents precedent (Gersekarát, school-bus cancellations) of funding-commitment collapse; a framing that assumes sustained funding without statutory floor protection ignores demonstrated political vulnerability.
- REJECTED: Consolidation treated as the default option without equivalent cost-accounting comparison to retention scenarios — reason: The scenario's evidence on cost savings is marked [evidence: moderate], acknowledging that estimates 'often undercount transport and other offsetting expenses'; treating consolidation as default without transparent multi-scenario cost comparison (per S5 framework) prejudges the outcome of analysis that has not yet occurred.

## S5
- CHOSEN: National framework with mandatory disaggregated data reporting and statutory funding protection for infrastructure continuity
- REJECTED: Registry and thresholds as advisory and non-binding mechanisms without political-neutrality enforcement — reason: The scenario correctly flags that the framework 'could itself become politicized'; framing it as advisory without statutory oversight and transparent methodology risks allowing successive administrations to selectively use or ignore thresholds depending on political priorities, converting the framework into a tool rather than a constraint.
- REJECTED: Evidence infrastructure without explicit equity-outcome disaggregation requirement — reason: The scenario notes that without mandatory capture and publication of data by SES, Roma/non-Roma, and disability status, the framework 'risks being equity-neutral in design while equity-consequential in effect'; this framing treats disaggregation as optional reporting rather than a structural requirement, leaving the framework unable to detect or prevent segregation harms.
- REJECTED: Registry design assuming district-level reporting compliance without audit mechanisms or prior-compliance verification — reason: The scenario identifies documented uncertainty about whether existing statutory obligations (expert-opinion requirements) are consistently observed; launching a registry without first auditing compliance with existing mandates and building audit enforcement into the design risks the same slow-walk pattern that has undermined prior statutory requirements.
