# Expert analysis: equity_and_social_mobility

## Findings (evidence)
- In Hungary, between-school segregation is markedly stronger than within-school segregation because local governments historically kept small single-class rural schools open rather than merging them, while non-Roma families increasingly enrol elsewhere ('white flight'). [evidence: moderate — CEU Center for Policy Studies, 'The Wider Context of the Segregation of Roma Pupils in Hungary' (2020), https://cps.ceu.edu/sites/cps.ceu.edu/files/attachment/publication/3258/cps-working-paper-educ-social-selection-education-hungary-2020.pdf]
- Once Roma enrolment share in a primary school passes roughly 50%, non-Roma exit accelerates, tipping the school into a self-reinforcing segregation spiral. [evidence: moderate — Havas–Liskó (2005), cited in CEU Center for Policy Studies working paper (2020)]
- Hungary's rural school network stayed largely intact through the 1990s–2000s despite falling funding shares; only centrally pressed amalgamations in 2004–2008 halved the number of independently run school units. [evidence: strong — Kovács & Váradi (2012), 'Rescuing a small village school in the context of rural change in Hungary', Journal of Rural Studies (ScienceDirect)]
- US administrative-data research finds school closures cause statistically significant drops in math and reading scores, and disproportionately raise absenteeism (7.3 percentage points) and disciplinary days (38% increase) among economically disadvantaged pupils, with long-term negative effects on higher-education attainment and wages. [evidence: strong — Kim (2024), reported in Education Week, June 2024, https://www.edweek.org/leadership/the-harm-of-school-closures-can-last-a-lifetime-new-research-shows/2024/06]
- In PISA 2022, Hungary's socioeconomic performance gap in mathematics ranks among the widest of all participating systems (just behind Romania and Slovakia) and has remained essentially unchanged since 2012. [evidence: strong — OECD, PISA 2022 Results Volume I, Hungary country note, https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/pisa-2022-results-volume-i-and-ii-country-notes_ed6fbcc5-en/]
- A Swedish quasi-experimental study using family fixed effects found school closures raise achievement only when displaced pupils move to substantially higher-performing peer groups; on average, no net achievement effect was detected across the sample studied. [evidence: moderate — ScienceDirect (2020), https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0927537120301147]
- US evidence shows that achievement gains for students displaced from closed low-performing schools often come at the cost of negative spillovers for incumbent peers in receiving schools, and gains materialise only when recipients move to significantly higher-performing schools. [evidence: moderate — Fordham Institute, https://fordhaminstitute.org/national/commentary/impact-school-closures-deep-dive-research]
- School closures and consolidation lengthen commutes, and longer bus rides are associated in urban and international studies with reduced attendance; causal rural-specific evidence on commuting burden remains limited. [evidence: moderate — Brookings Institution; Wiley/Canadian Geographic; ScienceDirect rural commuting studies]
- Schools serving a greater share of Black/marginalised students in the US are more likely to close regardless of school characteristics, and documented community-level harms include disruption to civic engagement and economic stability alongside individual-student displacement. [evidence: moderate — Research for Action, https://www.researchforaction.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/06/revisiting-research-on-school-closings-key-learnings-for-district-and-community-leaders.pdf]

## Interpretation
The Hungarian-specific evidence points to a distinct distributional dilemma: keeping tiny village schools open, absent active desegregation policy, tends to entrench between-school SES and ethnic sorting via selective out-migration rather than protect equity—Hungary already has one of the OECD's widest socioeconomic achievement gaps. Yet the international closure literature (mostly non-Hungarian) shows closure itself is not automatically equity-enhancing: benefits for displaced pupils depend entirely on whether the receiving school is genuinely stronger and integrated; closures without that guarantee replicate the achievement and attendance harms disproportionately borne by disadvantaged pupils. The two bodies of evidence identify different failure modes (segregative under-consolidation versus poorly managed over-consolidation) that both harm disadvantaged rural children and their communities.

## Assumptions
- Hungarian segregation dynamics documented mainly in settlement-level and larger samples generalise to sub-2,000-population villages in scope, though this has not been separately empirically verified. [assumption]
- Receiving-school quality and integration are the decisive moderators determining whether closure benefits or harms disadvantaged pupils, consistent with the international literature reviewed. [assumption]
- The mechanisms of selective out-migration observed in larger Hungarian settlements operate similarly in depopulating villages under 2,000 population, though the scale and speed may differ. [assumption]

## Position
Neither uniform closure nor uniform preservation of small rural schools serves equity in Hungary; the distributional outcome hinges on whether consolidation is paired with genuine desegregation and quality upgrading of receiving schools, versus keeping open under-resourced schools that, absent policy intervention, become vehicles for SES and ethnic sorting via selective out-migration.

## Uncertainties
- No causal, Hungary-specific study isolating the effect of small-village primary school closure on pupil achievement, attendance, or wellbeing has been located; quantitative closure-effect evidence is primarily from the United States, Sweden, Finland, and China. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A Hungarian administrative-data study (e.g. using KIR-STAT or national achievement registry data) comparing pupil trajectories before and after specific rural primary school closures with matched comparison controls.)
- The magnitude of current SES and ethnic sorting effects at the primary (grades 1–6) level specifically in sub-2,000-population villages, as distinct from settlement-level or secondary-level segregation figures, is not separately quantified in the sources reviewed. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Disaggregated national microdata (e.g. KSH/Eurydice/FRA sources) filtered by settlement population under 2,000 and primary grade level, with explicit reporting of between-school SES and ethnic sorting measures.)
- The extent to which rural primary school closure decisions in Hungary are influenced by equity considerations (desegregation, access for disadvantaged pupils) versus purely budgetary or administrative efficiency motives is unknown. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Interviews with and policy documents from municipal education authorities and regional government bodies detailing the rationale and equity-impact assessment (if any) of past closure and consolidation decisions.)
- Whether alternative service-delivery models (shared staffing, itinerant specialist support, digital learning supplements) could sustain educational quality and equity in very small primary schools without consolidation is not evidenced in the Hungarian context. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Pilot studies or implementation evaluations of alternative staffing, remote professional development, or blended-learning arrangements in Hungarian small rural primary schools, with outcome measures on equity and achievement.)
