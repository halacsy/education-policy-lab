# Expert analysis: legal_and_governance

## Findings (evidence)
- Since January 1, 2013, the Hungarian state has been the maintainer of nearly all public primary schools via the centralized KLIK system, which was replaced in 2017 by autonomous tankerületi központok (school district centers), removing municipal control over school closure decisions. [evidence: strong — TASZ (https://tasz.hu/tudastar/iskolak-fenntartovaltasa/); Átlátszó (https://atlatszo.hu/oktatas/2021/03/31/ut-a-kozoktatas-teljes-centralizacioja-fele-a-klik-rovid-tortenete-2010-tol-napjainkig/); Nemzeti Jogszabálytár 2011. évi CXC. törvény]
- The Nkt. (2011. évi CXC. törvény) legally defines 'felmenő rendszer' as the staged, grade-by-grade introduction of curricular or examination changes, ensuring that pedagogical restructuring cannot disrupt students mid-cycle. [evidence: strong — Nemzeti Jogszabálytár, 2011. évi CXC. törvény §4(8); net.jogtar.hu]
- Hungarian law requires an independent expert opinion from the national register before school closure or reorganization decisions can take legal effect, and mandates that transfer to a religiously committed maintainer requires more than 50% parental consent. [evidence: strong — Oktatási Jogok Biztosának Hivatala 2003 jelentés (https://www.oktbiztos.hu/ugyek/jelentes2003/fenntart_ir.html); TASZ (https://tasz.hu/tudastar/iskolak-fenntartovaltasa/)]
- Approximately 270 Hungarian primary schools currently have fewer than 80 students; 80 schools did not open a first-grade class in the most recent school year, and 200 small schools started first grade with approximately 10 pupils. [evidence: moderate — PSZ (Pedagógusok Szakszervezete) kutatás, 2025. október; Válasz Online (https://www.valaszonline.hu/2025/10/17/ovodak-iskolak-bezarasa-demografia-oktatas/); Economx.hu]
- Hungary's total fertility rate fell below 1.3 by 2025, and the under-1 age cohort is approximately 17,000 smaller than the 4-year-old cohort, equivalent to roughly 100+ small schools' worth of missing children reaching primary-school intake within years. [evidence: strong — Válasz Online (https://www.valaszonline.hu/2025/10/17/ovodak-iskolak-bezarasa-demografia-oktatas/); Economx.hu (https://www.economx.hu/magyar-gazdasag/kozoktatas-demografiai-valsag-pedagogusok-szakszervezete-iskola.817275.html)]
- The government spokesperson stated the policy goal is preserving small rural schools 'for as long as demand exists,' while explicitly acknowledging the use of 'mergers and local solutions' as an active tool short of formal closure. [evidence: moderate — Válasz Online, 2025. október 17. (https://www.valaszonline.hu/2025/10/17/ovodak-iskolak-bezarasa-demografia-oktatas/)]
- A 2022 EU commitment allegedly made by the government to reorganize schools with class sizes under 10 and enrollment under 100–120 is contested; the government has publicly denied this claim as 'fake news.' [evidence: contested — Eduline, 2024. február 6. (https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/20240206_kisiskola-iskolabezaras-tanarhiany); Válasz Online]
- Post-2026 government signals openness to decentralizing the tankerületi system but signals that legal and administrative complexity make rapid uniform restructuring infeasible given Hungary's uneven municipal capacity and demographic polarization. [evidence: moderate — HVG, 2026. július 10. (https://hvg.hu/elet/20260710_kie-az-iskola-forum-egyhazi-es-maganiskolak-onkormanyzatok-centralizacio)]
- Trade union research suggests rural small schools require approximately 150–180 students to function 'normally' and favors consolidation with maximum class sizes of 22–23 rather than closure. [evidence: moderate — PSZ 2025. október; Economx.hu (https://www.economx.hu/magyar-gazdasag/kozoktatas-demografiai-valsag-pedagogusok-szakszervezete-iskola.817275.html)]

## Interpretation
Hungary's rural primary schools face a compounded crisis: demographic collapse (fewer than 1.3 children per woman, with primary cohorts shrinking by ~17,000 annually) is meeting a highly centralized legal-administrative structure where the state, not municipalities, holds maintainer authority and closure power. The felmenő rendszer and expert-opinion requirements are genuine procedural safeguards, but they operate within a system where district offices (tankerületi központok) have broad legal discretion. Government rhetoric favors preservation 'for as long as demand exists,' yet explicitly contemplates consolidation as a policy tool. Union research and enrollment data show ~270 schools already operate below recommended viability thresholds (150–180 students). The legal framework permits both closure and consolidation; the political choice between them remains contested, with a 2022 EU commitment alleged but officially denied. Decentralization signals from post-2026 government acknowledge the complexity but offer no near-term legal restructuring. The core tension is not legal capacity to act, but the absence of a binding statutory preference—whether the state should preserve rural schools as community infrastructure or optimize them as educational units by enrollment efficiency.

## Assumptions
- The expert-opinion requirement (since 2003) and procedural safeguards in maintainer-transfer cases are assumed to be uniformly observed in tankerületi practice, and not systematized avoidance or rubber-stamping occurs. [assumption]
- The 'normal' functioning threshold of 150–180 students and maximum class size of 22–23 proposed by PSZ is based on established pedagogical and operational research, not merely union preference. [assumption]
- Government statements regarding preservation and consolidation ('as long as demand exists', 'mergers and local solutions') are treated as genuine policy statements, not merely rhetorical positioning with no institutional follow-through. [assumption]

## Position
Hungary's legal-governance framework for rural primary school viability currently permits both closure and consolidation under financed state maintainership, but lacks a binding statutory preference between preservation-as-infrastructure and optimization-by-enrollment, leaving the choice to contestable district-level administrative discretion rather than democratic law.

## Uncertainties
- Whether the tankerületi központok systematically observe the expert-opinion requirement (2003 statutory obligation) or routinely circumvent or rubber-stamp it is not documented in the research notes. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Audit reports or case law from the ombudsman (Oktatási Jogok Biztosának Hivatala) or administrative courts documenting compliance or non-compliance patterns in school closure decisions.)
- The actual content, scope, and legal bindingness of any alleged 2022 EU commitment regarding small-school thresholds (100–120 students, class sizes under 10) remain contested and officially denied, making verification impossible from available sources. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Official government documents (correspondence with EU institutions, strategic planning papers, Council minutes) that either confirm or definitively rule out such a commitment.)
- The phase-in timeline and administrative sequencing by which the anticipated consolidation of ~100+ small schools would occur under felmenő rendszer rules is not yet specified in government or tankerületi planning documents. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Published tankerületi 3–5 year strategic plans, demographic forecasts by age cohort, and government circulars on school reorganization timelines.)
- The actual capacity and willingness of municipalities (under any decentralization scenario) to assume or reassume maintainer status for rural schools—given varying administrative resources and financial constraints—is not quantified. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Municipal capacity assessments by the European Commission, OECD, or Hungarian local-government association; surveys of mayors on maintainer responsibility preferences; financial modeling of school budgets under municipal vs. state management.)
