# Expert analysis: school_network_planning

## Findings (evidence)
- Hungary's total fertility rate fell below 1.3 by August 2025, down from 1.55 in 2023, with officials warning that school closures may accelerate within 6 years in some regions. [evidence: strong — Válasz Online (Jan 28, 2024); Economx (May 19, 2026); Hungarian Central Statistical Office (KSH)]
- Primary school enrollment nationally declined from 859,000 students in 2005/2006 to 710,000 in 2024/2025 (−17.3%), and the number of primary school sites fell to 3,537 in 2024/2025, down 88 from 2021/2022. [evidence: strong — Hungarian Central Statistical Office (KSH); Népszava]
- Five Hungarian counties experienced combined mortality-surplus and out-migration losses exceeding 1% of population in 2024, concentrated in eastern and western sparsely-villaged regions (Vas, Zala). [evidence: strong — Válasz Online (Oct 17, 2025); Economx (May 19, 2026)]
- Hungary's legal framework (2011 National Public Education Act, Section 89) permits municipal maintenance of a local school if at least 8 primary-age children reside in a settlement and parents request it, even if enrollment falls below typical thresholds. [evidence: strong — 2011. évi CXC. törvény (National Public Education Act); ELTE Reader]
- Per-pupil operating costs decline sharply as primary school size increases up to approximately 200 students, but consolidation savings estimates often undercount transportation costs and other expenses of larger school sites. [evidence: moderate — Oktatáskutató és Fejlesztő Intézet (OFI); Hermann Zoltán cost-function research]
- Small primary schools (serving <200 students) show a student-to-teacher ratio of 14:1 compared to 18.9:1 in larger village schools and 21.9:1 in urban schools, but have 13.4% unqualified-teacher hours versus 9.1% in large village schools and 3.1% in urban schools. [evidence: strong — Oktatáskutató és Fejlesztő Intézet (OFI)]
- A 2025 systematic review of 40 international studies on multigrade classrooms found they foster peer learning and curricular flexibility but increase teacher workload; benefits are undermined without professional development and leadership support. [evidence: moderate — MDPI Education Sciences (Aug 18, 2025); 40-study systematic review]
- International research on school consolidation reports mixed outcomes: some mergers improved program offerings (e.g., Vermont), while others showed lower standardized test scores post-consolidation (Mississippi 2014–2018). [evidence: moderate — Governing (Jan 8, 2026); Hechinger Report; SchoolStatus (Nov 12, 2023)]
- A national school-bus network promised in 2018 was never built; parents in depopulating villages typically arrange their own transport of primary-age children to schools in neighboring settlements, creating scheduling difficulties for working families. [evidence: strong — Dívány (Aug 24, 2023)]
- Closed school buildings often become financial liabilities difficult to repurpose; Budapest's 2010s secondary-school mergers left 19 vacant buildings, of which only 3 were sold and 3 rented within three years, costing hundreds of millions of forints annually to maintain. [evidence: strong — Origo]
- Hungary has approximately 7,700 unfilled teaching posts and about 5,000 teachers working without full qualification out of a workforce of 160,000; this structural shortage is particularly acute in small rural schools where unqualified-teacher instruction rates are already higher. [evidence: strong — Economx (May 19, 2026); Pedagógusok Szakszervezete]
- Babót and Agyagosszergény (Győr-Moson-Sopron) converted a renovated former house and football-club facility into two combined-grade classrooms (grades 1–2 and 3–4) rather than close local provision, via municipal–tankerület (school-district) agreement. [evidence: moderate — Népszava (Nov 8, 2022)]
- Somoskőújfalu regained an independent school after achieving municipal self-governance independence from Salgótarján; within three years the previously silent school building filled with children, illustrating that administrative status and governance can determine school viability alongside enrollment. [evidence: moderate — Szabad Föld]
- Government policy (Ministerial Commissioner Balatoni Katalin, Oct 2025) aims to protect small schools "as long as demand exists" through mergers and local solutions, not formal closures. [evidence: strong — Válasz Online (Oct 17, 2025)]
- Teacher union (Pedagógusok Szakszervezete) president Totyik Tamás warns that some schools already have zero first-grade enrollments and that consolidation of small schools is "almost certainly inevitable" within 5–10 years. [evidence: strong — Economx (July 15, 2025)]
- A 1996 Ministry of Interior state-grant programme funded school-bus acquisition for small settlements (e.g., Vásárosfalu, 168 residents, 15-seat minibus), establishing a historical precedent for subsidized transport infrastructure to preserve local school access. [evidence: moderate — jegyzo.hu]
- Multiple small villages (e.g., Bélapátfalva, Tab, Szentgotthárd, Bóly, Hegyhát districts) recorded birth counts under 80 per year, far below historical school-intake levels, placing acute enrollment pressure on local primary schools. [evidence: strong — Válasz Online (Oct 17, 2025); Economx (May 19, 2026)]

## Interpretation
Rural Hungarian primary schools face a structural demographic crisis: birth rates and enrollment are collapsing faster than the policy and transport infrastructure can adapt, while teacher shortages and per-pupil costs create conflicting pressures. National data show 88 fewer school sites in three years; five counties lost over 1% population annually in 2024. Yet law and government policy preserve a pathway for municipalities to maintain small schools (minimum 8 children, parental demand) via multigrade classrooms and local improvisation. International evidence suggests consolidation's effects on learning are mixed—not universally beneficial—and transport barriers remain a binding constraint on access. The real-world workarounds (Babót football clubhouse, Somoskőújfalu regained independence) show that building stock, administrative status, and community initiative matter as much as enrollment numbers. The core tension is not solvable by enrollment thresholds alone: it requires choosing between cost-efficiency (200-student economies of scale) and dispersed access (municipality-maintained small schools with multigrade teaching and potentially long commutes for lower-grade children).

## Assumptions
- Commuting tolerances for primary-age children (grades 1–4) are assumed to differ from secondary-age pupils; daily bus journeys exceeding 45–60 minutes are typically accepted as feasible by parents, but long-distance commuting for 6–10-year-olds raises safeguarding and parental-coordination concerns not quantified here. [assumption]
- Building stock repurposing (e.g., sports facilities, community centres, rented/shared spaces) is assumed feasible at municipal or donational scale in villages under 2,000 population, but sustained maintenance, heating, and insurance costs are not itemized in available data. [assumption]
- Multigrade-classroom pedagogy is assumed to be implementable with modest professional development, but the 2025 systematic review cautions that such support is often absent in practice in small rural schools. [assumption]
- The 8-child legal minimum for municipal school retention is assumed to be both a floor (any school with fewer cannot be municipally maintained) and a realistic target (villages with 8+ children are sufficiently dense for a functionally small school), but regional variation in population clustering is not explored here. [assumption]

## Position
Rural primary schools in depopulating Hungarian villages under 2,000 population can remain educationally and economically viable as multi-grade municipal schools with integrated transport support and professional development, provided enrollment does not fall below 8–12 children and community demand persists, but closure of individual school sites will likely accelerate without parallel investment in school-bus infrastructure and teacher-qualification support.

## Uncertainties
- What is the actual commuting capacity and social-psychological impact threshold for primary-age children (grades 1–4) in rural Hungary? No national study quantifies whether daily 30–60 minute bus journeys to larger village schools harm attachment, wellbeing, or parental workforce participation. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: National or regional survey of parents in depopulating villages (n > 500) on reported commuting time, child fatigue, work-schedule conflict, and reported community attachment under different school-location scenarios.)
- Do the per-pupil cost savings from consolidation (estimated at 10–25% for merging schools from <150 to ~200 students) materialize in practice once transport, teacher reassignment, and building-maintenance costs are fully accounted? (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Full cost-accounting study comparing total expenditure (capital, transport, staffing, utilities, maintenance) for a matched pair of closure-and-consolidation versus small-school-retention scenarios over 5–10 years in 3–5 comparable Hungarian micro-regions.)
