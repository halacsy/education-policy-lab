# Expert analysis: implementation_planning

## Findings (evidence)
- Teacher shortages are most acute in rural areas, with the proportion of unqualified teachers in disadvantaged schools 2–3 times higher than in non-disadvantaged schools. [evidence: strong — EU Education and Training Monitor 2025 – Hungary]
- Trade unions estimate a national teacher vacancy of 12,000–16,000 positions, though government officials dispute whether a structural shortage exists. [evidence: contested — PDSZ (2023), Euronews (July 2023), Hungary Today]
- The pipeline of new teacher graduates has halved in recent years; fewer than 3,400 of 126,000 Hungarian university applicants in 2023 applied for pedagogical degrees, and dropout from pedagogical courses runs 40–50%. [evidence: strong — RFE/RL (June 2023)]
- Half of all primary and lower secondary schools had fewer than 150 pupils in 2020/2021, a fragmentation that directly constrains the ability to staff them with qualified subject teachers. [evidence: strong — European Commission country report (2022), Hungary Today]
- Hungarian law sets minimum class size at 14 and maximum at 27 for grades 1–4; maintainers decide class mergers unilaterally weeks before term start, often without prior municipal consultation, creating last-minute planning instability. [evidence: strong — Act CXC of 2011 Annex 4; Eduline (March 2025); Feol.hu (August 2023)]
- School closures and reorganizations are exclusive decisions of the state-appointed maintainer (since 2013 centralization), bounded by a requirement that continued service must not impose disproportionate burden on students/parents and must align with county development plans. [evidence: strong — National Public Education Act; Oktatási Jogok Biztosának Hivatala]
- Several municipalities discontinued school-bus services citing rising fuel and overhead costs despite buses being nearly the only transport option for children in outlying areas, demonstrating that transport-based consolidation is subject to withdrawal with little notice. [evidence: moderate — TASZ (autumn 2023/24)]
- National primary school enrollment is declining overall (2024/2025), while secondary and tertiary enrollment are rising, confirming that demographic pressure on rural primary schools is part of a broader national trend, not merely a village-level anomaly. [evidence: strong — KSH (2024/2025)]
- Multigrade teaching is a documented international alternative in rural, low-density areas (e.g. Spain's incomplete primary centers), but requires specific teacher training and materials rarely available and is documented to cost more per pupil than single-grade provision. [evidence: moderate — MDPI Education Sciences (2025); UNESCO IIEP (Brunswic)]
- Under Act LII of 2023, teacher salaries rose by an average of 32.2% in 2024, reaching 71.8% of average graduate earnings with a target of 80% by 2025, but the differential effect on rural recruitment has not yet been evaluated. [evidence: moderate — Eurydice; Act LII of 2023]

## Interpretation
Rural primary school viability in Hungary faces a multi-layered constraint. Demographic decline (primary enrollment down nationally) is compounded by severe, regionally concentrated teacher shortages (unqualified teachers 2–3 times more common in disadvantaged rural schools) and a collapsing teacher-preparation pipeline (new graduate applications halved, dropouts 40–50%). School fragmentation (half of primary schools under 150 pupils) directly impairs staffing capacity. Administrative rigidity—maintainers decide class mergers weeks before term, often bypassing local input—creates planning instability for families and municipalities. Recent salary increases (32% since 2024) are a necessary structural reform but show no measured rural-recruitment effect yet and are insufficient to reverse the applicant collapse within a normal 3–5 year planning window. Transport-based consolidation (school buses) is a fragile mitigation subject to municipal cost-cutting and can be withdrawn with little notice. Multigrade teaching is an international option but requires teacher retraining not yet in place and costs more per pupil. Together, these factors suggest that closing schools without comprehensive sequencing around teacher supply, administrative process reform, and sustainable transport/consolidation infrastructure will likely worsen rural depopulation and educational access, while preserving schools without addressing the staffing and demographic drivers will exhaust local and school-system resources.

## Assumptions
- Multi-year (3–5 year) demographic baselines are available and reliable for village-level primary school planning. [assumption]
- The legal requirement for 'no disproportionate burden' on families is a substantive constraint on closure decisions, not purely formal. [assumption]
- Salary reform alone (Act LII of 2023) will not reverse the applicant-pipeline collapse without complementary cultural and working-condition reforms. [assumption]
- Municipalities will prioritize school-bus maintenance if central funding is secured; absence of funding guarantees leads to discontinuation. [assumption]
- Administrative decentralization of class-opening decisions to local level is feasible within the current statutory framework. [assumption]
- Teacher training capacity exists (or can be rapidly expanded) to deliver multigrade-class pedagogy at scale if mandated. [assumption]

## Position
Rural primary school closure decisions in Hungary should be suspended for 3–5 years pending concurrent implementation of a teacher-recruitment support package (rural salary top-up, housing assistance, professional development priority), centralized school-bus funding guarantees, and a reformed administrative process that requires local-authority consultation 6 months before any reorganization decision, because current closures lack the staffing and transport infrastructure to ensure equitable educational access and will accelerate depopulation without addressing the documented supply-side constraints.

## Uncertainties
- The actual effect of the 32.2% teacher salary increase (2024) on rural recruitment rates and retention has not yet been measured or disaggregated by region. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Post-implementation evaluation data from Ministry of Education (2025–2026) comparing rural versus urban teacher hiring and retention in two cohorts (pre- and post-salary reform).)
- No quantitative evidence yet exists comparing the cost-effectiveness of school closure with consolidation (multigrade teaching, transport provision) in a Hungarian rural context. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Pilot cost analysis in 2–3 representative multi-village districts, comparing (a) status quo closure, (b) consolidated multigrade model with shared transport, and (c) hybrid models over a 5-year period, with transparent pupil and teacher cost accounting.)
- The administrative and governance feasibility of requiring 6-month local consultation on school reorganization, given current legal authority vesting exclusively in the centralized maintainer, is unclear. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Legal and governance review by the Office of the Hungarian Education Ombudsman (Oktatási Jogok Biztosának Hivatala) assessing whether Act CXC (2011) amendments could mandate local consultation without creating statutory conflicts or delaying decisions impermissibly.)
- The stability and sustainability of school-bus provision as a consolidated-school mitigation is not quantified; TASZ reports discontinuation due to municipal budget constraints, but no systematic audit of bus services nationwide exists. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A nationwide (or representative multi-county) audit of school-bus service continuity 2020–2025, documenting suspension/resumption events, funding sources, and operating costs, led by KSH or Ministry of Education.)
