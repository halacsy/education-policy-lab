# Expert analysis: education_finance

## Findings (evidence)
- By 2024, more than 1,200 of 3,155 settlements in Hungary had no public educational institutions, and primary education provision sites fell from 3,609 to 3,521 between 2021 and 2024. [evidence: strong — Shrinking, growing or overburdened? Territorial patterns in child demographics and institutional burden in Hungary's public education system after 2021 (2025), KIR data]
- Rural areas have child-teacher ratios as high as 24:1 in some disadvantaged settlements, compared to the OECD average of 14:1, with up to 94% of disadvantaged children concentrated in certain rural locations. [evidence: strong — Shrinking, growing or overburdened? Territorial patterns in child demographics and institutional burden in Hungary's public education system after 2021 (2025)]
- State funding of small village primary schools fell from 80–90% of total costs in the late 1990s to 50–70% by the early 2000s, reducing municipal capacity to sustain underenrolled schools. [evidence: moderate — Rescuing a small village school in the context of rural change in Hungary, ScienceDirect]
- Hungary spends USD 7,072 per full-time equivalent student in public primary education, compared to the OECD average of USD 11,914, constraining operational flexibility in small schools. [evidence: strong — OECD, Education at a Glance 2024, Hungary Country Note]
- Teacher shortages are most acute in rural disadvantaged schools, where the proportion of unqualified teachers is 2–3 times higher than in non-disadvantaged schools. [evidence: strong — European Commission, Education and Training Monitor 2025, Hungary]
- In 2022, 31% of Hungarian municipalities had no kindergarten, typically small villages where child cohort sizes fell below 8, signalling early demographic pressure on primary school networks. [evidence: strong — European Commission, Education and Training Monitor 2025, Hungary]
- An instrumental-variable study of Italian municipalities found that school closures caused measurable reductions in population and income, with effects concentrated in peripheral areas distant from alternative schools, demonstrating closure as a driver—not merely a consequence—of depopulation. [evidence: strong — Di Cataldo & Romani, Rational cuts? The local impact of closing undersized schools, Regional Science and Urban Economics 109 (2024)]
- Since 2022, Hungary's fertility rate has fallen to 1.38 and depopulation is accelerating; post-pandemic migration that had favoured villages reversed in 2023, with capital-ward migration resuming and village migration gains declining sharply. [evidence: strong — OSW Centre for Eastern Studies, Hungary's ongoing demographic decline (2025); KSH Population and vital events]
- OECD policy analysis recommends that where school closures are necessary, primary schools should be prioritised to remain open over secondary schools because primary school presence influences residential choices and sustains local community vitality. [evidence: strong — OECD, Preparing for Demographic Change in Campania, Italy (2025)]
- National-level coverage statistics (2024) show 18% of Hungary's 9.585 million population in Budapest, 53% in towns, and 30% in villages—but rural settlements are increasingly concentrated below size thresholds that support standalone schools. [evidence: strong — KSH Population and vital events; OSW Centre for Eastern Studies]
- Multigrade composite classrooms (összevont osztály) are used as an operational adaptation in small Hungarian village schools to reduce per-pupil cost below closure threshold, though they raise separate pedagogical-equity concerns outside the finance domain. [evidence: moderate — Multigrade rural school research and Hungarian education practice]
- In Ireland in 2025, eight primary schools closed with enrollments of 1–6 pupils; one school offered €2,000 relocation incentives to families rather than close immediately, indicating the threshold-crossing logic of ultra-small school viability. [evidence: moderate — RTE, Eight primary schools to close permanently from today (June 2025)]
- The operational sustainability of small village schools depends critically on state co-financing; when central support declines and municipalities cannot fully substitute, closure becomes inevitable without alternative revenue or model innovation. [evidence: moderate — Rescuing a small village school in the context of rural change in Hungary; historical funding mechanics]
- Digital and blended learning models are proposed by OECD as pedagogically viable alternatives to closure that can reduce per-pupil cost while preserving community access and engagement, though Hungarian implementation feasibility is uncertain. [evidence: moderate — OECD, Preparing for Demographic Change in Campania, Italy (2025)]

## Interpretation
Rural primary school viability in Hungary is under acute structural pressure from three interlocking trends: demographic decline (fertility falling, urban re-migration accelerating), fiscal constraint (per-pupil spending 40% below OECD norms, state co-financing eroded), and geographic polarization (teacher shortages and concentrations of disadvantage in isolated settlements). The evidence shows that closures, while superficially rational on efficiency grounds, function as drivers—not merely responses—to depopulation in spatially isolated areas. The territorial equity gap (1,200+ settlements without educational institutions, 24:1 ratios in some rural schools) now appears structurally unsustainable. However, the research also indicates that primary school presence—more than secondary school—influences residential choice and community vitality. The policy tension is real: maintaining every undersized school is operationally inefficient and fiscally unaffordable; yet closing them accelerates exit spirals that damage the viability of rural settlements themselves. Alternative models (multigrade composites, blended learning, voluntary associations of municipalities to pool resources and pupils) exist but require upfront investment and institutional coordination that current fiscal and governance structures do not automatically enable.

## Assumptions
- The demographic decline in villages (below-replacement fertility, outmigration) will continue or accelerate for at least the next 10 years in the absence of major policy interventions (e.g., housing subsidies, employment creation) outside the education sector. [assumption]
- Current per-pupil funding formulas in Hungary do not systematically adjust upward for sparsely populated areas; small schools are assumed to operate under the same per-pupil budget as larger ones, creating a structural cost disadvantage. [assumption]
- Closure of a primary school is assumed to reduce per-pupil cost in the system but increase transport burden and out-of-pocket expense for rural families, shifting costs from the state budget to households. [assumption]
- Teacher shortages in rural areas are driven primarily by geographical isolation and low career incentives, not by absolute teacher supply shortage (Hungary has trained teacher surpluses overall), implying that reallocation or differentiated incentives could improve rural staffing. [assumption]
- Small village schools can operate sustainably if they adopt multigrade composites, blended learning, or enter municipal associations that pool pupils and resources, but this requires local institutional capacity and willingness that varies widely. [assumption]
- Communities perceive the presence of a local primary school as a marker of local identity and settlement viability; closure signals decline and accelerates selective out-migration of families with children. [assumption]
- Central government retains the primary fiscal responsibility for primary education in Hungary even when schools are municipally managed, and any reduction in state support forces difficult trade-offs at the local level. [assumption]

## Position
Rural primary school closure in Hungary accelerates demographic decline in peripheral settlements and is therefore ill-suited as a primary cost-control mechanism without prior implementation of alternative operational models (multigrade composites, blended learning, municipal resource associations) and targeted fiscal support to offset the increase in household transport costs.

## Uncertainties
- The precise number of Hungarian primary schools currently operating below enrollment thresholds that trigger unsustainable per-pupil cost (e.g., <30, <50, <70 pupils) is not publicly available and would require KIR microdata access. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Publication of disaggregated KIR school-level data on 2024 enrollment, staff costs, and operational budgets by settlement size and region.)
- The operational feasibility and cost-benefit of blended learning or online teaching at scale in Hungarian rural primary schools has not been studied; it is unclear whether ICT infrastructure, teacher training capacity, or student engagement losses would outweigh per-pupil cost savings. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Pilot implementation of blended primary education in 5–10 Hungarian village schools with formal cost-tracking and pedagogical outcome measurement over 2–3 years.)
- The causal impact of school closure on subsequent out-migration from a Hungarian rural settlement (as distinct from its correlation with pre-existing decline) has not been empirically established; the Italian instrumental-variable study is suggestive but not directly transferable. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Longitudinal household-level administrative data linkage (KIR × tax office × migration registry) comparing settlements that closed schools to matched controls, controlling for pre-closure demographic trends.)
- The willingness and capacity of Hungarian municipalities to form resource-sharing associations (voluntary local government alliances) to sustain small schools is unclear; historical attempts (1997 onwards) show mixed results and limited uptake. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Survey of all Hungarian municipalities under 2,000 population on attitudes, barriers, and incentive structures for joining or forming school resource associations; case studies of successful and failed voluntary alliances.)
- The quantified impact of school closure on household transport costs, school start times, and student absenteeism in Hungarian rural areas is not documented; international estimates vary widely (USD 500–2000 per family per year). (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Detailed cost survey of rural families whose children are bussed to consolidated schools, combined with absenteeism and academic performance data from schools receiving bussed-in pupils.)
- The sustainability timeline for small village schools under different intervention scenarios (closure, consolidation, multigrade composite with support, municipal alliance) is not modelled for Hungary; forecasts depend on unknown future fertility, migration, and state funding trajectories. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Development of a multi-scenario cohort-component projection model for Hungarian primary school enrollment under fertility, migration, and central funding assumptions, disaggregated by settlement size and region to 2050.)
