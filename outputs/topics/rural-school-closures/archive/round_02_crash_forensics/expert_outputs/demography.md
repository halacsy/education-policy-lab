# Expert analysis: demography

## Findings (evidence)
- Hungary's total fertility rate fell to 1.31 children per woman in 2025, down from 1.39 in 2024, with only 72,000 births recorded—a 7.1% annual decline and the lowest level in 14 years. [evidence: strong — Daily News Hungary (https://dailynewshungary.com/hungary-population-drops-births-low/)]
- Hungary's population fell below 9.5 million for the first time in decades (9.489 million at end-2025), with natural population decrease of 52,200 in 2025 alone (4.5% worse than prior year). [evidence: strong — Daily News Hungary (https://dailynewshungary.com/hungary-population-drops-births-low/)]
- More than 1,200 of Hungary's 3,155 settlements had no public educational institutions by 2024, with five identified settlement typologies showing deepening inequality between dynamic urban zones and disadvantaged rural peripheries. [evidence: strong — ResearchGate study 'Shrinking, growing or overburdened?' (https://www.researchgate.net/publication/396483025_Shrinking_growing_or_overburdened_Territorial_patterns_in_child_demographics_and_institutional_burden_in_Hungary's_public_education_system_after_2021)]
- Internal migration patterns reversed from 2023 onward: the pandemic-driven rural repopulation trend stalled, with Budapest regaining migration gains while village migration gains declined significantly. [evidence: moderate — KSH Population and vital events (https://www.ksh.hu/population-and-vital-events)]
- Teacher shortages are most acute in rural areas, with unqualified teacher proportions in disadvantaged schools 2–3 times higher than non-disadvantaged schools; over half of all teachers in 2024/2025 were aged 50+, while only 7.2% were under 30. [evidence: strong — Education and Training Monitor 2025 – Hungary (https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html)]
- Primary school enrollment decreased in the 2024/2025 school year despite increases in kindergarten and secondary/tertiary levels, confirming demographic cohort squeeze is now hitting primary level specifically. [evidence: moderate — KSH Education page (https://www.ksh.hu/education)]
- UN medium-fertility projections show Hungary's population falling from 9.686 million (2023) to 7.461 million by 2100; the under-20 population is projected to decline from ~200,000 to 140,000 by 2060, reflecting school-age cohort contraction through 2040. [evidence: strong — Our World in Data (https://ourworldindata.org/profile/population-demography/hungary) and KSH SEEMIG projections (https://www.ksh.hu/docs/eng/seemig/outputs/SEEMIGPopulationProjectionsHUSK.pdf)]
- Hungary's teacher salary reform (2024–2031), backed by €1.8 billion in EU funds, aims to raise average teacher pay to 80% of graduate wage by 2025; this policy targets rural/disadvantaged school staffing viability as a secondary demographic forcing factor. [evidence: strong — European Social Fund Plus (https://european-social-fund-plus.ec.europa.eu/en/projects/hungary-raises-teachers-wages-eu-support)]
- Village population share stands at approximately 30% of Hungary's 9.585 million population (as of January 2024), while Budapest comprises 18% and towns 53%. [evidence: strong — KSH (https://www.ksh.hu/population-and-vital-events)]

## Interpretation
Hungary faces a structural demographic collapse that is hollowing out rural school networks through the joint effect of fertility collapse, natural decrease, reversed migration, and accelerating aging. The 2024–2025 data confirm the cohort squeeze has now reached primary level; meanwhile, >1,200 settlements already lack any public education, and rural teacher shortages (exacerbated by age imbalance) make small school staffing economically and operationally unviable independent of enrollment. The brief rural salary reforms mitigate but do not reverse this pressure. Under UN projections, school-age cohorts will continue contracting through 2040, making every rural closure decision made now effectively permanent—settlements losing their schools face near-irreversible loss of institutional connectivity and demographic gravitational pull. The policy forcing-function is not only budget but population arithmetic itself.

## Assumptions
- The 2024–2025 primary enrollment decline and projections to 2040 reflect continued net outmigration from villages and low birth replacement. [assumption]
- Village depopulation is driven primarily by fertility collapse and migration loss, not temporarily reversible economic cycles. [assumption]
- Settlements losing primary schools do not recover full educational access through remote/digital provision within a generation. [assumption]
- Teacher salary reform does not reverse the structural push toward school closures; it only delays or softens the consolidation pressure. [assumption]

## Position
Rural primary school closures through 2040 will be driven primarily by demographic cohort collapse and reversed migration, making school network consolidation inevitable unless structural policies (e.g., consolidated transport hubs, multi-settlement learning centers, or permanent subsidies) actively counteract the population arithmetic.

## Uncertainties
- Current precise tally of primary schools operating in villages under 2,000 population and the rate of closures/consolidations in 2023–2026 is unknown. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Direct access to KSH or Hungarian Ministry of Education databases disaggregated by settlement size and school status (operating / closed / merged) for 2024–2026.)
- Long-term behavioral response of families to school closures—whether they accelerate out-migration, accept longer commutes, or seek private alternatives—is not quantified in available research. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Longitudinal household migration and education choice surveys in regions experiencing rural school closures, tracking actual family movements and school enrollment choices over 5–10 years.)
- The viability and uptake of alternative educational models (consolidated learning hubs, multi-settlement shared schools, remote/hybrid provision) in Hungarian rural contexts has not been tested at scale. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Pilot programme results from 3–5 settlements testing consolidated transport-linked school centers or inter-settlement learning networks, with 2–3 year outcome data on enrollment, transport burden, and community engagement.)
