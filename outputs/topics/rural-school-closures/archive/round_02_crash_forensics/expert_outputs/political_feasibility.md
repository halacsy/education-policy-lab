# Expert analysis: political_feasibility

## Findings (evidence)
- A new education minister (Lannert Judit, Tisza Party, appointed May 2026) has explicitly committed to no top-down closures of small-settlement primary schools and promised a transparent national school-network audit. [evidence: strong — Borsodi Hír, https://borsodihir.hu/hir/a-tisza-nem-tervezi-a-kistelepulesi-iskolak-bezarasat; Népszava, https://nepszava.hu/3326468_helyreallitjak-a-pedagogusok-sztrajkjogat-a-tisza-kormany-tarsadalmi-egyeztetesre-bocsatotta-a-torvenytervezetet]
- The new government plans to abolish KLIK (Klebelsberg Institution Maintenance Center) and restore school-level and municipal decision-making authority over maintenance and operational matters, shifting power away from district-level authorities. [evidence: strong — Eduline, https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/20260414_oktatasi-tisza-part-magyar-peter]
- Legislation in public consultation (closed June 27, 2026) would restore free strike rights for education employees and broaden school directors' employer powers, strengthening formal leverage of rural teachers and unions in future consolidation negotiations. [evidence: strong — Népszava, https://nepszava.hu/3326468_helyreallitjak-a-pedagogusok-sztrajkjogat-a-tisza-kormany-tarsadalmi-egyezzetesre-bocsatotta-a-torvenytervezetet]
- Birth numbers in Hungary have dropped approximately twenty percent over the past four years, creating structural demand collapse: at least 600–800 kindergarten groups' worth of children are missing, equivalent to capacity of 100+ small schools. [evidence: strong — Válasz Online, https://www.valaszonline.hu/2025/10/17/ovodak-iskolak-bezarasa-demografia-oktatas/]
- A municipal-church-shared-maintenance model (Gersekarát, Vas county: Diocese + 3 municipalities, 128 million HUF annual cost) collapsed after one year despite a signed five-year contract, with municipalities announcing withdrawal from the cost-sharing agreement. [evidence: strong — Ugytudjuk.hu, https://ugytudjuk.hu/cikk/2026-05-14_a-felso-tagozatok-bezarasnak-reme-fenyegeti-a-gersekarati-iskolat]
- The PDSZ union successfully mobilized 3,600+ NOKS (support staff) respondents with 94% strike-willing-ness in early 2026, though a scheduled March 30 strike did not proceed after government legal countermeasures, demonstrating both union capacity and state willingness to use courts to prevent teacher support-staff actions. [evidence: strong — Népszava, https://nepszava.hu/3316769_noks-ovodai-iskolai-dolgozok-berrendezes-pdsz; Eduline, https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/20260608_pdsz-sztrajk-noks-dolgozok-beremeles-oktatasi-miniszter]
- Grassroots mobilization capacity for school closures has diminished since the 2000s closure wave: in most small villages today, resistance is muted because declining child numbers and rising costs are already visible, and financially drained municipalities cannot absorb deficits without external support. [evidence: moderate — Válasz Online, https://www.valaszonline.hu/2025/10/17/ovodak-iskolak-bezarasa-demografia-oktatas/]
- International comparative evidence indicates that school district consolidation frequently fails to deliver expected cost savings, a finding likely to be invoked by teacher unions and parent groups opposing closures in Hungarian discourse. [evidence: moderate — Rural Schools Open, https://ruralschoolsopen.org/fight-closure/]
- The PDSZ union is seeking a gross wage increase for roughly 67,000 NOKS support staff from approximately 410,500 HUF to 718,000 HUF (estimated annual cost 134.6 billion HUF, ~0.3% of central budget); resolution status as of June 2026 remains uncertain, with potential for renewed strike action in autumn 2026. [evidence: strong — Népszava, https://nepszava.hu/3316532_noks-ovodai-iskolai-dolgozok-berrendezes-pdsz; Eduline, https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/20260608_pdsz-sztrajk-noks-dolgozok-beremeles-oktatasi-miniszter]

## Interpretation
The political environment for rural primary-school policy has shifted decisively. The new Tisza government has publicly committed to protecting small schools and has begun structural decentralization (KLIK abolition, restored strike rights, local governance), creating formal space for rural schools to persist. However, this political commitment faces two critical durability risks. First, demographic collapse is structural and independent of political will: birth rates have fallen 20% in four years, creating inevitable pressure for consolidation regardless of ministerial rhetoric. Second, alternative funding models (church-municipality partnerships, multigrade support systems) have proven fragile even when contractually committed, as shown by the Gersekarát collapse. The NOKS wage dispute signals a test case: if the new government fails to fund support-staff adequately, the infrastructure for multigrade teaching in small schools will erode, forcing closures by attrition rather than policy. Current low grassroots mobilization reduces political risk of unrest but also reduces pressure for compensatory investment. The durability of the "protection" pledge depends entirely on fiscal commitment and successful operational models—neither yet proven.

## Assumptions
- The new government will maintain political continuity on rural-school protection rhetoric through at least one budget cycle (2027–2028), absent a major electoral shock or cabinet resignation. [assumption]
- Teacher unions will actively mobilize in favour of rural-school preservation if workload and compensation conditions are not met, exercising restored strike rights. [assumption]
- Multi-stakeholder (church, municipality, state) funding arrangements will be attempted for at least some pilot locations, despite the Gersekarát precedent. [assumption]
- Multigrade teaching, if rolled out, will require sustained in-service training, workload adjustment, and support-staff expansion—none of which are currently budgeted at scale. [assumption]
- The political salience of rural-school closures remains low in national discourse absent major local incidents, meaning reversals could occur without triggering significant public resistance. [assumption]
- Demographic projections (20% birth decline) are accurate and will translate into proportional classroom closures by 2030 absent migration inflows or immigration policy changes. [assumption]

## Position
The new Hungarian government's pledge to protect small rural primary schools is politically durable in rhetoric and formal governance structure (decentralization, restored rights) but vulnerable to demographic collapse and operational funding failure, particularly if the NOKS wage dispute remains unresolved and no proven multigrade-teaching model emerges by 2027.

## Uncertainties
- Will the government actually fund the NOKS wage increase (134.6 billion HUF), or will fiscal pressure force a de facto compromise that leaves support-staff inadequately resourced for multigrade expansion? (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Autumn 2026 budget announcement and NOKS wage negotiation outcome; specific line-item allocation for NOKS in the 2027 budget; PDSZ strike committee activity or restraint in Q4 2026.)
- Will the KLIK abolition actually restore meaningful decision authority to rural schools and municipalities, or will district-level or ministry-level control persist informally, undoing the decentralization intent? (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Implementation of KLIK-abolition legislation (text, timeline, transition authority); documented case studies of rural schools making autonomous decisions (maintenance, staffing, pedagogy) by mid-2027; municipalities' actual budget and staffing authority post-transition.)
- What proportion of small schools will adopt multigrade teaching models, and will quality outcomes (literacy, numeracy, social development) be comparable to single-grade cohorts, or will multigrade instruction mask inequality without teacher training? (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Ministerial guidance or regulation on multigrade pedagogy (pedagogical standards, teacher training requirements, classroom resource norms); pilot evaluation reports comparing multigrade and single-grade cohorts on standardized assessments (reading, numeracy, social-emotional) by 2028; teacher satisfaction surveys on workload and preparation time.)
