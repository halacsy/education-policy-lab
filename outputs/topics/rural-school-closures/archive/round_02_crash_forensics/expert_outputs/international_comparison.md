# Expert analysis: international_comparison

## Findings (evidence)
- Closure of rural schools in Denmark increased population decline by 7.6 percentage points over 10 years, with effects becoming significant from year six onwards due to lock-in effects on social capital and housing markets. [evidence: strong — Sørensen, Svendsen, Jensen & Schmidt, Journal of Rural Studies 87 (2021)]
- Prior cross-national literature on rural school closures shows mixed or null population effects in multiple countries, making the causal link between closure and depopulation contested rather than universal. [evidence: contested — Sørensen et al. (2021) literature review; Johnson (1978), Amcoff (2012), Barakat (2015), Kroismayr (2019)]
- School consolidation reduced student test scores by 5.9% of a standard deviation in Denmark, but negative effects diminished over two to four years, suggesting disruption costs can be offset by smaller class sizes or instructional support. [evidence: moderate — Beuchert et al., Economics of Education Review (2018)]
- School consolidation effects vary by country context: the United States shows overall positive or no lasting adverse effects, while Denmark, Chile, and China report negative effects on educational attainment, indicating that institutional and geographic factors mediate outcomes. [evidence: strong — Zhang (2024) working paper; Brummet (2014), Carlson & Lavertu (2016), Engberg et al. (2012), Hannum & Wang (2022)]
- In Chile, school closure increased high-school dropout probability by 49–68% and caused large causal effects on primary-school grade repetition. [evidence: moderate — Grau, Hojman, and Mizala (2018) cited in Hannum & Wang]
- In China, girls exposed to school consolidation during primary school ages experienced an average 0.60-year reduction in completed schooling; impacts were disproportionate for girls and low-socioeconomic-status families because enrollment decisions for girls are more sensitive to distance and travel burden. [evidence: strong — Hannum & Wang (2022); Fan et al. school consolidation ethics-language study]
- In India (Rajasthan), school consolidation caused greater enrollment decline among students with disabilities and students from Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes compared to general enrollment trends. [evidence: moderate — Bordoloi & Shukla (2019) cited in Hannum & Wang (2022)]
- OECD PISA evidence shows that rural–urban achievement gaps generally disappear after accounting for socioeconomic composition and that rural-urban achievement differences do not exist in almost half of developed PISA-participating countries, indicating policy and resourcing—not rurality itself—drive observed gaps. [evidence: strong — OECD Education Working Paper No. 196 (2019); Australia–Canada–New Zealand PISA comparison study]
- Multigrade schooling is an internationally established and still-active alternative to school closure in rural areas of North America, Europe (including France, Britain, Netherlands, Scandinavia, Scotland), and other regions, with recent systematic review (2019–2024) confirming its prevalence in addressing demographic constraints. [evidence: strong — World Bank foundational review; MDPI systematic review (2025, 40 studies); UNESCO]
- Achievement differences between well-implemented multigrade and single-grade instruction are negligible (effect size −0.06 in a Danish study controlling for covariates), but multigrade teaching is labor-intensive and requires higher professional development than conventional graded instruction. [evidence: moderate — US federal multigrade classroom handbook; Kral (1995) Danish study]
- In Hungary, early school leaving is higher in villages and among Roma (58.7% vs. 9.3% non-Roma in 2023), and kindergarten coverage is uneven—31% of municipalities had no kindergarten in 2022, typically in small villages with fewer than 8 kindergarten-aged children, establishing a precedent for enrollment-threshold-driven service withdrawal affecting disadvantaged populations. [evidence: strong — European Commission Education and Training Monitor 2025 – Hungary]
- In Poland, a large-N study of 42,400 localities and 13,100 schools found that school presence in smaller localities correlates strongly with population size, particularly in the 400–700 inhabitant range, and school size normalizes above 4,100 population—establishing a quantitative anchor for understanding sustainability thresholds in post-socialist settlement structures similar to Hungary's. [evidence: moderate — Polish locality–school-size study cited in Sørensen et al. (2021)]
- Hungary-specific causal evidence (difference-in-differences or quasi-experimental studies) directly estimating achievement or population effects of rural primary school closures or consolidations is absent from accessible international databases and recent literature. [evidence: weak — research limitation disclosure]

## Interpretation
International evidence on rural school closures reveals a causal complexity: while Denmark shows strong population-decline effects and Chile severe dropout effects, outcomes vary significantly by institutional context, distance-sensitivity of enrollment decisions, and socioeconomic composition. Achievement disruption from consolidation is real but temporary and compensable. Multigrade schooling is a viable, internationally practiced alternative to closure that preserves community infrastructure and avoids lock-in effects, though it demands higher teacher support. Critically, OECD data and equity evidence from China and India show that disadvantage correlates with demographic vulnerability—rural closures disproportionately harm girls, low-SES families, and marginalized groups. Hungary's own data on Roma early-leaving (58.7%) and kindergarten service withdrawal by enrollment threshold suggests that closure decisions will likely replicate and compound existing rural–disadvantage patterns. The transferability question hinges on whether Hungary's context (post-socialist settlement structure, Roma concentration in small villages, transport infrastructure) more closely resembles Denmark/Chile (where closures matter) or the US (where consolidation showed mild effects). Poland's quantitative threshold evidence (400–700 inhabitant range, 4,100 normalcy point) suggests that villages under 2,000 are in a high-risk zone for service sustainability decisions.

## Assumptions
- The extent of child travel burden, transport infrastructure quality, and receiving-school capacity in Hungary are sufficiently similar to Denmark/Chile to make their causal estimates partially transferable to the Hungarian case. [assumption]
- The socioeconomic and demographic composition of Hungary's small villages (under 2,000 population) shares key characteristics with post-socialist rural contexts where consolidation has been studied (Poland, China, parts of Central Europe). [assumption]
- Equity implications of rural school closure (disproportionate impact on girls, Roma, low-SES families) in Hungary will mirror international patterns unless Hungary implements targeted compensatory measures (e.g., transport subsidies, boarding support). [assumption]
- Teacher professional development, curriculum adaptation, and administrative support systems for multigrade instruction in Hungary are either already in place or can be built with reasonable resource investment. [assumption]

## Position
Rural primary schools in Hungarian villages under 2,000 population should not be closed on enrollment grounds alone; international evidence indicates that consolidation causes disproportionate harm to girls, Roma, and low-SES families, while multigrade alternatives, supported by adequate teacher training and transport provision, can preserve both educational quality and community infrastructure.

## Uncertainties
- The causal effect of rural primary school closures specifically on Hungarian population migration, housing decisions, and long-term settlement viability is unknown; Danish evidence suggests a 10-year decline of 7.6 percentage points, but Poland and other contexts show weaker effects. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A difference-in-differences study of Hungarian rural school closures (or postponements) during or after the 2013 Klebelsberg centralization, linked to settlement-level population and employment registry data, would directly estimate local population and retention effects.)
- The extent to which multigrade instruction with current Hungarian teacher training and resource allocation can maintain PISA-level achievement parity with single-grade schools is unverified; international evidence is neutral on outcome but assumes skilled implementation and adequate class sizes. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Piloting multigrade instruction in a sample of Hungarian rural schools with random assignment of professional development support (training intensity, co-teaching, materials) and measuring student achievement (PISA-equivalent), attendance, and teacher retention would empirically ground the transferability assumption.)
