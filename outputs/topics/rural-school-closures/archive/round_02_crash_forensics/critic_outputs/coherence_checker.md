# Critique: coherence_checker

## S1.assumptions
Objection: The scenario asserts (assumption) that "Teacher supply for multigrade classrooms is adequate without systemic training reform," yet elsewhere in the same scenario it (a) schedules multi-year recurring in‑service training as a necessary implementation step and (b) cites national data of 7,700 unfilled teaching posts and a halved graduate pipeline. This is a direct internal contradiction: the mechanism (multigrade teaching preserves access/costs) depends on adequate staffing, but the scenario simultaneously assumes staffing adequacy while providing evidence and steps that imply it is inadequate. The claim that multigrade can scale without systemic recruitment/training/reward changes is therefore unsupported.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Severity: high
Suggested revision: Replace the under-specified adequacy assumption with an explicit, evidence‑based staffing constraint and contingency plan. Concretely: (1) revise assumptions to state that current teacher supply is insufficient for large-scale multigrade rollout unless supplemented by specific measures; (2) add a quantitative staffing model estimating additional teachers, multigrade‑certified FTEs, and trainer capacity required per 100 retained village schools; (3) incorporate concrete recruitment and retention measures (salary/top‑up incentives for rural multigrade posts, targeted scholarship/placement programs for graduates, emergency locum pools); (4) fast-track expansion of teacher‑training institute throughput (with targets and budget lines) and tie multigrade scale-up to achieving those supply milestones (i.e., only expand multigrade in an area after hitting X% certified multigrade-staff coverage); and (5) include monitoring triggers (e.g., vacancy rate >Y%) that pause further retention commitments until staffing metrics are restored.

## S4.implementation_steps
Objection: The scenario's goal is to make closure the default but mitigate harms via statutory transport guarantees and TEIP-style support; however the implementation steps do not specify binding, ring‑fenced funding sources, enforceable legal instruments, or verified unit-costing for the transport and compensatory programmes. This contradicts the scenario's own mechanism caveat that the cited transport cost range (800–1,500 EUR/pupil/year) is unverified and that school‑bus provision has been cancelled in practice. Without explicit, budgeted funding and enforceable commitments the mitigation measures are aspirational, leaving the core policy (consolidation with mitigation) unmoored from operational feasibility.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Severity: high
Suggested revision: Make the implementation steps fiscally and legally operational: (1) add a statutory funding floor and dedicated budget line for the transport guarantee and TEIP‑style programme with multi‑year commitments (e.g., 5 years) and legal protection against year‑to‑year appropriation cuts; (2) require a full costing annex in year‑1 feasibility studies including route‑level transport unit costs calibrated to Hungarian occupancy/route-length data and sensitivity analysis; (3) define enforceable maximum-commute thresholds by grade in statute and a legal mechanism for families to claim relief/enforcement; (4) pilot the transport procurement in 3 regions with escrowed funds and independent monitoring to validate the 800–1,500 EUR estimates before national rollout; and (5) specify sanction/remediation clauses (automatic additional funding or temporary local subsidies) if contracted transport services are cancelled or fail service-level metrics.

## S5.evidence_status
Objection: S5's central evidence claim is that a Hungarian closure/outcome registry does not exist; the scenario properly downgrades this claim to 'weak' because it rests on an empty curated source registry rather than an exhaustive audit. Yet the implementation timeline proceeds to design registry architecture and expects district compliance within 1–2 years—an implicit confidence inconsistent with the admitted weak/evidence‑gap status and with documented district resistance in other scenarios. The scenario therefore overstates feasibility before verifying the foundational evidence claim.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Insert a required, explicit preliminary audit and legal scoping step before full registry design. Concretely: (1) add a mandatory month‑6 exhaustive audit task to search KIR‑STAT, KSH, ministry, academic and NGO datasets to confirm whether any closure/outcome tracking already exists; publish the audit results; (2) condition the full registry design timeline on audit outcomes and a legal/GDPR scoping memo that identifies necessary statutory changes; (3) include binding compliance mechanisms in the implementation plan (statutory reporting obligations, financial penalties for non‑reporting, independent audit power) and pilot district reporting with early enforcement trials to test compliance risk; (4) budget for legal support and data‑use agreements up front and delay district‑wide mandatory reporting until GDPR/legal clearance and pilot compliance are demonstrated.

## S2.assumptions
Objection: S2 assumes Hungary's institutional context is 'sufficiently similar' to Poland's 1999 reform for transferability, yet the scenario itself notes that Poland reversed the reform in 2016–17 and labels comparative evidence on achievement effects as contested. This is a substantive contradiction: the scenario leans on Polish precedent to justify restructuring upper grades while failing to integrate the political reversibility and contested causal evidence into its transferability assumption and risk design.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Convert the transferability assumption into a conditional hypothesis with explicit safeguards. Concretely: (1) replace the blanket transferability assumption with a set of context‑matching criteria (political entrenchment, funding permanence, commuting infrastructure, municipal governance capacity) that must be met before scale implementation; (2) require randomized or quasi‑experimental grade‑reorganization pilots (pre‑registered evaluation) in diverse Hungarian microregions with pre‑specified outcome metrics (achievement, attendance, transport cost, gender/SES heterogeneity) and decision rules tied to pilot results; (3) add legal/political entrenchment measures to the implementation plan (cross‑party statutory commitments, supermajority budgeting, multi‑year capital pledges) to reduce reversal risk; and (4) include a contingency plan if Poland‑style reversal signals materialize (e.g., staged reversion triggers, retraining/redeployment funds, criteria for reinstating local grades).
