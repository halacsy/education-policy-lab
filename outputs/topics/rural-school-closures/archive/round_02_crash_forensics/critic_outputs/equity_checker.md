# Critique: equity_checker

## S1.equity_impact
Objection: The equity_impact asserts a specific tipping point — 'selective non‑Roma out‑migration once Roma enrolment share passes roughly 50%' — without Hungary‑specific causal evidence and presents the threshold as a near‑deterministic outcome. The scenario's own Evidence status section admits Hungary‑specific causal evidence at sub‑2,000 settlement scale is absent, so reporting a precise ~50% trigger overstates what the cited 'moderate' evidence supports and risks prescribing policy on an unsupported numeric benchmark (unsupported).
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Remove the unsupported ~50% numeric tipping claim or reframe it explicitly as a hypothesis: state the claim as speculative, cite the comparative studies that suggest demographic tipping in other contexts, and require a concrete evidence step (short panel study or registry analysis) before using any numeric threshold in policy design. Add a concrete mitigation: mandate mandatory, annually published, disaggregated enrollment-and-mobility monitoring (by settlement, SES, Roma/non‑Roma, grade) with pre‑specified trigger rules (e.g., fast‑track desegregation planning if a school’s disadvantaged share rises X% year‑on‑year) only after empirical calibration from Hungarian microdata.

## S5.equity_impact
Objection: S5's equity_impact warns the framework 'risks being equity‑neutral' absent an explicit mandatory requirement to capture/disaggregate/publish SES, Roma/non‑Roma and disability data — but this contradicts S5's Implementation steps which already call for 'mandatory disaggregated data reporting (SES, Roma/non‑Roma, disability status)'. The equity_impact therefore mischaracterizes the scenario's content and fails to identify concrete enforcement, privacy, and oversight gaps that determine whether reporting will yield equitable outcomes (incoherence + missing enforcement detail).
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Align equity_impact with the Implementation steps and specify the missing design elements that make reporting actually equity‑effective: (1) make disaggregated reporting statutory with defined data fields and publishing cadence; (2) require GDPR‑compliant anonymized public extracts and secure identifiable data for authorised researchers only; (3) create an independent oversight/ombudsperson with audit and sanction powers for non‑reporting districts; (4) list explicit equity metrics to be published (enrollment by SES/Roma/disability, attendance, achievement, transport times, school assignment outcomes) and define automatic policy responses when equity red flags exceed thresholds. Add a short timeline and budget line for registry operationalization and audit capacity to close the implementation→equity gap.

## S4.equity_impact
Objection: S4's equity_impact correctly flags that mitigation measures (maximum commute time, receiving‑school quality verification, disaggregated reporting, legally binding funding floors) are required but claims these are 'not yet specified' — yet the Implementation steps already propose a statutory transport guarantee and mandatory receiving‑school verification. The concrete flaw is failure to translate those commitments into explicit, enforceable equity standards (e.g., grade‑specific maximum commute times, disability accommodations, funding‑floor formulas, and monitoring/reporting rules), leaving mitigation aspirational rather than operational (implementation gap).
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Replace vague mitigation statements with concrete, enforceable standards: propose grade‑specific maximum one‑way commute times (for example, ≤30 minutes for grades 1–4, ≤45 minutes for grades 5–8) subject to empirical pilot adjustment; require door‑to‑door or assisted transport for pupils with disabilities; define a statutory transport funding floor calculated per kilometer and occupancy with indexed multi‑year funding; mandate pre‑transfer receiving‑school quality/integration audits using defined metrics (teacher qualification mix, pupil‑teacher ratio, disaggregated achievement/attendance) and publish audit results before reassignment. Require independent annual compliance audits with corrective action deadlines and tied budgetary penalties for districts that fail to meet standards.
