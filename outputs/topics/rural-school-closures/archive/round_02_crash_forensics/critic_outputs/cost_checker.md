# Critique: cost_checker

## S1.cost_categories
Objection: The S1 cost_categories omit several material recurring and transition cost items and conflate transition training with steady-state costs. Specifically: (a) no line-item for rural-teacher recruitment/retention premiums or relocation incentives even though the scenario flags 7,700 unfilled posts and a collapsing graduate pipeline (this is central to feasibility); (b) no explicit allowance for substitute-teacher/agency costs and overtime during phased multiyear in‑service training (year 1–4), which can sharply raise short-term recurring payroll outlays; (c) no separation of one-off transition costs (curriculum/materials, initial intensive training, mentoring) from ongoing lower steady-state PD costs, preventing any sensible short-run fiscal estimate or sensitivity analysis. Because teacher supply and training costs are the binding fiscal constraint in the scenario, these omissions make the cost picture incomplete and likely biased low.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add explicit cost lines and ranges: (1) rural-teacher salary/top-up premium (per-post annual range) and projected number of posts needing premium using the 7,700 vacancy figure; (2) one-off transition costs per school for intensive multigrade rollout (initial training cohort costs, substitute coverage, mentoring for years 1–3) distinct from recurring PD costs; (3) substitute/agency and overtime contingency (percent of payroll) during rollout; (4) present per-pupil and per-school ranges and a fiscal-sensitivity table (best/central/worst) driven by vacancy rates and training uptake. Use Hungarian vacancy and payroll data to ground the ranges and model a 3‑year transition vs steady-state cost path.

## S2.cost_categories
Objection: S2 lists severance/redeployment and capital expansion but fails to quantify or model key transition and fiscal-sensitivity elements: (a) no estimate or range for severance/redeployment costs tied to statutory redundancy rules and average teacher salaries (this omission hides a potentially large up-front fiscal shock); (b) capital-expansion costs are listed but not amortized or tied to financing assumptions (per‑seat construction/modular-classroom costs, interest rates, procurement lead times), so net-present-cost comparisons to retention are impossible; (c) transport is only mentioned as an operating cost category without separating capital procurement of buses (buy vs lease), depot/maintenance, and lifecycle replacement schedules — all of which materially affect multi-year cashflows and O&M. The lack of these distinctions risks underestimating short-term fiscal pressures and misrepresenting when consolidation reaches net savings.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Extend cost_categories to include: (1) severance/redeployment modeled as per-teacher payout ranges (e.g., months of salary × number of affected staff) plus retraining costs; (2) capital-expansion unit costs (per additional classroom/seat), financing terms (grant vs loan, interest rate scenarios), and amortization schedules so yearly budget impact is explicit; (3) transport capital (bus fleet purchase/lease, maintenance depot), per-km operating cost sensitivity to fuel and occupancy, and lifecycle replacement budgets; (4) produce NPV comparisons across consolidation vs retention under multiple interest-rate and construction-inflation scenarios to show fiscal sensitivity.

## S4.cost_categories
Objection: S4 relies on a loosely-cited 800–1,500 EUR/pupil/year transport range but does not stress-test or localize this figure and omits important contingencies: (a) the cited Central European transport range is labeled 'unverified' but is then used without bottom-up modelling for Hungarian route lengths, occupancy rates, and school-hour scheduling; (b) no cost allowance for transport-service reliability risks (contingency contracts, emergency alternative transport, fines/enforcement) despite documented bus cancellations (Hódmezővásárhely 2023); (c) welfare/indirect costs (parental lost earnings from longer commutes, increased after-school care costs) are mentioned elsewhere but not monetized in cost_categories, biasing total-cost comparisons toward consolidation.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Replace the indicative range with a bottom-up transport-cost model: (1) compute per-route and per-pupil O&M using Hungarian average route lengths, km rates, occupancy assumptions, driver wages, and maintenance; (2) include capital (bus purchase/lease) and contingency/resilience line-items (backup contracts, service-failure mitigation) and an enforcement/administration cost for guarantees; (3) add monetized estimates for parental welfare impacts (lost work hours, informal care costs) as distributional transfer costs or externalities; (4) run sensitivity scenarios (low/high occupancy, fuel shocks, partial cancellations) and present results as ranges, not a single weakly-sourced number.

## S5.cost_categories
Objection: S5 understates the true lifecycle and governance costs of a national registry and evidence infrastructure. The listed items omit recurring legal/GDPR compliance and independent-audit costs, the expense of secure cross‑dataset linkage (education × tax × migration), and the budget implications of an independent oversight office or statutory funding protection mechanism. Without these, the one-off IT-build and 'recurring data-analytic staffing' lines undercount ongoing fixed costs and the political cost of ring‑fencing funding.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Broaden cost_categories to capture full lifecycle and governance costs: (1) one-off IT build plus estimated multi-year operating costs (hosting, cybersecurity, backups) with cloud vs on-premises cost options; (2) recurring GDPR/legal compliance budget (external counsel, periodic DPIAs, breach response insurance), independent audit and data-quality assurance teams, and an oversight unit with its own staffing and operational budget; (3) costs of secure record-linkage (matching services, licenses, inter-agency data agreements) and the technical/legal work to enable longitudinal household linkages; (4) model scenarios that include a statutory funding-protection mechanism (administrative cost to administer ring-fenced funds) and provide 5‑ and 10‑year budget projections under optimistic and pessimistic staffing assumptions.
