# Policy brief — The Future of Rural Primary Schools

This brief addresses the future of small rural primary schools in Hungary under demographic decline. Ten expert scenario analyses and a systematic stakeholder stress-test converge on a shared factual core — falling fertility, shrinking enrollment, and severe rural teacher shortages — but diverge sharply on what policy should follow. The brief sets out five policy scenarios (S1-S5), grades every claim by evidence strength, and answers every argument raised in the stakeholder ledger. Disagreement is preserved throughout: this is a map of an unsettled decision, not a recommendation for one path.

## Scenario key (full detail: scenarios.en.md)
- S1 — Status quo plus incremental adaptation — keep the village school open via multigrade classes and ad hoc local negotiation, without a national framework.
- S2 — Structural grade reorganization — lower grades stay in the village, upper grades centralize in a larger town (Polish 1999-model pattern).
- S3 — Networked retention — inter-municipal clustering, association-run schools, or multi-service community hubs.
- S4 — Consolidation with mitigation — closure as the default, paired with transport guarantees and targeted (TEIP-style) support.
- S5 — National systemic framework — transparent viability thresholds, cost-visibility rules, and a closure/outcome registry to inform (not dictate) local decisions.

## What we know
- Hungary's fertility rate has fallen sharply (to roughly 1.3 in 2025) and primary-school enrollment has been contracting for two decades, with the demographic squeeze now hitting primary level directly. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- The number of primary-school sites in Hungary has declined measurably in recent years (roughly 88 fewer sites between 2021 and 2024), and hundreds of settlements now have no public educational institution at all. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Teacher shortages, and especially unqualified-teacher instruction, are markedly more severe in rural/disadvantaged schools than in urban ones, constraining any rural school model regardless of retention or closure policy. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- US evidence shows school closures cause statistically significant drops in math/reading scores and disproportionately raise absenteeism and disciplinary incidents among disadvantaged pupils; displaced-pupil gains occur only when receiving schools are substantially higher-performing. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- An Italian instrumental-variable study found school closures cause measurable reductions in local population and income, particularly in peripheral areas — evidence that closure functions as a depopulation driver, not merely a response to it. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- School transport (school-bus) provision in Hungary is fragile and has been documented to be cancelled or curtailed with little notice (e.g. Hódmezővásárhely 2023), and a promised 2018 national school-bus network was never built. [fact] (evidence: moderate)
- The felmenő rendszer (grade-by-grade phase-out) legal mechanism prevents mid-cycle disruption to pupils by phasing in structural or curricular changes grade-by-grade, and is well documented internationally as a design pattern. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Achievement differences between well-implemented multigrade and single-grade instruction are negligible internationally when adequately supported (e.g. a Danish effect size of only -0.06). [fact] (evidence: moderate)
- A documented multi-stakeholder funding arrangement (diocese plus three municipalities, the 'Gersekarát' case) collapsed after one year despite a signed five-year contract — a concrete precedent for the fragility of multi-stakeholder cost-sharing models. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Poland's own 1999 structural reform — the model S2 is patterned on — was reversed in 2016-17 after 17 years, undercutting confidence that consolidation reforms are politically durable. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- No causal, Hungary-specific study currently isolates the effect of small-village primary-school closure on pupil achievement, attendance, population retention, or wellbeing; this is an absence-of-evidence finding based on an empty curated source registry for this topic, not a confirmed exhaustive audit. [fact] (evidence: weak)

## What we consider likely
- Without active desegregation policy, retaining small village schools can entrench between-school SES and ethnic sorting via selective non-Roma out-migration once Roma enrolment share passes roughly 50%. [estimate]
- Consolidation into larger schools can achieve economies of scale up to roughly 200 students, but these estimates often undercount transport and offsetting expenses, so real net savings for Hungarian conditions are unverified. [estimate]
- Post-closure transportation costs are indicatively in the range of roughly 800-1,500 EUR per pupil per year in Central European contexts, though this figure's applicability to specific Hungarian route lengths and occupancy rates is unverified. [estimate]
- TEIP-style compensatory support, adapted from Portugal, would likely reduce early school-leaving in Hungary but would probably not close academic-performance gaps, consistent with Portugal's own 15-year partial-failure record. [estimate]
- Multigrade teacher supply cannot realistically be scaled without systemic training reform, given roughly 7,700 unfilled teaching posts nationally and a halved graduate pipeline — though these specific figures are not registry-verified and should be treated as widely cited but unaudited claims. [estimate]
- Association-run village schools face structural viability limits even under favourable governance, with Polish evidence suggesting a threshold around 70 pupils below which such schools eventually 'disappear from the school network.' [estimate]

## Where experts disagree
### Should small rural primary schools be closed/consolidated, or preserved through multigrade and community-hub models?
- **finnish_reform, polish_reform, school_network_planning**: Rural primary schools should be preserved as long as feasible via multigrade teaching and community-hub investment, because closure accelerates depopulation and consolidation savings are often illusory once transport and social costs are counted. [value] Why: Danish and Finnish studies show closures accelerate self-reinforcing depopulation cycles; Poland's association-run schools and split-grade models demonstrate viable delay mechanisms.
- **portuguese_reform, equity_and_social_mobility** (minority): Preservation without pedagogical investment, desegregation policy, or quality upgrading can itself cause harm — entrenching segregation or failing to close achievement gaps — so neither closure-first nor preservation-first is defensible without conditional safeguards. [value] Why: Portugal's 15-year TEIP experience shows clustering met efficiency targets but did not close PISA-measured achievement gaps; Hungarian evidence shows segregation is driven by keeping small schools open while non-Roma families selectively exit.
### Is school closure a primary driver of rural depopulation, or mainly a consequence/coincident marker of it?
- **finnish_reform, education_finance, demography**: School closure functions as a causal driver of depopulation, not merely a lagging response — Danish and Italian quasi-experimental evidence shows closures cause measurable population and income decline, particularly in peripheral areas. [value] Why: Sørensen et al. (2021) find a 7.6-point 10-year population-decline effect in Denmark with lock-in effects from year six; Di Cataldo & Romani (2024) use an instrumental-variable design in Italy showing causation, not mere correlation.
- **international_comparison** (minority): The causal link between school closure and depopulation is contested rather than universal — multiple countries show mixed or null population effects, so closure's causal weight relative to pre-existing decline is not established generally. [value] Why: The literature review cites Johnson (1978), Amcoff (2012), Barakat (2015) and Kroismayr (2019) showing mixed or null effects across contexts, explicitly flagging the causal link as contested rather than settled.
### Can Hungary's government protection pledge ('as long as demand exists') durably prevent closures, or is it rhetorically fragile against fiscal and demographic pressure?
- **political_feasibility, legal_and_governance**: The government's commitments (KLIK abolition, restored strike rights, no top-down closures) represent a genuine structural shift creating institutional space for rural-school protection, though durability depends on unproven fiscal follow-through. [value] Why: Minister Lannert Judit has explicitly pledged no top-down closures and a transparent audit; KLIK abolition and restored strike rights formally shift power toward schools/municipalities.
- **political_feasibility, hungarian_education_system** (minority): The protection pledge is vulnerable to demographic collapse and operational funding failure — the Gersekarát model collapsed within a year despite a signed 5-year contract, and unresolved wage disputes threaten the staffing base multigrade teaching requires. [value] Why: Documented collapse of the Gersekarát five-year contract after one year, plus the unresolved NOKS wage dispute, are cited as concrete evidence that formal commitments face fiscal and operational fragility independent of political intent.
### Is multigrade teaching a genuinely viable substitute for closure, or a resource-intensive stopgap with unproven Hungarian feasibility?
- **international_comparison, finnish_reform, polish_reform**: Multigrade schooling is an internationally established, pedagogically defensible alternative to closure with negligible achievement differences when well implemented, and should be scaled with adequate teacher training. [value] Why: A Danish study found an achievement effect size of only -0.06 for multigrade vs single-grade instruction; the model is widely used across France, Britain, Netherlands, Scandinavia, Scotland and North America.
- **implementation_planning, portuguese_reform** (minority): Multigrade teaching costs more per pupil, requires teacher retraining and materials rarely available in Hungary, and its rollout is constrained by an acute, worsening teacher-supply crisis and an aging teacher workforce nearing mass retirement. [value] Why: MDPI/UNESCO sources confirm multigrade teaching costs more per pupil and requires training rarely available; Portugal's teacher-age data illustrates the structural staffing constraint on any rural model.

## What we don't know
- Whether teacher supply for multigrade classrooms can be scaled without systemic training reform, given the documented but unverified figures of 7,700 unfilled posts and a halved graduate pipeline. [estimate]
- Whether the true net financial effect of consolidation, once transport, capital-liability, and receiving-school capacity costs are fully accounted, is positive or negative for Hungarian conditions specifically. [assumption]
- Whether a comprehensive Hungarian closure/outcome registry truly does not currently exist in any form (state, academic, or NGO-maintained) — this is a known-unknown, since the claim rests on an empty curated source registry for this topic, not a confirmed exhaustive audit. [assumption]
- Whether the 8-child statutory minimum for municipal school retention functions as both a viable floor and a realistic target across regions with differing population clustering, or whether it needs regional adjustment. [assumption]
- Whether retaining small schools without desegregation measures causally reproduces SES/ethnic sorting patterns at sub-2,000-population village level specifically, as distinct from the moderate evidence at larger settlement scale. [estimate]
- Whether sufficient political will and statutory funding protection exist to sustain any national evidence infrastructure (S5) or multi-stakeholder funding arrangement (S3) across changing government administrations. [assumption]
- Whether district-level (tankerületi) authorities will comply fully and promptly with mandatory disaggregated data-reporting obligations, or systematically delay/circumvent them as documented for other statutory obligations (e.g. the expert-opinion requirement). [estimate]

## What could be done
- **S1 — Status quo plus incremental adaptation**: Keep the village school open as long as feasible via multigrade classrooms and felmenő rendszer phase-out, negotiated locally without a national framework. Legal safeguards (expert-opinion requirement, phase-in protections) offer procedural protection, but the model depends on teacher-supply reform that is not yet secured and offers no built-in desegregation safeguard.
- **S2 — Structural grade reorganization**: Restructure so only lower grades remain in the village while upper grades centralize in a larger town, following the Polish 1999 pattern. Preserves early-childhood proximity but the achievement-gap benefit is contested, the Polish model itself was reversed after 17 years, and it requires currently unspecified redeployment and transport guarantees for teachers and families.
- **S3 — Networked retention: clustering, association-run and multi-service hubs**: Retain village schools through inter-municipal networks, association-run publicly-subsidized schools, or multi-service hubs. Offers real operational flexibility and institutional pluralism, but requires civic organizing capacity unevenly distributed across villages, and the documented Gersekarát collapse shows multi-stakeholder funding arrangements are fragile without statutory protection.
- **S4 — Consolidation with mitigation**: Close low-enrollment village schools and reassign pupils to a consolidated center school, paired with statutory transport guarantees and TEIP-style targeted support. International evidence shows closures can harm disadvantaged pupils and drive depopulation, and the scenario's own compensatory measures are not yet specified as binding, enforceable preconditions.
- **S5 — National systemic framework**: Establish transparent minimum-viability thresholds, cost-visibility rules, teacher-training standardization, and a closure/outcome registry to inform (not dictate) local decisions under any of S1-S4. Addresses a documented evidence gap, but its equity value depends entirely on mandatory disaggregation and enforcement mechanisms that are not yet guaranteed.

## What each option costs
- **S1**: Costs fall mainly on teachers (workload intensification without guaranteed training/salary protection) and, absent desegregation monitoring, on Roma pupils who risk being left in increasingly segregated classrooms as non-Roma families exit; municipalities bear recurring multigrade training and capital-maintenance backlog costs. [assumption]
- **S2**: Teachers bear redeployment/redundancy risk without binding placement guarantees; families bear commuting burden and welfare cost for upper-grade pupils, disproportionately affecting lower-SES and Roma families; non-state maintainers lose institutional coherence when the grade cycle is split; municipalities/state bear capital and severance costs. [assumption]
- **S3**: Poorly organized, low-civic-capacity villages — disproportionately Roma-majority settlements — risk being left behind without a compensatory mechanism, creating a two-tier outcome where well-organized communities retain schools and others do not; association/church staff risk precarious employment without statutory compensation parity; all stakeholders bear the documented risk of Gersekarát-style multi-year funding collapse. [assumption]
- **S4**: Disproportionate travel burden and achievement/attendance risk fall on lower-SES, disability-affected and Roma families in peripheral settlements; teachers face workload intensification and redeployment without guaranteed placement; rural settlements face accelerated out-migration and service loss; the state bears reputational and fiscal risk if transport/TEIP funding commitments are broken as documented precedent suggests they may be. [assumption]
- **S5**: Sets up one-off registry design, legal/GDPR clearance, and threshold-consultation costs, plus recurring data-analytic staffing and teacher-training rollout costs; the equity risk is that without mandatory disaggregation, the framework remains equity-neutral in design but equity-consequential in effect, since undisaggregated data cannot reveal segregation or disproportionate harm; district authorities may resist reporting if it exposes prior non-compliance. [assumption]

## What research could resolve
- A pilot implementation of multigrade teaching with random assignment of professional-development intensity, tracking teacher retention and student achievement over 3-5 years, would clarify whether multigrade viability depends on systemic training reform.
- Disaggregated national microdata filtered by settlement population under 2,000 and primary grade level, reporting between-school SES and ethnic sorting measures, would show whether small-school retention without desegregation actually reproduces sorting patterns at that scale.
- A Hungary-specific quasi-experimental or difference-in-differences study of any pilot grade-reorganization, plus an updated comparative meta-analysis, would distinguish correlation from causal transfer for the S2 achievement-gap claim.
- A full multi-year cost-accounting comparison of consolidation versus retention scenarios in 3-5 matched Hungarian micro-regions would resolve the unverified net-fiscal-effect uncertainty shared by S2 and S4.
- Longitudinal data on association-school closure rates and demographic/enrolment thresholds at which they fail, plus comparative cost-benefit analysis against consolidated alternatives, would clarify the true long-run viability threshold for S3.
- A systematic audit of KIR-STAT, KSH, academic institutional repositories, and NGO archives specifically for existing closure/outcome tracking datasets would verify or refute the claim that no Hungarian registry currently exists.
- Longitudinal household-level administrative data linkage (education registry x tax office x migration registry) comparing settlements that closed schools to matched controls would establish whether closure causally drives out-migration in the Hungarian context specifically.

## What people must decide
- Whether the burden of proof for closure should rest with those proposing consolidation (closure as last resort, only after retention options are exhausted and documented) or with those defending retention (demand-based protection subject to fiscal sustainability limits) — this is a value choice about who bears the risk of an unproven policy, not a fact that evidence alone can settle. [value]
- How much weight to give to local community autonomy and subsidiarity versus centrally enforced equity and desegregation guarantees, when the two values point in different directions for the same village school. [value]
- Whether Hungary should accept the political and fiscal cost of statutory, ring-fenced multi-year funding floors for transport, teacher training, and TEIP-style support — a binding commitment that constrains future budget flexibility but is what multiple stakeholder clusters say is the minimum precondition for trusting any of S1-S5. [value]
- Whether a national evidence registry and viability thresholds (S5) should be designed as binding, automatically triggering closure/retention consequences, or purely advisory to local decision-makers — a political choice about how much discretion districts retain versus how much central accountability is imposed. [value]
- Whether the state should guarantee funding parity and legal eligibility for non-state (church, foundation, association) maintainers, accepting the political risk of being framed as privatization, in order to preserve institutional pluralism in rural school governance. [value]

## What to verify with real stakeholders
- A1 (Retaining village schools with multigrade teaching is pedagogically defensible and protects family/community welfare, but only if genuine multi-year teacher-training investment, salary/workload protections, and enrolment monitoring precede or accompany implementation, given the documented teacher-supply shortfall.): policy_design_fixable — The core pedagogical claim (multigrade equivalence) is reasonably well evidenced internationally; what remains is a design choice — statutory training funding, salary protection, and an implementation registry can be legislated. This is a matter of political will and legislative design, not an unresolvable value clash.
- A2 (Without an active desegregation policy and monitoring, retaining small village schools risks entrenching between-school SES and ethnic (Roma/non-Roma) sorting as non-Roma families exit once Roma enrolment share rises.): policy_design_fixable — The reciprocity round shows near-universal agreement, including from conservative subsidiarity advocates, that a mandatory ethnic/SES composition monitoring and triggered intervention mechanism can be added to S1 without contradicting local-autonomy values. The fix is a specific design addition (disaggregated monitoring plus a defined threshold trigger), not a fundamental value conflict — though it requires political will to legislate the trigger, which remains genuinely uncertain.
- A3 (Splitting primary retention from consolidated upper-grade schooling (S2) rests on contested evidence (Poland's 1999 reform was reversed after 17 years) and lacks statutory guarantees for receiving-school quality verification, transport, and teacher redeployment, making it currently unsafe to adopt.): needs_more_info — The evidence status is explicitly contested and no Hungary-specific quasi-experimental data exists; the appropriate response is a cautious pilot with pre-specified safeguards, not adoption or rejection on current evidence. This is genuinely not yet decidable given the missing data.
- A4 (Association-run, church-maintained, and networked school models offer valuable operational flexibility and institutional pluralism, but the documented Gersekarát collapse shows these arrangements are fragile without statutory funding protection and legal reform.): policy_design_fixable — Multiple stakeholder voices converge on the same remedy: the failure mode is a legal/contractual design gap (no statutory enforcement, no penalties for unilateral withdrawal), not an inherent flaw in the association-governance concept. Legislating binding multi-year funding protection and enforcement mechanisms directly addresses the documented failure.
- A5 (Association-run and networked school models require civic organising capacity unevenly distributed across villages, risking a two-tier system where well-organized villages retain schools while poor, low-capacity communities are left behind without compensatory support.): irreducible_tradeoff — Even with a compensatory capacity-building programme, the underlying tension between rewarding local civic initiative (a core value of the S3 model) and guaranteeing uniform access regardless of organizing capacity cannot be fully resolved — building capacity takes time the demographic clock may not allow, and some genuinely low-capacity communities may never catch up. A compensatory grant programme can narrow but not eliminate this gap.
- A6 (Closure-as-default (S4) treats consolidation as the starting assumption rather than a last resort, and its mitigation measures (transport guarantees, TEIP-style support) remain aspirational rather than statutorily enforced, given documented precedents of broken transport promises.): policy_design_fixable — Nearly every stakeholder voice, including the government-realist cluster, converges on the same remedy: sequence the safeguards before implementation (statutory funding floor, mandatory quality verification, enforceable thresholds) rather than after. This is achievable through legislative design, though it requires political will that is currently untested — hence the response is design-fixable, not an irreducible conflict, but implementation depends on the value choice under 'what people must decide' regarding burden of proof.
- A7 (International evidence shows school closures disproportionately harm low-SES, Roma, disabled, and female pupils through distance-sensitive attendance and enrolment loss, and function as a driver of settlement-level depopulation rather than merely a response to it.): evidence_answerable — The disproportionate-harm pattern is supported by moderate-to-strong international evidence cited within the scenario itself (US, China, India, Portugal, Italy); the remaining open question — whether a well-designed Hungarian mitigation package could neutralize this — is itself answerable through the Hungary-specific pilot and cost-accounting studies proposed under research priorities, not a matter of unresolvable value disagreement.
- A8 (Consolidation benefits depend critically on receiving schools being verifiably higher-quality, but S4 as framed lacks a mandatory pre-transfer quality and integration verification step, risking that pupils are moved into equally or more under-resourced schools.): policy_design_fixable — This is a specific, addressable design gap: a mandatory pre-transfer verification step, published quality data, and a halt-trigger if standards are not met can be written into legislation. Every stakeholder cluster raising this converges on the same concrete fix, indicating it is not a value conflict but an implementation omission.
- A9 (Consolidation without non-state maintainer eligibility in receiving schools forecloses institutional pluralism and accelerates settlement-level depopulation, converting closure into a demographic-decline signal for already vulnerable rural communities.): value_conflict — This raises a genuine value tension between administrative efficiency through unified state/large-municipal school governance and institutional pluralism (church/foundation presence). Even with statutory eligibility for non-state maintainers in receiving schools, the underlying question of whether the state should actively preserve non-state institutional presence as a value in itself, versus treating it as incidental to pupil outcomes, is a political choice, not a technical one.
- A10 (A national evidence infrastructure (registry, thresholds, standardized training) addresses a documented data gap and should replace ad hoc administrative discretion, but only if disaggregation by SES, Roma/non-Roma status, disability, and gender is mandatory, not optional.): policy_design_fixable — A broad coalition across nearly every stakeholder position converges on the same specific design requirement (mandatory disaggregation, statutory funding protection, independent governance). This is a legislative design question with a widely agreed answer among stakeholders, not a fundamental value clash — the remaining uncertainty is political will and implementation timeline, not disagreement about the right design.
- A11 (A transparency and threshold-setting framework (S5) risks becoming 'transparency theatre' or a tool for legitimizing pre-decided closures if it lacks mandatory action thresholds, enforcement mechanisms, and independent oversight rather than self-policing by district authorities.): needs_more_info — Whether visibility and publication alone change district-level closure behaviour without hard enforcement is an empirical question with no existing Hungarian evaluation of comparable frameworks — the reciprocity round explicitly notes this has 'not been tested in the specific Hungarian tankerületi context because the registry itself does not yet exist.' The remedy proposed (independent oversight, binding action thresholds) is reasonable but its sufficiency cannot be verified until piloted.
- A12 (Village schools serve as community anchors that preserve rural liveability, family stability, and settlement viability, and their retention avoids the documented welfare losses of consolidation such as travel fatigue, attendance risk, and family disruption for young children.): value_conflict — This is fundamentally a value statement about what a school is for — a community-anchoring institution versus a pure delivery mechanism for standardized instruction. Both framings are legitimate and the evidence on welfare costs of consolidation versus segregation risks of retention does not resolve which value should take priority; this is a genuine, irreducible value choice that different communities may reasonably answer differently.
- A13 (The quoted transport cost range (800-1,500 EUR/pupil/year) and the overall net fiscal benefit of consolidation remain unverified for Hungarian conditions, undermining confidence in the scenario's core cost-savings claim.): evidence_answerable — This is a direct empirical gap with a clear resolution path: a Hungary-specific cost-accounting study bundling transport, capacity-expansion, capital-liability, and depopulation effects across matched micro-regions would directly settle whether consolidation's claimed savings hold. Both proponents and critics agree this study, once done, would change the assessment.
- A14 (Grade consolidation (S2) risks shedding rural teaching posts and forcing redeployment or redundancy without binding placement guarantees or salary/benefit parity, disproportionately affecting older teachers and those with family constraints.): policy_design_fixable — A statutory no-redundancy guarantee with mandatory placement and union-negotiated severance/redeployment terms directly addresses this concern, as proposed by teacher-union stakeholders themselves; this is a legislative and negotiation design question, not an unresolvable conflict, though it requires political will to legislate before any consolidation proceeds.
- A15 (Even association-run and networked schools face structural viability limits and will eventually have to close below a sustainable enrolment threshold (roughly 70 pupils in the Polish precedent), meaning S3 is a transition strategy rather than a permanent solution.): not_decision_relevant — This observation is honest and worth stating, but it does not change the immediate decision of whether to pursue S3 as one option among several for schools currently above the viability threshold — it is a longer-horizon planning caveat (addressed via the proposed mandatory 'pathway planning' requirement) rather than a factor that shifts the near-term policy choice.
- A16 (Digital-economy and employer interests view unresolved teacher-supply shortfalls as a systemic risk that defers rather than solves the future workforce-readiness cost of rural skills gaps.): not_decision_relevant — While the concern about a future digital-skills pipeline is legitimate, the causal chain from current multigrade-classroom staffing gaps to specific regional digital-workforce shortfalls is speculative and would not, on its own, change which of S1-S5 is chosen; the underlying teacher-supply crisis it points to is already captured and addressed more directly under A1 and A14.

## Where the red herrings are
- A15: The debate over the exact numeric viability threshold for association-run schools (roughly 70 pupils in the Polish case) generates significant attention but would not change whether S3 is worth pursuing for schools currently well above any plausible threshold; it matters for long-range planning, not for the near-term decision.
- A16: The projected link between current rural teacher shortages and a future 'digital-ready workforce' deficit draws attention from employer-interest voices but does not add decision-relevant information beyond what is already established by the direct, better-evidenced teacher-supply arguments (A1, A14); it restates the same underlying problem in labour-market language without changing the policy calculus.
