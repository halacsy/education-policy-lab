# Research Notes: Demography — Rural Primary Schools in Hungary (Round 2)

**1. National birth rate collapse continuing through 2025.** Hungary's total fertility rate fell to 1.31 children per woman in 2025 (down from 1.39 in 2024), the lowest level in 14 years; only 72,000 children were born in 2025, 7.1% fewer than in 2024, while 124,200 people died, producing a natural population decrease of 52,200 (4.5% worse than the prior year). Hungary's population fell below 9.5 million for the first time in decades, standing at 9.489 million at end-2025. Source: "Hungary's population drops below key threshold as births hit 14-year low," Daily News Hungary, https://dailynewshungary.com/hungary-population-drops-births-low/

**2. 2024 birth data confirms accelerating decline.** In 2024, only 77,500 children were born in Hungary — the lowest figure since records began (since 1949) — a 9.1% decline (7,725 fewer) from 2023; the total fertility rate fell to 1.38 from 1.51. Natural population decrease reached 50,000, up 16% year-on-year. Source: "Hungary's Population Decline Accelerates as Birthrate Drops in 2024," Budapest Business Journal, https://bbj.hu/economy/statistics/figures/hungarys-population-decline-accelerates-as-birthrate-drops-in-2024/ ; also OSW Centre for Eastern Studies, https://www.osw.waw.pl/en/publikacje/osw-commentary/2025-04-14/hungarys-ongoing-demographic-decline-increase-birth-rates-only

**3. Direct evidence of school-network hollowing-out: 1,200+ settlements with zero public education institutions by 2024.** A 2025 peer-reviewed study using k-means clustering of Hungarian settlements found that by 2024, more than 1,200 of Hungary's 3,155 settlements had no public educational institutions at all, and identified five settlement typologies (e.g., "Disadvantaged Declining Areas," "Moderately Declining Balanced Areas") showing a deepening divide between dynamic urban/suburban zones and disadvantaged rural peripheries, with disadvantaged children increasingly concentrated in rural areas (up to 94% in some settlements). Source: "Shrinking, growing or overburdened? Territorial patterns in child demographics and institutional burden in Hungary's public education system after 2021," ResearchGate, https://www.researchgate.net/publication/396483025_Shrinking_growing_or_overburdened_Territorial_patterns_in_child_demographics_and_institutional_burden_in_Hungary's_public_education_system_after_2021

**4. Migration flows are reversing away from villages since 2023.** KSH notes that during the pandemic, internal migration flowed from larger settlements to smaller ones, but from 2023 onward there was a migration gain in Budapest while gains in villages declined significantly — meaning the brief rural repopulation trend has stalled or reversed. Source: KSH, "Population and vital events," https://www.ksh.hu/population-and-vital-events

**5. Rural/village population share and teacher shortage severity.** As of 1 January 2024, Hungary's population of 9,585,000 was distributed roughly 18% in Budapest, 53% in towns, and 30% in villages. Separately, the 2025 EU Education and Training Monitor country report states teacher shortages are greatest in rural areas, with the proportion of unqualified teachers in disadvantaged schools 2–3 times higher than in non-disadvantaged schools, and that half of all teachers in 2024/2025 were aged 50+ while only 7.2% were under 30. Sources: KSH, https://www.ksh.hu/population-and-vital-events ; Education and Training Monitor 2025 – Hungary, https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html

**6. Primary enrollment declining even as other levels grow.** In the 2024/2025 school year, 1.9 million children/students participated across all education levels in Hungary; compared to the previous year, kindergarten and secondary/tertiary enrollment increased, but primary school student numbers decreased — confirming the demographic cohort squeeze is now hitting primary level specifically. Source: KSH Education page, https://www.ksh.hu/education

**7. Long-run population and school-age cohort projections point to continued shrinkage to mid-century.** Our World in Data's synthesis of UN projections shows Hungary's population, estimated at 9.686 million in 2023, is projected to fall to 7.461 million by 2100 under the medium-fertility scenario; earlier KSH/Demographic Research Institute projections (SEEMIG project) show the under-20 population dropping from about 200,000 (2012 cohort reference) to 140,000 by 2060 in a comparable age-band metric, reflecting sustained school-age cohort contraction across the 2020–2040s window relevant to this brief. Sources: Our World in Data, https://ourworldindata.org/profile/population-demography/hungary ; KSH/SEEMIG, "Population projections for Hungary and Slovakia," https://www.ksh.hu/docs/eng/seemig/outputs/SEEMIGPopulationProjectionsHUSK.pdf

**8. Historical extreme depopulation cases ("aprófalvak").** Academic literature on Hungary's "small villages" (aprófalvak, defined as under 500 inhabitants) documents that more than 40 villages had populations under 25 people according to the 2011 census, with out-migration and natural decrease now running in parallel, aging the residual population — an extreme end-point illustrating the demographic pressure driving school non-viability in the smallest settlements. Source: "Villages on the Edge of Extinction – the Hungarian Situation," ResearchGate, https://www.researchgate.net/publication/326181217_Villages_on_the_Edge_of_Extinction_-_the_Hungarian_Situation

**9. Teacher salary reform (2024–2031) as a partial mitigating policy lever, not a demographic fix.** Backed by €1.8 billion in EU funds (2024–2031), Hungary's teacher salary reform aims to raise average teacher pay to 80% of the average graduate wage by 2025 (up from ~60%), explicitly intended to address teacher shortages that are most acute in rural/disadvantaged schools — relevant because staffing viability, not just pupil counts, is a forcing factor on small rural school closures. Source: European Social Fund Plus, "Hungary raises teachers' wages with EU support," https://european-social-fund-plus.ec.europa.eu/en/projects/hungary-raises-teachers-wages-eu-support

**10. Note on data gap:** I could not find a recent (2024–2026) KSH or ministry dataset giving the precise current count of operating primary schools in villages under 2,000 population, nor a specific national tally of rural primary-school closures/consolidations in the last two years — this remains an explicit uncertainty for the structured output.
