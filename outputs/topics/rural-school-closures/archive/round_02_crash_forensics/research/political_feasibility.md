# Research Notes: Political Feasibility of Rural Primary School Reform in Hungary (Round 2)

## Finding 1: Major government change (April–May 2026) — new ruling party, new education ministry
Hungary underwent a historic power transfer: 
Magyar Péter and the Tisza Party won the 2026 parliamentary election, ending sixteen years of Fidesz–KDNP governance.
 
The Tisza Party won the 2026 parliamentary elections, meaning for the first time since 2010 there can be a standalone education ministry.
 Lannert Judit was appointed Education and Child Affairs Minister; 
the incoming education minister was described as being driven by developing those falling behind, learning from mistakes, and unleashing creativity.

## Finding 2: New government's explicit stance against top-down rural school closures
The outgoing government's own commissioner denied closure plans just before the election: 
the Pedagógusok Szakszervezete union again urged school closures, but the government chose a different path, stating the goal is not closure but protecting small settlements and small schools as long as there is demand for them, according to the PM's commissioner.
 After the change of government, the new education minister confirmed no closures were planned but promised a transparency exercise: 
Minister Lannert Judit stated they do not plan to close small-settlement schools, but committed to preparing a comprehensive report on the national school network, unlike what she called the harmful practice of the past 16 years.

Sources: Magyar Hírlap, https://www.magyarhirlap.hu/oktatas/20251007-ismet-napirenden-az-iskolabezaras ; Borsodi Hír, https://borsodihir.hu/hir/a-tisza-nem-tervezi-a-kistelepulesi-iskolak-bezarasat

## Finding 3: Structural decentralization pledge — KLIK abolition, school autonomy

The Tisza Party pledges to return freedom to schools and teachers, and to immediately abolish the Klebelsberg Center — the Klebelsberg Institution Maintenance Center and the district education authorities were created in 2013 by the Fidesz government after it took over school maintenance from municipalities.
 
Despite a 2016 reorganization, centralization still meant in 2026 that district-level authorities often make decisions far from the schools even on minor matters — a broken door or a projector bulb replacement could take weeks.
 This directly bears on reversal risk: a durable rural-school policy would need to survive this planned re-decentralization, which could shift closure/consolidation decision authority from tankerület (district) level back toward institutions/local stakeholders.
Source: Eduline, https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/20260414_oktatasi-tisza-part-magyar-peter

## Finding 4: Teacher union mobilization capacity and demonstrated strike willingness
The PDSZ union demonstrated significant mobilization capacity in early 2026: 
a PDSZ pre-survey on strike willingness among NOKS workers was completed by over 3,600 respondents, with 94% indicating willingness to join a two-hour warning strike.
 The strike was called for March 30, 2026, though 
the PDSZ declared a two-hour warning strike in March after wage negotiations with the previous government failed, but the strike ultimately did not happen after the Interior Ministry and state secretary Zoltán Maruzsa took the matter to court.
 This shows both union capacity and government willingness to use legal countermeasures against teacher-support-staff strikes — a durability risk indicator for any reform requiring extra unpaid/underpaid labor (e.g., multigrade teaching, extra transport supervision).
Sources: Népszava, https://nepszava.hu/3316769_noks-ovodai-iskolai-dolgozok-berrendezes-pdsz ; Eduline, https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/20260608_pdsz-sztrajk-noks-dolgozok-beremeles-oktatasi-miniszter

## Finding 5: New government restoring teachers' strike rights and school-level governance rights
Under the new government, 
draft legislation from Minister Lannert Judit would restore the teaching staff's decision-making rights over institutional operations, remove obstacles restricting free strike rights for education employees, broaden school directors' employer powers at district-maintained schools, and abolish the rule that professional matters could only be negotiated with the National Pedagogue Chamber.
 
Public consultation on the bill restoring teachers' free strike right closed June 27, 2026.
 This is a major durability-relevant shift: rural teachers/unions will have stronger formal leverage over any future consolidation plan than under the Fidesz-era framework.
Source: Népszava, https://nepszava.hu/3326468_helyreallitjak-a-pedagogusok-sztrajkjogat-a-tisza-kormany-tarsadalmi-egyeztetesre-bocsatotta-a-torvenytervezetet

## Finding 6: Demographic collapse pressure making closures harder to avoid regardless of politics
Independent of political will, demographic pressure is intensifying: 
kindergartens are already closing, and schools also need to prepare for the enormous challenge posed by the roughly twenty percent drop in birth numbers over the past four years.
 
Today there are 17,000 fewer children under one year old in Hungary than four-year-olds — equivalent to 600-800 kindergarten groups/school classes, meaning at least a hundred small-school's worth of children will be missing.
 This creates a structural tension for any government pledge to "protect small schools as long as there is demand" — demand itself is collapsing, which the ministerial commissioner implicitly conceded.
Source: Válasz Online, https://www.valaszonline.hu/2025/10/17/ovodak-iskolak-bezarasa-demografia-oktatas/

## Finding 7: 2000s closure wave produced strong grassroots resistance repertoire — now more muted
Historical precedent for mobilization tactics: 
during the big closure wave of the mid-2000s, buildings were locked from one day to the next; parents protested, collected signatures, formed human chains around schools, and opposition politicians — then Fidesz — accused the government of treason.
 Currently, 
in most places there is no serious resistance, because child numbers are already falling while costs rise, and the drained municipalities cannot absorb the resulting deficit.
 This is a key finding for reversal-risk modeling: the political salience of closures may be structurally lower now than in the 2000s wave, reducing mobilization risk but also reducing pressure for compensatory investment.
Source: Válasz Online, https://www.valaszonline.hu/2025/10/17/ovodak-iskolak-bezarasa-demografia-oktatas/

## Finding 8: Local case study — municipal reversal on shared-maintenance (association/church) model
A concrete local instability case: in Gersekarát (Vas county), 
the Szombathelyi Diocese took over as maintaining body in 2025/2026, with the diocese covering 78 million HUF and three municipalities (Gersekarát, Telekes, Sárfimizdó) contributing 10 million HUF each of the 128-million-forint annual cost.
 Despite this, 
although the municipalities had committed to a fixed 5-year contract, after just 1 year the local leaders announced they would withdraw from the maintenance agreement.
 This is direct evidence that multi-stakeholder (church-municipality-state) funding models for keeping rural schools open are fragile even when contractually locked in — a critical durability risk finding relevant to any "alternative maintainer" model proposed in the reform.
Source: Ugytudjuk.hu, https://ugytudjuk.hu/cikk/2026-05-14_a-felso-tagozatok-bezarasnak-reme-fenyegeti-a-gersekarati-iskolat

## Finding 9: Comparative evidence school consolidation frequently fails to deliver expected savings (relevant to feasibility framing)
International rural-school literature commonly cited by advocacy groups fighting closures: 
despite widespread assumed efficiencies from district consolidation, several major studies say consolidation doesn't save money.
 This argument is used by opponents in comparable contexts and is likely to be imported into Hungarian discourse by teacher unions and parent groups opposing closures.
Source: Rural Schools Open, https://ruralschoolsopen.org/fight-closure/

## Finding 10: NOKS support-staff pay dispute as proxy battleground shaping reform durability

The PDSZ union sought a raise for non-teaching NOKS staff (roughly 67,000 people, including school secretaries, librarians, teaching assistants, IT staff) from around 410,500 HUF gross to 718,000 HUF, costing an estimated 134.6 billion HUF annually — about 0.3% of the central budget.
 As of June 2026, 
if no meaningful progress occurs by autumn, the PDSZ may continue advocacy through its strike committee, having asked the new government to treat the wage increase as a top priority.
 This unresolved dispute is a leading indicator of whether the new government's rural-school-friendly rhetoric will be backed by budget — support staff are essential for keeping small multigrade rural schools operational.
