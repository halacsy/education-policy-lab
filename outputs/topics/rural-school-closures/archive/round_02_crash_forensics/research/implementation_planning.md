# Research Notes — Implementation Planning Domain: Rural Primary Schools in Hungary

## Finding 1: Severe and worsening teacher shortage, concentrated in rural areas
The 2025 EU Education and Training Monitor country report for Hungary states that half of all teachers in the 2024/2025 academic year were 50 or older, while only 7.2% were under 30, and that this age imbalance is partly due to a 2022 relaxation of rules for rehiring retired teachers. Critically for rural sequencing: "Teacher shortages are greatest in rural areas, with the proportion of unqualified teachers in disadvantaged schools 2 to 3 times higher than in schools not disadvantaged" (Varga, cited in report).
Source: Education and Training Monitor 2025 – Hungary, https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html

## Finding 2: Teacher union estimates of national vacancy scale
Trade unions (PSZ, PDSZ) have repeatedly estimated national teacher vacancies in the 12,000–16,000 range in recent years, with PDSZ stating in 2023 that "the country's school system is already short of 16,000 teachers." This is a contested figure — government officials have publicly denied a structural shortage exists, calling vacancy counts normal turnover.
Source: Euronews, July 2023, https://www.euronews.com/2023/07/19/i-didnt-want-to-be-silenced-how-hungarys-new-law-affects-teachers
Source: Hungary Today, https://hungarytoday.hu/thousands-teacher-shortage-public-education/

## Finding 3: Collapsing pipeline of new teacher graduates
RFE/RL reporting (2023) found that of 126,000 Hungarian university applicants in 2023, fewer than 3,400 applied for pedagogical degrees, with only 1,595 listing teaching as their primary major; an education researcher noted the applicant pool "has halved over the past few years" and that dropout from pedagogical courses runs 40-50%. This directly constrains any sequencing plan that assumes new teachers can be recruited to rural posts within a normal 3-5 year horizon.
Source: RFE/RL, June 2023, https://www.rferl.org/a/hungary-shortage-teachers/32475124.html

## Finding 4: Fragmentation of the school network itself is a driver of shortage
An EC country report finding (relayed by Hungary Today, 2022) states that "half of all primary and lower secondary schools had fewer than 150 pupils in 2020/2021," directly linking system fragmentation (many small schools) to the difficulty of staffing them with qualified subject teachers. This is structurally relevant: consolidation reduces the number of small staffing units needed but each merger is an administrative/legal event.
Source: Hungary Today, May 2022, https://hungarytoday.hu/workload-low-salaries-teacher-public-education-ec-country-report/

## Finding 5: Legal minimum/maximum class-size thresholds create a hard administrative trigger point
Hungarian law (Act CXC of 2011, Annex 4) sets a minimum class size of 14 students and maximum of 27 for grades 1-4 of primary school. Multiple 2023-2025 local cases (e.g., Kajászó/Dunaújváros tankerület, August 2023) show that maintainers (tankerületi központ, i.e., the centralized Klebelsberg-system school district authority) decide class mergers/openings unilaterally close to the start of term, often without prior consultation with the local municipality, which legally "has no right of opinion" on the number of classes started. This is a key sequencing/administrative-bandwidth finding: decisions are frequently made at the last minute (weeks before September start) because enrollment numbers fluctuate until August, creating planning instability for families and local governments alike.
Source: Eduline, March 2025, https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/20250303_A-nap-kerdese-maximum-hany-gyerek-jarhat-egy-osztalyba
Source: Feol.hu, August 2023, https://www.feol.hu/helyi-kozelet/2023/08/megsem-vonjak-ossze-az-osztalyokat-osztol

## Finding 6: Centralized maintainer authority limits local sequencing control
A legal information source confirms that under the National Public Education Act, the decision to establish, reorganize, or close a public education institution rests exclusively with the maintainer (fenntartó) — since 2013 centralization, this is the state (via Klebelsberg Center/tankerületi központok) rather than the municipality for most state schools, though the closure decision is bounded by a requirement that continued service must not impose a "disproportionate burden" (aránytalan teher) on students/parents, and requires an assessment tied to a county development plan.
Source: Oktatási Jogok Biztosának Hivatala, https://www.oktbiztos.hu/ugyek/jelentes2000/iskola.html

## Finding 7: School-bus transport is a fragile, cost-sensitive substitute infrastructure
Hungarian civil-liberties organization TASZ reported in autumn 2023/24 that several municipalities discontinued school-bus (iskolabusz) services citing rising fuel and overhead costs, despite this being "nearly the only option" for children in outlying areas (tanyák) to reach school; the organizing obligation rests with the school maintainer. This shows that "transport as mitigation" for consolidation is not a stable, guaranteed-permanent solution but is itself subject to municipal budget cycles and can be withdrawn with little notice — a major timeline/sequencing risk for any policy relying on bus consolidation as the alternative to closure.
Source: TASZ, https://tasz.hu/cikkek/panaszmintat-keszitettunk-az-iskolabuszok-ujrainditasa-erdekeben/

## Finding 8: KSH confirms declining primary enrollment nationally amid rising other-sector enrollment
Hungarian Central Statistical Office (KSH) data for the 2024/2025 school year note that "the number of children in kindergartens and students in secondary and tertiary education has increased, while that of primary school students has decreased" nationally — confirming that the demographic pressure on rural primary schools is part of a broader national primary-enrollment decline, not merely a village-level anomaly, meaning any sequencing plan needs a multi-year (not single-year) demographic baseline.
Source: KSH, https://www.ksh.hu/education

## Finding 9: Multigrade teaching is an internationally documented, sustainability-contested alternative
A 2025 systematic review (MDPI, 40 studies 2019-2024) frames multigrade classrooms as "a widely extended educational modality in rural contexts and territories with low demographic density," citing Spain's "incomplete Infant and Primary Education Centers (CEIP)... where the operation of certain levels is consolidated due to the progressive decline in enrolment" as an organizational parallel to what Hungarian villages already do informally (merging grades 1+3, 2+4). International literature flags multigrade teaching as requiring specific teacher training and materials rarely available, meaning adopting it formally as national rural policy would require lead time for teacher retraining, not just administrative permission.
Source: MDPI Education Sciences 2025, https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7102/15/8/1052
Source: UNESCO IIEP (Brunswic), https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/ED496622.pdf — notes multigrade teaching "costs more per pupil, and hence the issue is whether to close the school."

## Finding 10: Teacher salary reform underway but implementation lags rural recruitment needs
Eurydice reporting confirms that under Act LII of 2023 (New Career Path for Teachers), teacher pay rose by an average of 32.2% in 2024 to reach 71.8% of average graduate earnings, with further raises planned toward an 80% target by 2025, partly financed by the European Social Fund. However, this national reform's rollout (2024-2025) has not yet been evaluated for its differential effect on rural recruitment, and the pipeline data (Finding 3) suggests salary alone may not reverse the applicant collapse within a realistic multi-year window.
Source: Eurydice, https://eurydice.eacea.ec.europa.eu/eurypedia/hungary/national-reforms-early-childhood-education-and-care
