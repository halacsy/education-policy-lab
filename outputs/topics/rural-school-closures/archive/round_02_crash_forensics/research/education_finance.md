# Research Notes: Rural Primary Schools in Hungary — Education Finance Domain

**Finding 1 — Territorial equity gap is severe and worsening.** A 2025 study using settlement-level KIR (Köznevelési Információs Rendszer) data found that 
by 2024, more than 1,200 of 3,155 settlements had no public educational institutions, raising concerns about territorial equity
. More specifically on primary schools: 
in 2021, there were 9,046 school sites (feladatellátási hely), which decreased to 8,590 by 2024, and of these, 3,609 provided primary education (ISCED 1–2) in 2021, a number that declined to 3,521 by 2024
. Source: Shrinking, growing or overburdened? Territorial patterns in child demographics and institutional burden in Hungary's public education system after 2021 (2025), https://www.researchgate.net/publication/396483025_Shrinking_growing_or_overburdened_Territorial_patterns_in_child_demographics_and_institutional_burden_in_Hungary's_public_education_system_after_2021

**Finding 2 — Rural/urban divide in resourcing and staffing.** The same study documents 
a deepening divide between dynamic urban/suburban zones and disadvantaged rural peripheries, with rapidly growing urban settlements facing rising child-teacher ratios (up to 24:1 in some areas) while disadvantaged children have become increasingly concentrated in rural areas (up to 94% in certain settlements)
. For OECD context, 
OECD countries report approximately 14 children per teacher in public education on average
, making Hungary's rural extremes far outside typical benchmarks.

**Finding 3 — Historical funding mechanics of small village schools.** A case study on rescuing a small village school documents that 
the funding of public services was secured through co-financing: central government covered the greater part (in the case of primary education 80–90% of costs were paid centrally) while municipalities provided the rest
, but by the early 2000s 
the share of state support had dropped to 60–70% on average and 50% in small village schools
. Notably, 
closures or amalgamations occurred only rarely; the rural school network was kept relatively intact until as late as 2004 as a result of state support provided specifically to small villages to maintain schools, and a new act passed in 1997 promoting voluntary associations of local governments
. Source: "Rescuing a small village school in the context of rural change in Hungary," ScienceDirect, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0743016712000216

**Finding 4 — Per-pupil spending is well below OECD average, constraining small-school viability.** 
In Hungary, government sources spend USD 7,072 per full-time equivalent student in public primary institutions, while the figure is USD 7,361 per full-time equivalent student in private ones (the OECD averages are USD 11,914 for public primary institutions and USD 7,867 for private ones)
. Source: OECD, Education at a Glance 2024, Hungary Country Note, https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2024/09/education-at-a-glance-2024-country-notes_532eb29d/hungary_faab9f42/1b2a3510-en.pdf

**Finding 5 — Rural teacher shortage compounds the small-school cost problem.** 
Teacher shortages are greatest in rural areas, with the proportion of unqualified teachers in disadvantaged schools 2 to 3 times higher than in schools not disadvantaged
. Also, 
regional coverage of kindergartens remains uneven: in 2022, 31% of municipalities had no kindergarten, typically in small villages where the number of kindergarten-aged children did not reach 8
 — an early indicator of the same demographic pressure that later hits primary schools. Source: European Commission, Education and Training Monitor 2025, Hungary, https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html

**Finding 6 — Causal evidence that closures accelerate depopulation (not just result from it).** A rigorous Italian instrumental-variable study found 
municipalities affected by school closures experience significant reductions in population and income, with the effect primarily driven by peripheral municipalities located far away from economic centres and distant from the next available primary school
. The broader conclusion: 
policies aimed at 'rationalising' service provision by reducing the number of undersized nodes in the public school network can lead to population decline, especially in spatially isolated areas lacking valid alternatives to the removed services
. This is directly relevant to the brief's tension between efficiency and community viability. Source: Di Cataldo & Romani, "Rational cuts? The local impact of closing undersized schools," Regional Science and Urban Economics 109 (2024), https://doi.org/10.1016/j.regsciurbeco.2024.104057 (open access via https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0166046224000887)

**Finding 7 — OECD policy recommendation on sequencing closures.** In an OECD demographic-change study of a comparable EU region (Campania, Italy), the policy takeaway was explicit: 
school closures should be tailored to minimise adverse impacts on communities and prevent further depopulation in underserved areas; where the closure of underutilised schools is necessary, municipalities should prioritise closing secondary schools, as primary schools influence residential choices and sustain the vitality of local areas
. The report also floats 
piloting the adoption of online or blended learning models, integrating the benefits of digital and in-person teaching that ensure student engagement and preserve the social dimension of learning
 as an alternative to closure. Source: OECD, "Preparing for Demographic Change in Campania, Italy" (2025), https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/preparing-for-demographic-change-in-campania-italy_071b9897-en/full-report/the-present-and-future-of-service-provision-in-campania_851ff9c4.html

**Finding 8 — Demographic driver: Hungary's fertility decline and rural depopulation are accelerating again, not stabilizing.** 
Since 2022, Hungarian birth rates have once again been falling, with the fertility rate dropping to 1.38 by 2024, and depopulation is now progressing at its fastest rate to date
. At the national level, 
Hungary's population on 1 January 2024 was 9 million 585 thousand people, with nearly 18% living in the capital city, almost 53% in towns, and 30% in villages
. However, migration patterns have shifted post-pandemic: 
during the pandemic, migration was directed from larger settlements to smaller ones; however, from 2023, there was a migration gain in the capital city, while the gains in the villages declined significantly
 — meaning the modest counter-urbanization trend that had been propping up some village school rolls is reversing. Sources: OSW Centre for Eastern Studies, "Hungary's ongoing demographic decline" (2025), https://www.osw.waw.pl/en/publikacje/osw-commentary/2025-04-14/hungarys-ongoing-demographic-decline-increase-birth-rates-only ; KSH Population and vital events, https://www.ksh.hu/population-and-vital-events

**Finding 9 — Comparative small-school closure practice (Ireland, recent, illustrates threshold logic).** As a comparative data point on how ultra-small schools are handled elsewhere: 
eight primary schools closed permanently in Ireland in 2025, all rural schools with tiny enrolments of between just one and six pupils
, and one school even 
resorted this year to offering €2,000 to families who chose to relocate and enrol their children
 elsewhere rather than close — indicating how far some systems will go to avoid closure before finally consolidating. Source: RTE, "Eight primary schools to close permanently from today" (June 2025), https://www.rte.ie/news/education/2025/0627/1520515-primary-school-closures/

**Finding 10 — Multigrade/composite classrooms remain a live pedagogical adaptation, not merely a stopgap.** Research on multigrade rural schools abroad notes structurally that 
it is possible to find schools with three students at different levels that live far from the nearest city, typically with a single teacher in charge of the entire school, conducting the teaching processes for all students
 — the same "összevont osztály" (composite class) model used in small Hungarian village schools as a lower-cost alternative to closure, though it raises separate pedagogical-equity questions (STEM/technology access) that fall more into the pedagogy domain than finance domain.

I also could not find recent (post-2020) Hungarian-specific data on school bus/"iskolabusz" transport costs or travel-time burdens for consolidated rural pupils — searches returned mostly historical/foreign material.
