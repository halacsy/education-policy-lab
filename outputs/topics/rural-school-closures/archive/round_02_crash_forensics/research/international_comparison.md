# Research Notes: International Comparison — Rural Primary School Closure/Retention Evidence

**Finding 1 — Denmark: rural school closures cause long-term population decline (DiD, strong methodological design).**
A quasi-experimental study of eight village schools closed simultaneously in 2011 in one Danish peripheral municipality finds: 
"Overall, the results show clear evidence of a negative population effect of rural school closures. The ordinary DiD analysis shows a population decline of 7.6 percentage points during the 10-year post-closure period."
 Critically, 
"The flexible DiD analysis points to long-term effects, as the population decline first becomes statistically significant from the sixth year following the closures and onwards."
 Qualitative interviews suggest 
"lock-in effects in terms of social capital and housing markets, which helps to understand the dominance of long-term population effects from school closures."
 Source: Sørensen, Svendsen, Jensen & Schmidt, "Do rural school closures lead to local population decline?", Journal of Rural Studies, Vol 87, 2021. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0743016721002801 (also https://portal.findresearcher.sdu.dk/en/publications/do-rural-school-closures-lead-to-local-population-decline)

**Finding 2 — Mixed/contested prior literature on population effects (important caveat/contested label).**
The same paper notes that 
"Most previous studies have found either mixed results (Elshof et al., 2015; Lourenço Marques et al., 2021) or no population effect of rural school closures (Johnson, 1978; Amcoff, 2012; Barakat, 2015; Kroismayr, 2019)"
, and the authors discuss possible reasons their Danish result differs (geography, area unit of analysis, municipality vs. parish level). This means the "closure → depopulation" causal link is evidence-graded as contested at the cross-national level, even though the single best-identified DiD study (Denmark) finds a strong effect. Source: same as above, https://www.videnomlandet.dk/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/1_s2.0_S0743016721002801_main.pdf

**Finding 3 — Denmark: short-term achievement disruption from consolidation, fading over time (DiD).**
A separate Danish study using 2010-2011 consolidation variation finds 
"individual student test scores declined with consolidation and that the negative overall effect of consolidation appears to be driven by school closings; test scores of students exposed to school closure decrease by 5.9% of a standard deviation (SD)"
, but 
"comparing the two- and four-year achievement gains, the results indicate that the detrimental effect of consolidations seems to diminish over time"
. The authors interpret this as 
"evidence that a disruption cost exists but that the magnitude is not larger than could be compensated for by, for example, smaller class sizes or having teacher's aides."
 Source: Beuchert et al., "The short-term effects of school consolidation on student achievement: Evidence of disruption?", Economics of Education Review, 2018. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0272775717306155

**Finding 4 — US evidence is more mixed/positive than Denmark/China/Chile (cross-country divergence, strong for pattern claim).**
A China-focused study summarizing the international literature states: 
"Studies in the United States have shown an overall positive impact or no lasting adverse effects of school consolidation on children (Brummet, 2014, Carlson and Lavertu, 2016, Engberg et al., 2012), while in countries like Denmark, Chile, and China, negative effects on educational attainment have been found (Beuchert et al., 2018, Grau, Hojman, and Mizala, 2018, Hannum, Liu, and Wang, 2021, Hannum and Wang, 2022)."
 This is directly useful for the disagreement map: consolidation effects are NOT universal — institutional/geographic context (distance, transport quality, receiving-school capacity) mediates outcomes. Source: Zhang, "The Divergent Effects of School Consolidation in Rural China" (2024 working paper). https://pengzhang.weebly.com/uploads/3/1/7/6/31762679/school_consolidation.pdf

**Finding 5 — Chile: large dropout/repetition effects of closure (strong for magnitude, single-country).**

"Grau, Hojman, and Mizala (2018) estimate that school closure increases the probability of high-school dropout between 49 and 68 percent (1.8 and 2.5 percentage points). The authors also identify large causal effects of school closure on grade repetition in primary school."
 Source: cited within Hannum & Wang, "Estimating the Effects of Educational System Consolidation: The Case of China's Rural School Closure Initiative." https://www.researchgate.net/publication/359647128_Estimating_the_Effects_of_Educational_System_Consolidation_The_Case_of_China's_Rural_School_Closure_Initiative

**Finding 6 — China: consolidation costs fall disproportionately on girls and low-SES families (equity/heterogeneity, moderate-strong).**

"For girls exposed to closure during their primary school ages, we find an average decrease of 0.60 years of schooling by 2011"
. Related work finds 
"rural girls are particularly affected by school consolidation, possibly because enrollment decisions for boys are more sensitive to quality, while those for girls are more sensitive to distance"
, and boarding as a coping mechanism has costs: 
"Families were more likely to send boys than girls to be early-grade boarders... early-grade boarding was associated with lower test scores, more depression, and bullying victimization."
 A divergent-effects study also finds SES-differentiated benefits: 
"Following the consolidation of schools, children from high-SES families have a higher likelihood of completing middle school, with a 2.6 percentage point increase for each additional closure of a primary school per 1,000 students."
 Sources: https://fanwangecon.github.io/assets/HannumWangSchoolConsolidationEthLang.pdf ; https://arxiv.org/pdf/2204.01196 ; https://pengzhang.weebly.com/uploads/3/1/7/6/31762679/school_consolidation.pdf

**Finding 7 — India (Rajasthan): consolidation enrollment losses concentrated among disabled/disadvantaged castes (equity, moderate — single-state study).**

"Basic infrastructure was better in consolidated schools, though the number of students per classroom did increase post consolidation (22). However, Bordoloi and Shukla (2019, 23) report a greater decline in enrollment in consolidated schools compared to all government schools across Rajasthan, and that impacts were not equally borne."
 The authors conclude 
"the decline in [enrollment] seems to be the highest for students with disability, followed by that of [Scheduled Caste] and [Scheduled Tribe] students."
 Source: cited in Hannum & Wang (arxiv), https://arxiv.org/pdf/2204.01196

**Finding 8 — OECD/PISA: rural-urban achievement gap largely explained by socioeconomic composition, not ruralness per se (strong, cross-national).**
The OECD's rural education working paper based on PISA 2015 finds that 
"Rural-urban gaps in academic performance generally disappear after accounting for"
 socioeconomic status, and notably 
"some of the perceived challenges of providing rural education, such as an inadequate infrastructure or a lack of quality teachers, are far from universal."
 A related PISA-based comparison of Australia, Canada, and New Zealand confirms the raw gap magnitude: 
"the academic performance of 15-year-old students in cities with 100,000 or more residents is, on average, about one-half year of schooling greater than their peers in smaller towns and communities"
, but adds the crucial qualifier that 
"Analysis of individual countries has shown, however, that urban/rural achievement differences do not exist in almost half of the developed countries that participate in PISA."
 This strongly implies policy/context (not rurality itself) drives the gap — directly relevant to whether Hungary's rural gap is a "closure problem" or a "resourcing/segregation problem." Sources: OECD Education Working Paper No. 196, "Learning in rural schools", https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2019/03/learning-in-rural-schools_75ae062d/8b1a5cb9-en.pdf and https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/learning-in-rural-schools_8b1a5cb9-en.html ; comparison study https://www.researchgate.net/publication/328139989_A_Comparison_of_Rural_Educational_Disadvantage_in_Australia_Canada_and_New_Zealand_Using_OECD's_PISA

**Finding 9 — Multigrade schooling as an internationally established alternative to closure (moderate-strong, World Bank + recent systematic review).**
The World Bank's foundational review states plainly: 
"Multigrade schools offer small towns and villages an alternative to closing uneconomical single grade schools. Keeping village schools alive also helps to preserve the identity of the local communities."
 It notes this is not a developing-world-only phenomenon: 
"The multigrade school, however, is still important as an organizational form in many suburban and rural areas of North America and Europe. Multigrade schools provide quality services in rural parts of Scotland, the Scandinavian countries, Canada, France and the United States."
 and 
"In France, Britain and the Netherlands, multigrade schools have been established in small communities in response to declining pupil intakes and staff reductions."
 A 2025 systematic review of 40 international studies (2019–2024) confirms this remains active policy practice: 
"In light of the challenges posed by demographic and geographical constraints in rural contexts, multigrade classrooms have become a prevalent educational strategy (Karlberg-Granlund, 2023)."
 UNESCO is cited as supportive conditional on quality inputs: 
"International organizations such as UNESCO have promoted research on the effectiveness of such measures, highlighting their role in guaranteeing the right to education in isolated communities, provided that adequate conditions such as teacher training, adapted resources, and institutional support are in place."
 Sources: https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/907201468739447287/pdf/multi-page.pdf ; https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7102/15/8/1052

**Finding 10 — Achievement evidence on multigrade vs. single-grade is roughly null (moderate, older Danish/Dutch data).**
A US federal resource handbook summarizing research (including a Danish study by Kral, 1995, comparing small multigrade schools <110 students vs. large single-grade schools >250 students) reports: 
"Kral (1995) examined the effects of multigrade versus single-grade classes on mathematics, language, and reading performance of second-, fourth-, and sixth-grade students in Denmark... The small (urban and rural) elementary schools instructed their students in multigrade classes encompassing two or three grade levels, while the large schools instructed their students in single-grade classes."
 After controlling for covariates, 
"No systematic differences were found between the combination and single-grade class (total ES = -.06)."
 This supports the claim that well-run multigrade instruction need not sacrifice achievement, but implementation is demanding: 
"The multigrade classroom is labor intensive and requires more planning, collaboration, and professional development than the conventional graded classroom."
 Source: https://educationnorthwest.org/sites/default/files/multigrade-classroom-books1-7.pdf

**Finding 11 — Hungary context data: rural/Roma dropout gap and uneven early-childhood coverage in small villages (national statistic, strong/official EU source).**
Hungary's 2025 EU Education and Training Monitor country report states early school leaving 
"is higher in the least developed regions, in villages (község) and among Roma (58.7%; non-Roma: 9.3% in 2023)"
, and on the earlier education stage, 
"regional coverage of kindergartens remains uneven: in 2022, 31% of municipalities had no kindergarten (Varga, 2024), typically in small villages where the number of kindergarten-aged children did not reach 8."
 This establishes that Hungary already has a pattern of small-settlement service withdrawal tied to enrollment thresholds (a proxy for how a "under-8-pupils" rule might extend to primary schools), and that closures/non-provision fall disproportionately on Roma/disadvantaged rural populations — directly relevant to equity framing. Source: European Commission, Education and Training Monitor 2025 – Hungary. https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html

**Finding 12 — Poland: locality size threshold below which primary schools tend not to be sustained (moderate, recent large-N dataset — useful comparator for Hungary's similar post-socialist settlement structure).**
A large Polish dataset study finds 
"drawing on detailed demographic and educational data from over 42,400 localities and 13,100 schools in Poland... the presence of schools in smaller localities is closely related to population size, particularly within the 400–700 inhabitant range."
 and 
"average school size is statistically associated with locality size – up to a population of 4,100, roughly."
 Beyond this locality size, school size normalizes. This is a useful quantitative anchor for the ~2,000-population villages in scope of the Hungary brief. Source: cited in the Danish population-decline paper's reference list (Poland settlement-size study), retrievable via https://www.researchgate.net/publication/355001456_Do_rural_school_closures_lead_to_local_population_decline
