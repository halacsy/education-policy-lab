Below are the research notes (prose, with full URLs) gathered on Poland's rural primary school experience — the closest structural analogue in my domain to Hungary's rural depopulation/school-closure question, since Poland's 1999 and 2016-2019 reforms both forced repeated "school network" reorganizations that hit rural/village primary schools hardest.

**Finding 1 — Scale of rural school closures during the 1999 reform.** The 1999 reform's shortening of primary school from 8 to 6 years (with 3-year gimnazjum added) reduced the financial and demographic viability of many village primary schools; local authorities were compelled to close them and bus pupils elsewhere. Source: "Reforming Education in Poland," Springer, https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-41882-3_7 — "Because education financing was dependent on the number of students, the funds allocated to small rural schools were insufficient. Many of them were closed, and pupils had to be transported to larger establishments."

**Finding 2 — Independent confirmation from a practitioner account.** A rural-schools activist reported receiving requests for help from 800 villages fighting closures after the 1999 reform, and described closure in a village as fundamentally different from urban closure. Source: HuffPost, "Rescuing Rural Schools in Poland," https://www.huffpost.com/entry/rescuing-rural-schools-in_b_6678580 — "Closing a school in the village basically means the slow degradation and decline of the entire community."

**Finding 3 — Grassroots "social school" (association-run) alternative model.** As a direct community response to closures after 1999, roughly 500-600 schools were established/rescued by parent-and-teacher associations acting as new "citizens'" organizations akin to charter schools, replacing closed public schools. Source: HuffPost (same as above) — "At the moment around 500 to 600 schools were established as a result of this initiative." This is corroborated by academic literature noting a rising share of rural schools run by "stowarzyszenia" (associations) rather than municipalities, a trend that has continued: Semczuk (2018), "Zmiany w sieci szkół podstawowych," https://yadda.icm.edu.pl/baztech/element/bwmeta1.element.baztech-f863d048-de94-41f2-8fc6-f8f5cd4cd030/c/Biuletyn_KPZK_272_Semczuk.pdf — notes a rule-of-thumb that a commune needs at least ~70 pupils to sustain a village school, and that even association-run schools "despite lower operating costs... will have to disappear from the school network" under continued demographic decline (translated from Polish).

**Finding 4 — National closure statistics, 2007-2012 (demographic decline period, pre-2017 reform).** Poland closed 954 primary schools between 2007 and 2012, 90% liquidated by local governments, mostly small branch/combined-class rural schools, driven simply by lack of pupils — the average school size (~180 pupils) did not change, indicating a supply-matching-demand closure pattern rather than austerity per se. Source: IBE (Educational Research Institute), "Ile naprawdę zamknięto szkół w Polsce," https://www.ibe.edu.pl/pl/aktualnosci/328-ile-naprawde-zamknieto-szkol-w-polsce — "Od 2007 r. do 2012 r. zamknięto 954 szkoły podstawowe (90 proc. z nich to szkoły zlikwidowane przez samorządy)."

**Finding 5 — Political-economy of local closure decisions.** The same IBE report found closures cluster in election years and are more frequent/contested in rural gminas because a local councillor is a known neighbor, not an anonymous politician, making closure decisions more socially costly and closely watched than in cities. Source: IBE, https://www.ibe.edu.pl/pl/aktualnosci/328-ile-naprawde-zamknieto-szkol-w-polsce — "W gminach wiejskich radny to często sąsiad czy kolega ze szkoły."

**Finding 6 — Long-run network shrinkage 2000-2021.** A 2025 peer-reviewed study found that nationally the number of primary schools fell 17% while pupil numbers fell only 3% over 2000-2021 (two phases: 2000-2013 pupil decline ~40% in both cities and villages; 2014-2021 pupil numbers actually rose, more in cities). Crucially, urban school counts rose 6.5% while rural school counts fell 28% over the period — rural areas absorbed nearly all the net closures nationally, and losses concentrated in eastern Poland, far from metro regional centers. Source: "Przemiany w szkolnictwie podstawowym w Polsce (2000–2021)," Prace Geograficzne 2025, https://ejournals.eu/czasopismo/prace-geograficzne/artykul/przemiany-w-szkolnictwie-podstawowym-w-polsce-2000-2021-uwarunkowania-demograficzne-i-przestrzenne — "Liczba szkół w miastach ostatecznie jednak się zwiększyła (6,5%), a na obszarach wiejskich zmniejszyła (28%)."

**Finding 7 — Renewed wave of closures tied to current demographic collapse (2025 data).** In 2025 Polish local governments (samorządy) closed 148 public primary schools; 123 of these had ≤70 pupils, and 146 of the 148 were in rural areas, concentrated in Lubelskie (22), Podkarpackie (18) and Dolnośląskie (15) voivodeships. This is explicitly linked to record-low birth rates (238,000 births in 2025, lowest since WWII) and a Ministry of Education projection of over one million fewer pupils in the next decade. Source: Money.pl, "Demograficzna zapaść. Kryzys już widać. Likwidują szkoły," https://www.money.pl/gospodarka/demograficzna-zapasc-kryzys-juz-widac-likwiduja-szkoly-7265905495185728a.html — "W 2025 roku samorządy zlikwidowały łącznie 148 publicznych szkół podstawowych... aż 123 to placówki liczące do 70 uczniów, a 146 likwidacji dotyczyło szkół na wsi."

**Finding 8 — 2015 funding-formula fix to help small rural schools survive.** In response to the closure wave, Poland's Ministry of Education introduced (2014 regulation, effective 2015) a higher per-pupil subvention multiplier for rural primary schools (villages under 5,000 residents), raising the per-child subsidy for the smallest schools (≤70 pupils) to roughly 8,400 PLN — an explicit fiscal tool to keep small village schools financially viable rather than force mergers. Source: Semczuk (2018), https://yadda.icm.edu.pl/baztech/element/bwmeta1.element.baztech-f863d048-de94-41f2-8fc6-f8f5cd4cd030/c/Biuletyn_KPZK_272_Semczuk.pdf — describes the "wyższy przelicznik dla uczniów szkół podstawowych z terenów wiejskich."

**Finding 9 — Statutory "shrink, don't close" alternative model.** Poland's education law (per Eurydice) allows a small school to formally continue operating only grades I-III (ages 7-9, closest to home) while transferring grades IV-VIII to a larger, better-equipped hub school, and permits repurposing unused parts of school buildings as community/cultural centers, day-care, or senior activities — an explicit policy alternative to outright closure aimed at preserving rural community hubs. Source: Eurydice, "National reforms in school education," https://eurydice.eacea.ec.europa.eu/eurypedia/poland/national-reforms-school-education — "The Ministry of Education is keen to introduce such regulations as an alternative to the closure of small primary schools," and "the infrastructure of schools... becoming disused due to demographic changes, can thus be an opportunity to meet the needs of parents returning to the labour market."

**Finding 10 — Documented consequence chain of rural school closure (comparative literature).** Academic synthesis across countries (including Poland) finds that closing a village school typically lowers the settlement's rank in the local service hierarchy, triggering reduction/loss of bus routes, shops, post offices, and kindergartens — i.e., closure is a driver, not just a symptom, of further rural decline. Source: "Reorganization of the Primary School Network in the Polish Carpathians," Journal of Settlements and Spatial Planning, https://geografie.ubbcluj.ro/ccau/jssp/arhiva_JSSPSI2021CSPTER/07JSSPSI2021CSPTER.pdf — "lowers the rank of the village in the local settlement structure due to the reduction or closure of bus routes, shops, post offices or kindergartens."

**Finding 11 — Poland's own gimnazjum-reversal (2017) also reshuffled rural school networks a second time.** The 2016-2017 "Good School" reform, which abolished gimnazjums and restored 8-year primary schools, required roughly 7,000 junior high schools nationwide to close/merge and forced a second wholesale reconfiguration of local school networks within about 10 months of the law's passage — a repeat of the same rural disruption pattern seen in 1999, this time in reverse. Source: Euronews, "Poland rips up school system in rapid reform," https://www.euronews.com/2017/09/08/poland-rips-up-school-system-in-rapid-reform — "some 7,000 junior high schools will have to be closed."
