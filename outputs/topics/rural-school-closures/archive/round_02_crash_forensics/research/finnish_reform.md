## Research Notes — Finnish Rural School Policy & Reform History (for Hungary rural primary school brief)

**1. Scale of Finland's ongoing school-network consolidation (2023-2025 data).** 
The number of comprehensive schools in Finland has continued to decline, dropping to 1,974 by the end of 2024, down from 2,014 schools in 2023 and a sharp fall from 2,498 a decade ago—a 21% reduction over ten years.
 
During 2024, a total of 78 schools were closed or merged with other institutions, according to data from Statistics Finland, with the highest number of closures or mergers occurring in Uusimaa and North Ostrobothnia.
 
Over the past decade, the relative decline in school numbers has been most pronounced in South Karelia, where 39% of comprehensive schools have disappeared.
 Source: Helsinki Times, Feb 2025, https://www.helsinkitimes.fi/themes/themes/education/26140-number-of-primary-schools-in-finland-drops-below-2-000.html

**2. Future projections tied to birth-rate collapse.** 
This dramatic predicted drop in the number of students will make it impractical for some schools, especially ones in rural areas, to remain open,
 and 
some areas in Finland will no longer be able to offer elementary school education to local children, meaning that they will have to travel to neighbouring municipalities to attend school.
 
Finland's birth rate has long been in decline, with the downward trend leading to the nation's fertility rate hitting a historic low of 1.25 in 2024.
 Source: Yle, Sept 2025, https://yle.fi/a/74-20182333

**3. EDUFI 2020 scenario report on network shrinkage.** 
The mildest scenario put forward by EDUFI sees the country closing around a quarter of schools, while a second scenario suggested the national network could shrink by 37 percent — "it's more likely a third of schools will close," said Kari Nyyssölä, counsellor of education at EDUFI.
 
In 2018, Finland had 100 schools with over 700 students, up from 13 schools of that size in the year 2000, while the average size of the student body increased from 145 to 236 pupils.
 
Between 2000 and 2018 the number of schools in Finland decreased by 42 percent, with small schools with fewer than 50 pupils most at risk of closure.
 Cost driver: 
"It's definitely cheaper [to have a bigger school]. The cost per pupil can be twice as high in Oulu's smaller schools," explained Ritaharju school principal Pertti Parpala.
 But 
few follow-up studies have been conducted to determine whether municipalities actually saved money by closing small schools.
 Community-hub argument: 
Urban geographer Venla Bernelius from Helsinki University pointed out that schools should be seen as more than providers of education — "schools are central to the appeal of an entire area."
 Source: Yle, Aug 2020, https://yle.fi/a/3-11478615

**4. Causal evidence that closure accelerates depopulation (strongest empirical finding).** A peer-reviewed difference-in-differences study 
examines the impacts of school closures on the population development of local communities using DID regression modelling combined with matching methods, focusing on the years 2010–2018, and finds that in Finland after school closure, the population of the surrounding community decreased more than before school closure.
 
In rural communities the impacts of school closure have been slightly more negative, suggesting a higher risk of self-reinforcing population decline.
 
The increasing depopulation after a school closure means that school closures do not just raise questions about the individual school or the municipal school network, but more broadly about the vitality of the municipality and its development.
 Source: ScienceDirect (Journal of Rural Studies), 2021, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0743016721000115

**5. Small rural schools as last remaining service / community hub — quantified.** 
Over 30% of primary schools in Finland are small schools with 2–4 permanent teachers and teaching groups, most located in sparsely populated areas, and almost 20% of each age group studies at a rural primary school with less than 100 pupils.
 
In many municipalities the small rural pre-school and primary school is the last basic service located in the village—health services, shops and post-offices having already been centralised in the populated areas.
 
Research strongly indicated that village schools have been forced to consciously take part in the development of the surrounding community by functioning as a hub for community activities.
 Source: Kalaoja & Pietarinen, International Journal of Educational Research, ScienceDirect, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0883035509000160

**6. Multi-grade teaching as the pedagogical model that keeps small schools viable.** 
Multi-grade teaching is implemented in order to prevent schools from closing, as it enables the stabilization of learning-group sizes in rural areas in which birth rates have declined and out-migration has increased.
 A comparative Austria–Finland study describes 
discussions about maintaining or closing small rural schools, [in which] pedagogical arguments have often been neglected.
 A 2025 follow-up on teacher training argues 
that ITE and CPD for teachers in Finland should increase the effectiveness of all teachers in multi-grade settings,
 identifying benefits such as 
freedom of teaching and learning, cooperation with parents, getting to know the child, a safe environment... and the possibility to create learning situations for all kinds of learners.
 Sources: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0883035515000749 ; https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0883035525001582

**7. Historical origin: rural areas were the reform's testbed, not its casualty (transferable design principle).** 
The comprehensive school reform was implemented between 1972 and 1977, beginning first in the northern and north-eastern (rural) areas of Finland and continuing towards the south; the state supported municipalities in constructing new schools, and while this resulted in the closure of some smaller rural schools, the state supported regional diversity by emphasizing the importance to children of the local school.
 Preconditions for reform success included 
the common hardships and experiences of WWII creating consensus, the simultaneous emphasis on social equity and economic growth backed by economic and social theories, a multi-party system fostering compromise, and involvement of all relevant stakeholders in the reform process.
 Sources: Oxford Academic "Great Policy Successes," https://academic.oup.com/book/42635/chapter/358101849 ; ResearchGate, https://www.researchgate.net/publication/275634576_A_history_of_educational_reforms_in_Finland

**8. Local resistance / stakeholder voice research.** A Journal of Research in Rural Education study notes 
that the network of small rural schools in Finland has been radically weakened since the global recession of the 1990s, and focuses on the social role of rural schools and the phenomenon of school closures, aiming to look at rural schools from the viewpoint of local residents.
 It uses 
social capital... to understand the role of a school in the community: its role as a producer and maintainer of local social capital.
 Source: https://jrre.psu.edu/sites/default/files/2019-08/29-1.pdf

**9. Direct Hungary comparison — a directly relevant transfer case.** A study titled "Rescuing a Small Village School in the Context of Rural Change in Hungary" reports that 
small scale rural municipalities in Hungary are facing grave financial difficulties, and pressed amalgamations/closures between 2004 and 2008 halved the numbers of independently run units.
 It also found 
an estimated 10% of small schools were closed, whilst some 30–35% were amalgamated and continued under the administration of a central unit,
 and importantly 
parental choices and community actions might resist state policies, with inter-community and external networks and social capital generated by them determining the achievements of community action.
 Sources: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/257246275_Rescuing_a_Small_Village_School_in_the_Context_of_Rural_Change_in_Hungary ; https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0743016712000216

**10. Hungary's own historical rural-school-building tradition (pre-communist parallel to Finland's 1972 rollout).** In the 1920s, 
Klebelsberg's ministry launched a national program of building "people's schools" in 1926, and by 1930 had established 5000 new elementary schoolrooms and flats for teachers, with spatial focus on rural areas with small villages and scattered farms (tanya), where two thirds of the new physical infrastructure was installed.
 Source: Springer, "Ideology, Spatial Planning, and Rural Schools," https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-18799-6_6

**11. Contemporary Hungarian rural depopulation and school-linked anecdote.** 
One rural mayor's office now occupies the space where the village school once stood — "the school had to close because there were no more children."
 
The northern Hungarian region has experienced a significant population decrease, with the number of residents dropping by 184,000 in the most recent census.
 Source: CGTN, Sept 2023, https://newseu.cgtn.com/news/2023-09-17/Hungary-s-worrying-population-decline-as-prospect-of-ghost-towns-looms-1n9y7GDlOpy/index.html

**12. Territorial/institutional-burden framing for current Hungarian system.** A 2025 study notes 
in many countries, school closures and teacher shortages disproportionately affect peripheral or economically deprived regions, exacerbating territorial disadvantaged cycles,
 and identifies Hungarian settlements where 
these settlements have a relatively small child population (typically around 50–70 children), [and] their near-total HH prevalence signals concentrated educational marginalization.
 Source: ResearchGate, Oct 2025, https://www.researchgate.net/publication/396483025_Shrinking_growing_or_overburdened_Territorial_patterns_in_child_demographics_and_institutional_burden_in_Hungary's_public_education_system_after_2021
