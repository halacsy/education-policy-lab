## RESEARCH NOTES (prose, for later structuring — full findings + URLs)

**1. Between- vs within-school segregation in Hungary.** A 2020 CEU working paper argues that Hungarian local governments historically preserved small, single-class-per-grade rural schools rather than merging or closing them, which reduced the *within-school* sorting of pupils by ability/ethnicity but left ample room for *between-school* segregation as non-Roma families increasingly enroll children in schools of other settlements ("white flight"). Source: CEU Center for Policy Studies, "The Wider Context of the Segregation of Roma Pupils in Hungary" (2020). https://cps.ceu.edu/sites/cps.ceu.edu/files/attachment/publication/3258/cps-working-paper-educ-social-selection-education-hungary-2020.pdf

**2. Tipping-point dynamics.** The same paper cites Havas–Liskó (2005): once the share of Roma pupils in a primary school passes roughly 50%, the exit of non-Roma pupils to other schools accelerates sharply, pushing the school into what the author calls an "irreversible spiral of segregation." Same source as above.

**3. Bulgaria counter-example.** The CEU paper notes that a centrally administered 2006 Bulgarian program that closed hundreds of very small rural schools in mountainous regions *reduced* Roma segregation — evidence that consolidation can cut segregation when between-school sorting (rather than within-school sorting) is the dominant mechanism. Same source.

**4. Hungary kept its rural network largely intact for decades.** A 2012 ScienceDirect case study on the village of Erdőkürt documents that although state funding for small village schools fell to roughly 50% of costs by the turn of the millennium, closures/amalgamations were rare; only centrally pressed reforms between 2004–2008 halved the number of independently operated school units, against a backdrop of extreme municipal fragmentation (Hungary's ~3,200 local authorities averaged population 3,200 in 2008, far more fragmented than Czechia/Slovakia). Source: "Rescuing a small village school in the context of rural change in Hungary," Journal of Rural Studies / ScienceDirect (2012). https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0743016712000216

**5. FRA/EC data on segregation scale.** EU Fundamental Rights Agency research (cited via RomArchive) found segregation in mainstream education affects between 33% and 58% of Romani children across the Czech Republic, Hungary, Slovakia, and Greece, and that on average one in ten Roma children has attended a special school or class mainly for Roma. An earlier European Commission communication put Hungary's share of marginalized Romani children in segregated classes at 45%, versus 29% (Bulgaria), 33% (Czech Republic), 26% (Romania). Sources: https://www.romarchive.eu/en/roma-civil-rights-movement/history-school-desegregation-roma/ and https://www.errc.org/news/hungary-a-short-history-of-segregation

**6. Recent ECtHR ruling.** In March 2023 the European Court of Human Rights ruled against Hungary in the case of Jókai Mór Primary School (Piliscsaba), finding a violation of the right to education combined with non-discrimination, and obliging Hungary not just to desegregate that school but to develop broader public-policy measures against racial segregation in education. Source: ERRC press release, 30 March 2023. https://www.errc.org/press-releases/european-court-orders-hungary-to-desegregate-romani-school-and-pay-damages

**7. Hungary's PISA 2022 SES gap.** OECD's PISA 2022 Volume I finds the performance gap related to students' socio-economic status is widest in Romania and the Slovak Republic, "followed by Hungary, Israel and Chinese Taipei," and Hungary's country note states this SES-linked math gap has remained stable between 2012 and 2022. Sources: https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2023/12/pisa-2022-results-volume-i_76772a36/full-report/the-state-of-learning-and-equity-in-education-in-2022_e65c570e.html and https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/pisa-2022-results-volume-i-and-ii-country-notes_ed6fbcc5-en/hungary_3df2ae68-en.html

**8. US closure-effects research (disadvantaged pupils).** A 2024 study (reported by Education Week) using administrative data found a statistically significant drop in math/reading scores among students who experienced a school closure, a 7.3-percentage-point increase in absenteeism for economically disadvantaged students specifically, a 38% increase in days of disciplinary action for that group, and longer-term negative effects on higher-education attainment and wages; effects were more pronounced for Hispanic and economically disadvantaged students. Source: EdWeek, "The Harm of School Closures Can Last a Lifetime, New Research Shows" (June 2024). https://www.edweek.org/leadership/the-harm-of-school-closures-can-last-a-lifetime-new-research-shows/2024/06

**9. Peer-effects/selection evidence from Sweden.** A 2020 ScienceDirect study using family fixed effects to correct for selection into closure found that closures need to generate relatively large positive peer effects to raise achievement, and that, overall, school closures showed no net effect on student achievement across the sample studied. Source: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0927537120301147

**10. Receiving-school spillovers.** Fordham Institute's research summary describes multiple US studies (Michigan, Philadelphia, Steinberg & MacDonald 2019) showing that achievement gains for displaced students from closed low-performing schools often come at the cost of disruption and negative spillovers for incumbent peers in the "receiving" schools, and that displaced-student gains only materialize when they move to *significantly* higher-performing schools. Sources: https://fordhaminstitute.org/national/commentary/impact-school-closures-deep-dive-research and https://fordhaminstitute.org/national/commentary/short-and-long-term-impacts-school-closure-student-outcomes

**11. Distributional targeting of closures.** A Research for Action synthesis notes that schools serving a greater number of Black/marginalized students are more likely to close "regardless of school characteristics like enrollment," and documents broader community-level harms of closures (disruption to civic engagement, economic stability, gentrification), alongside the general finding that displacement to higher-performing schools mitigates harm. Source: https://www.researchforaction.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/06/revisiting-research-on-school-closings-key-learnings-for-district-and-community-leaders.pdf

**12. Rural school-bus/commuting burden (transport equity).** Multiple sources confirm that school closures/consolidation lengthen commutes, and that longer bus rides are associated (in NYC and Canadian studies) with reduced attendance and, for some groups, worse academic outcomes, though effects are heterogeneous and rural-specific causal evidence remains thin. Sources: https://www.brookings.edu/articles/can-school-buses-improve-access-for-students-without-driving-down-academic-outcomes/ ; https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/cag.12957 ; https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0743016718308611
