# Research Notes: School-Network Planning for Rural Hungarian Primary Schools

## Finding 1: Record-low birth rates accelerating school-closure pressure
Hungary recorded its lowest-ever number of births in 2023, and this decline continued into 2024-2025. Data show total fertility rate fell below 1.3 by August 2025, down from 1.55 in 2023. Officials warn this is not a distant risk: kindergarten groups may close within 3 years and school classes/whole schools within 6 years in some regions.
Source: "Megdőlt a negatív születési rekord..." Válasz Online, Jan 28, 2024, https://www.valaszonline.hu/2024/01/28/demografia-szuletesszam-lakossag-fogyas-magyarorszag-szuletesek-negativ-rekord/
Source: "Katasztrofális a helyzet a közoktatásban..." Economx, May 19, 2026, https://www.economx.hu/belfold/2026/05/19/robbanas-elotti-allapot-totyik-tamas-psz-demografiai-kozoktatas-gyermekszam-pedagogusok/

## Finding 2: Sharp national decline in primary enrollment, accelerating school-count reduction
KSH data show 710,000 students in daytime primary education in 2024/2025 vs 712,000 the year before (-0.3%), with first-graders down 1.9%. Compared to 2005/2006 (859,000 students), the drop is 17.3%. The number of primary "feladatellátási hely" (school sites) fell to 3,537 in 2024/2025 — 13 fewer than the previous year, 38 fewer than 2022/2023, and 88 fewer than 2021/2022.
Source: "Egyre kevesebb az általános iskolás Magyarországon," Népszava, https://nepszava.hu/3287108_altalanos-iskola-diakok-letszam-csokkenes-magyarorszag-ksh

## Finding 3: Union warns closures/mergers are "almost inevitable"
Totyik Tamás (Pedagógusok Szakszervezete president) states some schools already receive zero first-grade enrollments, and within 5-10 years serious rethinking of the school network will be needed; he calls consolidation of small schools "almost certainly inevitable."
Source: "Hatalmas változás készül: rengeteg iskola bezárhatja a kapuit," Economx, July 15, 2025, https://www.economx.hu/magyar-gazdasag/2025/07/15/altalanos-iskola-ksh-gyermek-diak-kozoktatas-psz-totyik-tamas-osztaly-812691/

## Finding 4: Government's official stance — protect small schools "as long as demand exists," via mergers not closures
Ministerial commissioner Balatoni Katalin stated the goal is not closure but protection of small settlements/small schools "amíg erre van igény" (as long as there is demand), with government doing everything to keep education running "akár összevonásokkal és helyi megoldásokkal" (even through mergers and local solutions).
Source: "Óvodák már lakat alatt, de itt lappang az iskolabezárási hullám is," Válasz Online, Oct 17, 2025, https://www.valaszonline.hu/2025/10/17/ovodak-iskolak-bezarasa-demografia-oktatas/

## Finding 5: Regional concentration of demographic collapse — five counties lost >1% population/year
In 2024, five Hungarian counties saw combined mortality-surplus + out-migration losses exceeding 1% of population in a single year — three in eastern Hungary, two in western Hungary (the sparsely-villaged, aging Vas and Zala regions). District-level birth data show extremes: Bélapátfalva district had only 55 births last year, Tab district 67, and Szentgotthárd/Bóly/Hegyhát districts under 80.
Source: Válasz Online, Oct 17, 2025 (link above)
Source: Economx, May 19, 2026 (link above)

## Finding 6: Cost-efficiency threshold around 200 students, but savings estimates are overstated
Hermann Zoltán's cost-function research (cited in OFI analysis) found per-pupil operating costs decline sharply as school size increases up to roughly 200 students, after which economies of scale flatten — suggesting consolidation below this threshold could yield real savings. However, the same analysis explicitly cautions that such savings estimates are an upper bound because they do not account for student transportation costs or other added expenses of larger school size.
Source: "A kistelepülések kisiskolái – társulás?" Oktatáskutató és Fejlesztő Intézet, https://ofi.oh.gov.hu/tudastar/onkormanyzat-kozoktatas/kistelepulesek

## Finding 7: Small schools have fewer students per teacher but more unqualified teaching and more remedial students
In small schools ("kisiskolák"), the student-to-teacher ratio is 14, versus 18.9 in larger village schools and 21.9 in urban schools. Yet small schools have a much higher share of unqualified-teacher hours (13.4%) versus large village schools (9.1%) and urban schools (3.1%), and combined-grade classes and remedial-catch-up enrollment are significantly higher in small settlements at both lower and upper primary levels.
Source: OFI, https://ofi.oh.gov.hu/tudastar/onkormanyzat-kozoktatas/kistelepulesek

## Finding 8: Legal minimum-enrollment threshold for local school retention (Hungarian law)
Hungary's 2011 National Public Education Act (2011. évi CXC. törvény), Section 89, sets a rule: in a settlement where at least 8 kindergarten-age or 8 lower-primary-age children reside and demographic data suggest this will hold for at least 3 years, and if parents request it, the local municipality may initiate school maintenance provisions (multi-grade/combined classes).
Source: "2011 Közoktatás, pedagógusképzés, neveléstudomány," ELTE Reader, https://www.eltereader.hu/media/2013/06/Uj_kutatasok_2011_opt.pdf

## Finding 9: Real-world workaround when heating/budget forces closure — school in a football clubhouse
In Babót and Agyagosszergény (Győr-Moson-Sopron county), when a school was emptied citing energy-saving reasons and parents refused busing to a neighboring village (Fertőd), the municipality converted a renovated former-family-house/football-club changing room into two combined-grade classrooms (grades 1-2 and 3-4) rather than closing local provision entirely, via an agreement between the school-maintaining "tankerület" and the municipality.
Source: "Több faluban megadta magát az iskolafenntartó..." Népszava, Nov 8, 2022, https://nepszava.hu/3174892_oktatas-magyarorszag-falu-iskola-fenntarto-soproni-tankeruleti-kozpont

## Finding 10: Community reclaiming school via municipal independence — Somoskőújfalu case
A village (Somoskőújfalu) that had lost its school when merged administratively into the town of Salgótarján regained an independent school after achieving municipal self-governance independence; after three years of a silent building, classrooms filled again with children — illustrating that administrative/status changes, not just enrollment numbers, can determine whether a village keeps a functioning school.
Source: "Kell-e nekünk kisiskola?" Szabad Föld, https://szabadfold.hu/hazai-elet/kell-e-nekunk-kisiskola-202959/

## Finding 11: International research on consolidation is genuinely mixed, not settled
US research on school district consolidation shows contested results: peer-reviewed studies find "surprisingly little research has directly investigated its effectiveness," and empirical findings on consolidation's effect on student achievement are mixed (citing Beuchert et al. 2018, De Haan et al. 2016, Humlum & Smith 2015). A Mississippi analysis of seven small rural districts merged 2014-2018 found standardized test scores were higher pre-consolidation than post-consolidation for both language and math. Conversely, Vermont's 2024 state agency report on Act 46-era mergers found some merged districts were able to add/restart programs like literacy intervention services and world languages — but researchers caution that moving students to bigger, better-resourced schools does not automatically translate into improved achievement or wellbeing given the disruption of transition.
Source: "As Rural Schools Disappear, Policy Battles Emerge Over Consolidation," Governing, Jan 8, 2026, https://www.governing.com/policy/as-rural-schools-disappear-policy-battles-emerge-over-consolidation
Source: "School closures are accelerating in rural America..." Hechinger Report, https://hechingerreport.org/schools-are-closing-across-rural-america-heres-how-a-battle-over-small-districts-is-playing-out-in-one-state/
Source: "Closures & Consolidation: Evidence of Impact," SchoolStatus, Nov 12, 2023, https://www.schoolstatus.com/blog/closures-and-consolidation-impact

## Finding 12: Multigrade classrooms — mixed but not negative pedagogical evidence
A 2025 systematic review of 40 international studies (2019-2024, PRISMA methodology, Scopus/ERIC databases) on multigrade teaching found it fosters "curricular and methodological flexibility" and peer-learning benefits, but also documented persistent challenges including increased teacher workload and limited multigrade-adapted instructional materials, concluding benefits are often undermined without sustained professional development and leadership support.
Source: "Challenges and Opportunities of Multi-Grade Teaching: A Systematic Review," MDPI Education Sciences, Aug 18, 2025, https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7102/15/8/1052

## Finding 13: No national school-bus network was built despite 2018 promise
In 2018 the then-innovation minister said a national school-bus network would be built by 2020, but this "végül nem történt meg" (ultimately did not happen). Parents in villages typically must arrange their own transport of children to schools in other settlements, which is described as difficult for lower-grade pupils or when parents' work schedules are inflexible.
Source: "Megoldás a reggeli dugókra, és még a gyerekek is élvezik," Dívány, Aug 24, 2023, https://divany.hu/szuloseg/iskola-busz/

## Finding 14: Historical school-bus grant model (1996 BM program) — small-scale precedent
A 1996 Ministry of Interior grant program funded school-bus acquisition for small settlements lacking their own school/kindergarten, entirely state-financed. Example: Vásárosfalu (168 residents) received a 15-seat minibus to transport 10-15 children roughly 3 km to schools in Beled and Rábakecöl.
Source: "Iskolabusz a kistelepüléseken," jegyzo.hu, https://jegyzo.hu/iskolabusz-a-kistelepuleseken-20012/

## Finding 15: Closed school buildings often become financial liabilities, hard to repurpose
Budapest's 2010-era secondary-school mergers left 19 vacated school buildings; three years later only 3 had been sold and 3 rented out, while the empty buildings ("szellemházak") cost hundreds of millions of forints annually to maintain. Buyers were largely limited to other public/retail users (e.g., Lidl) due to buildings' specialized, hard-to-convert layouts.
Source: "Senkinek nem kellenek a főváros bezárt iskolái," Origo, https://www.origo.hu/ingatlan/2010/03/fovarosi-kozepiskolak-osszevonasa-valsagban-nehezen-ertekesitheto-vagy-kiadhato-ingatlanok

## Finding 16: Teacher shortage compounds the small-school sustainability problem
Hungary has roughly 7,700 unfilled teaching posts nationally and about 5,000 teachers working without full qualification, out of a workforce of about 160,000 — a structural constraint layered on top of demographic enrollment decline, particularly acute in small rural schools which already show higher rates of unqualified-teacher instruction (Finding 7).
Source: "Katasztrofális a helyzet a közoktatásban," Economx, May 19, 2026, https://www.economx.hu/belfold/2026/05/19/robbanas-elotti-allapot-totyik-tamas-psz-demografiai-kozoktatas-gyermekszam-pedagogusok/ (see also Economx demográfiai apokalipszis piece: https://www.economx.hu/magyar-gazdasag/kozoktatas-demografiai-valsag-pedagogusok-szakszervezete-iskola.817275.html)
