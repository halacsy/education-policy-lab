# Policy scenarios

## S1 — Status quo plus incremental adaptation
**Goal**
Keep the existing municipal/state-maintained village school open as long as feasible via multigrade classrooms, felmenő rendszer phase-out and ad hoc local negotiation, without a national framework.

**Mechanism**
- Multigrade (összevont osztály) teaching keeps per-pupil costs below the closure threshold while preserving local access. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- The Nkt. felmenő rendszer rule prevents mid-cycle disruption to pupils by phasing in structural or curricular changes grade-by-grade. [evidence: strong] [evidence: strong]
- Achievement differences between well-implemented multigrade and single-grade instruction are negligible when adequately supported. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- Local social capital and community mobilization can delay or prevent amalgamation, as documented in the 2003–2007 closure wave (e.g. Erdőkürt). [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- Without active desegregation policy, retaining small village schools can entrench between-school SES and ethnic sorting via selective non-Roma out-migration once Roma enrolment share passes roughly 50%. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
moderate — Multigrade pedagogy and phase-out mechanisms are internationally well-documented, but Hungary-specific causal evidence on long-run viability and equity effects at sub-2,000-population scale is absent.

**Assumptions**
- Teacher supply for multigrade classrooms is adequate without systemic training reform — contested by the scenario's own implementation timeline, which schedules multi-year recurring in-service training, and by national data showing 7,700 unfilled teaching posts and a halved graduate pipeline. [assumption]
- Local decision-makers have comparable financial and demographic data to negotiate fair, transparent retention arrangements — undermined by the absence of any national closure/outcome registry (see S5). [assumption]
- The 8-child legal minimum for municipal school retention functions as both a viable floor and a realistic target across regions with differing population clustering. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Preserves the village school as a community hub, sustaining local identity and settlement attractiveness. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- Avoids the travel burden and disruption associated with abrupt closures. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- Legal safeguards (expert-opinion requirement, felmenő rendszer) provide procedural protection against arbitrary reorganization. [evidence: strong] [evidence: strong]

**Equity impact**
Effects are ambiguous and evidence-thin: retention may reduce short-term travel-burden inequity for lower-SES families who cannot arrange private transport, but absent desegregation measures it risks entrenching between-school SES/ethnic sorting as non-Roma families selectively exit once Roma enrolment share rises, and it offers no mechanism to track or counteract this — a gap the scenario does not address.

**Cost categories**
- Recurring multigrade teacher salary and in-service training costs
- Capital-maintenance backlog on under-enrolled buildings (roof, heating, accessibility)
- Opportunity cost of maintaining spare capacity relative to consolidated alternatives
- Ad hoc local negotiation and administrative overhead (expert opinions, parental consent processes)

**Implementation steps**
- Municipality and tankerületi központ (district center) — Jointly assess enrollment against the 8-child statutory minimum and negotiate continuation terms (timeline: year 1) (timeline: year 1)
- School leaders and teaching staff — Undertake in-service multigrade-pedagogy training, recognizing this requires multi-year lead time and delivery capacity beyond current teacher-training-institute throughput (timeline: year 1-4, recurring) (timeline: year 1-4, recurring)
- Parents and local community associations — Organize demand-demonstration (petitions, enrollment commitments) to sustain the 'demand exists' protection criterion (timeline: year 1-2, ongoing) (timeline: year 1-2, ongoing)
- Ministry / tankerület — Publish annual catchment (körzet) and transport obligation notices per legal requirement (timeline: year 1, annual) (timeline: year 1, annual)

**Political risks**
- Government's 'as long as demand exists' pledge could be reversed or reinterpreted administratively without triggering formal legal change, especially if the NOKS wage dispute remains unresolved and undermines multigrade staffing capacity.
- Absence of a binding statutory preference between preservation and enrollment-based optimization leaves the decision to contestable district-level administrative discretion.
- Demographic collapse (birth rate down ~20% in four years) makes the 'demand exists' criterion increasingly unattainable regardless of political will, risking a credibility gap between pledge and outcome.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether teacher supply for multigrade classrooms can be scaled without systemic training reform, given 7,700 unfilled posts nationally and a halved graduate pipeline. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Pilot implementation of multigrade teaching with random assignment of professional-development intensity, tracking teacher retention and student achievement over 3-5 years.)
- Whether the 'demand' criterion for school protection can be operationalized without political capture or administrative convenience, and how conflicts between parental preference and fiscal sustainability are resolved. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Legal/regulatory clarification of the demand threshold plus transparent cost-sharing rules and case-study analysis of contested past closures.)
- Whether retaining small schools without desegregation measures replicates SES/ethnic sorting patterns documented at larger settlement scales, specifically at sub-2,000-population village level. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Disaggregated national microdata filtered by settlement population under 2,000 and primary grade level, reporting between-school SES and ethnic sorting measures.)

## S2 — Structural grade reorganization (lower grades stay, upper grades centralize)
**Goal**
Restructure so only lower grades (1-4/1-6) remain in the village while upper grades or a new ISCED2-type school are consolidated in a larger town, following the Polish 1999 structural-reform pattern.

**Mechanism**
- Shortening the local primary cycle and centrally siting later-stage schooling reduces per-pupil costs for upper-grade instruction by achieving economies of scale. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- Delayed secondary tracking/centralization is associated in some comparative literature with reduced achievement gaps, but the estimate is contested: most comparative studies show association, not settled causal magnitude, and effects vary strongly by institutional context (Poland's own 1999 reform was reversed in 2016-17). [evidence: contested] [evidence: contested]
- Poland's 2015 rural per-pupil subsidy multiplier (up to ~8,400 PLN for schools with ≤70 pupils) demonstrates a fiscal tool that can partly offset consolidation pressure on remaining lower-grade schools. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- Post-1999-reform closure of Polish village schools accelerated loss of complementary rural services (buses, shops, post offices), amplifying rather than merely reflecting rural decline. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
contested — The core achievement-gap claim is contested in the literature despite being tempting to label strong, and the Polish 1999 reform this scenario models was itself reversed after 17 years, undercutting confidence in durable transfer to Hungary.

**Assumptions**
- Hungary's institutional and municipal governance context is sufficiently similar to Poland's for the 1999-style structural reform and its (contested) PISA-related gains to transfer meaningfully. [assumption]
- Local commuting infrastructure and family logistics can absorb daily upper-grade travel to a centrally sited school without unacceptable welfare or attendance cost. [assumption]
- Polish education-law mechanisms (funding multipliers, split-grade retention, association-school status) are transferable to Hungary's constitutional/administrative structure without fundamental redesign. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Preserves early-childhood proximity (grades 1-4/1-6 stay local), maintaining school-based community anchoring for younger children and their families. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- Achieves economies of scale for upper-grade specialist teaching, potentially reducing unqualified-teacher instruction rates found to be 2-3 times higher in small rural schools. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- May reduce peer-effect losses currently caused by selective gimnázium entry removing top performers from feeder schools, by consolidating a broader upper-grade cohort. [evidence: weak] [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
Effects are genuinely mixed: consolidating upper grades may reduce unqualified-teacher exposure for older pupils, but Hungary's already-wide OECD socioeconomic achievement gap and international evidence (China, India) that consolidation disproportionately harms girls, low-SES and marginalized pupils via distance-sensitive enrollment decisions means benefits are conditional on receiving-school quality and integration, which this scenario does not guarantee or measure.

**Cost categories**
- Severance, redeployment or retraining costs for teachers made redundant by grade consolidation
- Capital costs for expanding or building the centrally sited ISCED2-type school (modular classrooms, capacity scale-up)
- Statutory school-bus/transport establishment and ongoing operating costs for upper-grade commuters
- Local labour-market and service-loss costs in villages as complementary rural services (shops, transit) follow school-function loss

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry / tankerület, in consultation with municipalities — Conduct feasibility and site-selection study for centrally sited ISCED2 school, recognizing administrative, capital, and procurement lead times exceed a single year (timeline: year 1-3) (timeline: year 1-3)
- Ministry / district budget authority — Design and legislate statutory transport guarantee and funding formula, informed by Poland's rural subsidy-multiplier precedent (timeline: year 1-2) (timeline: year 1-2)
- School district / municipality HR units — Negotiate teacher redeployment, severance or retraining pathways for staff affected by grade consolidation (timeline: year 2-4) (timeline: year 2-4)
- Central ISCED2 school and receiving municipality — Build or procure capacity (modular classrooms, staffing scale-up) ahead of first cohort transfer (timeline: year 3-5) (timeline: year 3-5)

**Political risks**
- Poland's 1999 reform was politically reversible and was in fact reversed after 17 years; without explicit cross-party entrenchment mechanisms (statutory supermajority requirements, multi-year budget commitments) the Hungarian version faces the same reversal risk.
- Local councillors face disproportionate electoral pressure from visible closure/consolidation decisions compared to urban counterparts, risking politically timed reversals around election cycles.
- Teacher union mobilization capacity (restored strike rights, PDSZ/PSZ activity) creates a credible threat to consolidation timelines if redeployment terms are perceived as inadequate.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether delayed-tracking/centralization achievement effects generalize to the Hungarian institutional context, given contested comparative evidence and Poland's own reversal of the reform. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A Hungary-specific quasi-experimental or difference-in-differences study of any pilot grade-reorganization, plus updated comparative meta-analysis distinguishing correlation from causal transfer.)
- Whether commuting infrastructure and subsidized transport can sustain daily upper-grade trips without unacceptable welfare cost, especially for lower-SES and disability-affected families. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: National survey of parents on commuting time, fatigue and workforce-participation conflict, combined with a full cost-accounting study of transport, staffing and maintenance across matched consolidation pilots.)
- The true net financial effect of grade-consolidation once severance, capital scale-up, and transport costs are fully accounted remains unverified for the Hungarian case. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Full multi-year cost-accounting comparison of consolidation versus retention scenarios in 3-5 matched Hungarian micro-regions.)

## S3 — Networked retention: clustering, association-run and multi-service hub models
**Goal**
Retain village primary schools through inter-municipal pedagogical networks (RPI-style clustering), association-run publicly-subsidized schools (Polish model), or multi-service hubs combining school with other civic functions.

**Mechanism**
- Association-run village schools (Polish stowarzyszenia model) demonstrate operational flexibility and resilience beyond municipal-only maintainership, at least until roughly 70-pupil viability thresholds are breached. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- Church/foundation maintainers (e.g. Kolping) can bundle school transport with existing rural social-service infrastructure, offering an alternative delivery pathway with demonstrated but limited-scale case examples. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- Multi-service hub models (school combined with childcare, elderly services, community centre) are legally permitted in comparable systems (Poland's building-repurposing provisions) but have limited direct outcome evidence. [evidence: weak] [evidence: weak]
- Even association-operated small village schools face structural viability limits under continued demographic decline, per Polish research finding they will eventually 'disappear from the school network' below sustainable pupil counts. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
moderate — Association-run and clustering models have real-world precedent in Poland and France with moderate evidence of resilience, but Hungarian legal eligibility, funding parity, and long-run sustainability are unverified.

**Assumptions**
- Polish association-run, publicly-subsidized rural schools have achieved genuine operational sustainability — contradicted by the scenario's own evidence that even association schools face structural viability limits and 'will have to disappear' below sustainable enrollment. [assumption]
- A legal mechanism analogous to Poland's association-run school amendment either exists or can be readily established in Hungary's Nkt. framework without fundamental maintainer-law redesign. [assumption]
- Church and foundation maintainers can scale their takeover model beyond a few showcase schools to meaningfully absorb rural-school retention needs nationally. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Offers operational flexibility outside the centralized tankerületi system, potentially responding faster to local viability threats. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- Multi-service hubs may preserve broader rural service infrastructure (childcare, elderly care) alongside the school, partially offsetting the settlement-service-rank decline documented after closures. [evidence: weak] [evidence: weak]
- Inter-municipal pedagogical networks (RPI-style) can pool specialist teaching resources across villages without full physical consolidation. [evidence: weak] [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
Administrative-capacity requirements for association-run or networked models risk disadvantaging the poorest, least-organized villages, which lack the civic infrastructure, grant-writing capacity, or church/foundation presence to establish these arrangements, potentially widening rather than narrowing the gap between well-organized and structurally weak communities; this scenario provides no compensatory mechanism for villages unable to organize such networks.

**Cost categories**
- Association/foundation setup and governance costs (legal registration, grant management, board administration)
- Funding-parity subsidy top-ups to match municipal per-pupil budget levels
- Building repurposing and multi-service-hub retrofit costs (accessibility, multi-use zoning, utilities)
- Inter-municipal network coordination overhead (shared staffing logistics, joint scheduling administration)

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Justice / education legal drafting unit — Review Nkt. founder-status provisions and draft amendment enabling association/foundation-run schools with funding parity, acknowledging this requires changing maintainer law, not merely administrative practice (timeline: year 1-3) (timeline: year 1-3)
- Municipalities and parent/teacher associations — Form pilot associations in 3-5 representative villages to test governance and financial viability (timeline: year 2-4) (timeline: year 2-4)
- Church/foundation maintainers (e.g. Kolping network) — Evaluate and, where feasible, scale bundled transport+social-service takeover models beyond current showcase sites (timeline: year 2-5) (timeline: year 2-5)
- Groups of neighbouring municipalities — Establish RPI-style inter-municipal pedagogical networks with shared specialist staffing agreements (timeline: year 2-4) (timeline: year 2-4)

**Political risks**
- Association-run and church-maintainer models risk being perceived as creeping privatization of public education, drawing organized union resistance beyond what the scenario currently anticipates.
- Multi-stakeholder cost-sharing arrangements have already proven fragile in Hungary: the Gersekarát diocese-plus-3-municipalities model collapsed after one year despite a signed five-year contract, indicating a concrete, documented precedent for arrangement failure that this scenario must design against, not merely acknowledge.
- Legal reform of maintainer status to permit association-run schools with funding parity may face administrative resistance from the centralized tankerületi system, which currently holds exclusive closure/reorganization authority.

**Uncertainties**
- The long-run sustainability threshold for association-run village schools is unknown; Polish evidence suggests ~70 pupils, but the boundary between hard operational and soft community-cohesion viability is unquantified. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Longitudinal data on association-school closure rates and demographic/enrolment thresholds at which they fail, plus comparative cost-benefit analysis against consolidated alternatives.)
- Whether Hungary's legal eligibility and governance rules permit association/civic-organization operators to manage primary schools with funding parity, given the sector remains dominated by state (post-2013 KLIK/tankerület) maintainership. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Legal review of Nkt. founder-status provisions specifically addressing non-municipal civic/foundation operators' eligibility and funding parity.)
- Whether multi-stakeholder cost-sharing arrangements (church-municipality-state) can be structured to avoid the documented Gersekarát-style collapse pattern. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Case-study analysis of the Gersekarát collapse identifying specific contract-design failure points, and pilot testing of alternative binding multi-year funding-commitment structures.)

## S4 — Consolidation with mitigation (closure as default, compensatory measures)
**Goal**
Close low-enrollment village schools and reassign pupils to a consolidated center school, paired with statutory transport guarantees, targeted support (TEIP-style), and transparent cost/consultation rules.

**Mechanism**
- Consolidation into larger schools achieves economies of scale, reducing per-pupil operating cost up to roughly 200 students, though these estimates often undercount transport and other offsetting expenses. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- Post-closure transportation costs are reported in Central European contexts in a range of roughly 800-1,500 EUR per pupil per year, though this figure's applicability to specific Hungarian route lengths and occupancy rates is unverified and should be treated as indicative, not precise. [evidence: weak] [evidence: weak]
- School closures in the US cause statistically significant drops in math/reading scores and disproportionately raise absenteeism and disciplinary incidents among disadvantaged pupils, though displaced-pupil achievement gains occur only when receiving schools are substantially higher-performing. [evidence: strong] [evidence: strong]
- An Italian instrumental-variable study found school closures cause measurable reductions in local population and income, particularly in peripheral areas — evidence that closure functions as a depopulation driver, not merely a response to it. [evidence: strong] [evidence: strong]
- TEIP-style compensatory support in Portugal reduced early school-leaving and retention rates over 15 years but generally failed to close academic-performance gaps between supported and non-supported schools. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
contested — Consolidation's cost savings and achievement effects are genuinely mixed across countries (positive/null in the US, negative in Denmark/Chile/China), and TEIP-style mitigation has a demonstrated partial-failure record on closing performance gaps.

**Assumptions**
- Current commuting infrastructure and subsidized public transport can sustain daily trips of 20+ km for primary-age children without unacceptable welfare cost — an assumption directly challenged by documented school-bus cancellations (e.g. Hódmezővásárhely 2023) and the fact that a promised 2018 national school-bus network was never built. [assumption]
- Receiving-school quality and integration are decisive moderators determining whether closure benefits or harms disadvantaged pupils, consistent with international literature, but this scenario does not build in a mandatory quality/integration verification step before reassignment. [assumption]
- Municipalities and the central budget will sustain multi-year funding commitments for transport guarantees and TEIP-style support beyond the initial implementation period, despite documented precedent (Gersekarát, school-bus cancellations) of funding-commitment collapse. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Reduces unqualified-teacher exposure by concentrating pupils in larger schools with better staffing ratios. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- Simplifies system-wide teacher-supply allocation by reducing the number of dispersed small-school posts that are hardest to staff. [evidence: weak] [evidence: weak]
- Statutory transport guarantees and TEIP-style targeted support can measurably reduce early school-leaving rates, per Portuguese precedent, even where they do not close achievement gaps. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]

**Equity impact**
Disproportionate travel burden falls on lower-SES, disability-affected, and Roma families in peripheral settlements, mirroring international patterns (China: 0.6-year schooling loss for girls; India: greater enrollment decline among disabled and Scheduled Caste/Tribe pupils; Portugal: persistently higher TEIP-school dropout in rural/border municipalities); the scenario's compensatory measures require concrete enforcement thresholds (maximum commute time by grade, mandatory receiving-school quality/integration verification before reassignment, published disaggregated outcome data by SES/ethnicity/disability, and a legally binding funding floor for transport and TEIP support) which are not yet specified and without which mitigation remains aspirational rather than operative.

**Cost categories**
- Post-closure transport operating costs (indicative Central European range, unverified for Hungarian route lengths and occupancy)
- TEIP-style targeted support programme costs, split into one-off design/setup and recurring per-pupil delivery costs
- Indirect costs: closed-building maintenance liability (heating, insurance) for unsold/unrepurposed former school buildings
- Receiving-school capacity expansion (classrooms, staffing) to absorb transferred pupils without quality dilution
- Consultation and transparency-process administration (legally mandated cost/consultation disclosure)

**Implementation steps**
- Tankerületi központ (district center) — Publish transparent cost/consultation disclosure at least 6 months before any closure decision, per proposed reform to the current process (timeline: year 1, ongoing per decision) (timeline: year 1, ongoing per decision)
- Ministry / district transport authority — Legislate and fund statutory transport guarantee with enforceable maximum-commute-time thresholds by grade, learning from documented school-bus cancellations (timeline: year 1-2) (timeline: year 1-2)
- Receiving (consolidated center) school leadership — Undergo mandatory quality/integration verification before pupil reassignment begins (timeline: year 1-2, before each transfer cohort) (timeline: year 1-2, before each transfer cohort)
- TEIP-style national programme office — Roll out targeted compensatory support with published disaggregated outcome data by SES, ethnicity and disability status (timeline: year 2-5, recurring annual reporting) (timeline: year 2-5, recurring annual reporting)

**Political risks**
- Multi-year funding commitments for transport guarantees and TEIP-style support require sustained budget lines across administrations; without a statutorily protected funding floor (not merely an annual appropriation), commitments are vulnerable to fiscal-pressure reversal as seen in documented school-bus service withdrawals.
- Closure-as-default framing risks triggering organized local resistance analogous to the 2003-2007 closure wave, particularly where community hub value is high and alternative models (S1, S3) are locally viable.
- Restored teacher strike rights and PDSZ/PSZ mobilization capacity increase the credible threat of industrial action if consolidation-linked redeployment or support-staff (NOKS) funding disputes remain unresolved.

**Uncertainties**
- The true net financial effect of consolidation once post-closure transport, capital-liability, and receiving-school capacity costs are fully accounted remains unverified; the quoted 800-1,500 EUR/pupil/year transport range is not confirmed for Hungarian conditions. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Full cost-accounting study comparing total expenditure (capital, transport, staffing, maintenance) for matched closure-and-consolidation versus retention scenarios in 3-5 Hungarian micro-regions over 5-10 years.)
- The causal impact of school closure on subsequent settlement-level out-migration in Hungary specifically (as distinct from correlation with pre-existing decline) has not been empirically established. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Longitudinal household-level administrative data linkage (education registry x tax office x migration registry) comparing settlements that closed schools to matched controls, controlling for pre-closure demographic trends.)
- Whether TEIP-style compensatory support, if adapted to Hungary, would replicate Portugal's partial-failure pattern (reduced early school-leaving but persistent achievement gaps) or achieve better results given different institutional starting conditions. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Pilot implementation and rigorous 5-10 year outcome evaluation of a TEIP-adapted programme in a stratified sample of Hungarian receiving schools, with disaggregated achievement and dropout tracking.)

## S5 — National systemic framework: transparency, thresholds and evidence infrastructure
**Goal**
Establish a national framework of transparent minimum-viability thresholds, cost-visibility rules, teacher-training standardization, and a closure/outcome registry to inform (not dictate) local decisions across any of the above models.

**Mechanism**
- Transparent minimum-viability thresholds and cost-visibility rules can reduce ad hoc administrative discretion at district level, though this depends on political will to make and keep them binding rather than advisory. [evidence: weak] [evidence: weak]
- A national closure/outcome registry would fill a documented data gap: no current Hungary-specific causal study isolating closure effects on achievement, attendance or migration has been located in the accessible literature, and the curated source registry for this topic itself contains no such registry, so the gap claim should be read as an absence-of-evidence finding, not a definitively verified fact about all possible unpublished data. [evidence: weak] [evidence: weak]
- Teacher-training standardization for multigrade pedagogy, if implemented with sufficient intensity, is associated internationally with negligible achievement differences versus single-grade instruction. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- Poland's quantitative threshold evidence (school presence correlating strongly with population in the 400-700 inhabitant range, normalizing above 4,100) provides a transferable analytical anchor for setting Hungarian viability thresholds, though not a directly transplantable number. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
weak — The core justifying claim — that no Hungarian registry or comparable evidence infrastructure exists — is itself a data-gap finding based on an empty curated source registry for this topic, not a confirmed exhaustive audit, so it is downgraded from strong to weak pending verification.

**Assumptions**
- Sufficient political will exists to fund and maintain a long-term evidence infrastructure (registry, thresholds) across changing government administrations — this is presented as an implementation precondition, not a proven fact, and is directly challenged by the Gersekarát collapse precedent and the unresolved NOKS wage dispute, both signalling fragile multi-year commitment capacity. [assumption]
- Minimum-viability thresholds, once published, will be used to inform rather than mechanically dictate local closure/retention decisions, avoiding the risk of thresholds becoming a de facto mandatory closure trigger. [assumption]
- District-level (tankerületi) authorities will comply with data-reporting obligations feeding the registry without systematic circumvention or delay, given documented uncertainty over whether existing statutory obligations (e.g. expert-opinion requirements) are consistently observed. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Reduces information asymmetry between district authorities and local communities in closure/retention negotiations, potentially improving the fairness of ad hoc local processes under S1 and S3. [evidence: weak] [evidence: weak]
- Standardized teacher-training modules for multigrade pedagogy could address the documented professional-development gap identified as undermining multigrade benefits across S1, S2 and S3. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- A functioning registry would, over time, enable evidence-based revision of thresholds and policy, converting the currently 'weak'-evidence Hungarian rural-school debate into an evidence-tracked one. [evidence: weak] [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
As a procedural/enabling framework, S5's equity effects depend entirely on downstream use by local actors under S1-S4; without an explicit mandatory requirement that the registry and threshold-setting process capture, disaggregate and publish outcome data by SES, Roma/non-Roma status, and disability status, the framework risks being equity-neutral in design while equity-consequential in effect, since data that is not disaggregated cannot reveal or prevent the segregation and travel-burden harms documented elsewhere in this brief.

**Cost categories**
- One-off registry design and IT-build costs (data architecture, legal/GDPR compliance review)
- Recurring data-analytic staffing and district-level reporting-compliance costs
- One-off threshold and standard design/publication costs (expert consultation, public comment process)
- Recurring standardized teacher-training-module rollout and delivery costs, distinct from one-off curriculum design

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education, with legal and statistics offices — Design registry data architecture and secure legal/GDPR clearance for district-level data collection, recognizing this technical and legal groundwork alone requires more than one year (timeline: year 1-2) (timeline: year 1-2)
- Expert panel (education researchers, statisticians, stakeholder representatives) — Draft and publicly consult on minimum-viability thresholds, informed by but not directly copying the Polish 400-700/4,100 population anchors (timeline: year 1-3) (timeline: year 1-3)
- Tankerületi központok (district centers) — Begin mandatory disaggregated data reporting (SES, Roma/non-Roma, disability status) feeding the registry once legal clearance is secured (timeline: year 2-4) (timeline: year 2-4)
- Teacher-training institutes — Develop and roll out standardized multigrade-pedagogy training modules, sequenced after (not concurrent with) curriculum design (timeline: year 2-5) (timeline: year 2-5)

**Political risks**
- The registry and thresholds, though framed as advisory and politically neutral, could themselves become politicized — used selectively by different administrations either to justify closures or to claim protection is working — without a statutory guarantee of non-partisan, transparent methodology and independent oversight.
- Long-term evidence infrastructure requires funding and institutional continuity across changing administrations; the Gersekarát multi-stakeholder funding collapse and the unresolved NOKS wage dispute are concrete, documented precedents suggesting such continuity cannot be assumed without statutory funding protection.
- District-level authorities may resist or slow-walk mandatory disaggregated data reporting if it exposes prior non-compliance with existing statutory obligations (e.g. the expert-opinion requirement), creating an incentive against transparency that the framework must explicitly counteract.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether a comprehensive Hungarian closure/outcome registry truly does not currently exist in any form (state, academic, or NGO-maintained), given this claim rests on an empty curated source registry for this topic rather than a confirmed exhaustive audit. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A systematic audit of KIR-STAT, KSH, academic institutional repositories, and NGO archives (e.g. TASZ, PSZ, Emberi Lánc) specifically for existing closure/outcome tracking datasets.)
- Whether sufficient political will and statutory funding protection exist to sustain the registry and thresholds across changing government administrations, given the Gersekarát and NOKS precedents of fragile multi-year commitment. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Multi-year budget tracking of the registry's funding line across at least one government transition, plus statutory-protection design review comparable to independent-agency funding models.)
- Whether district-level authorities will comply fully and promptly with mandatory disaggregated data-reporting obligations, or systematically delay/circumvent them as has been documented for other statutory obligations (expert-opinion requirement). (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Audit reports or case law from the education ombudsman documenting compliance patterns with existing statutory obligations, used as a leading indicator for likely registry-reporting compliance.)
