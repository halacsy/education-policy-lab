# Policy scenarios

## S1 — Status quo plus incremental adaptation
**Goal**
Keep the existing municipal/state-maintained village school open as long as feasible via multigrade classrooms, felmenő rendszer phase-out, and ad hoc local negotiation, without a national framework.

**Mechanism**
- Statutory összevont osztály (multigrade class) provisions let schools combine grades 1-4 to keep enrollment above class-launch thresholds without formal closure. [evidence: strong] [evidence: strong]
- Felmenő rendszer phase-out (halting new first-grade enrollment while existing cohorts finish) lets tankerületek avoid abrupt closure conflict, spreading depopulation adjustment over 3-8 years. [evidence: strong] [evidence: strong]
- Multigrade classrooms, when paired with adequate teacher training and institutional support, can deliver high-standard education and reduce regional inequalities, per international systematic review. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- Without systemic teacher-training reform, multigrade viability is undermined by rural teacher shortages: half of Hungarian primary teachers are 50+, only 7.2% under 30, and unqualified-teacher rates are 2-3x higher in disadvantaged rural schools. [evidence: strong] [evidence: strong]
- An informal, legally unconfirmed ~14-pupil single-grade threshold means many micro-region cohorts (e.g., Bélapátfalva 55, Tab 67 total annual births) will not sustain even multigrade viability without cross-settlement consolidation. [evidence: weak] [evidence: weak]

**Evidence status**
contested — Legal mechanisms for multigrade and phase-out are well-documented, but their pedagogical adequacy and teacher-supply feasibility under current demographic and staffing trends are contested by the dossier's own findings.

**Assumptions**
- Assumes teacher supply and readiness for multigrade pedagogy will remain adequate without systemic training reform, despite the dossier's own evidence of acute rural teacher shortages and an aging workforce. [assumption]
- Assumes tankerületi központ maintainers will continue tolerating incremental, locally negotiated adaptation rather than imposing top-down consolidation despite central authority over closure decisions. [assumption]
- Assumes the statutory 'disproportionate burden' (aránytalan teher) safeguard is interpreted and enforced consistently enough to prevent de facto access collapse as phase-out proceeds. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Preserves the school's community-hub function and avoids the abrupt-closure civic backlash seen in the mid-2000s consolidation wave. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- Avoids at least short-term travel-burden increases for pupils compared to immediate consolidation, since children remain locally schooled during phase-out. [evidence: weak] [evidence: weak]
- Political reversibility risk is lower than abrupt closure, since incremental phase-out gives affected families and local politicians time to organize alternatives or resistance. [evidence: weak] [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
S1 does not resolve, and may entrench, Hungary's dominant rural equity problem: between-school segregation via exit of higher-SES/non-Roma families to church or urban schools, which can concentrate disadvantage precisely in the villages this scenario aims to protect (nearly 300 'ghetto schools', three-quarters rural). Multigrade classrooms taught by unqualified or overstretched teachers in aging, understaffed rural schools risk delivering lower-quality education to the most disadvantaged pupils who cannot exit, while OFI evidence shows small-settlement schools already have 3-4x larger parental-education gaps than other school types. Status quo without active school-choice regulation is therefore not equity-neutral.

**Cost categories**
- Recurring multigrade teacher-training and in-service upskilling costs (one-off transition cost years 1-4, then recurring annual cost)
- Capital-maintenance/backlog costs for aging small-school buildings, including stranded-asset risk from recent EU-financed energy retrofits (e.g., 24 billion HUF Széchenyi 2020 across 68 institutions)
- Teacher retention incentive costs (e.g., Magyar Falu Program service-housing model, currently only 5 billion HUF for 100 settlements — a small fraction of need) as a recurring or escalating budget line
- Administrative and legality-review costs for annual felmenő rendszer compliance monitoring by government offices (recurring)
- Opportunity cost of foregone consolidation savings (unquantified, offset partly by avoided transport and capital-construction costs per international evidence)

**Implementation steps**
- Tankerületi központ (district center) — Formally authorize and fund multigrade class formation and felmenő rendszer phase-out plans for schools below single-grade viability threshold, with published, non-discretionary criteria. (timeline: year 1-2)
- Ministry of Education / teacher-training institutions — Launch dedicated multigrade-pedagogy in-service training modules and rural recruitment incentives, acknowledging the ~4-year new-teacher pipeline lag. (timeline: year 1-4)
- Local municipality and school leadership — Negotiate transparent, published retention/phase-out terms with parents and staff, including formal consultation on felmenő timelines and disproportionate-burden thresholds. (timeline: year 1-3, ongoing)
- KSH / Klebelsberg Központ — Publish annual building-utilization, enrollment, and teacher-vacancy data by settlement to enable evidence-based monitoring of viability thresholds. (timeline: year 2 onward)

**Political risks**
- Even incremental phase-out can trigger localized parental mobilization if a specific village's cohort crosses the informal viability threshold abruptly, as seen in prior closure-wave protests and the Kánya case.
- Absent a binding national framework, tankerület-level discretion over felmenő timelines and multigrade thresholds creates inconsistent, potentially politically motivated application across regions.
- Teacher shortage and aging workforce risk undermining the scenario's core premise mid-implementation, forcing either de facto closure or reliance on unsustainable 'traveling teacher' models that school leadership already dislikes.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether multigrade pedagogy in Hungarian rural schools can achieve comparable outcomes to international evidence, given local teacher-training curricula and resource availability. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Pilot multigrade programmes in selected Hungarian rural primary schools with matched comparison groups and implementation-fidelity audits.)
- No authoritative statutory minimum enrollment threshold for launching a single-grade class exists; the widely-cited ~14-pupil figure is unconfirmed by law text. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Official written guidance from the Ministry of Education clarifying the minimum enrollment rule for launching a single-grade class.)
- Whether teacher-training pipeline expansion will materialize by 2028-2029 to relieve rural staffing pressure, or whether shortages will deepen despite policy intervention. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Annual tracking of teacher-training enrollment, graduation, and first-employment placement rates in rural districts.)

## S2 — Structural grade reorganization (lower grades stay, upper grades centralize)
**Goal**
Restructure the school system so only lower grades (1-4/1-6) remain in the village while upper grades or a new ISCED2-type school consolidate in a larger town, following the Polish 1999 structural-reform pattern.

**Mechanism**
- Poland's 1999 gimnazjum introduction forced consolidation of older grades into larger town-based institutions, providing a documented structural template for shortened-primary-plus-centralized-later-stage schooling. [evidence: strong] [evidence: strong]
- Shortening village-based schooling to lower grades reduces the fixed-cost burden of maintaining full subject-teacher rosters for upper grades in small schools, addressing a documented structural cost diseconomy. [evidence: strong] [evidence: strong]
- International literature on whether delayed/centralized secondary-stage tracking improves achievement outcomes is explicitly contested; Poland's own gimnazjum reform was later reversed (2017-2019) without clear evidence of net academic benefit. [evidence: contested] [evidence: contested]
- Centralizing upper-primary pupils into a town-based ISCED2 site increases daily commute distance and time for upper-primary children, and commutes exceeding 30 minutes are associated with lower achievement and higher absenteeism in comparative evidence. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- The Big-Fish-Little-Pond self-concept effect from moving to a larger, higher-achieving school is significantly weaker at primary/early-secondary level than in full secondary school, somewhat reducing (not eliminating) one psychological risk of this transition. [evidence: strong] [evidence: strong]

**Evidence status**
contested — The Polish structural template is strongly documented as historically implemented and later reversed, but its net academic and equity benefit remains genuinely contested in both the Polish and broader international literature.

**Assumptions**
- Assumes existing or upgradable commuting infrastructure and informal transport arrangements can safely and affordably carry upper-primary pupils daily to centralized ISCED2 sites, despite documented rural bus-service cancellations and 1.5-hour walking-commute cases. [assumption]
- Assumes Poland's 1999 achievement and equity outcomes (contested, later reversed) would translate more favorably in Hungary's different governance and settlement context. [assumption]
- Assumes centralized ISCED2 sites will have sufficient capacity, qualified staff, and receiving-school quality to avoid the finding that most displaced rural students do not move to stronger schools. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Reduces per-pupil fixed-cost diseconomy for upper grades by concentrating subject-teacher provision in fewer, larger sites. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- Retains community-hub function of the village school for younger children and their families, potentially mitigating some depopulation-acceleration risk relative to full closure. [evidence: weak] [evidence: weak]
- May ease rural teacher-shortage pressure by requiring fewer dispersed subject-teacher postings for upper grades, concentrating scarce specialist staff. [evidence: weak] [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
S2 shifts the travel burden onto upper-primary pupils, who are disproportionately from lower-income and Roma families already facing 58.7% Roma vs 9.3% non-Roma early-school-leaving rates; without a statutory transport guarantee and subsidized route funding (transport is currently an unsubsidized municipal task that has led to service cancellations), commute burden will fall hardest on families least able to absorb it, risking a widening rather than narrowing of the dropout gap this scenario claims to address through school-continuity retention.

**Cost categories**
- One-off capital investment for designating, constructing, or expanding ISCED2 receiving-site capacity (major, currently unestimated in HUF terms)
- Recurring statutory transport costs, with rural per-student bus costs approximately 40% higher than urban and marginal per-rider cost 2.5x urban levels; a single route (Délegyháza precedent) cost ~35 million HUF/year for ~50 students
- Teacher severance/redeployment costs and retraining for upper-grade subject teachers displaced from village schools (one-off, years 1-3)
- Financing/interest costs for capital projects if debt-financed, and ongoing facility operating costs at the consolidated site (recurring)
- Legal/procedural costs for körzethatár (catchment) redesignation and parental-consultation processes required under current governance rules

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education / tankerületi központ — Conduct feasibility and site-capacity assessment for candidate ISCED2 towns, including realistic multi-year procurement and construction timelines (not compressed). (timeline: year 1-3 (feasibility and procurement))
- Municipality / tankerület jointly — Negotiate and legally codify statutory transport guarantees and route-safety standards, including subsidized funding formula for rural bus services, before any upper-grade transfer takes effect. (timeline: year 2-4)
- Tankerület / labour unions (PSZ) — Conduct binding consultation on upper-grade teacher redeployment terms, severance, and retraining pathways. (timeline: year 2-3)
- Local school leadership and parents' councils — Participate in formal körzethatár (catchment) redesignation consultation with documented, appealable outcomes. (timeline: year 3-4)
- KSH / Klebelsberg Központ — Monitor and publish post-transition commute-time, attendance, and achievement data for transferred cohorts. (timeline: year 4 onward)

**Political risks**
- Poland's 1999 reform was politically reversible (reversed 2017-2019); Hungary risks the same fragility absent binding legal entrenchment or multi-year funding commitments protecting the new structure from reversal.
- Parental mobilization against upper-grade transfer is plausible given precedent (Budapest XVIII merger withdrawal after 4,000-signature petition), especially if transport guarantees are perceived as inadequate.
- Teacher unions may resist redeployment terms perceived as involuntary relocation without adequate compensation or housing support.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether delayed/centralized secondary-stage tracking produces net achievement gains in the Hungarian context, given contested and reversed international precedent. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A Hungary-specific pilot with matched-comparison villages tracking achievement, attendance, and dropout before and after upper-grade centralization.)
- The actual cost and safety adequacy of scaled rural transport for daily upper-primary commuting is unquantified at national scale. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A national household-level transport-cost and safety audit of routes serving villages under 2,000 population, cross-referenced with existing cancelled-route case data (e.g., Délegyháza).)
- Whether receiving ISCED2 schools will have sufficient capacity and staff quality to avoid the 'most displaced pupils do not move to stronger schools' pattern documented internationally. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Administrative-data tracking of receiving-school teacher qualifications, class sizes, and achievement trajectories for transferred cohorts over 3-5 years.)

## S3 — Networked retention: clustering, association-run and multi-service hub models
**Goal**
Retain village primary schools through inter-municipal pedagogical networks (French RPI-style clustering), community/association-run publicly-subsidized schools (Polish model), or multi-service hubs combining school with other civic functions.

**Mechanism**
- Poland's post-1990 non-public 'social schools' (association/NGO-run, state-subsidy-eligible) rescued or established approximately 500-600 rural schools in direct response to 1999-reform-triggered closures. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- Poland's progressive 'rural weight' per-pupil subsidy multiplier (raised to 0.4 by 2024 for the smallest-locality schools) demonstrates a concrete, transferable funding-formula lever to strengthen small-school financial viability. [evidence: strong] [evidence: strong]
- Even under NGO/social-school management, 269 of 1,076 rural primary schools in Małopolska fell below the ~70-student sustainability threshold, showing networked models do not eliminate viability limits at extreme depopulation levels. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- Hungarian law already recognizes plural maintainers (state, municipality, church, private/foundation, nationality self-government), providing an existing legal pathway for association-run models without requiring wholesale legislative reform. [evidence: strong] [evidence: strong]
- Hungary's post-2013 tankerületi központ centralization removed municipal maintainer authority over most rural primary schools, creating a governance barrier to inter-municipal or association-run network models not present in the Polish or French comparators at the time of their adoption. [evidence: strong] [evidence: strong]

**Evidence status**
moderate — Polish and French network-model mechanisms are moderately-to-strongly documented internationally, but their legal and operational transferability to Hungary's centralized post-2013 governance structure is a documented, unresolved barrier, not merely an assumption.

**Assumptions**
- Assumes Hungary's central government would permit reallocation of maintainer authority or create a new legal category enabling inter-municipal networks or association-run schools to operate with public subsidy eligibility comparable to the Polish model, despite the post-2013 centralization trend running in the opposite direction. [assumption]
- Assumes rural municipalities and civic associations in Hungary possess or can rapidly build the administrative capacity to run schools under a network or association model, despite most having lost this capacity since 2013 nationalization. [assumption]
- Assumes rural stakeholders would engage with novel governance models (NGO-run, multi-service hubs) if financial sustainability and community benefit were clearly demonstrated, though no Hungarian pilot evidence yet confirms this uptake. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Retains local school access and community-hub function without requiring pupils to commute to a centralized site, avoiding travel-burden equity harms documented in consolidation scenarios. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- A rural funding-weight multiplier (Polish precedent) could offset structural per-pupil cost diseconomy in small schools without requiring closure. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- Multi-service hubs combining school with other civic functions may improve cost-efficiency of shared facilities and slow rural service-desert formation, though this specific combined-function model lacks direct outcome evaluation in the dossier. [evidence: weak] [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
Networked/association-run models plausibly protect the poorest, most isolated villages from closure by pooling pedagogical and administrative resources across settlements, but the dossier's own evidence shows this is not guaranteed: administrative-capacity burdens (grant applications, compliance, governance boards) may systematically exclude precisely the villages most in need, since fiscally exhausted municipalities already lack capacity even under the current centralized system, and the Małopolska data show 25% of NGO-run rural schools still fell below sustainability thresholds. Without dedicated capacity-building support targeted at the poorest municipalities, this model risks reproducing a two-tier outcome: better-resourced villages network successfully, the most disadvantaged do not.

**Cost categories**
- One-off legal/regulatory design cost to create a Hungarian legal category enabling network or association-run public-subsidy-eligible schools (major, currently unquantified since no such category exists)
- Recurring rural per-pupil funding-weight subsidy (Polish 0.4 multiplier precedent; Hungarian budget line and fiscal-transfer mechanism not yet designed or costed)
- Capacity-building and administrative-support costs targeted at the poorest municipalities to prevent exclusion from network models (recurring, at least during a multi-year rollout)
- Inter-municipal coordination and pedagogical-network administration costs (RPI-style clustering requires shared staffing and scheduling infrastructure)
- Multi-service hub retrofitting/capital costs if combining school with other civic functions (library, health point, community center) in shared buildings

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education / Parliament — Draft and pass legal amendments creating a Hungarian association-run/network-school category with defined public-subsidy eligibility, addressing the post-2013 centralization barrier explicitly. (timeline: year 1-3 (legislative design and passage))
- Ministry of Finance / Ministry of Education — Design and cost a rural per-pupil funding-weight multiplier analogous to Poland's, with a phased introduction and fiscal-impact assessment. (timeline: year 1-2 (design), year 2-4 (phased rollout))
- Pilot municipalities and civic associations (2-3 regions) — Establish pilot inter-municipal networks or association-run schools with dedicated capacity-building support, prioritizing the poorest-capacity municipalities to test inclusion safeguards. (timeline: year 2-5)
- Independent evaluators / KSH — Evaluate pilot outcomes (viability, equity of access, administrative burden by municipal capacity tier) before national scale-up decisions. (timeline: year 5-6)

**Political risks**
- Teacher unions and existing tankerület administrators may perceive network/association models as a threat to centralized job security and oversight, generating institutional resistance to the necessary legal amendments.
- The state retains coercive reversal capacity: the 2023 law allowing churches to claim public buildings for education free of charge without local discretion (and similar future legal changes) illustrates that any devolved or plural-maintainer model lacks binding legal protection against future re-centralization or asset reclamation.
- Political perception risk: association-run models may be framed by opponents as a step toward privatization or church takeover, echoing the 2011 church-maintainer wave controversy, generating parental distrust absent transparent consent safeguards.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether Hungary's central government would legally permit and sustain a devolved association-run/network category given the post-2013 centralization trend and demonstrated coercive reversal capacity (2023 building-transfer law). (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Publication of a concrete legislative proposal or pilot authorization from the Ministry of Education explicitly creating this category, with binding protections against unilateral reversal.)
- Whether Polish-style association-run models remain financially sustainable at scale beyond initial enthusiasm, given Małopolska evidence that 25% still fell below sustainability thresholds. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Longitudinal financial audits of a sample of Polish social schools (n≥20) spanning 2000-2024, comparing cost per pupil, teacher retention, and closure/failure rates.)
- Whether Hungary's poorest, lowest-capacity municipalities would be systematically excluded from network/association models due to administrative burden, reproducing rather than resolving territorial equity gaps. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Pilot programme evaluation disaggregating uptake and outcome by municipal fiscal-capacity tier, with qualitative interviews on administrative-burden barriers.)

## S4 — Consolidation with mitigation (closure as default, compensatory measures)
**Goal**
Close low-enrollment village schools and reassign pupils to a consolidated center school, paired with statutory transport guarantees, targeted support (TEIP-style), and transparent cost/consultation rules to offset travel and equity harms.

**Mechanism**
- Portugal's TEIP4 programme design emphasizes local autonomy and endogenous resource mobilization for flexible, community-tailored intervention, offering a transferable design principle for targeted support at consolidated receiving schools. [evidence: strong] [evidence: strong]
- Portugal's rigid 2005-2007 21-student enrollment-threshold closures (1,615 schools closed) showed no correlation between concentrating students in larger 'school centers' and improved educational success rates over a 2005-2021 longitudinal study. [evidence: strong] [evidence: strong]
- Portugal's TEIP programme is documented to have reduced dropout and failure rates in targeted schools over its multi-cycle history, yet the same evaluation body found it failed over 15 years to close the overall performance gap with non-TEIP schools. [evidence: contested] [evidence: contested]
- Cross-national quasi-experimental evidence (Denmark, Finland) shows closures causally depress local population over the following decade, with effects often only emerging significantly after 5-6 years, meaning short-run cost analyses systematically understate long-run community costs. [evidence: strong] [evidence: strong]
- Displacement to higher-quality destination schools minimizes psychological and academic harm, but most displaced rural students internationally do not move to demonstrably stronger schools, limiting the mitigating effect of consolidation alone. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
contested — TEIP-style targeted support has documented, evaluated partial success (dropout/failure reduction) alongside documented, evaluated partial failure (persistent performance gap over 15 years), making the scenario's core compensatory mechanism genuinely contested rather than simply weak.

**Assumptions**
- Assumes TEIP-style targeted support and statutory transport guarantees will meaningfully offset closure equity harms, despite the scenario's own cited evidence that TEIP reduced dropout/failure but failed over 15 years to close the underlying performance gap. [assumption]
- Assumes consolidated receiving schools will have the capacity and quality to avoid the internationally documented pattern that most displaced rural pupils do not reach demonstrably stronger schools. [assumption]
- Assumes transparent cost/consultation rules will be genuinely binding rather than procedural formality, despite current Hungarian law treating closure as the maintainer's exclusive prerogative with only 'interest injury' (not legal injury) available to parents. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Reduces structural per-pupil cost diseconomy by concentrating teaching staff and facilities in fewer, larger sites, though international evidence shows these savings are frequently offset by capital and transport costs. [evidence: contested] [evidence: contested]
- TEIP-style targeted support has demonstrated dropout and failure-rate reductions in Portuguese targeted schools, offering a partial mitigation mechanism for displaced pupils. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- Statutory transport guarantees, if genuinely funded and enforced, could reduce (though not eliminate) the commute-burden equity harm documented in comparative literature. [evidence: weak] [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
Closure-as-default disproportionately affects lower-income and Roma families who cannot easily relocate or absorb commute burdens, compounding Hungary's already-highest-in-EU socioeconomic school segregation; the scenario's own cited evidence shows Portugal's TEIP reduced dropout/failure yet failed over 15 years to close the underlying performance gap, meaning statutory transport and targeted support are documented partial mitigations, not full offsets — closure paired with mitigation still likely produces a net equity cost for the most disadvantaged families relative to well-resourced retention alternatives, unless destination-school quality and transport funding are both independently verified as adequate before closure proceeds.

**Cost categories**
- Capital investment for expanding consolidated center-school capacity (major, unquantified in dossier)
- Recurring statutory transport costs at rural per-student rates ~40% above urban costs, with marginal per-rider cost 2.5x urban levels
- TEIP-style targeted-support programme costs (staffing, community-liaison roles, endogenous-resource-mobilization grants) at receiving schools (recurring)
- Community-level indirect fiscal effects: property-value decline, municipal tax-base shrinkage, and local-service contraction in closed-school villages (largely unmonetized in current Hungarian budget analyses)
- Building-repurposing or disposal costs for closed school buildings, with Portugal's experience showing ~20% of nearly 4,000 closed schools remain vacant and unused absent a repurposing mandate
- Teacher severance and redeployment costs (one-off, years 1-3)

**Implementation steps**
- Tankerületi központ / Ministry of Education — Publish transparent, pre-established cost/consultation criteria for closure decisions, including binding (not merely advisory) parental consultation with documented appeal rights, before any closure proceeds. (timeline: year 1 (framework design))
- Ministry of Education / Ministry of Finance — Legislate and fund statutory transport guarantees with verified route-safety and travel-time standards, funded before closure implementation, not concurrently. (timeline: year 1-2 (legislation and funding), before any closure)
- Consolidated receiving schools / tankerület — Establish and staff TEIP-style targeted-support units with community-liaison capacity and endogenous-resource-mobilization funding, verified operational before pupil transfer. (timeline: year 2-3, verified before transfer)
- Independent evaluator / KSH — Conduct pre-closure quality audit of destination schools (capacity, teacher qualifications) as a precondition for closure approval. (timeline: year 1-2, before closure decision finalized)
- Local municipality — Develop and fund a mandatory repurposing plan for closed school buildings (community hub, civic-service use) to avoid the ~20% abandonment rate documented in Portugal. (timeline: year 3-5)

**Political risks**
- Closure decisions remain the maintainer's exclusive legal prerogative in Hungary, with parents having only 'interest injury' (not legal injury) recourse; consultation rules risk being procedural theater unless legally binding appeal rights are created.
- Visible closure announcements historically trigger parental mobilization (Budapest XVIII reversal, Kánya case); government has adopted incremental 'phase-out' language specifically to avoid this dynamic, suggesting political resistance to naming this scenario as 'closure as default' explicitly.
- Long-run population-decline effects (emerging significantly only after 5-6 years per Danish/Finnish evidence) create a political-accountability mismatch: decision-makers who approve closures will not bear the visible consequences within their own electoral or administrative tenure.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether TEIP-style targeted support, if implemented in the Hungarian consolidated-school context, would replicate Portugal's partial dropout/failure reduction or its persistent 15-year performance-gap failure. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A Hungarian pilot TEIP-style programme with matched-comparison consolidated schools, tracking dropout, failure, and achievement-gap trajectories over at least 5-10 years.)
- The magnitude and timing of long-run community population decline following Hungarian rural school closures is unmeasured; Danish/Finnish effect sizes may not transfer directly. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A Hungary-specific difference-in-differences study comparing closure and non-closure villages over a 10+ year window, tracking population, migration, and housing-price trajectories.)
- Whether binding legal appeal rights for parents (beyond current 'interest injury' status) can realistically be created within existing Hungarian administrative law without triggering broader governance reform resistance. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Legal feasibility analysis by education-law scholars of amending Nkt. §102(2) to create an enforceable parental appeal right, with comparative reference to jurisdictions with binding closure-review procedures (e.g., Poland).)

## S5 — National systemic framework: transparency, thresholds and evidence infrastructure
**Goal**
Establish a national framework of transparent minimum-viability thresholds, cost-visibility rules, teacher-training standardization, and a closure/outcome registry to inform (not dictate) local decisions across any of the above models.

**Mechanism**
- No authoritative statutory minimum enrollment threshold currently exists in Hungarian law (the widely-cited ~14-pupil figure is unconfirmed community knowledge, not legal text), creating an evidentiary gap that a formal registry and threshold framework would directly address. [evidence: weak] [evidence: weak]
- Poland's institutionalized legal requirement that regional authorities evaluate learning/safety conditions, travel burden, and demographic justification before approving closure demonstrates a transferable procedural-transparency mechanism, though enforcement and substantive oversight vary even in Poland. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]
- An advisory, non-binding national registry and threshold system, absent enforcement teeth, risks being used selectively to legitimize pre-determined closure decisions rather than genuinely informing them, since Hungarian law already treats closure as the maintainer's exclusive discretionary prerogative. [evidence: contested] [evidence: contested]
- Standardizing teacher training for multigrade and rural-specific pedagogy could partially address the documented rural teacher-qualification gap (2-3x higher unqualified-teacher rates in disadvantaged schools), though the ~4-year training pipeline lag limits how quickly this mechanism can take effect. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
weak — The framework's individual components (registries, thresholds, training standards) have moderate international precedent, but no Hungarian or comparable evidence demonstrates that an advisory (non-binding) national framework changes actual closure outcomes rather than being selectively invoked.

**Assumptions**
- Assumes sufficient sustained political will and cross-administration funding will exist to build and maintain a national registry, thresholds, and training-standardization infrastructure, despite the dossier's own evidence of a 2026 government softening campaign promises to dismantle centralized structures. [assumption]
- Assumes a non-binding, advisory registry and thresholds will be used neutrally by tankerületi központok rather than selectively invoked to justify pre-determined closure decisions. [assumption]
- Assumes the legal and administrative lead time to collect and publish disaggregated closure/outcome data (by settlement, population cohort, and maintainer type) is shorter than the multi-year timelines documented for comparable Hungarian data-transparency efforts. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Fills a documented evidentiary gap: no authoritative inventory of at-risk schools or confirmed minimum-viability threshold currently exists in public Hungarian sources. [evidence: weak] [evidence: weak]
- Could enable evidence-based comparison across S1-S4 model choices at the local level, supporting genuinely informed (rather than purely fiscal or politically timed) decision-making, if the registry is operationally independent of maintainer discretion. [evidence: weak] [evidence: weak]
- Teacher-training standardization for multigrade/rural pedagogy could partially reduce the documented qualification gap over a multi-year horizon, benefiting whichever retention or consolidation model a locality adopts. [evidence: moderate] [evidence: moderate]

**Equity impact**
A transparency-and-threshold framework is a potential equity enabler by making currently invisible territorial disparities (1,200+ schoolless settlements, 10-30% rural child-population contraction) visible and comparable, but the framework's own contested evidence status means an advisory, aggregate registry could equally be co-opted to legitimize inequitable closure decisions already favored by tankerületi központok, particularly against the poorest, least-organized villages that lack the political voice documented in successful resistance cases like Kánya. Without binding force or disaggregation by Roma/non-Roma and settlement-poverty tier, the registry risks producing transparency without accountability.

**Cost categories**
- One-off registry-design and data-infrastructure build cost, including legal-compliance and data-protection design (GDPR-equivalent) for disaggregated settlement-level data (unquantified; comparable Hungarian data-transparency projects have taken multiple years)
- Recurring registry-operation and data-maintenance costs (annual data collection, verification, and publication)
- Teacher-training-standardization curriculum development and rollout costs across teacher-training institutions (one-off design, years 1-2; recurring delivery cost thereafter)
- Fiscal-responsibility allocation costs: unclear which government tier (ministry, tankerület, municipality) bears ongoing registry-funding responsibility, risking underfunding or discontinuation absent explicit budget-line designation
- Independent-oversight/audit costs to verify the registry is not being selectively invoked to justify pre-determined closures (a distinct cost category from data collection itself)

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education / KSH — Legislate a binding (not merely advisory) statutory minimum-viability threshold and closure-review procedure, explicitly assigning fiscal responsibility for registry funding to a named budget line. (timeline: year 1-2 (legislative design))
- KSH / Klebelsberg Központ — Build and populate the closure/outcome registry with disaggregated data (by settlement, Roma/non-Roma composition, maintainer type, and poverty tier), accounting for realistic legal and data-protection compliance lead times. (timeline: year 2-5 (realistic build given legal/compliance lead time))
- Independent oversight body (e.g., ombudsman office or newly designated auditor) — Establish and operate an audit function verifying the registry and thresholds are not selectively invoked to legitimize pre-determined closures, with published annual findings. (timeline: year 3 onward, ongoing)
- Teacher-training institutions / Ministry of Education — Design and roll out standardized multigrade/rural pedagogy training modules, acknowledging the ~4-year pipeline lag before effects are felt in classrooms. (timeline: year 2-6 (design year 2, rollout years 3-6))

**Political risks**
- Without binding force, thresholds and the registry risk becoming a legitimizing instrument for decisions tankerületi központok have already made on other grounds, undermining the framework's stated purpose of informing (not dictating) local decisions.
- Cross-administration funding commitments for a multi-year registry and training-standardization programme are politically fragile given the documented 2026 government softening of campaign promises to decentralize, suggesting the framework could be underfunded or deprioritized after initial announcement.
- Disaggregating registry data by Roma/non-Roma composition risks political controversy and potential misuse (stigmatization of specific settlements) if not accompanied by strict data-governance and anti-discrimination safeguards.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether a non-binding, advisory registry and threshold framework will be used neutrally or will be selectively invoked to justify pre-determined closure decisions by tankerületi központok. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Post-implementation tracking of tankerület closure decisions against registry data and thresholds over 3-5 years, with independent audit of decision rationales.)
- The legal and administrative lead time required to collect and publish disaggregated closure/outcome data (by settlement, Roma composition, maintainer type) in compliance with data-protection law is unestimated; comparable transparency efforts elsewhere have taken multiple years. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: A formal legal-feasibility and timeline assessment by the data-protection authority and KSH, published before registry-design finalization.)
- Whether sustained cross-administration political will and funding will survive multiple government cycles given the documented fragility of 2026 decentralization promises once in office. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Tracking of budget-line continuity and registry-operation status across at least two subsequent government terms or election cycles.)
