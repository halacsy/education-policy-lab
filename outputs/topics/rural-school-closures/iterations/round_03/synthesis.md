# Synthesis

## Overview
Twelve expert analyses converge on a structural, demographically-driven crisis facing Hungary's rural primary schools: birth rates have collapsed (to a 1.25-equivalent low, with 2024 recording only 77,500 births), over 1,200 of 3,155 settlements already lack any public school, and enrollment has fallen 17.3% over two decades — trends that make the status quo unsustainable in many villages within 5–10 years. Governance since 2013 has concentrated closure-adjacent decisions in state-run tankerületi központok, leaving municipalities and parents with only consultative or discretionary (non-legal) recourse, while a 2023 property-transfer law and church-maintainer expansion have added privatization and asset-loss dynamics. Cross-national evidence (Denmark, Finland, China, Poland, Portugal) broadly finds that closures causally depress local population and, in several contexts, achievement — with effects sometimes delayed five to six years — while multigrade and small-school models can perform adequately with sufficient investment. Yet the record is not one-sided: some small rural schools are already sites of severe ethnic/SES segregation ('ghetto schools'), and keeping them open without regulating school-choice exit (white flight, church-school competition) can entrench rather than reduce inequity. Finance experts find the budgetary case for closure weak once transport, capital, and lagged demographic costs are counted, while implementation-focused experts stress that acute teacher shortages (16,000 unfilled positions, a four-year training pipeline lag) and political reversibility (parent mobilization has blocked several announced closures) push Hungary toward a gradual 'phase-out' approach rather than abrupt closure. Legal safeguards (multi-grade classes, the 'disproportionate burden' rule, maintainer-change pathways) exist on paper but their enforcement and interpretation vary, and the overall picture is one of genuine, unresolved trade-offs between territorial equity, fiscal efficiency, segregation risk, and community survival rather than a single correct policy direction.

## Disagreement map
### Does keeping small rural schools open reduce or worsen educational inequity?
- **hungarian_education_system, demography, school_network_planning, polish_reform, portuguese_reform, finnish_reform**: Small rural schools serve an irreplaceable equity and community-anchoring function; closure accelerates depopulation and disproportionately harms disadvantaged, commuting, and Roma pupils who lack alternatives. Why: Supported by cross-national quasi-experimental evidence (Denmark, Finland, China, Portugal) showing closures causally depress local population and achievement, and by Hungarian demographic data showing disadvantage concentrating in schoolless settlements.
- **equity_and_social_mobility** (minority): Keeping small village schools open, absent regulation of school-choice exit (white flight to church/urban schools), can itself deepen segregation by concentrating disadvantaged and Roma pupils in the schools left behind ('ghetto schools'); closure is not automatically the equity-harming option. Why: Nearly 300 'ghetto schools' (over 50% Roma pupils) exist in Hungary, three-quarters in villages, showing rural small schools — not urban ones — are the primary site of extreme segregation; church-school expansion draws non-Roma pupils away, deepening residual concentration.

### Is school closure justified primarily by demographic/fiscal necessity, or is it a policy choice that outpaces genuine need?
- **school_network_planning, demography, finnish_reform**: Demographic collapse (birth-rate decline, shrinking cohorts below viability thresholds) makes phase-out closures economically and demographically inevitable in many villages within 5–10 years, regardless of governance design. Why: Specific micro-regions already show birth cohorts too small to sustain even multi-grade classes (e.g., under 80 births/year in some districts); Finland's own demographic collapse (fertility 1.25) is driving comparable closures despite a historically protective small-school culture.
- **polish_reform, education_finance, legal_and_governance** (minority): Closure often outpaces demographic necessity because of policy design (funding-formula incentives, governance centralization, weak procedural oversight), not enrollment decline alone; the financial case for closure is frequently weak once full costs are counted. Why: Poland's school network contracted 17% while pupils fell only 3%; Hungarian per-pupil cost disadvantages are structural (fixed staffing) but transport, capital, and lagged demographic costs typically exceed salary savings from consolidation.

### Should closure decision-making be centralized (state/district level) or decentralized (municipal/local level)?
- **hungarian_education_system, legal_and_governance, political_feasibility**: Centralized tankerületi központ authority since 2013/2017 has stripped municipalities and parents of decisive influence over closure, reducing local responsiveness; decentralization (or at minimum binding local consultation) is needed to protect territorial access rights. Why: Under Nkt. §102(2), closure is exclusively the maintainer's prerogative; communities suffer only an 'interest injury,' not a legal injury, with no formal appeal — a structural asymmetry documented across legal, education-system, and political-feasibility analyses.
- **education_finance** (minority): Returning full financial autonomy/maintainer responsibility to individual municipalities could impose higher administrative unit costs (new posts, compliance structures) than the current centralized model, and poorer municipalities explicitly cannot self-finance operating costs. Why: A 2026 mayoral survey found wealthier municipalities (Budaörs) welcomed returned responsibility while poorer settlements (Balatonkeresztúr, Zamárdi) cited inability to self-finance since the 2012 state takeover, suggesting decentralization without ring-fenced co-financing risks worsening regional inequity.

### Are multigrade/small-school pedagogical alternatives a genuinely viable substitute for consolidation, or a stopgap of limited scalability?
- **international_comparison, educational_psychology, finnish_reform**: Multigrade classrooms, when operated to minimum quality standards with adequate teacher training and institutional support, can deliver high-standard education and reduce regional inequalities, offering a genuine evidence-backed alternative to closure. Why: A 2025 systematic review of 40 international studies found multigrade classrooms viable under quality conditions; small schools show no consistent achievement disadvantage but significantly better self-esteem, safety, and school climate.
- **implementation_planning, school_network_planning** (minority): The 'traveling teacher' model and multigrade expansion are operationally unsustainable at scale in the current Hungarian context, given acute teacher shortages (16,000 unfilled posts) and a four-year training pipeline lag; alternatives are stopgaps, not durable substitutes. Why: Trade-union officials and school directors report the traveling-teacher model as operationally unsustainable and disliked; specific micro-regions already face birth cohorts too small for single-grade viability even under multi-grade clustering rules.

### Is the church-maintainer transfer pathway (fenntartóváltás) a legitimate rescue mechanism or a problematic privatization/asset-loss dynamic?
- **legal_and_governance, polish_reform**: Maintainer-change to church, private, or NGO status is a legally permitted pathway that has kept schools open (Poland's 500–600 rescued rural schools; Hungary's statutory annual window), representing institutional adaptation rather than pure loss. Why: Documented survival cases exist (Beregdaróc consolidation model; Polish social schools); Hungarian law requires majority parental support for such transfers, providing at least a nominal safeguard.
- **hungarian_education_system** (minority): The 2023 property-transfer law allowing churches to claim state/municipal buildings free of charge, with no local discretion, represents a substantial fiscal and asset transfer from municipalities (e.g., Hódmezővásárhely's ~2 billion HUF reported loss), raising equity and governance concerns distinct from mere school survival. Why: Municipalities cite substantial documented asset losses with no local discretion in the process, and church schools receive an additional ~50% operational-budget supplement (~5 billion HUF annual system-wide cost), shifting public resources toward non-state providers.

## What the experts agree on
- Hungary's rural primary schools face a structural demographic crisis (collapsing birth rates, shrinking cohorts, accelerating population decline) that is not cyclical and will intensify pressure on school viability over the coming decade. [evidence: strong]
- Rural and disadvantaged schools face disproportionately severe teacher shortages, with older, less qualified staff concentrated in these areas compared to urban/advantaged schools. [evidence: strong]
- Cross-national quasi-experimental evidence (Denmark, Finland, China) shows school closures are associated with, and in several designs causally linked to, local population decline and/or reduced student achievement, with effects sometimes emerging only years after closure. [evidence: strong]
- No Hungary-specific quasi-experimental (difference-in-differences or equivalent causal) study exists isolating the effect of rural school closure on local population decline or student achievement; all causal claims for Hungary rely on extrapolation from other countries. [evidence: contested]
- School transport is a major, often underfunded cost and logistical burden in rural school-network decisions, with commute-time increases independently linked to worse academic, social, and wellbeing outcomes. [evidence: moderate]
- Implementation of closure in Hungary is currently proceeding via gradual 'phase-out' (halting new enrollment, allowing schools to empty over years) rather than abrupt closure, partly to avoid the civic backlash seen in the mid-2000s consolidation wave. [evidence: moderate]
- Multigrade classroom pedagogy can achieve acceptable or even beneficial outcomes for students, particularly for struggling readers and school climate, but its success depends heavily on system-level implementation quality (training, resources, supervision), not just the pedagogical model itself. [evidence: moderate]
