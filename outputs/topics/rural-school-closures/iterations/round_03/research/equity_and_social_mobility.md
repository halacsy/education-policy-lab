# Research Notes: Equity & Social Mobility Dimension — Rural Primary School Closures in Hungary

**Finding 1 — Institutional desertification is already advanced.** By 2024, more than 1,200 of Hungary's 3,155 settlements had no public educational institution at all, and disadvantaged children have become increasingly concentrated in rural areas (up to 94% in certain settlements), while urban/suburban zones face rising child-teacher ratios (up to 24:1). This comes from a settlement-level clustering study (2021–2024 data) identifying five territorial types (Overburdened Growth Areas, Disadvantaged Declining Areas, etc.), showing a "deepening divide between dynamic urban/suburban zones and disadvantaged rural peripheries."
Source: "Shrinking, growing or overburdened? Territorial patterns in child demographics and institutional burden in Hungary's public education system after 2021" — https://www.researchgate.net/publication/396483025_Shrinking_growing_or_overburdened_Territorial_patterns_in_child_demographics_and_institutional_burden_in_Hungary's_public_education_system_after_2021

**Finding 2 — 2004 reform halved village schools; consolidation is not new.** Hungary's rural school network was kept largely intact until 2004, protected by state support and a 1997 act on voluntary local-government associations. A 2004 Act on Multi-Functional Micro-Regional Local Government Associations (MLGAs) rechanneled incentives toward amalgamation, and "within four years the number of villages maintaining primary schools dropped by a half," triggering the 2007 "Human Chain for Rural Schools" protest movement.
Source: "Rescuing a small village school in the context of rural change in Hungary," Journal of Rural Studies — https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0743016712000216

**Finding 3 — Small-school retention paradoxically increased between-school segregation via "white flight."** Because Hungarian local governments historically responded to shrinking enrollment by cutting parallel classes rather than closing/merging schools, this "led to a large offer of small schools, but reduced the latitude for the within-school separation of pupils," meaning segregation shifted to the *between-school* level: non-Roma/higher-SES parents increasingly enroll children in schools in other (often larger, non-village) settlements — the phenomenon "often being referred to as 'white flight.'" This directly implicates SES gradients: keeping small village schools open (without addressing school-choice dynamics) can concentrate disadvantage rather than prevent it.
Source: CEU Center for Policy Studies working paper, "The Wider Context of the Segregation of Roma Pupils in Education" (2020) — https://cps.ceu.edu/sites/cps.ceu.edu/files/attachment/publication/3258/cps-working-paper-educ-social-selection-education-hungary-2020.pdf

**Finding 4 — Scale of "ghetto schools" concentrated in villages.** Using national competency-assessment (NABC) databases, researchers identified nearly 300 "ghetto schools" (Roma rate above 50%), of which "three-quarters operate in villages" and nearly half are located in Northern Hungary. This establishes that rural small schools are the primary site of extreme ethnic/SES segregation in the current system, not urban schools.
Source: "Education of Roma and Educational Resilience in Hungary," Springer — https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-52588-0_6

**Finding 5 — Small rural schools can also be "resilient" outperformers relative to their intake.** The same NABC-based study found that "resilient schools are more likely to be located in rural areas and in localities where the school-choice options are more restricted" — i.e., where families cannot easily exit, small rural schools sometimes achieve better-than-predicted results for disadvantaged intakes, suggesting closure is not uniformly the efficient/equitable answer and that school-level factors matter alongside composition.
Source: same as Finding 4 — https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-52588-0_6

**Finding 6 — Church-school expansion is siphoning non-Roma pupils from rural state schools, deepening residual segregation.** Reporting notes that "the rise of church-run schools has drawn many non-Roma children away from state institutions," and that "church schools benefit from stronger funding and the ability to select high-performing students, leaving socially and economically marginalised children concentrated in struggling public schools." A 2025 case: authorities closed Methodist Evangelical Church schools that mainly served disadvantaged children despite court rulings against the closures.
Source: Humanium, "Children of Hungary" — https://www.humanium.org/en/hungary/

**Finding 7 — Legal/EU pressure remains unresolved.** The ECtHR ruled against Hungary in March 2023 (Piliscsaba, Jókai Mór Primary School case) ordering desegregation and damages; "Hungary remains the subject of an ongoing infringement procedure by the European Commission for the systemic segregation of Romani children in education." This is directly relevant scope: many segregated schools are in small settlements with no alternative state school in the village.
Source: European Roma Rights Centre press release — https://www.errc.org/press-releases/european-court-orders-hungary-to-desegregate-romani-school-and-pay-damages

**Finding 8 — Cross-national quasi-experimental evidence: closures cause local depopulation, creating a reinforcing spiral.** Italian evidence (Di Cataldo & Romani 2024, Regional Science and Urban Economics) using TWFE + IV finds "municipalities affected by school closures experience significant reductions in population and income," an effect "primarily driven by peripheral municipalities located far away from economic centres and distant from the next available primary school." Danish evidence (Sørensen et al. 2021, Journal of Rural Studies) on simultaneous 2011 closure of eight village schools found "a population decline of 7.6 percentage points during the 10-year post-closure period," with effects only becoming statistically significant from year six onward — a long-lag, long-term effect. This is a strong empirical counterweight to "rational" closure arguments framed purely around enrollment efficiency.
Sources: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0166046224000887 ; https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0272775717306155

**Finding 9 — International consolidation/achievement evidence is genuinely mixed, not uniformly pro- or anti-closure.** A review (arXiv 2203.17101) notes literature is inconsistent: Chile found school closure "increases the probability of high-school dropout between 49 and 68 percent," and identified "large causal effects of school closure on grade repetition in primary school" (Grau, Hojman & Mizala 2018); Denmark found consolidation had "adverse effects on achievement in the short run, but effects appeared to weaken over time," suggesting a disruption effect rather than a permanent peer/resource effect (Beuchert et al.); but other contexts (e.g., closing poor-performing Amsterdam primary schools) showed no negative impacts. US evidence is similarly split: Texas closures showed "long-term negative effects on high school graduation, college attainment, employment, and earnings" (Univ. of Houston 2023), while Arkansas's Act 60 consolidations had "small positive effects on achievement but did little to improve efficiency."
Sources: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2203.17101 ; https://tpcref.org/research-school-consolidation-and-closure/

**Finding 10 — Roma dropout gap remains extreme and is concentrated in rural/small-settlement schooling.** In 2023, 58.7% of Roma 18–24-year-olds were school dropouts versus 9.3% among non-Roma, and "among early school leavers, the most vulnerable groups are those living in rural areas, Roma and people with disabilities" (2024 data). Teacher shortages compound this: "the proportion of unqualified teachers in disadvantaged schools [is] 2 to 3 times higher than in schools not disadvantaged," and rural areas have the greatest shortages.
Sources: EACEA National Policies Platform Youthwiki, Hungary 6.1 — https://national-policies.eacea.ec.europa.eu/youthwiki/chapters/hungary/61-general-context ; EU Education and Training Monitor 2025, Hungary country report — https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html

**Finding 11 — Peer-effect mechanism specifics.** Recent consolidation literature (edworkingpapers.com 2024, "The Long Shadow of School Closures") frames closures as intended to deliver "access to better-resourced schools, higher-achieving peers, and the advantages of economies of scale," but cites Denning, Murphy & Weinhardt (2023) finding that "40% of peer effects can be offset by ordinal rank effects" — i.e., moving disadvantaged/lower-performing rural pupils into larger, higher-achieving consolidated schools can hurt their relative rank/self-concept even as absolute peer quality rises, a nuance directly relevant to predicting distributional effects on non-selected/rural pupils.
Source: "The Long Shadow of School Closures: Impacts on Students' Educational and Labor Market Outcomes" — https://edworkingpapers.com/sites/default/files/ai24-963.pdf
