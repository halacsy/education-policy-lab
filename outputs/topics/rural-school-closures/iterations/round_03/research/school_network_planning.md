# Research Notes — School-Size Economics, Building Stock & Commuting Tolerances in Rural Hungary

**1. National enrollment decline is accelerating and structural, not cyclical.** KSH data (published in Magyarország 2024) show that in the 2024/2025 school year, primary school (általános iskola) enrollment fell to 710,000 students nationally, down from 712,000 the previous year (–0.3%), with first-graders down 1.9% to 95,000. Compared to 20 years earlier (2005/2006, 859,000 students), the decline is now 17.3%. Number of primary school sites (feladatellátási hely) fell to 3,537 in 2024/2025 — 13 fewer than the prior year, 38 fewer than 2022/2023, and 88 fewer than 2021/2022. Source: Népszava/KSH, https://nepszava.hu/3287108_altalanos-iskola-diakok-letszam-csokkenes-magyarorszag-ksh (July 2025).

**2. Birth-rate collapse means the pipeline problem will worsen sharply in coming years.** Births fell from 110,800 in 2015 to 84,300 in 2024 (–22.5% in a decade) and to just 77,600 in 2025. The total fertility rate dropped below 1.3 by August 2025 (from 1.55 in 2023). PSZ (teachers' union) president Totyik Tamás cites specific micro-regions (járás) with extremely low annual birth counts: Bélapátfalva (55 births/year), Tab (67), Szentgotthárd, Bóly, and Hegyhát (under 80 each in 2024). Source: Economx, https://www.economx.hu/belfold/2026/05/19/robbanas-elotti-allapot-totyik-tamas-psz-demografiai-kozoktatas-gyermekszam-pedagogusok/ (May 2026).

**3. Some rural schools already cannot fill a first-grade class.** Union leadership states plainly: 
"Információink szerint már most is van több olyan általános iskola, ahova egyetlen első osztályos diákot sem vettek fel"
 and predicts consolidation is nearly unavoidable. Source: https://nepszava.hu/3287108_altalanos-iskola-diakok-letszam-csokkenes-magyarorszag-ksh; also https://www.economx.hu/magyar-gazdasag/2025/07/15/altalanos-iskola-ksh-gyermek-diak-kozoktatas-psz-totyik-tamas-osztaly-812691/ (July 2025).

**4. Government policy explicitly favors "quiet" phase-out over abrupt closure.** A Válasz Online investigative piece describes the current method: rather than shutting a school overnight (as happened in the mid-2000s, provoking protests, human chains, and "hazaáruló" accusations), maintainers (tankerületek) now simply stop enrolling new first grade/kindergarten cohorts, let the institution empty out over 3–8 years, and gradually transfer or dismiss staff — described as the 
"az utolsó kapcsolja le a villanyt"-módszert
 ("last one turns off the lights" method). Government commissioner Balatoni Katalin is quoted: 
"A cél nem a bezárás, hanem a kistelepülések és a kisiskolák védelme mindaddig, amíg erre van igény... A kormány azonban mindent megtesz, hogy az oktatás a végsőkig fennmaradjon, akár összevonásokkal és helyi megoldásokkal."
 Source: https://www.valaszonline.hu/2025/10/17/ovodak-iskolak-bezarasa-demografia-oktatas/ (Oct 2025).

**5. Legal class-size and merged-class (összevont osztály) framework.** Hungarian law (Nkt. 2011. évi CXC, 4.§(24)) defines an "összevont osztály" (multi-grade class) as combining at least 2 and at most 4 grades within lower primary (grades 1–4), and mandates that when grades differ in weekly required lesson hours, the higher grade's hourly requirement governs. Source: https://tudasbazis.ekreta.hu/plugins/viewsource/viewpagesrc.action?pageId=4065048. Statutory maximum class size for grades 1–4 is 27 students (with a legally permitted 20% override by the maintainer), a threshold unchanged from earlier practice which set the max at 26. Sources: https://www.oktbiztos.hu/ugyek/jelentes2016/kozv_jogok.html; https://www.oktbiztos.hu/ugyek/jelentes2007/fenntartoi.html. A commonly cited (but not law-text-confirmed in my search) informal minimum class-launch threshold is around 14 pupils per grade cohort, below which schools are pushed into merged classes — this figure comes from a non-authoritative community Q&A source and should be treated as weak evidence: https://www.gyakorikerdesek.hu/emberek__tanarok-iskolatarsak__9976975 (2019, unverified/anecdotal).

**6. Real progression pattern observed in practice:** community reports describe a sequence — first isolated merged classes (typically grades 1–3 and 2–4), then merging all lower grades, then full school closure with consolidation into a neighboring town's school, as birth cohorts shrink year over year. Source: https://www.gyakorikerdesek.hu/emberek__tanarok-iskolatarsak__9976975.

**7. Teacher shortage compounds rural viability problems.** Per the EU's Education and Training Monitor 2025 country report for Hungary: 
half of all teachers in the 2024/2025 academic year were 50 or older, while only 7.2% of teachers were under 30... Teacher shortages are greatest in rural areas, with the proportion of unqualified teachers in disadvantaged schools 2 to 3 times higher than in schools not disadvantaged
. Source: https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html.

**8. Public sentiment strongly opposes closures/disruption and impact concentrates on villages/small towns.** An Ipsos survey found 
a gyermekes családok háromnegyede számára jelentene nehézséget az óvodák és iskolák ideiglenes vagy tartós bezárása... a megkérdezettek 45%-ának bevallása szerint rövid távon sem lenne megoldható a gyerekek elhelyezése
, and further analysis showed 
az iskolabezárások által legkritikusabban érintett háztartások 45%-a nem csak egy, de több kiskorúval is bír, és a gyermekek elhelyezésének problémája 78%-ban kis vidéki városok vagy falvak lakóit érintené
 — i.e., 78% of the most-affected households are in small rural towns/villages. Source: https://www.ipsos.com/hu-hu/nagy-terhet-jelentene-az-iskolabezaras-magyar-csaladok-szamara (Ipsos, referencing survey data; article dated 2025 but underlying survey appears COVID-era — note ambiguous dating in source).

**9. Building-stock investment continues in parallel with demographic decline — a sunk-cost/stranded-asset tension.** The Klebelsberg Központ (national school-maintenance authority) reports large EU-co-financed energy retrofit programs: e.g., 
more than 1.5 billion HUF worth of energy-efficiency retrofits were completed across multiple educational institutions in Heves county... insulation work, window replacement, heating system upgrades and solar panel installations
, and nationally 
68 educational institutions have been renovated with EU and Hungarian state support, using nearly 24 billion HUF under the Széchenyi 2020 program
. This signals continued capital investment into buildings that may face shrinking or zero enrollment within years — a stranded-asset risk. Sources: https://www.heol.hu/helyi-kozelet/2026/02/milliardos-energetikai-fejlesztesek-heves-varmegyeben-mutatjuk-mely-iskolak-ujultak-meg; https://hirado.hu/belfold/cikk/2023/12/19/energetikai-fejlesztesekkel-ujult-meg-a-klebelsberg-kozpont-tobb-oktatasi-intezmenye.

**10. Comparative Nordic/Central-European evidence on closure–depopulation feedback loop.** A Finnish DID-regression study found 
using DID regression modelling combined with matching methods, focusing on the years 2010–2018... the results indicate that in Finland after school closure, the population of the surrounding community decreased more than before school closure
, supporting the "recipe for depopulation" hypothesis, though a Saxony-based counter-study found 
while school location may play a certain role for some individuals looking for housing areas, in general there is no evidence of a localised depopulating effect of primary school closures
 (contested evidence). Sources: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0743016721000115; https://www.researchgate.net/publication/262115739_A_'Recipe_for_Depopulation'_School_Closures_and_Local_Population_Decline_in_Saxony. Additionally, Austrian evidence indicates 
In Austria, the decrease in the population is due mainly to outmigration rather than to falling numbers of births
 (Kroismayr 2019), suggesting closure effects may be confounded with prior outmigration trends rather than purely causal.

**11. Hungary-specific academic research confirms uneven geographic burden.** A 2025 peer-reviewed study on Hungary's public education system post-2021 finds 
numerous rural settlements in Western Transdanubia, Southern Baranya, and some low-density regions of Northern Hungary recorded stagnation or decline... areas where the child population contracted by 10–30% over the period, often coinciding with a broader demographic decline and the closure or downgrading
 of local schools, and identifies settlements with concentrated disadvantage where 
Boldogkőújfalu, Magosliget, and Bakonyoszlop, with disadvantage rates above 92%... these settlements have a relatively small child population (typically around 50–70 children)
. Source: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/396483025_Shrinking_growing_or_overburdened_Territorial_patterns_in_child_demographics_and_institutional_burden_in_Hungary's_public_education_system_after_2021.

**12. Transport/commuting friction is an emerging practical barrier, not just a policy abstraction.** Reporting notes that when local schools close, public bus operators sometimes restrict unaccompanied children from regular routes, forcing improvised parent-organized "bus duty" rotations: 
Totyik Tamás szerint a Volán több helyen nem engedi fel felügyelet nélkül a gyerekeket a menetrendszerinti járataira... ki kísérje őket, ha már nincs iskola helyben? Például szülők vállalnak felváltva „buszügyeletet"
. Source: https://www.valaszonline.hu/2025/10/17/ovodak-iskolak-bezarasa-demografia-oktatas/.

**13. Comparative regional policy: Slovakia is moving toward explicit network rationalization with funding changes from 2030.** Slovak Education Minister Tomáš Drucker stated that state support for small schools has not delivered expected results, and financing rules will change, likely from 2030, with proposed models including 
iskola-összevonás, klaszterek vagy kihelyezett tagozatok létrehozása
 (school mergers, clusters, or satellite branches) — offered as non-centrally-mandated options, allowing regional flexibility. This is a useful comparative policy-design reference point for Hungary. Source: https://felvidek.ma/2025/07/07/nem-celunk-az-iskolak-bezarasa/ (July 2025).

**Uncertainties flagged for follow-up:** (a) I could not confirm an authoritative statutory minimum class-launch enrollment figure (the "14 students" figure is from an unverified community forum, not a legal text); (b) no Hungary-specific quantitative study was found isolating the local depopulation effect of rural primary school closure (comparative evidence is Finnish/Austrian/Saxon, not Hungarian); (c) exact current building-utilization/vacancy rates for sub-2,000-population village schools nationally were not found in searchable form; (d) no specific data located on optimal school size/per-pupil cost curves for Hungarian rural primaries.
