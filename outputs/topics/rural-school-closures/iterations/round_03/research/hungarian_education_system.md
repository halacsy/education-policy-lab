# Research Notes: Hungarian Rural Primary Schools — Structure, Catchments, Maintainers

**1. Settlements with no public educational institution (2024)**
A 2025 spatial-analysis study found that 
by 2024, more than 1,200 of 3,155 settlements had no public educational institutions, raising concerns about territorial equity
. The same study identified 
five settlement types via k-means clustering—Overburdened Growth Areas, Declining but Stabilizing Areas, Disadvantaged Declining Areas, Moderately Declining Balanced Areas, and Balanced Growth Areas—revealing a deepening divide between dynamic urban/suburban zones and disadvantaged rural peripheries
. Full source: "Shrinking, growing or overburdened? Territorial patterns in child demographics and institutional burden in Hungary's public education system after 2021" — https://www.researchgate.net/publication/396483025 and https://www.academia.edu/144471812

**2. The 2004–2008 consolidation wave (historical baseline, still the reference point for current debates)**
A peer-reviewed case study on Hungarian village school closures notes that 
closures or amalgamations occurred only rarely; the rural school network was kept relatively intact until as late as 2004 as a result of state support provided specifically to small villages to maintain schools
. However, 
in 2004 an Act on Multi-Functional Micro-Regional Local Government Associations came into force which rechanneled state incentives and promoted the amalgamation of rural schools... within four years the number of villages maintaining primary schools dropped by a half
. This triggered civic backlash: 
the closure and merger of rural schools as a consequence of top-down pressure on local governments attracted considerable media attention, and one of the most eminent NGOs ('Human Chain') campaigned against closures
. Source: "Rescuing a small village school in the context of rural change in Hungary," Journal of Rural Studies — https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0743016712000216

**3. Church/private takeover mechanism and catchment (körzet) reassignment**
Current legal guidance (updated July 2024) explains that when a municipal school converts to church or other non-state maintainer, 
the government office modifies the catchment boundaries of primary schools: the catchment of the school that becomes church-run is attached to another school's catchment, and this "new" obligatory-admission state school is likewise required to admit children in that catchment
. Crucially, 
a school can only be transferred to a maintainer committed to religious/ideological education if more than half of the parents (or students, if of age) enrolled support it — this is a decision-making right, not merely a consultative opinion
. Source: TASZ, "Mit kell tudni szülőként az iskolák fenntartóváltásáról?" — https://tasz.hu/tudastar/iskolak-fenntartovaltasa/

**4. Scale and drivers of the church-transfer wave (historical, 2011)**
Reporting from the first major transfer wave found 
the education state secretariat said 83 schools nationally were taken over by churches, with the number of church-run schools growing by at least a quarter in half a year — while previously at most seven or eight new church-run primary schools were created per year; even so, out of roughly two thousand state-maintained primary schools, church schools remained about ten percent
. Geographically, 
the transfer wave hit the Alföld [Great Plain] and Northern Hungary counties hardest, while in Transdanubia it barely occurred
. Financial incentive: 
church-run schools are guaranteed by the state a supplementary contribution matching roughly the other half of their budget on top of the per-capita quota... an estimated annual extra cost of five billion forints, since 23,000 students moved to church maintainers with the supplement averaging 230,000 forints per student per year
. Source: nyest.hu, "Egyházi fennhatóság alá kerülő iskolák" — https://www.nyest.hu/hirek/egyhazi-fennhatosag-ala-kerulo-iskolak

**5. Recent (2023) renewed property-transfer wave to churches**
A 2023 law allowed churches to claim state/municipal buildings used for church-run education free of charge: 
the Parliament passed an amendment allowing churches to lay claim to state or municipally owned buildings if religious-educational functions are or would be carried out there; these buildings must be handed over completely free of charge, with the law leaving no discretion
. Municipalities pushed back over asset losses (e.g., Hódmezővásárhely citing 
about 2 billion forints of damage from the free-transfer obligation
). Source: eduline.hu — https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/20230404_oktatasi-intezmenyek-iskola-ovoda-onkormanyzat-egyhazak-torveny

**6. Governance architecture since 2013**
State primary schooling is centrally run: 
since 1 January 2013 task-financing replaced the earlier normative financing, and maintainer responsibilities (except kindergarten) passed to the state via the Klebelsberg Institution Maintaining Center (KLIK); from 1 January 2017, county-level "tankerületi központok" (district centers) replaced KLIK as maintainers, decentralizing the system while fully taking over operation responsibilities from municipalities
. This means village municipalities today typically no longer own the maintainer decision for state primary schools — decisions sit with the tankerület — which is central to why "closure" decisions for rural schools are often made regionally, not locally.

**7. Kindergarten threshold effect (upstream indicator for primary viability)**

In 2022, 31% of municipalities had no kindergarten, typically in small villages where the number of kindergarten-aged children did not reach 8
. This threshold dynamic (minimum group-size rules) is analogous to the pressure rural primary schools face and often precedes primary-school non-viability. Source: EU Education and Training Monitor 2025, Hungary country report — https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html

**8. Teacher shortage concentrated in rural/disadvantaged schools**

Teacher shortages, linked to an ageing workforce, are a growing concern: per KSH 2025 data, the number of teachers aged 30–44 in primary schools has declined, and half of all teachers in 2024/2025 were 50 or older while only 7.2% were under 30
. Specifically, 
teacher shortages are greatest in rural areas, with the proportion of unqualified teachers in disadvantaged schools 2 to 3 times higher than in non-disadvantaged schools
. Early leaving is also spatially patterned: 
early school leaving is now at 10.3% nationally, but higher in the least developed regions, in villages (község), and among Roma (58.7% vs 9.3% non-Roma in 2023)
. Same source as #7.

**9. 6/8-grade gimnázium "creaming" effect on the primary-school pipeline**
Research summarized by Válasz Online / qubit.hu describes both mechanism and measured impact. 
A 2018 study (Horn et al.) found that when top students leave a primary-school class for a "kisgimnázium," it has a mildly negative average effect on remaining classmates' 8th-grade test scores, grades, and further-study intentions — though the effect is mixed: the strongest remaining students lose somewhat, while weaker students may even gain
. Only 
roughly 9–10% of all students attend a kisgimnázium
, but this share is concentrated among urban/higher-SES families, meaning rural schools disproportionately lose their strongest pupils to nearby towns via a separate competitive entrance exam that operates outside normal catchment (körzet) admission obligations. Sources: https://qubit.hu/2022/06/15/olyan-gyerek-akinek-kozmunkasok-a-szulei-hat-olyan-itt-nincs and https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/hat_es_nyolcosztalyos_gimnazium_pro_es_kont_GSKH47

**10. Small-school student profile and equity function**
An OFI (Educational Research Institute) study on small-settlement schools found 
small-settlement schools have a lower share of local resident pupils than typical primary schools, and a higher share of daily commuting pupils, because the 8-grade small schools typical of micro-village regions draw significant numbers of pupils from surrounding settlements that have no school at all, or only a lower-division (grades 1–4) school
, and 
small-settlement school populations are consistently less advantaged across every socioeconomic dimension studied, with parental-education gaps of 3-4x compared to other school types
. Source: https://ofi.oh.gov.hu/kistelepulesi-iskolak-es-eredmenyesseg

**Additional context note (not Hungary-specific but comparative):** A Finnish DID-regression study found 
that in Finland after school closure, the population of the surrounding community decreased more than before school closure
, supporting the community-anchor hypothesis raised in the brief, though this is Finnish evidence, not Hungarian, and should be treated as suggestive/comparative rather than direct Hungarian evidence.
