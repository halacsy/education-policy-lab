# Research Notes: Portugal's Rural Primary School Experience — Consolidation, TEIP, and Retention

## Finding 1: Portugal's historic rural school closure wave used a rigid enrollment threshold
Portugal's school network reorganization, begun under PM José Sócrates in 2005/06, **closed all primary schools with fewer than 21 students** as a blanket national criterion. Only in the first two school years (2005/06–2006/07) did **1,615 schools close**. Source: "O processo de reorganização da rede escolar teve início com o Governo de José Sócrates, que decidiu encerrar todas as escolas primárias com menos de 21 alunos... Só nos nos anos letivos 2005/2006 e 2006/2007 encerraram 1.615 es[colas]" — ECO, May 15, 2023. https://eco.sapo.pt/2023/05/15/ministerio-da-educacao-vai-fechar-duas-escolas-no-proximo-ano-letivo-por-falta-de-alunos/

By 2023, the closure wave had largely run its course: only 2 schools were slated to close for the 2023/24 year, while 37 received "exceptional operating authorization" to stay open despite low enrollment — indicating a policy shift toward flexibility/exceptions rather than rigid thresholds. Same source as above.

## Finding 2: Rigorous longitudinal study found NO correlation between school consolidation and improved outcomes (2005–2021)
A peer-reviewed 2023 study analyzing the Portuguese case (2005–2021) directly interrogates whether concentrating students in fewer schools improves educational outcomes. Citing research by Silva (2022) across multiple municipalities: "não tendo encontrado correlação entre os 'centros escolares' construídos para receber os alunos das pequenas escolas rurais e a evolução das taxas de (in)sucesso" (no correlation found between the "school centers" built to receive students from small rural schools and the evolution of success/failure rates). Source: "Concentrar os alunos em menos escolas melhora os resultados educativos? Uma análise do caso português (2005–2021)" http://www.scielo.br/j/ensaio/a/ZKRmfKM83KXq58D86djkt4n/?lang=pt

The same article notes that smaller, isolated rural schools likely benefited LESS from complementary national programs (Plano Nacional de Leitura, Ciência Viva, Novas Oportunidades, full-time school/AEC) that drove Portugal's 2000–2015 PISA gains, because these programs required infrastructure and staff often unavailable in remote, small-enrollment locations — a resource-allocation equity concern cutting against blanket consolidation. Same source.

## Finding 3: Documented community/demographic harm from closures — the "acceleration of desertification" critique
Portuguese sociological literature explicitly links school closures to accelerated rural depopulation, not just a response to it. Rui d'Espiney (sociologist, president of the Instituto de Comunidades Educativas) is quoted: "Tirar as crianças das zonas rurais acelerou a sua desertificação. A partir do momento em que as crianças saíram, os pais também saíram." Source: PÚBLICO investigative feature, "Escolas primárias encerradas desde 2005" https://acervo.publico.pt/sociedade/escolas-primarias-encerradas

The same reporting found that of nearly 4,000 closed schools tracked, **about 20% remain unused** (i.e., left as vacant, decaying community assets) — highlighting a missed-repurposing opportunity rather than a managed transition. Same source. It also documents a countervailing local response: repurposing closed school buildings as intergenerational community hubs (e.g., "Casa das Avós" in Reguengos de Monsaraz), suggesting a viable alternative-use model for buildings even where educational operation ends. Same source.

## Finding 4: Academic critique frames the 21-student threshold as too blunt, ignoring rural school's non-academic value
A conference paper (Portuguese Sociological Association) argues rural first-cycle schools sit in "comunidades isoladas, resultado do despovoamento e envelhecimento" (isolated communities resulting from depopulation and aging) and that the last decade's closures applied "apenas o limiar de crianças – 21 alunos" (only the enrollment threshold of 21 students) without weighing pedagogical or community-cohesion benefits — namely that rural schools "permite utilizar os conhecimentos existentes e desenvolver sinergias entre" school and surrounding community/environment. Source: https://associacaoportuguesasociologia.pt/ix_congresso/docs/final/COM0667.pdf
The paper proposes a "Matriz de baixa densidade" (low-density-territory matrix) as an alternative rural-network reorganization model tied to territorial cohesion policy, rather than pure enrollment-count closure rules. Same source.

## Finding 5: TEIP program (targeted support for vulnerable-territory schools) — status and design, not itself a rural-specific tool, but transferable design logic
TEIP (Territórios Educativos de Intervenção Prioritária) is now in its 4th generation (TEIP4), launched 2024/2025, running six school years, targeting school clusters "situados em territórios com elevado número de crianças e jovens em risco de vulnerabilidade social" to raise inclusion, achievement, and reduce dropout. Source: DGE (Direção-Geral da Educação) https://www.dge.mec.pt/teip
The redesign explicitly sought "conferindo uma maior autonomia às comunidades educativas, de modo a potenciar intervenções mais flexíveis, inovadoras e ajustadas às necessidades dos alunos e das suas famílias e à mobilização de recursos educativos endógenos" (greater autonomy for local flexible, innovation, endogenous-resource-based interventions) — a design principle relevant to rural transferability even though TEIP is primarily an urban/peri-urban poverty-concentration tool historically. Source: Despacho n.º 7798/2023 https://diariodarepublica.pt/dr/detalhe/despacho/7798-2023-216305811
The EU's 2025 Education and Training Monitor country report confirms TEIP's stated aim of "Improving basic skills is a main objective of Portugal's programme for priority intervention educational areas." Source: https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/portugal.html

## Finding 6: Retention (grade repetition) trend — reduced then reversed after pandemic-era administrative pressure eased
Journalistic analysis based on DGEEC data shows that administrative guidance to reduce grade retention drove down repetition rates up to 2019/2020, but the trend reversed afterward: "A taxa de retenção (incluindo por desistência) mais do que duplicou entre 2019/2020 e 2022/2023" for grades 7–9 (3rd cycle). Source: "Chumbos no 7º ao 9º ano quase duplicam entre 2020 e 2023" https://www.paginaum.pt/2024/07/22/chumbos-no-7o-ao-9o-ano-quase-duplicam-entre-2020-e-2023
This suggests retention reduction in Portugal was substantially an administrative/policy-lever effect (orientações administrativas) rather than a stable pedagogical achievement — important caveat when citing Portugal's "reduced retention" as an outcome-improving lever for transfer.

## Finding 7: Persistent teacher belief in retention's benefits complicates policy-driven reduction
A 2023 study of 300 teachers in Madeira, Portugal found that "os professores apresentam crenças positivas face à retenção" (teachers hold positive beliefs about grade retention), and that these beliefs shape decisions especially absent external guidance — meaning top-down non-retention policy can be undercut by teacher practice/culture. Source: Ensaio: aval. pol. públ. educ., Oct-Dec 2023, https://doi.org/10.1590/S0104-40362023003103925 (via https://www.scielo.br/j/ensaio/a/7LHWDWsZQynQT3Pb7qZwLmD/?lang=pt)

## Finding 8: Portugal's current PISA/TIMSS performance context shows system-wide strain, tempering "reform success" narratives
The 2025 EU Education and Training Monitor country report for Portugal notes concerning declines: "TIMSS 2023 further highlighted a concerning drop in the performance of Portuguese students in maths and science, especially in the eighth grade, placing Portugal last among the 15 participating EU Member States." It also finds socio-economic background has a strong influence, with "46.9% underachieving in maths in 2022, compared with 39.4% in 2018." Source: https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/portugal.html
This is important context: Portugal's earlier (2000–2015) PISA gains are not matched by recent trends, so "what worked" claims must be time-bounded and not overstated as an ongoing success story.

## Finding 9: Formal legal/administrative mechanism for exceptional small-school survival exists and is actively used
Portugal's 2024/25 school network ordinance formally lists "escolas básicas às quais foi concedida autorização excecional de funcionamento para o 1.º ciclo do ensino básico até ao final do presente ano letivo, num total de 38" (38 basic schools granted exceptional operating authorization for 1st cycle through the end of the school year) — evidence of an institutionalized escape valve/case-by-case review process rather than pure rule-based closure, which is a transferable procedural mechanism. Source: Portaria n.º 116/2025/1, Diário da República, https://spzc.pt/files/SPZClegis/2025/Anx_02_Port_116_2025_1_Unid_Orga%CC%82n_MECI_Agrup_Esc_e_Esc_Na%CC%83o_Agrup_2024_2025.pdf

## Finding 10: Full-time school / curriculum enrichment activities (AEC) linked to achievement, but infrastructure-dependent — equity risk for rural
Research (Abrantes, 2023, cited in Finding 2's source) found Portugal's "Escola a Tempo Inteiro" full-time school program with curriculum enrichment activities (introduced 2005) "tem apresentado também impactos nos resultados escolares" (has also shown impacts on school outcomes), but implementation "intensificou a necessidade de instalações de melhor qualidade e alocação de profissionais que, por vezes, não estão disponíveis em lugares remotos e com poucas crianças" (intensified the need for better-quality facilities and staff allocation, sometimes unavailable in remote places with few children). Source: same Scielo article as Finding 2, https://doi.org/10.7821/naer.2023.1.1166 (cited within http://www.scielo.br/j/ensaio/a/ZKRmfKM83KXq58D86djkt4n/?lang=pt)
