## Research Notes (round 3)

**1. China rural primary school consolidation (RPSCP, 2001) — long-term migration and human capital effects.** A quasi-experimental study using the 2001 Rural Primary School Consolidation Program found the policy "has a persisting negative impact on rural students' future migration, with a more pronounced effect among girls and younger students," operating through reduced human capital accumulation. Source: Zhao, Yu et al., 2024, *Journal of Asian Economics*, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1049007824000058. A related causal study (Hannum, Liu & Wang 2021, *Economic Development and Cultural Change*) used variation in villages' year of closure and children's age at closure across 728 villages/7 provinces to estimate consolidation's effect on educational attainment; a companion working paper version reports girls exposed to closure during primary-school ages experienced "an average decrease of 0.60 years of schooling by 2011." Source: https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/10.1086/711654 and https://www.researchgate.net/publication/359647128 (arXiv version: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2203.17101). Note: China's rural school count fell from 512,993 (1997) to 210,894 (2010).

**2. Denmark — Tønder Municipality closure of 8 schools (2011), DiD population effect.** Using difference-in-differences on a case where 8 of 17 public schools closed simultaneously, the study found "a population decline of 7.6 percentage points during the 10-year post-closure period," with the flexible DiD showing effects becoming statistically significant only from year six onward. Source: Sørensen, Schmidt et al., 2021, *Journal of Rural Studies* 87, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0743016712000216 (full text: https://www.videnomlandet.dk/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/1_s2.0_S0743016721002801_main.pdf).

**3. Denmark — short-term achievement effects of consolidation.** Beuchert, Humlum, Nielsen & Smith (2018, *Economics of Education Review*) found "individual student test scores declined with consolidation" and the negative overall effect "appears to be driven by school closings; test scores of students exposed to school closure decrease by 5.9% of a standard [deviation]." Source: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0272775717306155.

**4. Finland — nationwide closure wave, population effects (Lehtonen 2021).** A DiD/genetic-matching study of 518 Finnish school closures (2011–2018, 66% rural) "found a clear negative population effect when analysing the community-level population effect of rural school closures." Source: cited in Sørensen et al. 2021, https://www.videnomlandet.dk/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/1_s2.0_S0743016721002801_main.pdf.

**5. Finland — student achievement after displacement (Izadi 2015, VATT).** A study of 600 rural Finnish students displaced by 1999–2000 school closures (cost-driven, not performance-driven) compared displaced students to controls to isolate closure effects on grades and high-school graduation, noting displacement also meant "longer journeys to school and increased school size." Source: https://ideas.repec.org/p/fer/wpaper/63.html.

**6. Finland — qualitative/social-capital evidence.** Interview-based research on Finnish rural communities found closures function as a loss of a key "producer and maintainer of local social capital," and that closures have been especially concentrated in Northern and Eastern Finland's sparsest municipalities. Sources: https://jrre.psu.edu/sites/default/files/2019-08/29-1.pdf; https://yle.fi/a/74-20081895 (2024 reporting: "The school closures first started in the further flung municipalities and now have moved closer to places like Asikkala").

**7. Hungary — village-level correlates of school presence/absence (2015 CSO data study).** A study using 2015 Hungarian Central Statistical Office territorial data found villages with schools "had better demographic performance, partly better living and infrastructural conditions, but the employment and the welfare characteristics were worse, compared to those without" a school — i.e., school presence does not straightforwardly track socioeconomic advantage. Source: https://www.academia.edu/30449769/The_Free_School_Bus_Helping_or_Worsening_the_Problem_of_Village_Depopulation.

**8. Hungary — historical school-network resilience and its limits.** Historical/case-study research notes that "closures or amalgamations occurred only rarely; the rural school network was kept relatively intact until as late as 2004 as a result of state support provided specifically to small villages," but that small municipalities' financial difficulties have since intensified pressure toward amalgamation. Source: Kovács & Farkas or similar, *Journal of Rural Studies*, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0743016712000216.

**9. Hungary — post-2021 territorial/demographic pressures on schools (2025 study).** Recent territorial analysis states that spatial sorting in Hungarian education is "shaped by catchment policies, school closures in peripheral areas, and residential mobility toward more advantaged localities," and that some low-density Northern Hungary/Baranya regions show child-population contraction of 10–30% coinciding with school closure or downgrading. Source: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/396483025.

**10. Hungary — PISA 2022 segregation and socioeconomic gradient (load-bearing equity context).** "Hungary has the highest level of segregation of students from less and more advantaged socio-economic groups into different schools," and "Hungary's segregation of the most and least disadvantaged students was the highest in the EU in the 2022 PISA study," with 62% of Roma children aged 6–15 attending schools where all/most pupils are Roma. Socio-economic status "accounted for 25% of the variation in mathematics performance in PISA 2022 in Hungary (compared to 15% on average across OECD countries)." Source: EU Education & Training Monitor 2025, Hungary, https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html; OECD PISA 2022 Country Note Hungary, https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/pisa-2022-results-volume-i-and-ii-country-notes_ed6fbcc5-en/hungary_3df2ae68-en.html.

**11. OECD cross-national — rural school resourcing and multigrade teaching (PISA/TALIS working paper).** The OECD's rural-schools working paper notes limited enrolments "may be challenging from a financial perspective and also require particular pedagogical strategies... (e.g. effective multigrade teaching)" but also "creates opportunities for schools and families, for instance in terms of teacher support and adaptive teaching, especially for disadvantaged and struggling students." It also finds that in several countries — explicitly including **Hungary** — "rural schools are, overall, better equipped than city schools" on some material-resource measures, while city schools have an edge in science-specific resources. Source: OECD Education Working Paper No. 196, 2019, https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2019/03/learning-in-rural-schools_75ae062d/8b1a5cb9-en.pdf.

**12. Multigrade teaching — systematic review (2025).** A PRISMA-guided review of 40 international studies (2019–2024) concludes multigrade classrooms, "when operated in accordance with minimum standards, have the capacity to deliver an education of a high standard... and contribute to the reduction in regional inequalities," but require greater investment in teacher training and dedicated policy. Source: https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7102/15/8/1052.

**13. US — scale and mechanism of consolidation historically and recently.** Historical US data: public school count fell from 248,117 (1929–30) to 98,271 (2013–14); in 2017–18 alone there were 1,310 closures affecting an estimated 266,777 previously enrolled students. Source: Hannum & Wang working paper (arXiv), https://arxiv.org/pdf/2203.17101.
