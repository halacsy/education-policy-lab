# Research Notes (Educational Psychology domain)

**1. School closures — mixed/negative long-run effects (Texas, US).** A 2024 EdWorkingPaper using Texas administrative data found 
experiencing school closures results in disruptions in both test scores and behavior, and while the drop in test scores is recovered within three years, behavioral issues persist
. Source: Kim, J. (2024), "The Long Shadow of School Closures," EdWorkingPaper 24-963, https://edworkingpapers.com/ai24-963 (full text https://edworkingpapers.com/sites/default/files/ai24-963.pdf).

**2. Ordinal rank / peer effects mechanism.** The same paper notes prior research showing 
40% of peer effects can be offset by ordinal rank effects in 3rd grade students
, suggesting reshuffling of peer groups after consolidation can hurt some students' relative standing even when average school quality rises. Source: same as above.

**3. Literature review: inconsistent closure effects internationally.** A review accompanying the China rural-school-closure study states 
literature on the impact of school closures on affected students shows inconsistent results, with some studies suggesting negative effects on performance and outcomes
, citing e.g. Chile where 
school closure increases the probability of high-school dropout between 49 and 68 percent (1.8 and 2.5 percentage points)
, while other contexts (Amsterdam) showed no negative impact, and Denmark showed short-run harm that 
had adverse effects on achievement in the short run, but effects appeared to weaken over time, suggesting part of the effect was due to disruption
. Source: arXiv preprint, Estimating the Effects of Educational System Consolidation (China), https://arxiv.org/pdf/2203.17101

**4. China rural closure — largest harm to disadvantaged minority/poor villages.** University of Houston project summary: 
ethnic minority youth were negatively affected by closure, in terms of impact on both educational attainment and written Mandarin facility, with penalties occurring only in the poorest villages, and generally heavier for girls
. Source: https://www.uh.edu/class/economics/cego/research/economic-development/school-closure-project/

**5. Displacement effects depend on destination school quality.** 
students displaced by school closures can experience adverse effects on test scores and attendance, but these effects can be minimized when students move to higher quality schools; the negative effect on attendance disappears after the first year
. However, a rural-schools advocacy compilation notes most displaced students do **not** land in stronger schools: 
if students land in an academically stronger school, they can see test score gains; if not, their scores decline—and research also shows that most students do not land in stronger schools
. Sources: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2203.17101 ; https://ruralschoolsopen.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/school-closure-effects-talking-points.pdf

**6. Rural students face longer travel burden after closure.** 
Students, especially in rural areas, also often face longer bus rides or travel time
. Source: https://ruralschoolsopen.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/school-closure-effects-talking-points.pdf

**7. Multigrade teaching — pedagogically viable with adequate support.** A 2025 systematic review states multigrade classrooms are 
characterized by mixed-age groups within a single classroom, a practical solution to limitations of conventional level/age-based organization, with mounting interest in their pedagogical potential
, and 
UNESCO has promoted research on their effectiveness in guaranteeing the right to education in isolated communities, provided adequate conditions such as teacher training, adapted resources, and institutional support
. Source: MDPI 2025 systematic review, https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7102/15/8/1052

**8. Multigrade can benefit low-achieving readers specifically.** A Caribbean small-island study found 
multigrade schools are particularly effective at promoting the reading progress of low-achieving students
, hypothesized to be 
because multigrade classes give students more opportunity for small-group work, whereas monograde classrooms tend toward undifferentiated whole-class teaching
. Source: ResearchGate, https://www.researchgate.net/publication/321217303_Multi-grade_Teaching_A_Daunting_Challenge_for_Rural_Teachers

**9. Multigrade requires specific teacher skill; system-level barriers matter more than pedagogy per se.** Austria/Finland comparative study of schools 
with fewer than fifty students, usually employing two or three teachers who teach different grades in the same classroom (multi-grade or multi-age teaching)
 found 
the results illustrate how differently multi-grade teaching can be realized and how it can effectively support individual student learning
. A separate international systematic review notes 
challenges in multigrade education often stem from policy, financing, deployment, and supervision issues rather than pedagogy alone
. Sources: ScienceDirect, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0883035515000749 ; IJERIP, https://ijerip.erio.org.ph/index.php/articles/article/download/34/30

**10. Older US meta-review: multigrade "neither better nor worse" academically, but better on affect.** A classic Research in Rural Education synthesis concluded multigrade classrooms are 
neither better nor worse in multigrade classrooms; however, areas relating to student affect significantly favor
 multigrade settings (student attitudes/self-concept). Source: https://jrre.psu.edu/sites/default/files/2019-08/7-1_1.pdf

**11. School size effects — curvilinear; very small and very large both suboptimal for achievement, but small favors climate/wellbeing.** A Dutch review states 
both "very small" and "very large" school sizes are less conducive to the quality of education
 per multiple meta-analyses/reviews, but 
among other outcomes are student self-esteem, physical safety, and social behavior; the evidence on these outcomes is meager, but always in favor of smaller schools
. A related conceptual review notes 
perceptions on school climate appeared to be more positive in smaller schools
, and for elementary specifically 
the overall tendency in a study on 254 elementary schools was that small schools did better, both in terms of direct and indirect effects
, mediated by 
the willingness for teachers to take responsibility for students' achievement
. For secondary schools, Lee & Smith found 
students learned more in middle-sized secondary schools (600–900), as compared to smaller, but particularly to larger high schools
, an effect stronger for high-poverty schools. Sources: https://www.nko.nl/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/PROO+School+size+effects+revisited+Hans+Luyten+ea.pdf ; https://www.woodlandschools.org/storage/file/5446/School%20Size%20Effects-%20Review%20and%20Conceptional%20Analysis.pdf

**12. Big-Fish-Little-Pond Effect (BFLPE) — robust, moderated by age.** A 2018 meta-analysis (33 studies, N=1,276,838) found 
the random effects model led to a mean of the BFLPE at β = −0.28 (p < 0.001); moderator analyses revealed that BFLPE is an age-based process and an intercultural phenomenon, stronger among high school students, in Asia and when verbal self-concept is considered
. This is directly relevant: consolidating small rural schools into larger, higher-achieving regional schools may lower students' academic self-concept even if raw achievement rises — a genuine trade-off, and BFLPE appears weaker at primary-school age than in high school, moderating the risk in grades 1-6. Source: Frontiers in Psychology, https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6124391/

**13. Long commutes harm wellbeing and academic outcomes, effect concentrated among the already-disadvantaged.** A nationwide Chinese survey found 
long commuters have poorer health, lower cognitive abilities, weaker academic performance, and less time for daily activities than their counterparts whose commutes are shorter; long commuters tend to live in rural areas, their parents are less educated, and their families are poorer
, and 
long commutes affect teenagers' wellbeing regardless of transport mode (active or passive)
. Quantile regression shows 
students on the lower quantiles of the conditional distribution of PWB tend to suffer more than the others when commuting time increases
. Source: ScienceDirect, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2214140523000415 (open PMC version: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9658989/)

**14. Rural school bus commutes specifically flagged as longer and behaviorally costly (US).** 
Rural school bus commutes are longer than their urban counterparts and often exceed 30 minutes; students with longer school bus travel times tend to skip breakfast and report lower grades, poorer levels of fitness, fewer social activities, and worse study habits; longer commutes disproportionately burden students in lower income households and aggravate absenteeism
. Source: arXiv, Rural School Bus Routing and Scheduling, https://arxiv.org/pdf/2507.19538

**15. Primary-to-secondary transition literature is directly relevant to rural school mergers (transition-like disruption).** A review notes 
moving to a new school can provide helpful resources for adolescents but can also present challenges, especially when the new environment is misaligned with adolescents' developing needs for autonomy, relatedness, and competence; longer term trajectories of academic disaffection can quickly form after changing schools
. A Norwegian study specifically compared 
the transition from primary to secondary level in smaller and larger rural schools in Norway, comparing differences in context and social meaning
 (Kvalsund, 2000) — directly analogous to consolidation scenarios, though I could not retrieve the specific effect-size findings from the abstract snippet, only the citation. Sources: https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/psychology/school-transitions ; https://www.acamh.org/blog/primary-secondary-school-transition-wellbeing/

**16. NAEP rural learning-loss data (post-pandemic, US, contextual not causal to closures).** 
In mathematics, rural grade 4 performance declined by two points and rural grade 8 performance declined by eight points in 2022 compared to 2019; in reading, rural grade 4 performance declined by one point and rural grade 8 performance declined by four points
. Source: CSBA blog on "Rural Matters 2023" report, https://blog.csba.org/rural-education2023/

**Note on Hungary-specific evidence:** I did not find Hungary-specific peer-reviewed studies on rural school closure/consolidation psychological effects in this search round; the Hungarian item retrieved (PMC11352284) concerns an unrelated health-promotion program, not school closure/rural viability. This remains a gap — Hungarian-context findings (e.g., on the 2000s–2010s Hungarian school-maintenance-center consolidations) should be flagged as an explicit uncertainty in the structured analysis, as all quantitative effect-size evidence above is from the US, China, Chile, Denmark, Norway, and cross-national reviews, not Hungary.
