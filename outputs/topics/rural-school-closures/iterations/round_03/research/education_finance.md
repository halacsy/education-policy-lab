I'll research current evidence on the finance and cost dimensions of rural primary school viability, closures, and alternatives.
# Research Notes — Rural Primary School Finance & Transition Costs (Hungary focus)

**1. Diseconomies of scale are structural, not incidental, in small village schools.** Hungarian legal/policy analysis of the pre-2013 municipal financing era states plainly that per-pupil (fajlagos) costs in small schools are far higher than in large ones, because upper-primary grades still require a full subject-teacher roster regardless of class size, creating a large fixed cost base. Source: "oktatás finanszírozás" (PPKE reform paper), https://jak.ppke.hu/uploads/articles/11252/file/OKTAT%C3%81S%20finansz%C3%ADroz%C3%A1s%C3%A1nak%20reformja.pdf

**2. Historical funding trajectory in Hungary shows state co-financing eroding, forcing local exposure.** Central government originally covered 80–90% of primary-education costs under the co-financing system introduced after the 1990 Local Government Act reforms of 2004–2005, but by the turn of the millennium the state's share had dropped to 60–70% on average and to only ~50% in small village schools, pushing costs onto increasingly weak municipal budgets. Source: "Rescuing a small village school in the context of rural change in Hungary," ScienceDirect, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0743016712000216

**3. Post-2013 centralization (Klebelsberg Center/tankerület system) shifted maintenance to the state but did not resolve the small-school cost problem, and reversal would itself carry large transition costs.** A 2026 opinion piece in Eduline argues Hungary's core unresolved issue is "kistelepülési oktatás jövője" — the future of small-settlement education — and estimates that if the district-center (tankerület) system were dismantled overnight and full financial autonomy returned to individual schools, this could require creating hundreds or even thousands of new administrative posts, "jelentős bérköltség" (significant wage costs), and new compliance/procurement structures, likely at higher unit cost than the current centralized system. Source: Eduline, "Pukli István: Nem azt kell először eldöntenünk..." (June 22, 2026), https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/20260622_pukli-istvan-iskola-fenntartas-onkormanyzat

**4. School transport ("iskolabusz") is a major and financially fragile line item, often unfunded by the state.** Hungarian legal-aid NGO TASZ documents that school buses have typically been organized by municipalities as a voluntary (not centrally funded) task, financed entirely from local revenue; rising fuel and overhead costs in 2023–2024 caused several settlements to cancel services entirely, leaving children — especially in outlying/tanya areas — unable to reach school or facing disproportionate hardship. Source: TASZ, "Panaszmintát készítettünk az iskolabuszok újraindítása érdekében" (updated Jan 16, 2024), https://tasz.hu/cikkek/panaszmintat-keszitettunk-az-iskolabuszok-ujrainditasa-erdekeben/

**5. Concrete Hungarian cost figure for a single village bus route.** In Délegyháza, the district center (Szigetszentmiklósi Tankerületi Központ) discontinued its 20% cost-share for the local school bus after 12 years; restarting the route was estimated to require **35 million forints/year**, and roughly 50 students used the service; without it, children face up to 1.5 hours of daily walking on roads without lighting or pavement. Source: Index, "Megszűnt az iskolabusz Délegyházán..." (Sept 26, 2024), https://index.hu/belfold/2024/09/26/iskolabusz-megszunik-delegyhaza-tankerulet-gyerekek-gyaloglas-diakok-oktatas-kozlekedes/

**6. National-level capital investment in school transport (2024).** The Klebelsberg Center secured 100% funding for 46 electric school buses (43 mini + 3 large) at a total cost of **1.91 billion HUF**, to be deployed across 29 towns by April 2027 under the "Zöld Busz Program," financed via HUMDA. This signals a state-level shift toward centrally funded, capital-intensive transport infrastructure as a partial alternative to closing schools, but scale (46 buses) is small relative to national need. Source: Klebelsberg Center press release, June 7, 2024, https://kk.gov.hu/negyvenhat-elektromos-iskolabuszra-nyert-tamogatast-a-klebelsberg-kozpont-a-humda-tol

**7. International evidence: closures do not reliably produce net savings once transport and capital costs are counted.** A synthesis of consolidation studies (McKenzie 1993; Nunn 1991; Holland & Baritelle 1975) concludes that staff savings from merging small rural schools "may be outweighed by the costs of constructing new school facilities and transporting students greater distances," and that rural consolidation savings are "unlikely to achieve large savings in expenditures." Source: Witham, ERIC ED415036, https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/ED415036.pdf

**8. Rural transport cost multipliers are large and consistent across US data.** Recent operations-research literature reports that bus operating costs per student are about 40% higher in rural districts than urban ones, the marginal cost of an additional rural rider is about 2.5× the urban marginal cost, and an additional bus-mile in a rural district costs roughly 10× more than in an urban district (citing Ellegood, Riley & Berg 2024). Source: "Rural School Bus Routing and Scheduling," arXiv, https://arxiv.org/pdf/2507.19538

**9. Closure has measurable, lagged negative effects on local population — a community/economic cost typically excluded from school-budget accounting.** A quasi-experimental Danish study of eight simultaneously closed village schools (2011) finds a statistically robust negative population effect: the difference-in-differences estimate shows local population would have been **7.6 percentage points more favorable** over the following decade had the schools stayed open, with the effect becoming significant only from year six onward (a "lock-in effect" via social capital and housing markets). Source: "Do rural school closures lead to local population decline?", ScienceDirect, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0743016721002801

**10. Hungarian empirical work suggests school presence correlates with demographic resilience but not uniformly with socioeconomic advantage.** Analysis of Hungarian Central Statistical Office territorial data (2015 base year) found villages retaining a school had somewhat better demographic performance and infrastructure, but worse employment and welfare indicators than villages without a school — complicating any simple "school = local prosperity" narrative. Source: "Shrinking, growing or overburdened? Territorial patterns in child demographics... after 2021," https://www.academia.edu/144471812/Shrinking_growing_or_overburdened_Territorial_patterns_in_child_demographics_and_institutional_burden_in_Hungarys_public_education_system_after_2021

**11. Scale threshold evidence from Poland (comparable post-socialist rural structure).** A large-N study of 42,400 localities and 13,100 schools found school presence is closely tied to locality population, particularly in the 400–700 inhabitant range, and that economies/diseconomies of scale effects on school size persist up to roughly a 4,100-population threshold, beyond which size effects flatten — useful as an analog for Hungarian village-size thresholds. Source: "Do rural school closures lead to local population decline?" (ResearchGate secondary discussion of Polish data), https://www.researchgate.net/publication/355001456_Do_rural_school_closures_lead_to_local_population_decline
