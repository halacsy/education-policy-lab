# Research Notes — Poland's Rural Primary School Experience (for Hungary rural-schools brief)

**1. Scale of rural school network contraction, 2000–2021 (Polish Carpathians/national).** A peer-reviewed study of the primary school network found that between 2000 and 2021 the number of primary schools nationally fell by 17% while the number of pupils fell by only 3% — meaning closures outpaced demographic decline. The decline had two phases: 2000–2013 saw ~40% pupil decline in both cities and rural areas, but school closures were concentrated in **rural areas, where school numbers fell 28%**, while urban school numbers actually rose 6.5% (partly due to new private urban schools). Eastern Poland was hit hardest. Source: Ejournals/Kraków, "Przemiany w szkolnictwie podstawowym w Polsce (2000–2021)," https://ejournals.eu/czasopismo/prace-geograficzne/artykul/przemiany-w-szkolnictwie-podstawowym-w-polsce-2000-2021-uwarunkowania-demograficzne-i-przestrzenne

**2. The 1999 reform (creation of gimnazjum) as a direct driver of rural school closures.** Analysis by Piwowarski explicitly links closures of small village schools not just to falling child numbers but to **political decisions restructuring rural education** — first the Soviet-era "collective commune schools" (zbiorcze szkoły gminne), then the 1999 reform's introduction of gimnazjums, which required consolidating older grades into larger, often town-based institutions. Source: Piwowarski, "Szkoła dla wsi w Polsce i na świecie," CEJSH, https://cejsh.icm.edu.pl/cejsh/element/bwmeta1.element.ojs-doi-10_15804_em_2022_03_04/c/articles-2118889.pdf.pdf

**3. Financial incentive: municipalities profit from closing schools even short-term.** A Polish Supreme Audit Office (NIK) review of 39 municipalities (cited in Semczuk 2018/2020) found that **converting or closing a primary school produced real budget savings from the very first year**, even after accounting for increased busing costs and severance pay for dismissed teachers. Source: Semczuk, "Zmiany w sieci szkół podstawowych na obszarach wiejskich województwa małopolskiego," https://yadda.icm.edu.pl/baztech/element/bwmeta1.element.baztech-f863d048-de94-41f2-8fc6-f8f5cd4cd030/c/Biuletyn_KPZK_272_Semczuk.pdf

**4. The "citizen/social school" (NGO-run rural school) model as an alternative to closure.** Since 1990, Polish law has allowed local associations to take over a threatened public school and run it as a non-public "social school," retaining state subsidy eligibility while cutting costs. According to a first-hand account from the founder of the rural-schools rescue movement, **around 500–600 rural schools were established/rescued this way after 1990**, explicitly framed as a response to closures caused by the 1999 (Buzek/Handke) reform: "The most serious effect of this reform was closing schools in small villages." The founder also describes this as similar to US charter schools, with lower operating costs than municipality-run equivalents, though "not without blind spots" given how fast reforms were implemented. Source: HuffPost, "Rescuing Rural Schools in Poland," https://www.huffpost.com/entry/rescuing-rural-schools-in_b_6678580; supporting detail on the legal/associational model: Our Kids, https://www.ourkids.net/pl-en/social-schools.php and Eurydice, https://eurydice.eacea.ec.europa.eu/national-education-systems/poland/organisation-single-structure-education

**5. Regional prevalence of NGO-run rural schools (Małopolska case study).** In Małopolska voivodeship, **123 of 1,076 rural primary schools** were already run by social organizations/associations rather than the municipality by the mid-2010s, concentrated in Tarnów county (30 schools) and mountainous/peripheral areas. A rule of thumb cited in the literature: a municipality needs **at least ~70 students** to sustain a village school under normal public funding; **269 schools in Małopolska fell below that threshold**, and some of these had already transferred to NGO/association management despite lower costs still not fully solving viability. Source: Semczuk (Biuletyn KPZK PAN), https://yadda.icm.edu.pl/baztech/element/bwmeta1.element.baztech-f863d048-de94-41f2-8fc6-f8f5cd4cd030/c/Biuletyn_KPZK_272_Semczuk.pdf

**6. Targeted funding formula ("rural weight" in the subwencja oświatowa) as a policy lever to keep small schools open.** Poland's central per-pupil education subsidy (subwencja oświatowa) uses a weighted algorithm; since 2015 a higher multiplier ("waga wiejska"/rural weight) has applied to pupils in rural primary schools and towns under 5,000 residents, with the smallest schools (under ~70 pupils) benefiting the most — subsidy per child rising to roughly **8,400 PLN**. The weight for small-locality schools (P3) was raised further, from 0.25 to 0.33 for 2023 and to **0.4 for 2024**, explicitly to strengthen support for schools in smaller localities. Sources: Piwowarski (CEJSH, as above, section on financing algorithm); portalsamorzadowy.pl, https://www.portalsamorzadowy.pl/edukacja/subwencja-oswiatowa-w-2024-r-wiecej-pieniedzy-na-jednego-ucznia-i-dla-malych-szkol,500329.html; prawo.pl, https://www.prawo.pl/oswiata/subwencja-oswiatowa-dla-malych-miejscowosci-w-2023-r,518516.html

**7. Legal safeguards on rural school closure procedure.** Polish education law requires that a local government liquidating a school must demonstrate it will not worsen pupils' learning/safety conditions, will not worsen travel/commute conditions for pupils (and preschoolers, where relevant), and that the closure is justified by demographic change or enrollment forecasts; the regional education authority (kurator oświaty) evaluates not just legal compliance but the substantive justification (zasadność) of the closure. Source: prawo.pl, https://www.prawo.pl/oswiata/subwencja-oswiatowa-dla-malych-miejscowosci-w-2023-r,518516.html

**8. 2017–2019 reversal (liquidation of gimnazjums) caused a second wave of network disruption, though with less rural-specific documentation.** The gimnazjum liquidation reform eliminated 5,181 of 6,571 public gimnazjums between 2017 and 2019, with only a 6% increase in public primary schools accompanying this (versus a 20% increase in private primary schools), and a big rise in pupils-per-public-primary-school (from 181 to 254), suggesting overcrowding pressure rather than new small rural schools opening. Source: ciekaweliczby.pl, https://ciekaweliczby.pl/reforma-edukacji/

**9. Ongoing rural depopulation context.** Poland's rural population has been declining (14.79 million in 2022 to 14.63 million in 2024), and small villages (under 200 residents) are depopulating fastest, with their share of all villages more than tripling since 1950 — the structural driver behind enrollment decline that both the 1999 and 2017 reforms interacted with. Source: macrotrends.net, https://www.macrotrends.net/global-metrics/countries/pol/poland/rural-population; SCIEPublish/Wesołowska 2023, https://www.sciepublish.com/uploads/2023/09/25/e06e7ab8efd8e93e05fb397cb98e6f5f.pdf

**10. Current national scale (2024/2025).** As of preliminary 2024/2025 data, Poland has roughly 3.2 million pupils in 14,000 primary schools nationally, with rural schools on average smaller than urban ones due to demographic factors. Source: Eurydice, https://eurydice.eacea.ec.europa.eu/national-education-systems/poland/organisation-single-structure-education
