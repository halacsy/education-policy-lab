RESEARCH NOTES — Finland's school system and reform history, with rural/small-school policy focus (Round 3)

1. **Scale of small-school network historically**: A ScienceDirect study on Finnish small rural primary schools states that "over 30% of primary schools are small schools with 2–4 permanent teachers and teaching groups," most located in sparsely populated areas, and "almost 20% of each age group studies at a rural primary school with less than 100 pupils." Source: "Small rural primary schools in Finland: A pedagogically valuable part of the school network," ScienceDirect, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0883035509000160

2. **Community-hub function of small schools**: The same source notes the historical function of small rural schools "has traditionally been one of a community centre, serving as a meeting place and a social and cultural mid-point for community activities," and that this value "has been emphasised particularly after school closure has taken place." Source: same URL as above, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0883035509000160

3. **Historical trajectory — peruskoulu reform temporarily helped small schools, then 1990s crisis reversed it**: The paper explains closures "started to increase significantly in the late 1960s," then "the position of small rural primary schools improved only for a while at the end of the 1970s when the current comprehensive school system was founded," which "ensured equal educational opportunities for all citizens regardless of socio-economic background or dwelling place." But "at the end of the 1980s and early 1990s, major economic depression forced municipalities to restart massive financial cuts on basic services," and "since then the position of small rural primary schools as a part of the school network has been at risk." Source: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0883035509000160

4. **1990s governance decentralization as the mechanism transmitting fiscal pressure to local closures**: An Oxford Academic chapter on the Finnish comprehensive school states "a radical change came with the decentralization and deregulation of governance during the 1990s, influenced by New Public Management trends," and "these ideas also enabled the transfer of unpopular budgetary cuts to the local level in a period of economic crisis," significantly increasing municipal autonomy over school provision while the state lost inspection and financial control. Source: "The Finnish Comprehensive School: Conflicts, Compromises, and Institutional Robustness," Oxford Academic, https://academic.oup.com/book/42635/chapter/358101849

5. **Recent/current closure statistics (2024)**: Helsinki Times reports comprehensive school numbers in Finland "dropped to 1,974 by the end of 2024," down from "2,014 schools in 2023 and a sharp fall from 2,498 a decade ago—a 21% reduction over ten years," with "78 schools... closed or merged" during 2024 alone; closures were concentrated in Uusimaa and North Ostrobothnia, and South Karelia saw "39% of comprehensive schools" disappear over the decade. Source: "Number of primary schools in Finland drops below 2,000," Helsinki Times, https://www.helsinkitimes.fi/themes/themes/education/26140-number-of-primary-schools-in-finland-drops-below-2-000.html

6. **Demographic driver now shifting from growth to decline**: The same article notes that despite closures, Finland's school-age population "was still growing throughout the last decade," but "in the past two to three years, the number of primary school students has started to decline," suggesting municipalities "carried out [mergers] in anticipation of population forecasts." Source: same as above.

7. **Future projection scenarios (EDUFI/Statistics Finland)**: Yle reports that Finland had "550,000 primary pupils (grades 1-9)" in 2018, projected to drop to "425,000" by 2040; the National Agency for Education's most drastic scenario suggests "slashing the number of schools by half," down to "1,300 schools... from 2,300 today," while the agency's own expert anticipated "it's more likely a third of schools will close" (a 37% shrinkage scenario), with a milder scenario at roughly a quarter of schools closing. Source: "Small schools in danger of closing as Finland continues to centralise education," Yle, https://yle.fi/news/3-11478615

8. **2025 Ministry-commissioned forecast**: Yle reports a Ministry of Education and Culture study found "there will be nearly 100,000 fewer schoolchildren in Finland by 2040," making it "impractical for some schools, especially ones in rural areas, to remain open," with some areas unable to offer local elementary education at all, forcing travel to neighboring municipalities; Finland's fertility rate hit "a historic low of 1.25 in 2024." Source: "Report: Half of Finland's elementary schools face closure due to plummeting birth rate," Yle, https://yle.fi/a/74-20182333

9. **Causal evidence on closure impact on student achievement**: A VATT Institute working paper examined "600 students at rural schools, who were displaced in the last years of their primary education due to school closures in 1999-2000," where closures were driven purely by cost-savings (not poor performance), and found displacement effects included "longer journeys to school and increased school size," with effects estimated on grades and high-school graduation via comparison to control students. Source: Izadi, R. (2015), "The impact of school closures on student achievement - evidence from rural Finland," VATT Working Papers 63, https://ideas.repec.org/p/fer/wpaper/63.html

10. **Causal evidence on closure impact on community population trajectories**: A ScienceDirect study using difference-in-differences regression with matching (covering 2010–2018 closures) found that "in rural communities the impacts of school closure have been slightly more negative suggesting a higher risk of self-reinforcing population decline," and the authors argue these negative impacts are "one incentive to look for new solutions and innovations to organize and develop school networks." Source: "Primary school closures and population development – is school vitality an investment in the attractiveness of the (rural) communities or not?", ScienceDirect, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0743016721000115

11. **Qualitative/social-capital evidence on local experience of closures**: A Journal of Research in Rural Education study states "the network of small rural schools in Finland has been radically weakened since the global recession of the 1990s," and examines closures "from the viewpoint of local residents," using social capital theory to understand the school's role "as a producer and maintainer of local social capital," based on interviews with residents of villages/municipalities that experienced closures. Source: "School Closures in Rural Finnish Communities," Journal of Research in Rural Education (2014), full text PDF: https://jrre.psu.edu/sites/default/files/2019-08/29-1.pdf (also indexed at https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ1029794)

12. **School size norms nationally (relevant to "what carried the effect")**: A Stanford CPRE brief notes "Finnish schools are generally small (fewer than 300 pupils) with relatively small class sizes (in the 20s), and are uniformly well equipped," with "the notion of caring for students educationally and personally" as a central principle — suggesting Finland's baseline school-size culture is itself small-scale even outside remote rural areas, which is relevant context for what "small school" means in Finland vs. Hungary. Source: "What We Can Learn from Finland's Successful School Reform," Stanford CPRE, https://edpolicy.stanford.edu/library/publications/543.html

13. **Structural school-network trend — consolidation into unified 1–9 schools**: The Helsinki Times/Statistics Finland report notes "the growing trend of merging lower and upper comprehensive schools into unified institutions covering grades 1–9. The number of such schools has now exceeded 500, compared to 381 a decade ago," which "also contributes to the increase in average school size." Source: https://www.helsinkitimes.fi/themes/themes/education/26140-number-of-primary-schools-in-finland-drops-below-2-000.html

14. **Hungary comparison — current policy stance and scale (2025)**: A Válasz Online investigative piece reports that in Hungary, "a Klebersberg Intézet" clarified school closures are handled at the tankerület (district) level, not centrally, and a government commissioner stated "A cél nem a bezárás, hanem a kistelepülések és a kisiskolák védelme" ("the goal isn't closure but protection of small settlements and small schools") "amíg erre van igény" (as long as there's demand), while acknowledging mergers ("összevonások") are underway; the piece also cites 2024 national data that "127,5 ezer ember halt meg, de csak 77,5 ezer gyerek született" (127,500 died vs. 77,500 births), and notes five Hungarian counties in 2024 had combined natural-loss-plus-migration population decline exceeding 1%. Source: "Óvodák már lakat alatt, de itt lappang az iskolabezárási hullám is," Válasz Online, https://www.valaszonline.hu/2025/10/17/ovodak-iskolak-bezarasa-demografia-oktatas/

15. **Hungary — 2022 EU commitment threshold and expert critique**: Eduline reports that per a trade-union official, in 2022 the Hungarian government promised the EU it would reorganize small-settlement schools where "az osztálylétszámok nem érik el a 10 főt, és az iskolába járó gyerekek száma is 100-120 fő alatt marad" (class sizes under 10, total pupils under 100-120), integrating them into a central institution; education researcher Judit Lannert cautioned that such reorganization "kellene érintenie" the whole school network, not just apply a blanket cut to small settlements, and stressed that "a kisiskolák fenntartása fajlagosan többe kerül, de az arányuk a teljes oktatási rendszerben alig 1 százalékot tesz ki" (small schools cost more per capita but represent barely 1% of the whole system), meaning cost alone doesn't necessitate closure; she also flagged the need for functioning school-bus transport if consolidation proceeds. Source: "A kisiskolák bezárása nem új ötlet, de nem enyhítené a tanárhiányt," eduline.hu, https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/20240206_kisiskola-iskolabezaras-tanarhiany

16. **OECD demographic comparator for Finland (2025)**: OECD Education at a Glance 2025 notes "Finland experienced a decline of 22% in the number of 0-4 year-olds" between 2013-2023, projected to see only a further "1%" decline 2023-2033, and that Finland has near-universal public funding of school education at "99.9% of total funding." Source: OECD "Education at a Glance 2025: Finland," https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/education-at-a-glance-2025_1a3543e2-en/finland_d8f44a5b-en.html
