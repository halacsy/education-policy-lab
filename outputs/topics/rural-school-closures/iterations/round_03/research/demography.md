# Research Notes — Demography Domain (Round 3)

**Finding 1: Hungary's national population continues to shrink at accelerating pace.** Macrotrends data show total population fell from 10,156,239 (2023) to 9,994,993 (2024) to 9,870,870 (2025) — a 1.59% and 1.24% year-on-year decline respectively. Source: "Hungary Population (1950-2026)" — https://www.macrotrends.net/global-metrics/countries/hun/hungary/population

**Finding 2: 2024 births hit record low.** According to OSW Centre for Eastern Studies analysis, "In 2024, only 77,500 children were born – the lowest figure since records began – representing a year-on-year decline of more than 7,000," and Hungary "recorded the third-largest annual population decline among EU member states" in 2023. Source: "Hungary's ongoing demographic decline: an increase in birth rates is the only hope," OSW, 14 April 2025 — https://www.osw.waw.pl/en/publikacje/osw-commentary/2025-04-14/hungarys-ongoing-demographic-decline-increase-birth-rates-only

**Finding 3: KSH confirms structural shift away from primary enrollment.** The Hungarian Central Statistical Office states that in the 2024/2025 school year, compared to the previous year, "the number of children in kindergartens and students in secondary and tertiary education has increased, while that of primary school students has decreased." Village residents make up 30% of Hungary's 9.585 million population as of 1 January 2024. Source: "Education – Hungarian Central Statistical Office" — https://www.ksh.hu/education

**Finding 4: Migration gains to villages have reversed.** KSH notes that "during the pandemic, migration was directed from larger settlements to smaller ones; however, from 2023, there was a migration gain in the capital city, while the gains in the villages declined significantly" — removing a key counterforce that had temporarily buoyed some rural populations. Source: "Population and vital events – KSH" — https://www.ksh.hu/population-and-vital-events

**Finding 5: A new 2025 settlement-level study is the single most load-bearing Hungary-specific dataset found.** It documents that "by 2024, more than 1,200 of 3,155 settlements had no public educational institutions, raising concerns about territorial equity," and that disadvantaged children have become "increasingly concentrated in rural areas (up to 94% in certain settlements)." The study identifies five settlement clusters including "Disadvantaged Declining Areas" and notes areas where "the child population contracted by 10–30%" between 2021-2024, often coinciding with school closure or downgrading. Source: "Shrinking, growing or overburdened? Territorial patterns in child demographics and institutional burden in Hungary's public education system after 2021" (2025), Interdisciplinary Dialogues, https://doi.org/10.15170/TTK-SZJSZK-2025B-4 — https://www.researchgate.net/publication/396483025

**Finding 6: Rural teacher shortage is compounding enrollment decline.** EU Education and Training Monitor 2025 reports "half of all teachers in the 2024/2025 academic year were 50 or older, while only 7.2% of teachers were under 30," and that "teacher shortages are greatest in rural areas, with the proportion of unqualified teachers in disadvantaged schools 2 to 3 times higher than in schools not disadvantaged." Source: "Hungary - Education and Training Monitor 2025" — https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html

**Finding 7: Hungarian school-choice/consolidation research shows net welfare gains but real losers.** An empirical study on Hungarian/Budapest school district consolidation found "significantly more than half of all students strictly benefit from district consolidation (between 69% to 75% of the students)... Only a small fraction of students (between 2% to 4%) are harmed by district consolidation." This is district/choice consolidation, not rural closure specifically, so caution in generalizing to small-village contexts. Source: "School choice with independent versus consolidated districts" — https://arxiv.org/pdf/2006.13209

**Finding 8: International evidence — school closure can trigger self-reinforcing rural decline.** A Nordic (Finland) longitudinal study found "in rural communities the impacts of school closure have been slightly more negative suggesting a higher risk of self-reinforcing population decline," reinforcing that closure is not a neutral efficiency measure but a demographic accelerant. Source: "Primary school closures and population development – is school vitality an investment in the attractiveness of the (rural) communities or not?" ScienceDirect, 2021 — https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0743016721000115

**Finding 9: School-as-hub literature (Sweden) quantifies daily-life disruption.** A Swedish time-geography case study of two closed rural schools found that "when local schools are closed, the management of these daily arrangements is stretched even further as families face longer distances to services," and that the school functions as "the hub of activities where social networks are created and strengthened." Source: "Village schools as a hub in the community," ScienceDirect, 2020 — https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0743016718310659

**Finding 10: OECD literature review flags mixed evidence on community/economic effects.** OECD Working Paper No. 106 notes claims that small rural schools "can have a positive economic impact by partly hindering the tendency of loss of young economically active adults through migration," and cites a New York study finding "housing values were higher and municipal structures more developed in small villages with schools, than in villages without them" — but flags this as correlational, not causal. Also: OECD reports that in most member countries "only 20 percent or less of children up to the age of 14 live in rural areas," creating "unsustainable" excess capacity in rural school networks. Source: "School Size Policies: A Literature Review," OECD Education Working Papers No. 106 — https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2014/11/school-size-policies_g17a2568/5jxt472ddkjl-en.pdf

---

**Uncertainty flag for structured phase:** I found no Hungary-specific quantitative study directly measuring travel-time burden or transportation cost increases from Hungarian rural school closures (only international analogues — China, Sweden, Finland, South Korea). This is a genuine evidentiary gap that should be marked explicitly rather than filled by analogy in the final claims.
