# Critique: equity_checker

## S1.equity_impact
Objection: The equity_impact section asserts that the status-quo (multigrade + phase-out) will 'entrench' between-school segregation via selective exit and deliver lower-quality instruction to the least mobile pupils, but it fails to quantify who is actually reached vs left out or to model the selection mechanism it invokes. Concretely: it cites OFI parental-education gaps and the existence of ~300 'ghetto schools' but provides no settlement-level mapping showing (a) what fraction of rural pupils can realistically 'exit' to church/urban schools, (b) which specific villages would therefore concentrate disadvantage, or (c) the projected change in school composition under the scenario vs alternatives. Without that distributional mapping, the claim reads as plausible but untested causation rather than a demonstrated equity outcome (unsupported vs. asserted). (speculative)
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add a settlement-level distributional analysis and counterfactual simulation: use school-choice microdata (or proxy household/commute data) to estimate exit probabilities by SES and Roma-status, produce maps/tables showing which settlements would see compositional shifts under S1 over 5 years, and report the share of pupils who would remain 'trapped' in potentially low-quality multigrade settings. If microdata are unavailable, run sensitivity scenarios (low/medium/high exit rates) and attach explicit policy mitigations (e.g., mandatory school-choice regulation, targeted retention scholarships, monitoring of Roma concentration thresholds) tied to trigger values from the simulation.

## S2.equity_impact
Objection: The equity_impact claim that upper-grade centralization shifts the travel burden onto lower-income and Roma families is directionally plausible but under-specified and lacks the household-level evidence needed to design equitable mitigation. Concretely: the scenario cites broad dropout-rate disparities and generalized transport-cost anecdotes (Délegyháza) but does not (a) quantify per-household transport cost as a share of income for affected families, (b) map expected commute-time increases for origin settlements, or (c) estimate how many pupils would face >30-minute commutes (the cited threshold) and the consequent attendance/dropout risk. Without these inputs the scenario cannot show whether statutory transport guarantees as currently conceived will reach the most vulnerable or how much subsidy is required.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Require an equity-first transport impact assessment before endorsing structural centralization: (1) produce origin-destination commute-time and cost matrices for candidate catchments, disaggregated by household income and Roma-status; (2) estimate the number of pupils exceeding key thresholds (>30 minutes, cost >X% of household income) and model expected attendance/dropout effects using the cited commute-outcome elasticities; (3) specify transport-subsidy design (per-route subsidy, means-tested student fares, insurer for route cancellations) with projected budget lines tied to the assessed needs; and (4) include a pre-transfer pilot that conditions any rollout on the pilot meeting concrete attendance and equity targets for Roma and low-income pupils.

## S3.equity_impact
Objection: The equity_impact section rightly flags administrative-capacity exclusion risk for poorest municipalities under network/association-run models but leaves the diagnosis operationally vague: it does not define how to identify the municipalities most likely to be excluded, nor what specific capacity gaps exclude them (grant-writing, payroll, compliance), nor minimum support levels to prevent exclusion. As written, the critique risks being a generic caution rather than a testable equity safeguard, making it hard to design pilots that ensure inclusion of the very villages the model aims to protect. (speculative)
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Replace the generic exclusion warning with concrete inclusion metrics and program design rules for pilots: (a) define municipal-capacity indicators (e.g., years-since-maintainer-experience, staff-FTEs for education admin, audited-budget shortfall, grant-success rate) and classify municipalities into tertiles; (b) mandate that early pilots prioritize the bottom tertile and pair each participating association-run school with a funded administrative hub (shared-service centre) providing payroll, procurement, and compliance support for a minimum 3–5 year window; (c) publish uptake and time-to-operationalize metrics disaggregated by poverty and Roma share, with a pre-specified remedial funding tranche if bottom-tertile uptake < X% after year 2.

## S5.equity_impact
Objection: S5's equity_impact correctly warns that an advisory registry could be co-opted to legitimize inequitable closures, but it stops short of specifying the governance and data safeguards necessary to prevent that exact co-option. The scenario does not specify (a) legal constraints that would prevent tankerületi központs from using the registry selectively, (b) how Roma/non-Roma disaggregation can be legally and ethically published given privacy risks, or (c) the independent enforcement mechanism that would translate transparency into accountability. Without these concrete design elements, the registry risks transparency-without-accountability that harms the poorest settlements most.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Redesign the registry proposal to include binding accountability and privacy-safe disaggregation: (1) legislate clear limits on how registry outputs can be used in closure decisions (e.g., closures may not proceed unless statutory thresholds and independently-audited impact assessments are satisfied); (2) establish an independent oversight body with standing power to suspend closure approvals that contradict registry-based thresholds and to publish reasoned decisions; (3) include Roma/non-Roma and poverty-tier disaggregation using privacy-preserving methods (small-cell suppression, differential privacy or aggregated ranges) and a legal data-governance protocol approved by the data-protection authority to prevent misuse; (4) secure a named budget line for registry operation and independent audits so the registry cannot be underfunded and then selectively invoked.
