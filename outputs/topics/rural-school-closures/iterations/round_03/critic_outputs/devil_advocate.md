# Critique: devil_advocate

## S1.assumptions
Objection: The scenario rests on the assumption that multigrade pedagogy can be scaled in Hungarian rural schools without systemic teacher-training reform or explicit teacher-supply remedies. This is a concrete collapse point because the dossier's own evidence shows acute rural teacher shortages and an aging workforce (half of primary teachers 50+, only 7.2% under 30) and labels the international multigrade-effect evidence only 'moderate'. The 'moderate' international systematic-review tag does not support equivalence of outcomes in contexts with severe staffing shortages and high unqualified-teacher rates; the cited evidence conditions effectiveness on substantial in-service training and sustained staffing — conditions the scenario otherwise assumes will materialize informally. If the teacher-supply assumption fails, multigrade classrooms become pedagogically ineffective and the scenario's goal (keeping schools open without major harm) collapses.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Replace the implicit assumption with explicit, testable teacher-supply commitments: (a) produce a costed teacher-supply model projecting vacancies, retirements, and recruitment under S1 over 5–8 years; (b) fund and legislate mandatory rural-recruitment incentives (salary/top-up + housing) sized to close the modeled gap, with a clear timeline and procurement of X extra trainees per year; (c) require a 2–3 year multigrade pilot in representative rural schools with matched comparison groups and pre-specified fidelity and student-outcome thresholds that must be met before scaling. Tie continuation of multigrade rollout to achieving those pilot metrics and to measurable hires/retention targets, rather than assuming adequacy.

## S2.mechanism
Objection: S2 treats Poland's 1999 gimnazjum introduction as a straightforward transferable template and labels that precedent '[evidence: strong]', but this is misleading: Poland's reform was politically reversed in 2017–2019 and the international literature on net academic/equity effects is explicitly contested. The scenario therefore overstates transferability and the strength of the Polish evidence. It omits the concrete mechanisms behind Poland's reversal (political backlash, contested outcomes, and context-specific governance and demographic factors), and it assumes receiving-site quality and transport feasibility without national-scale cost or safety data. If Poland's template is not transferable, the scenario's expected administrative and pedagogical gains may not materialize and could generate large commute/equity harms instead.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Reclassify the Polish precedent as contested and make the mechanism conditional: (a) add a comparative appendix analyzing why Poland reversed the reform (political drivers, measured outcomes) and how Hungary differs on governance and settlement geography; (b) require a Hungarian pilot that centralizes upper grades in a small set of matched regions with pre-registered outcome measures (achievement, attendance, commute time) and a 3–5 year follow-up before any national roll-out; (c) mandate pre-implementation costed transport guarantees and capacity audits for receiving ISCED2 schools (bus-route costing, safety audits, and teacher-quality projections) and include legal entrenchment or statutory protections to reduce the political-reversal risk identified in the dossier.

## S3.assumptions
Objection: S3 assumes Hungary's plural-maintainer legal recognition and the existence of association-run precedents make inter-municipal/networked or NGO-run models feasible, but this ignores the dossier's own evidence that post-2013 tankerületi centralization removed municipal maintainer authority and that the 2023 law lets churches claim public buildings. The concrete flaw is underestimating legal and asset-access barriers and the administrative-capacity gap created by nationalization: recognizing plural maintainers on paper is not the same as being able to transfer maintainership, assets, or operating-capacity to associations at scale. If the legal/asset-capacity barrier is not resolved, network/association models will be blocked or only work in already-capable municipalities, reproducing exclusion rather than enabling universal retention.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Make legal and asset-transfer feasibility a precondition for the model: (a) commission a binding legal feasibility and asset-audit report mapping which buildings, licences, and staff contracts could be transferred under current law and where new legislation is required; (b) draft and table explicit legal amendments (or model MOUs) that protect transferred assets and maintainers from unilateral reclamation (e.g., limits on the 2023 building-transfer mechanism), including transitional grandfathering for pilot sites; (c) attach a dedicated capacity-building and administrative-support fund for the poorest municipalities (defined by fiscal-capacity tier) and require that association-run pilots include legally binding staffing and asset-use agreements before subsidy eligibility is granted.

## S5.evidence_status
Objection: S5 labels the evidence for a national advisory registry and thresholds as 'weak' but then proposes legislating a binding statutory minimum-viability threshold and an institutional registry in its implementation steps. That is a direct inconsistency: weak international/transferred evidence does not justify a single, binding national threshold and risks creating perverse incentives or co-option by maintainers (a risk S5 itself flags). The dossier's own uncertainty notes that advisory registries can be used selectively; proposing binding statutory thresholds without Hungary-specific causal evidence or a tested registry will likely produce either legally contestable rules or arbitrary closures based on insufficiently validated metrics.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Convert the proposal into a phased, evidence-driven pathway: (a) pilot an independent, non-binding, disaggregated registry (settlement, Roma/non-Roma, poverty tier) for 2–3 years to test data flows, GDPR compliance, and decision-use patterns; (b) concurrently commission a Hungary-specific legal-feasibility and causal-evidence study (quasi-experimental where possible) to inform threshold-setting; (c) only consider a binding statutory threshold after the pilot demonstrates registry reliability and that thresholds predict harms/benefits robustly; (d) legislate accompanying accountability safeguards (independent oversight, appeal rights for parents, and mandatory pre-closure destination-school quality audits) to prevent registry co-option.
