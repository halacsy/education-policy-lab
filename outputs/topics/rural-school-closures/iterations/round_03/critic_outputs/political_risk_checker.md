# Critique: political_risk_checker

## S2.political_risks
Objection: The scenario cites Poland's 1999 structural template but fails to reckon with or mitigate the demonstrated political-reversibility risk: Poland's gimnazjum reform was reversed in 2017–2019, and S2 proposes large structural change without any entrenchment (statutory amendments, multi-year ring-fenced funding, or contractual protections). Its implementation steps call for feasibility, transport guarantees, and consultations but omit concrete legal or fiscal safeguards that would prevent a future government from reversing the reorganization and leaving receiving ISCED2 sites stranded. This is a substantive omission, not a procedural detail: comparative evidence shows structural school reorganizations are politically fragile unless backed by binding legal and budgetary commitments.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add explicit entrenchment measures to S2: (1) propose specific statutory amendments amending the Education Act to lock in the ISCED2 reorganization (e.g., multi-year minimum implementation period, supermajority or multi-party trigger for reversal); (2) require a ring-fenced multi-year budget line for receiving-site capital and transport (explicit HUF figures or formula and minimum 5–7 year guarantee) before transfers occur; (3) include template long-term contracts between Ministry/tankerület and receiving schools/municipalities guaranteeing funding and asset-use for a defined term with compensation clauses for unilateral cancellation; and (4) establish an independent closure-review tribunal or ombudsman standing that can enforce the statutory protections. Update implementation steps and timelines to require these legal/fiscal steps as preconditions to pupil transfers.

## S3.assumptions
Objection: S3 assumes central government will allow reallocation of maintainer authority or creation of an association-run legal category, but the dossier documents the opposite governance trend: post-2013 tankerületi centralization and the 2023 law enabling churches to claim public buildings demonstrate active coercive-reversal capacity. S3 therefore relies on an assumption that contradicts strong evidence of recent centralizing legal/political behavior and offers no concrete legal protections against asset seizure or maintainer-reversal. That gap directly undermines the model's feasibility in Hungary's current political-legal environment.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Revise S3 to treat central-government coercive capacity as a binding constraint and specify legal safeguards: (1) draft the exact statutory language (or model ministerial regulation) that would create an association-run maintainer category with guaranteed public-subsidy eligibility; (2) require ministry-issued multi-year maintainer licences/contracts for association-run schools that include explicit anti-seizure covenants and compensation clauses if buildings are reallocated; (3) build a compulsory pilot with Ministry-signed legal guarantees (e.g., 7–10 year guaranteed subsidy and protection from asset-transfer) for pilot sites before scaling; and (4) include contingency legal remedies (judicial/ombudsman standing, binding arbitration) to deter or remedy unilateral reversal. Make these legal-design tasks explicit in implementation steps and budget them in cost_categories.

## S5.political_risks
Objection: S5 correctly flags that a non-binding registry/threshold system can be co-opted, but it fails to propose concrete anti-co-option and budget-entrenchment mechanisms. The scenario both (a) calls for a binding statutory minimum in implementation steps and (b) elsewhere treats the registry as advisory — an internal inconsistency. More importantly, it does not specify how to protect the registry and its budget from future political capture (e.g., defunding, selective invocation, or misuse of disaggregated Roma data). Without specified legal standing, enforceable closure-review tied to registry thresholds, protected multi-year funding, and data-governance safeguards, the framework is likely to be used selectively to legitimize pre-determined closures rather than constrain them.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Resolve the inconsistency and harden S5 against political co-option: (1) make the registry legally independent (statutory creation of an independent agency or ombudsman mandate) with its budget set as a multi-year earmarked line in law (specify minimum 5–7 year funding commitment) to prevent annual defunding; (2) legislate enforceable closure-review procedures that require tankerülets to justify closures against registry thresholds and provide affected parties (parents, municipalities, NGOs) with legal standing and expedited appeal rights; (3) prescribe data-governance rules (anonymization, restricted-access protocols, penalties for misuse) particularly for Roma/non-Roma disaggregation, and publish an enforcement regime; (4) add a pre-launch pilot with legally binding non-reversal commitments for participating tankerülets to demonstrate neutral use of the registry. Update implementation_steps to require these entrenchment and oversight measures as prerequisites to public operation.
