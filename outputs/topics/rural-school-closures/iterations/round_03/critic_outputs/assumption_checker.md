# Critique: assumption_checker

## S1.assumptions
Objection: The scenario assumes teacher supply and pedagogical readiness for multigrade will remain adequate without systemic teacher-training reform, yet the dossier itself documents acute rural staffing shortfalls (half of primary teachers are 50+, only 7.2% under 30) and 2–3x higher unqualified-teacher rates in disadvantaged rural schools. This directly contradicts the scenario's viability premise: the effectiveness claim for multigrade rests on '[when paired with adequate teacher training]' (evidence: moderate), but S1 offers no concrete plan or quantified gap-closure for that training, making the assumption unsupported and optimistic.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Make multigrade viability conditional and explicit: (a) replace the assumption with a requirement that a funded teacher-training and recruitment package be in place before large-scale multigrade rollout, including targets (e.g., % of rural teachers certified in multigrade pedagogy within 4 years) and cost estimates; (b) quantify the current certification/training gap using the dossier teacher-age and qualification data and model the required annual training throughput to meet targets (accounting for the ~4-year pipeline lag); (c) add an implementation precondition that pilot multigrade programmes with matched-comparison schools and fidelity audits demonstrate comparable outcomes in Hungary before scaling.

## S3.assumptions
Objection: S3 assumes central government would permit reallocation of maintainer authority or create a new legal category enabling inter-municipal/association-run schools, but the dossier documents the opposite legal-politico reality: post-2013 tankerületi centralization is strong (evidence: strong) and the 2023 law allowing churches to claim public buildings illustrates active recent centralization/coercive-asset-transfer capacity. The scenario therefore underestimates the legal and political barrier and treats a politically unlikely policy change as a feasible assumption without evidentiary support.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Revise to treat legal/devolution changes as an explicit, high-bar precondition with a concrete pathway: (a) specify the exact legislative amendments needed (e.g., amendment to Nkt. to create an 'association-run' maintainer statutory category and limits on asset-transfer powers); (b) propose an achievable short-term alternative path that works within current law (e.g., targeted pilots using church or foundation maintainers where politically feasible, or formal memoranda of understanding with the Ministry authorizing time-limited pilots); (c) include a political-risk mitigation plan (stakeholder mapping, ministerial sign-off, and binding pilot-protection clauses) and a timeline with milestones for securing legal authorization before national scale-up.

## S2.mechanism
Objection: S2 uses Poland's 1999 gimnazjum template as a transfer model (evidence: strong) but insufficiently accounts for two crucial facts the dossier highlights: Poland later reversed the reform (2017–2019) and the international literature is contested on net academic/equity benefit. S2 therefore overstates transferability without (a) addressing why Hungary would not face the same political reversal risk, and (b) providing concrete transport-capacity and destination-school-quality preconditions even though rural bus cancellations and extreme commutes are documented. The scenario's mechanism lacks the preconditions needed to avoid the very failure modes it cites.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Amend the mechanism to explicitly treat the Polish template as conditional: (a) add a required legal-entrenchment or durable funding guarantee (e.g., multi-year statutory backing or constitutional-level clause) to reduce reversal risk, and model the fiscal implications of that entrenchment; (b) add a mandatory, quantified transport-safety-and-cost precondition (national audit of route capacity, per-route cost estimates, statutory funding formula) that must be met before transfers; (c) require a Hungary-specific pilot (matched villages) measuring achievement, attendance, and commute effects over at least 3–5 years before national rollout, and incorporate contingency plans if receiving schools do not demonstrably improve outcomes for displaced pupils.
