# Critique: evidence_checker

## S1.mechanism
Objection: Multiple mechanism claims are tagged as 'strong' for legal provisions ("Statutory összevont osztály" and "felmenő rendszer phase-out") but no primary legal citations, ministerial guidance, or administrative-practice sources are provided. The scenario asserts statutory permissibility and operational clarity as if legally settled; the concrete flaw is that the dossier gives a 'strong' evidence grade without naming the exact statute/paragraph, decree, or administrative guidance that would justify that grade. Without a citation it is impossible to verify whether the law plainly enables these practices, whether they are discretionary, or whether implementing guidance (or case law/administrative practice) limits them.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Either (a) attach precise legal references (statute name and section, e.g., Nkt. article/paragraph or ministerial decree number) and any relevant administrative guidance or precedent that supports the claim, and then retain a 'strong' tag only if those sources unambiguously support the mechanism; or (b) if no clear primary legal text exists, downgrade the evidence tag to 'weak/unsupported' and reword to reflect legal ambiguity (e.g., 'permissible in practice where tankerület guidance allows, but no clear statutory mandate'). Add a short legal-interpretation note summarizing what the cited text actually permits vs requires.

## S1.mechanism
Objection: The demographic/staffing statistics are presented as 'strong' evidence ("half of Hungarian primary teachers are 50+, only 7.2% under 30, and unqualified-teacher rates are 2-3x higher in disadvantaged rural schools") but no data source, year, or table is named. These are precise numeric claims that materially underpin the scenario's feasibility judgment; the concrete flaw is lack of provenance — we cannot verify whether the percentages are current, refer to all primary teachers or only certain maintainers, or whether the disparity metric (2–3x) uses an agreed definition of 'unqualified' and 'disadvantaged rural'.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Provide the exact data source(s) (e.g., KSH/OFI dataset table and year, Ministry of Education staff-statistics report with page/table, or peer-reviewed analysis) and the year(s) of measurement. If the numbers come from multiple sources, disaggregate them (age distribution source vs. qualification-gap source) and, if necessary, revise the evidence tag to 'moderate' until the figures are corroborated. If up-to-date national statistics are unavailable, re-label the claim as 'model-based' or 'unsupported' and describe the uncertainty interval and implications for scenario feasibility.

## S4.mechanism
Objection: The scenario asserts a strong, specific longitudinal finding for Portugal — that the 2005–2007 21-student closures (1,615 schools) "showed no correlation...with improved educational success rates over a 2005–2021 longitudinal study" — using a 'strong' evidence tag but without naming the study, authors, methods, or whether the result is causal or descriptive. The concrete flaw is twofold: (1) a strong causal-null claim over a 16-year window requires citation to a rigorous longitudinal or quasi-experimental evaluation (which is not supplied); (2) absent that citation, the phrasing overstates certainty and may conflate descriptive time-series with causal analysis.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Either (a) provide the exact reference (author(s), title, year, DOI or URL) of the longitudinal/quasi-experimental evaluation that supports this null-finding and summarize its identification strategy and key effect-size estimates so readers can judge external validity; or (b) if no such rigorous study exists, downgrade the tag to 'contested' or 'weak' and rephrase to: 'Some evaluations of Portugal's closures did not find clear evidence of improved educational outcomes; findings are mixed and depend on method and horizon.' Where possible, separate short-term vs long-term outcome evidence and explicitly note whether analyses control for selection and confounding.
