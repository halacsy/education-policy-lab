# Critique: feasibility_checker

## S1.implementation_steps
Objection: The timeline and sequencing for multigrade rollout and teacher upskilling is infeasible given Hungary's documented teacher-supply constraints: the implementation_steps call for launching in‐service multigrade modules and rural recruitment incentives with a 1–4 year timeline, yet the synthesis and S1 evidence note an aging workforce (≈50% of primary teachers 50+, only 7.2% <30) and acute rural shortages (16,000 unfilled positions cited in the dossier). The plan assumes training produces sufficient qualified rural teachers within 4 years and that tankerületi offices have bandwidth to ‘formally authorize and fund’ many micro-scale multigrade plans in year 1–2; in reality the 4-year pipeline lag plus high vacancy rates mean the scenario cannot reliably staff and quality-assure multigrade classes at scale on this schedule (internal inconsistency with S1’s own ‘4-year pipeline’ note).
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Recast implementation_steps into a staged, capacity-constrained rollout: (a) commit to a 6–8 year national multigrade scale-up timeline aligning with the four-year initial training pipeline plus 2–4 years of retention incentives; (b) fund and run 12–24 month intensive pilots (n≥30 schools) with full funded substitute/mentoring teams before any statewide mandate; (c) ring-fence dedicated national funding for rural recruitment incentives (housing + salary top-ups) and guarantee at least 3 years of retention payments per hire; (d) add tankerületi administrative-capacity grants (one-off) to hire/second a defined number of staff (education-planning officers) responsible for multigrade planning and compliance monitoring; (e) include explicit contingency triggers (vacancy rate >X% in pilot region → pause further rollouts) and published staffing metrics (qualified teacher-per-multigrade-class) as preconditions for broader scaling.

## S2.implementation_steps
Objection: The procurement/physical-capacity and transport-guarantee steps are under-timed and under-specified: S2 schedules feasibility/procurement for candidate ISCED2 towns in year 1–3 and statutory transport guarantees in year 2–4, but the scenario itself flags ‘not compressed’ procurement and cites examples (Délegyháza route cost ~35 million HUF/year) showing high transport costs and canceled rural services. The plan lacks a credible plan to ensure receiving-school capacity (construction/refit) and funded, regulated transport exist before transfers begin. In practice, constructing/retrofit procurement, safety-certification, and union-negotiated teacher redeployments commonly require 4–7 years and significant capital appropriation; the current timeline risks pupil transfers before capacity, safety, or funded transport are assured. (speculative: exact 4–7yr range is a plausible industry norm based on the scenario’s own 'not compressed' caveat.)
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Revise into a conditional, capacity-first schedule: (a) extend feasibility+procurement timeline to year 1–6 for each candidate receiving site, with mandatory 'receiving-capacity certification' (facility safety, teacher staffing, specialist-subject coverage) completed and publicly published before any pupil transfer; (b) create a national rural-transport fund with multi-year committed appropriations and ministerial rules (route-safety, max-commute times) and tie closure approvals to proof of funded routes for affected pupils; (c) require negotiated, signed redeployment/compensation agreements with teacher unions before closure approvals, and budget one-off redeployment/severance costs in the capital plan; (d) pilot transfers only where receiving sites are already available or can be upgraded within 18 months with pre-authorized capital; (e) publish unit-cost estimates (per-route and per pupil) for scale-up to avoid underfunded transport assumptions.

## S3.implementation_steps
Objection: The legal and administrative preconditions for association-run or inter-municipal network pilots are understated: S3 assumes a 1–3 year legislative window to create a new association-run/network-school category and pilots in years 2–5, but the dossier documents a post-2013 centralization that removed municipal maintainer authority and a political environment where the state can reassert control (2023 building-transfer law). The plan omits the concrete administrative-capacity deficit at municipal level (most municipalities lost school-running capacity since 2013) and therefore underestimates the time and funded support needed to build governance, grant-management, and compliance systems for non-state maintainers. Without explicit legal-pilot authorizations and sizable capacity-building grants, pilots will either be limited to already-capable municipalities or fail in poorer, high-need locations.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Replace the straight legislative-first approach with a pragmatic, legally-protected pilot pathway: (a) begin with an administrative-capacity audit (months 1–9) of candidate municipalities and associations; (b) secure ministerial-level pilot exemptions (ministerial decree) that temporarily authorize a small set (n≥10, stratified by municipal capacity) to operate as subsidized association-run schools without full legislative overhaul, protecting them from unilateral asset reclamation for the pilot duration; (c) allocate dedicated multi-year capacity-building grants (project management, compliance officers, procurement/legal support) and shared back-office services (finance/payroll) to under-capacity municipalities before pilot start; (d) extend pilot duration to 5 years with pre-registered continuation/scale criteria tied to objective metrics (financial viability, teacher retention, inclusion outcomes); (e) concurrently draft but do not rely on full legislation — use pilot evidence to inform parliamentary debate and to build political cover.

## S5.implementation_steps
Objection: The proposal to legislate a binding minimum-viability threshold in year 1–2 and to build a full disaggregated closure/outcome registry by year 2–5 underestimates legal lead times, data-protection review, and the political fragility noted in the dossier (weak evidence and low confidence in cross-administration political will). S5 itself notes comparable Hungarian transparency projects have taken multiple years and that disaggregating by Roma/non‑Roma risks controversy requiring strict governance. The step presumes rapid cross-ministerial budget-line designation and an independent oversight body, but provides no concrete plan to secure durable funding or to satisfy GDPR-equivalent DPA review — making the timeline and binding-legislation aspiration unrealistic in the stated window. (speculative: exact length of data-protection review depends on authority practices.)
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Adopt a cautious, evidence-building first phase before binding legislation: (a) commission an immediate legal-feasibility and data-protection assessment with the national data-protection authority and KSH (months 1–6) to define permissible disaggregation and anonymization protocols; (b) implement a limited pilot registry (year 1–3) for a subset of indicators and a limited set of settlements with explicit parental/community consent and legally-reviewed safeguards; (c) secure an explicit multi-year budget line (3–5 years) before promising binding thresholds; (d) condition any move to binding statutory thresholds on pilot results and an inter-ministerial memorandum of understanding committing funding continuity and independent oversight (ombudsman/auditor) to reduce risk of co-option; (e) if pilot demonstrates legal/compliance feasibility and neutral use, draft binding legislation in year 3–5 informed by pilot outputs rather than assuming immediate year 1–2 passage.
