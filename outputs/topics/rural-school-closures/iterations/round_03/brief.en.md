# Policy brief — The Future of Rural Primary Schools

Hungary's village primary schools face accelerating demographic collapse, a rural teacher shortage, and a governance system that concentrated closure authority at the state level after 2013. Twelve expert analyses and a stress test of ten stakeholder perspectives converge on a shared empirical core but diverge sharply on what follows from it. This brief lays out five policy scenarios, what evidence supports or undermines each, where experts and stakeholders genuinely disagree, and the value choices that no amount of further research will resolve on its own.

## Scenario key (full detail: scenarios.en.md)
- S1 — S1 — Status quo plus incremental adaptation: keep the village school open via multigrade classes and phased enrollment decline, without a national framework.
- S2 — S2 — Structural grade reorganization: lower grades stay in the village, upper grades centralize in a larger town (Polish gimnazjum-style template).
- S3 — S3 — Networked retention: inter-municipal clustering, association/church-run schools, or multi-service hubs to keep village schools viable.
- S4 — S4 — Consolidation with mitigation: closure as the default, paired with statutory transport guarantees and targeted (TEIP-style) support.
- S5 — S5 — National systemic framework: transparent viability thresholds, a closure/outcome registry, and standardized teacher training to inform (not dictate) local decisions.

## What we know
- Hungary's birth rate and rural child population are collapsing sharply (births fell from 110,800 in 2015 to 77,600 in 2025), structurally reducing primary-school enrollment demand regardless of policy choice. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Rural and disadvantaged-area schools face disproportionately severe teacher shortages and an aging, undersupplied workforce: half of Hungarian primary teachers are 50+, only 7.2% are under 30, and unqualified-teacher rates run 2-3x higher in disadvantaged rural schools. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Cross-national quasi-experimental evidence (Denmark, Finland, China) shows rural school closures causally depress local population, with effects often emerging only 5-6 years after closure — but no equivalent Hungary-specific causal study exists. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Hungarian law and current government policy formally prefer phase-out, mergers, and local/case-by-case solutions over abrupt mass closure. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- No authoritative statutory minimum enrollment threshold for launching a single-grade class exists in Hungarian law; the widely-cited ~14-pupil figure is unconfirmed community knowledge, not legal text. [fact] (evidence: weak)
- Hungarian parents currently hold only non-binding 'interest injury' (érdeksérelem) standing, not enforceable legal injury, to challenge a school-closure decision; closure remains the maintainer's exclusive legal prerogative. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Portugal's TEIP targeted-support programme reduced dropout and failure rates in targeted schools but failed over 15 years to close the overall performance gap with non-TEIP schools — a documented partial success and partial failure of the compensatory model S4 relies on. [fact] (evidence: contested)
- Poland's 1999 gimnazjum reform (the structural template for S2) was later reversed (2017-2019) without clear evidence of net academic benefit. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Rural per-student transport costs run approximately 40% higher than urban costs, with marginal per-rider cost 2.5x urban levels; a single documented route (Délegyháza) cost ~35 million HUF/year for ~50 students. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Hungary's post-2013 tankerületi központ centralization removed municipal maintainer authority over most rural primary schools, creating a governance barrier to inter-municipal or association-run network models (S3) not present in the Polish or French comparators at the time of their adoption. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Nearly 300 'ghetto schools' (over 50% Roma composition) exist in Hungary, three-quarters of them in villages, reflecting between-school segregation driven partly by higher-SES/non-Roma family exit to church or urban schools. [fact] (evidence: strong)

## What we consider likely
- Multigrade classrooms can deliver adequate educational quality only when paired with sufficient teacher training, resources, and institutional support; implementation quality likely matters as much as the model itself. [estimate]
- Transport/commute burden from school consolidation likely imposes independent, measurable costs on rural families, disproportionately affecting lower-income households and widening existing dropout gaps. [estimate]
- A non-binding, advisory national registry and threshold framework (S5) is likely to be selectively invoked to legitimize pre-determined closure decisions rather than genuinely inform them, absent independent audit and binding force. [estimate]
- Parental and local mobilization can sometimes reverse or block specific closure/merger decisions, but its effectiveness likely depends unevenly on local political and fiscal capacity, meaning the poorest, least-organized villages are least likely to succeed. [estimate]
- Association-run or networked school models are likely to reproduce a two-tier outcome — better-resourced villages networking successfully, the poorest excluded — unless dedicated, ring-fenced capacity-building funding targets the lowest-capacity municipalities. [estimate]

## Where experts disagree
### Is rural school closure/phase-out economically and demographically inevitable, or a policy choice with a weak net-savings case?
- **demography, school_network_planning**: Given the birth-rate collapse (-30% in a decade) and specific micro-regions already below viable cohort size, phase-out closures of small-village schools are economically and demographically inevitable within 5-10 years absent radical service redesign. [value] Why: Grounded in national fertility/enrollment trend data and settlement-level cohort counts (e.g., Bélapátfalva 55, Tab 67 births/year) showing structural, not cyclical, demand collapse.
- **education_finance** (minority): The financial case for closing rural primary schools is weak: transport, capital-construction, and lagged demographic costs typically exceed the salary savings from consolidation, and these aggregate costs fall on families and communities rather than the education budget. [value] Why: Grounded in cost-accounting evidence (Délegyháza bus-route case, Danish 7.6pp population-decline effect) showing that once transport and capital costs are counted, net savings are minimal or negative.
### Does keeping small rural schools open protect equity, or does it risk entrenching segregation ('ghetto schools')?
- **hungarian_education_system, demography, educational_psychology**: Rural primary schools serve an irreplaceable equity and community-anchoring function for disadvantaged and commuting pupils in depopulating villages; closure without alternative provision will concentrate harm among the poorest and most minority students. [value] Why: Grounded in territorial-equity data (1,200+ schoolless settlements, higher parental-education gaps in small-settlement schools) and psychological evidence on commute-driven wellbeing harm.
- **equity_and_social_mobility** (minority): Without regulating school-choice and church-school exit, keeping small village schools open can paradoxically increase between-school segregation as higher-SES/non-Roma families leave, concentrating disadvantage in the schools left behind — nearly 300 'ghetto schools' exist, three-quarters in villages. [value] Why: Grounded in a distinct selection/exit mechanism (white flight, church-school competitive selection) rather than a resource-access mechanism: the harm arises from who leaves, not from the school's mere presence or absence.
### What is the primary mechanism of harm to children from closure/consolidation?
- **educational_psychology** (minority): The dominant harm channel is psychological and logistical: peer-reshuffling rank effects, persistent behavioral disruption, and commute burdens (over 30 minutes) independently degrade achievement, attendance, and wellbeing, concentrated among disadvantaged and minority pupils. [value] Why: Based on peer-effects and rank-effect literature (Big-Fish-Little-Pond Effect meta-analysis) and commute-time health/achievement studies — a distinct psychological/behavioral causal pathway.
- **demography, international_comparison, finnish_reform**: The dominant harm channel is community-structural: closure removes a social-infrastructure hub and accelerates self-reinforcing local population decline via housing-market and social-capital mechanisms that emerge years after closure. [value] Why: Based on quasi-experimental population-effect studies (Danish 7.6pp decline, Finnish 518-closure DID study) that isolate a demographic/community-infrastructure causal pathway.
### Does centralized (state/tankerület) governance help or hinder rural school survival compared to local/decentralized control?
- **legal_and_governance, hungarian_education_system**: Post-2013 state-level tankerületi központ concentration of maintainer authority reduced municipal and parental agency over closure decisions, leaving communities with only discretionary, non-binding appeal channels despite statutory safeguards existing on paper. [value] Why: Grounded in statutory analysis (Nkt. §102(2), catchment-designation procedure) showing decision authority is legally exclusive to the maintainer, with communities lacking formal legal remedy.
- **political_feasibility** (minority): Devolving maintainer responsibility back to municipalities is not straightforwardly beneficial: poorer rural municipalities explicitly cite inability to self-finance operating costs since the 2012 state takeover, meaning decentralization without ring-fenced co-financing could worsen rural school durability. [value] Why: Grounded in a 2026 mayoral survey showing fiscal-capacity divergence between wealthy and poor municipalities — a fiscal-capacity mechanism distinct from the legal-authority mechanism above.
### Are alternative models (multigrade, NGO/church maintainers, phase-out, exception pathways) a genuine solution or a stopgap that redistributes rather than resolves the underlying problem?
- **polish_reform, portuguese_reform, hungarian_education_system**: Alternative governance and pedagogical models (Polish NGO-run schools, rural funding weights, Portuguese case-by-case exception authorizations, multigrade classrooms with adequate investment) can meaningfully preserve rural school access and are evidence of closure not being inevitable. [value] Why: Grounded in comparative policy evidence showing measurable institutional survival outcomes (500-600 Polish social schools rescued, 37 Portuguese exceptional-authorization schools) attributable to specific funding-formula and legal-procedure design choices.
- **education_finance, legal_and_governance** (minority): Maintainer-change to church/private providers and centralized district structures redistribute costs and authority without resolving the structural per-pupil cost disadvantage of small schools. [value] Why: Grounded in fiscal-transfer evidence (church supplementary funding costs, 2023 asset-transfer law, tankerület system preserving cost problem) — a distributional/fiscal-accounting mechanism.

## What we don't know
- Whether multigrade pedagogy in Hungarian rural schools can achieve outcomes comparable to international evidence, given local teacher-training curricula and resource availability, is not yet tested. [fact]
- There is no rigorous Hungary-specific quasi-experimental or longitudinal study isolating the causal effect of rural school closure on population, achievement, or psychological outcomes; all quantitative effect sizes are imported from other countries. [fact]
- Whether a legally binding, pre-funded transport guarantee with verified route safety can actually be designed, costed, and operationally verified before any pupil transfer (S2, S4) is unknown; no such scheme has been costed at national scale. [fact]
- Whether Hungary's poorest, lowest-capacity municipalities would be systematically excluded from network/association models (S3) due to administrative burden is not empirically tested; no Hungarian pilot data yet exists. [fact]
- Whether an advisory (non-binding) national registry and threshold framework (S5) would change actual closure outcomes, or merely be selectively invoked to legitimize pre-decided closures, is explicitly unresolved even in the source evidence. [fact]
- Whether teacher-training pipeline expansion will materialize by 2028-2029 to relieve rural staffing pressure, or whether shortages will deepen despite policy intervention, remains an open trend question. [estimate]
- The magnitude and timing of long-run community population decline following a Hungarian rural school closure is unmeasured; Danish/Finnish effect sizes may not transfer directly to Hungary's settlement geography and municipal finance system. [assumption]

## What could be done
- **S1 — Status quo plus incremental adaptation**: Keep the village school open as long as feasible via multigrade classrooms and felmenő rendszer phase-out, negotiated locally without a binding national framework. Relies on statutory multigrade provisions and phased enrollment decline to avoid abrupt closure conflict, spreading adjustment over 3-8 years.
- **S2 — Structural grade reorganization**: Restructure so only lower grades (1-4/1-6) remain in the village while upper grades or a new ISCED2-type school consolidate in a larger town, following the Polish 1999 structural-reform template — later itself reversed.
- **S3 — Networked retention: clustering, association-run and multi-service hub models**: Retain village schools through inter-municipal pedagogical networks (French RPI-style), community/association-run publicly-subsidized schools (Polish model), or multi-service hubs combining school with other civic functions — requires overcoming Hungary's post-2013 governance centralization.
- **S4 — Consolidation with mitigation**: Close low-enrollment village schools by default and reassign pupils to a consolidated center school, paired with statutory transport guarantees, TEIP-style targeted support, and transparent cost/consultation rules to offset travel and equity harms.
- **S5 — National systemic framework**: Establish a national framework of transparent minimum-viability thresholds, cost-visibility rules, teacher-training standardization, and a closure/outcome registry to inform (not dictate) local decisions across any of the S1-S4 models.

## What each option costs
- **S1**: Recurring multigrade teacher-training and in-service upskilling costs, capital-maintenance backlog for aging small-school buildings, and teacher-retention incentive costs (Magyar Falu Program currently only 5 billion HUF for 100 settlements — a small fraction of documented need); risks entrenching lower-quality education for the most disadvantaged pupils who cannot exit to church/urban schools, since small-settlement schools already show 3-4x larger parental-education gaps. [assumption]
- **S2**: Major, currently unquantified capital investment for ISCED2 receiving-site capacity; recurring statutory transport costs (~40% above urban per-student rates); teacher severance/redeployment costs; shifts travel burden onto upper-primary pupils who are disproportionately Roma/low-income (58.7% vs 9.3% early-school-leaving gap), risking widened rather than narrowed dropout inequality without a pre-funded transport guarantee. [estimate]
- **S3**: Major, unquantified one-off legal/regulatory design cost to create a Hungarian association-run legal category; recurring rural per-pupil funding-weight subsidy and capacity-building costs targeted at the poorest municipalities; risks a two-tier outcome where administrative-capacity burdens exclude precisely the villages most in need, as Małopolska evidence shows 25% of NGO-run rural schools still fell below sustainability thresholds even with support. [estimate]
- **S4**: Major, unquantified capital investment for center-school expansion; recurring statutory transport and TEIP-style support costs; unmonetized community-level costs (property-value decline, tax-base shrinkage); ~20% of Portugal's ~4,000 closed schools remain vacant absent a repurposing mandate; disproportionately affects lower-income and Roma families who cannot easily relocate, and the compensatory mechanism (TEIP) is itself evidence-'contested' — reduced dropout but did not close the 15-year performance gap. [estimate]
- **S5**: Unquantified one-off registry-design and data-infrastructure build cost (comparable Hungarian transparency projects have taken multiple years); recurring registry-operation costs; unclear fiscal-responsibility allocation risking underfunding; a non-binding version risks producing transparency without accountability, particularly if not disaggregated by Roma/non-Roma and settlement-poverty tier. [estimate]

## What research could resolve
- Pilot multigrade programmes in selected Hungarian rural primary schools with matched comparison groups and implementation-fidelity audits would test whether multigrade pedagogy can achieve comparable outcomes under Hungarian conditions.
- A national household-level transport-cost and safety audit of routes serving villages under 2,000 population, cross-referenced with existing cancelled-route case data, would quantify whether a national transport guarantee is fundable and safe at scale.
- A Hungary-specific difference-in-differences study comparing closure and non-closure villages over a 10+ year window, tracking population, migration, and housing-price trajectories, would quantify the long-run community cost of closure that current international estimates only approximate.
- Pilot programme evaluation disaggregating uptake and outcome by municipal fiscal-capacity tier would show whether network/association models (S3) systematically exclude the poorest municipalities or can be designed to include them.
- Post-implementation tracking of tankerület closure decisions against S5's registry data and thresholds over 3-5 years, with independent audit of decision rationales, would resolve whether a non-binding framework changes outcomes or is selectively invoked.
- A legal feasibility analysis by education-law scholars of amending Nkt. §102(2) to create an enforceable parental appeal right would clarify whether binding closure-review procedures are achievable within existing Hungarian administrative law.

## What people must decide
- Whether closure/consolidation should ever be named and adopted as a de facto default policy, or whether phase-out/incremental language should remain the governing framework even where it defers rather than resolves an underlying decision — a political-honesty versus political-manageability tradeoff. [value]
- Whether the value of local community continuity and subsidiarity should take precedence over concentrated, potentially higher-quality subject-teacher provision at centralized sites — a genuine value conflict, not a technical question. [value]
- Whether Hungary is willing to legislate binding parental/community appeal rights that constrain maintainer discretion, accepting slower or contested closure decisions in exchange for procedural fairness — a deliberate tradeoff between administrative efficiency and family/community voice. [value]
- Whether public funds should be committed to reversing post-2013 centralization to enable association/church/network models (S3), given competing claims on scarce education budgets and genuine uncertainty about political will to sustain such a reversal across government cycles. [value]
- How much weight to give to the interests of children who cannot yet vote or organize (commute burden, peer disruption, teaching quality) versus the interests of adult stakeholders (teacher job security, municipal fiscal capacity, maintainer institutional autonomy) when these interests conflict — an irreducible tradeoff, not a research question. [value]

## What to verify with real stakeholders
- A1 (Multigrade teaching is demanding and specialized; support for keeping village schools open via multigrade classes should be conditional on funded, mandatory training and workload/pay adjustment, not assumed to work without investment.): policy_design_fixable — S1's implementation steps already name multigrade in-service training as a required action item (year 1-4), but the brief's cost/uncertainty sections confirm no funding commitment currently exists — this is a design gap, fixable by making training funding a binding, monitored precondition (as multiple stakeholder conditions demand) rather than an aspiration, before phase-out proceeds.
- A2 (Multigrade classes and phased enrollment respect subsidiarity and community continuity, letting local actors adapt gradually rather than facing abrupt closure.): value_conflict — This is a genuine value position (subsidiarity/continuity vs. teaching-quality risk) that evidence cannot fully resolve: whether multigrade classrooms stay staffable over a 2-3 year horizon is an open empirical question, but the underlying preference for gradual local control over top-down restructuring is a value choice about who should decide and how fast.
- A3 (Without a national framework, state tankerület offices could quietly favour their own schools over church-maintained schools in the same catchment during multigrade/phase-out negotiations.): needs_more_info — The dossier itself labels this pattern 'contested and largely unknown' — no audited catchment-level case comparisons currently exist showing tankerület offices have actually favoured their own schools. The claim is a plausible institutional-incentive inference, not an established pattern; it requires published, non-discretionary viability criteria and monitoring data before it can be confirmed or dismissed.
- A4 (Forcing upper-primary pupils into daily commutes to centralized ISCED2 sites (S2) risks child safety and continuity, given documented links between 30+ minute commutes and lower achievement/higher absenteeism, and real cases of cancelled rural bus routes.): policy_design_fixable — S2's own implementation steps already specify that transport guarantees must be legally codified before transfer takes effect — the fix (binding, pre-funded, independently verified transport with route-safety standards operational before any pupil movement) is a concrete design change, not a values dispute, though it requires costed, national-scale verification that does not yet exist.
- A5 (Teacher-union resistance to involuntary relocation without compensation is a real risk; consolidation (S2) should not proceed without binding, pre-negotiated redeployment terms including severance, retraining, housing/transport support, and a specialization-wage supplement.): policy_design_fixable — This is addressed through binding negotiation sequencing rather than an unresolvable conflict: the reciprocity round shows union representatives proposing that redeployment terms be negotiated as a precondition before consolidation is announced (following Portuguese precedent), which is a concrete, implementable design fix, not a value clash — though the 15-20% wage-supplement figure's transferability remains an open empirical question.
- A6 (Centralizing upper grades without a pre-funded, legally guaranteed transport scheme would widen the already documented Roma/non-Roma dropout gap, since rural transport is currently an unsubsidized, cancellation-prone municipal task.): evidence_answerable — The existing dropout gap and current transport precarity are documented facts; what remains open is the causal magnitude of the projected widening effect — this is answerable by combining the national transport-cost audit with tracked attendance/dropout data for any pilot centralization, converting a projection into a measured outcome.
- A7 (S2's ISCED2 consolidation model is built on Poland's gimnazjum template, which Poland itself later reversed without demonstrated net academic benefit, undermining the evidentiary basis for structural centralization.): evidence_answerable — Whether Polish conditions transfer to Hungary is an empirical, testable question — a Hungary-specific pilot with matched comparison villages, as multiple stakeholders demand, would directly answer whether the reversal's causes (political vs. academic) and outcomes replicate in the Hungarian context, rather than remaining a matter of unresolved value disagreement.
- A8 (Consolidation could improve access to qualified STEM/ICT teachers, but only if receiving ISCED2 sites are pre-certified for digital infrastructure and teacher capacity before any transfer.): policy_design_fixable — This is a verifiable precondition, not an intractable disagreement: pre-transfer certification of receiving-site digital infrastructure and ICT-teacher qualifications is a concrete administrative step (analogous to the pre-closure quality audits demanded elsewhere in the record) that can be built into implementation sequencing.
- A9 (Network/association-run models are a legitimate extension of Hungary's existing plural-maintainer legal framework, restoring local, church, and community responsibility away from centralized bureaucratic control.): value_conflict — Whether authority over children's education should sit closer to families/communities/churches or be coordinated centrally is a subsidiarity-versus-central-oversight value question; evidence on Polish precedent informs feasibility but cannot settle which governing philosophy Hungary should adopt.
- A10 (Without ring-fenced capacity-building funding targeting the poorest municipalities, network/association models (S3) risk a two-tier system excluding the lowest-capacity, often Roma-concentrated villages.): policy_design_fixable — This is a design flaw with a specified fix widely agreed across stakeholder clusters: dedicated, ring-fenced capacity-building funds prioritizing the lowest-capacity municipalities, with monitored uptake disaggregated by poverty tier — the Małopolska evidence shows the risk is real but not inevitable if funding design explicitly targets it.
- A11 (Governance experimentation (S3) should not shift administrative burden or employment uncertainty onto already-stretched teaching staff or the poorest municipalities' capacity; pilots need employment-continuity guarantees and dedicated administrative-capacity funding.): policy_design_fixable — Explicit employment-continuity guarantees and ring-fenced administrative-capacity funding for pilot design are concrete, implementable safeguards that directly address the named risk, though no pilot data yet exists to confirm they would work in practice at scale.
- A12 (Hungary's post-2013 centralization removed municipal authority and is a documented legal barrier that S3 must explicitly overcome through parliamentary legislation, not assume away.): needs_more_info — The legal barrier's existence is a settled fact, but whether a legislative fix is politically feasible, how long it would take, or whether partial administrative workarounds could achieve similar effect short of full legislation, remains unresolved and requires a concrete legislative proposal or feasibility timeline before this can move to a design solution.
- A13 (Portugal's TEIP compensatory mechanism (S4) is labelled 'contested' in the evidence itself — it reduced dropout but failed over 15 years to close the underlying performance gap, so relying on it to offset closure harm is an unverified promise.): irreducible_tradeoff — This names a genuine tradeoff: proceeding with closure trades a certain, immediate harm (loss of local school access) against an uncertain, partial mitigation (TEIP's documented dropout-reduction but gap-persistence record). No amount of design tweaking eliminates the fact that the compensatory mechanism's own 15-year track record shows only partial effectiveness — sequencing and audits can reduce but not eliminate this residual risk.
- A14 (Hungarian parents hold only non-binding 'interest injury' standing, not enforceable legal injury, to challenge a closure decision, treating families as having no real legal recourse against a maintainer's unilateral prerogative.): policy_design_fixable — A legal-feasibility analysis of amending Nkt. §102(2) to create an enforceable parental appeal right is a concrete, achievable legislative fix, though its actual effect on closure outcomes remains untested — the fix is available even though its efficacy is an open empirical question.
- A15 (Closure (S4) should not proceed without pre-verified destination-school quality, a legislated and pre-funded transport guarantee, and binding parental/union appeal rights established before, not concurrently with, any closure decision.): policy_design_fixable — This convergent, seven-cluster demand names a sequencing fix (pre-verification before, not after, closure) that is procedurally implementable, though the reciprocity round notes it requires resolving who funds pre-verification audits and what happens if destination schools fail verification — a design detail, not an unresolvable conflict.
- A16 (Displaced pupils internationally most often do not move to demonstrably stronger schools, and Portugal's 2005-2007 closures found no link between larger 'school centers' and better outcomes, undermining the core assumption that consolidation improves quality.): evidence_answerable — Whether Hungarian receiving schools would replicate this international pattern or diverge from it is directly testable via the mandated pre-closure quality audits of destination schools (capacity, teacher qualifications) already named as an S4 implementation step — this converts an assumption into a measurable precondition.
- A17 (Church maintainers already have a statutory 2023 building-claim mechanism, yet S4's mandatory repurposing-plan step names only the local municipality as responsible, omitting church and other plural maintainers as eligible partners.): communication_fixable — The 2023 building-claim mechanism already exists in statute and operates in parallel; the gap is that S4's implementation step does not explicitly reference or integrate it, which is a drafting/communication omission rather than a substantive policy or value conflict — naming church and other plural maintainers explicitly as eligible partners in the repurposing step would resolve it.
- A18 (Multigrade phase-out (S1) lets government offices manage rural school decline gradually without a single dramatic closure decision, matching a preference for incremental, low-conflict reform.): not_decision_relevant — Whether phase-out is politically more manageable than abrupt closure is a real observation about political dynamics, but it does not change which underlying scenario is chosen or what conditions (training funding, viability criteria) must accompany it — it describes a political-communication preference layered on top of S1, not a factor that shifts the substantive decision between scenarios.
- A19 (A governing majority that adopted incremental phase-out language to avoid mobilization dynamics would reject naming closure as the explicit default policy (S4), since political exposure would not be matched by full substantive justification.): not_decision_relevant — This describes a political-framing preference (avoiding the word 'closure') rather than a substantive difference in what happens to schools or families; whichever label is used, the same underlying implementation requirements (transport guarantees, quality audits, appeal rights named elsewhere in this brief) determine actual outcomes for affected communities — the naming choice does not change the decision that must be made.
- A20 (A national registry (S5) fills a named evidentiary gap and gives families predictable information, but a non-binding framework risks being selectively invoked to legitimize pre-decided closures, so it must be made legally binding with independent audit to be trustworthy.): policy_design_fixable — Multiple stakeholder clusters converge on the same concrete fix (binding force plus independent audit function), and this is directly implementable through legislative design — the dossier's own evidence confirms the risk under a non-binding regime is real, but does not establish that a binding regime with audit fully closes the gap either, so this remains design-fixable in principle even though its efficacy needs post-implementation verification.
- A21 (S5's registry disaggregation is currently specified only by settlement, Roma/non-Roma composition, and poverty tier; without adding maintainer type and digital-infrastructure/ICT-teacher-qualification indicators, church networks, digital-sector partners, and equity observers cannot verify equitable treatment.): needs_more_info — Whether additional disaggregation categories (maintainer type, digital-infrastructure indicators) are technically and legally feasible without breaching data-protection rules in small settlements (re-identification risk) is an unresolved technical question requiring a formal legal-feasibility and data-protection assessment before the registry's design can be finalized.
- A22 (An advisory, non-binding registry (S5) matches a governing preference for evidence infrastructure that improves consultation quality without ceding final closure-decision authority to a legally binding external standard.): value_conflict — Whether maintainers should retain full discretionary authority over closure decisions or be bound by an external, legally enforceable standard is a value question about where final authority over children's schooling should rest — evidence on Polish enforcement variation informs the debate but cannot determine which allocation of authority Hungary should choose.

## Where the red herrings are
- A18: Whether phase-out language is politically more 'manageable' than naming closure explicitly consumes significant discussion, but it does not change which conditions (funded training, viability criteria, transport guarantees) must accompany S1 regardless of framing — the underlying implementation requirements are identical either way.
- A19: Debate over whether the government should explicitly call S4 'closure as default' versus using softer language absorbs political attention but does not alter the substantive implementation requirements (transport, quality audits, appeal rights) that determine actual outcomes for affected families — the label does not change the decision.
