# Expert analysis: international_comparison

## Findings (evidence)
- In Denmark's Tønder municipality, difference-in-differences analysis of the simultaneous 2011 closure of 8 schools found a 7.6 percentage-point population decline over the following decade, with effects becoming statistically significant only after year six. [evidence: strong — Sørensen, Schmidt et al. 2021, Journal of Rural Studies 87]
- A Finnish nationwide study of 518 school closures (2011–2018, 66% rural) using difference-in-differences with genetic matching found a clear negative population effect of rural school closures on surrounding communities. [evidence: strong — Lehtonen 2021, cited in Sørensen et al. 2021 (Journal of Rural Studies)]
- In Denmark, school consolidation is linked to decline in individual test scores, with the negative overall effect driven specifically by school closings (approximately 5.9% of a standard deviation), rather than by mergers per se. [evidence: strong — Beuchert, Humlum, Nielsen & Smith 2018, Economics of Education Review]
- China's 2001 Rural Primary School Consolidation Program had a persisting negative effect on affected students' later migration and human-capital accumulation, with larger effects for girls and younger children at time of closure. [evidence: strong — Zhao, Yu et al. 2024, Journal of Asian Economics; Hannum, Liu & Wang 2021, Economic Development and Cultural Change]
- In a 2015 Hungarian Central Statistical Office territorial analysis, villages retaining a local school did not show uniformly better socioeconomic status than schoolless villages: they had superior demographic and infrastructural indicators but worse employment and welfare characteristics. [evidence: moderate — Academia.edu 2015 Hungarian CSO territorial data study, 'The Free School Bus: Helping or Worsening the Problem of Village Depopulation']
- Hungary has the highest level of socioeconomic school segregation in the EU as measured in PISA 2022, and 62% of Roma children aged 6–15 attend schools where all or most pupils are Roma, indicating that closures and catchment reorganization risk compounding existing segregation. [evidence: strong — EU Education and Training Monitor 2025 (Hungary country report); OECD PISA 2022 Results Country Note (Hungary)]
- In several countries, including Hungary, rural schools are on average better equipped in material resources than city schools, though city schools retain an edge in science-specific resources, according to OECD PISA and TALIS-based comparative analysis. [evidence: moderate — OECD Education Working Paper No. 196 (2019), Learning in Rural Schools]
- A 2025 systematic review of 40 international studies concludes that multigrade classrooms, when operated to minimum quality standards, can deliver high-standard education and contribute to reducing regional inequalities, but require increased investment in teacher training and dedicated stakeholder support. [evidence: moderate — MDPI Education Sciences (2025), Vol. 15(8):1052, multigrade teaching systematic review]
- Historically, Hungary's rural school network was kept largely intact until 2004 through state support targeted at small villages, but subsequent financial strain on small municipalities has intensified consolidation pressure. [evidence: moderate — Kovács & Farkas, Journal of Rural Studies; 'Rescuing a small village school in the context of rural change in Hungary']

## Interpretation
The cross-national quasi-experimental literature (Denmark, Finland, China) converges on a robust empirical pattern: rural school closures causally depress local population and, in multiple settings, individual student achievement, with effects often only emerging or intensifying years after closure—meaning short-run cost analyses systematically understate long-run costs. Hungary's own PISA and segregation profile (highest EU socioeconomic segregation, large Roma school concentration) means closures and catchment redesign carry unusually high equity risk: consolidation funnelling disadvantaged rural children into fewer urban or regional schools could deepen—not reduce—the segregation gradient Hungary already leads the EU in. Simultaneously, OECD evidence shows Hungarian rural schools are relatively well material-resourced, and that multigrade teaching can deliver high standards with sufficient investment, suggesting school closure is not the only policy lever. Targeted quality investment in retained small schools represents an evidence-backed alternative, not merely a sentimental preference.

## Assumptions
- Findings from Denmark, Finland and China are assumed structurally transferable to Hungary's settlement geography and municipal finance system, although institutional context (school governance, transport infrastructure, municipal capacity) differs materially and no Hungary-specific quasi-experimental (difference-in-differences) closure study was located. [assumption]
- PISA-based segregation measures are treated as a reasonable proxy for closure-related equity risk in primary education, although PISA measures age 15, not the primary-school population (grades 1–6) directly in scope. [assumption]
- The reported population and achievement effects are assumed to operate through a combination of travel distance, school consolidation, and peer effects; the separate causal weight of each mechanism remains unspecified in most studies. [assumption]

## Position
Given strong quasi-experimental evidence that rural school closures causally accelerate community population decline and depress student achievement in multiple countries, while Hungary already exhibits the EU's highest socioeconomic school segregation, closure decisions for villages under 2,000 inhabitants should be treated as high-equity-risk interventions requiring exhaustive evaluation and implementation of quality-investment alternatives—such as well-supported multigrade teaching models and adequately resourced small schools—before consolidation is pursued as a primarily cost-driven measure.

## Uncertainties
- No Hungary-specific quasi-experimental (difference-in-differences or instrumental-variable) study estimating the causal effect of rural school closures on local population or student achievement was identified; existing Hungarian evidence is correlational or qualitative. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A Hungary-specific difference-in-differences study exploiting temporal or spatial variation in closure timing across comparable municipalities, analogous to the Danish and Finnish research designs.)
- The relative causal contribution of increased travel distance versus school-size consolidation versus peer-composition changes to the observed negative effects remains unclear; this distinction matters significantly for policy design (e.g., school bus provision versus keeping small schools open). (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Mediation or decomposition analyses within existing difference-in-differences studies, isolating the direct effect of each pathway (travel, consolidation, peer effects) on achievement and migration outcomes.)
