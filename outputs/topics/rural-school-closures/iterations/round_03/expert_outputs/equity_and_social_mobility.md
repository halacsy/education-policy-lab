# Expert analysis: equity_and_social_mobility

## Findings (evidence)
- Small village schools that remain open, absent school-choice regulation, can paradoxically increase between-school segregation as higher-SES/non-Roma families exit to schools in other settlements ('white flight'), concentrating disadvantage in the schools left behind. [evidence: moderate — CEU Center for Policy Studies working paper (2020), https://cps.ceu.edu/sites/cps.ceu.edu/files/attachment/publication/3258/cps-working-paper-educ-social-selection-education-hungary-2020.pdf]
- Nearly 300 'ghetto schools' (over 50% Roma pupils) exist in Hungary, three-quarters of them in villages, showing rural small schools are the primary site of extreme ethnic/SES segregation, not urban ones. [evidence: moderate — Springer, 'Education of Roma and Educational Resilience in Hungary', https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-52588-0_6]
- Some small rural schools are 'resilient': in localities with restricted school-choice, they achieve better-than-predicted results for disadvantaged intakes, indicating closure is not a uniformly equity-improving response. [evidence: moderate — Springer, 'Education of Roma and Educational Resilience in Hungary', https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-52588-0_6]
- The expansion of church-run schools, which can select higher-performing students and receive stronger funding, is drawing non-Roma pupils away from rural state schools, deepening residual segregation among those who remain. [evidence: weak — Humanium, 'Children of Hungary', https://www.humanium.org/en/hungary/]
- In 2023, 58.7% of Roma 18-24-year-olds in Hungary were early school leavers versus 9.3% of non-Roma, with rural residence identified as a compounding risk factor for dropout. [evidence: moderate — EACEA Youthwiki Hungary 6.1, https://national-policies.eacea.ec.europa.eu/youthwiki/chapters/hungary/61-general-context]
- Cross-national quasi-experimental evidence (Denmark, Italy) shows school closures cause measurable local depopulation over the following decade, with effects sometimes only emerging significantly after five to six years — a reinforcing spiral relevant to who bears the long-run mobility cost. [evidence: moderate — Sørensen et al. 2021, Journal of Rural Studies, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0272775717306155; Di Cataldo & Romani 2024, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0166046224000887]
- International consolidation evidence on achievement is genuinely mixed: Chile found closures raised dropout risk by 49-68%, Denmark found short-run achievement losses that faded, while some other contexts showed no measurable harm. [evidence: contested — arXiv 2203.17101 review, https://arxiv.org/pdf/2203.17101]
- Ordinal rank effects can offset up to 40% of the peer-quality gains from moving disadvantaged pupils into larger, higher-achieving consolidated schools, meaning consolidation's benefit for non-selected/rural pupils is not simply proportional to peer-quality improvement. [evidence: moderate — Denning, Murphy & Weinhardt (2023), cited in edworkingpapers.com 'The Long Shadow of School Closures', https://edworkingpapers.com/sites/default/files/ai24-963.pdf]

## Interpretation
The equity picture is genuinely two-sided and cannot be resolved by a blanket close/keep-open rule. Keeping small rural schools open without addressing school-choice and church-school competition risks entrenching 'ghetto schools' and between-school segregation — the current Hungarian pattern shows disadvantage concentrating precisely in the villages closure advocates target. Yet closure carries its own distributional costs: cross-national evidence on population decline and mixed/negative achievement effects (especially for already-disadvantaged pupils facing rank-effect losses) suggests consolidation is not automatically equity-improving, and its benefits depend heavily on transport quality, receiving-school capacity, and whether flight to selective/church schools continues in parallel. Any policy that ignores the segregation mechanism (exit options for higher-SES families) while focusing only on enrollment thresholds will likely reproduce or worsen the SES gradient regardless of whether schools stay open or close.

## Assumptions
- That findings from Denmark, Italy, and Chile on closure effects generalize reasonably to the Hungarian rural context, despite differences in transport infrastructure, settlement density, and social safety nets. [assumption]
- That current school-choice/exit patterns (white flight, church-school expansion) will persist under any future policy scenario unless explicitly regulated. [assumption]

## Position
Because between-school segregation (via school-choice exit to non-village and church schools) is already the dominant equity problem in Hungarian rural education, any closure-or-keep-open decision that does not simultaneously regulate school-choice and transport access will fail to reduce, and may worsen, the SES/ethnic gradient regardless of which option is chosen.

## Uncertainties
- Whether Hungary-specific quantitative estimates exist for the population/depopulation effect of rural primary school closures (analogous to the Danish 7.6pp finding), as opposed to inferring from other countries. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A Hungary-specific longitudinal study linking school closure dates to settlement-level population and migration data over a 10+ year window.)
- How much of the 'resilient small school' effect (Finding 5) is due to genuine school-level quality versus selection artifacts in which pupils remain when exit options are restricted. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Value-added analyses comparing resilient rural schools to matched non-resilient rural schools controlling for family background and exit-option availability.)
- The net direction of the rank-effect versus peer-quality trade-off (Finding 11) specifically for Hungarian rural-to-consolidated-school transitions, which has not been separately estimated. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A Hungarian administrative-data study tracking pupils who transitioned from closed village schools to consolidated schools, comparing achievement and self-reported wellbeing before/after.)
