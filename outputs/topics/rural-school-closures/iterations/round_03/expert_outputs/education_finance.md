# Expert analysis: education_finance

## Findings (evidence)
- Per-pupil costs in Hungarian small village primary schools are structurally higher than in larger schools because fixed costs (full subject-teacher rosters for upper grades) do not scale with declining enrollment. [evidence: strong — PPKE oktatás finanszírozása reformja]
- State co-financing of Hungarian primary education dropped from 80–90% in the 1990s to approximately 50% in small village schools by the early 2000s, shifting financial exposure to municipal budgets with declining local revenue. [evidence: strong — ScienceDirect: Rescuing a small village school in rural change in Hungary]
- School transport is typically organized and funded as a voluntary municipal task without central state subsidy; rising fuel and overhead costs in 2023–2024 led several Hungarian settlements to cancel bus services entirely. [evidence: strong — TASZ: Panaszmintát készítettünk az iskolabuszok újraindítása érdekében (Jan 16, 2024)]
- A single village school-bus route in Délegyháza required an estimated 35 million HUF annually to restart, serving roughly 50 students; discontinuation forced children to walk up to 1.5 hours daily on unlit roads. [evidence: strong — Index: Megszűnt az iskolabusz Délegyházán (Sept 26, 2024)]
- International comparative studies (US, Denmark, Poland) consistently find that school consolidation savings from staff reductions are often offset by capital construction costs and increased pupil-transport expenses, yielding minimal or negative net savings. [evidence: moderate — Witham ERIC ED415036; Ellegood/Riley/Berg 2024 rural transport cost analysis]
- Rural school-bus operating costs per student are approximately 40% higher than urban costs; marginal cost per additional rural rider is 2.5 times urban marginal cost; per-mile cost in rural districts is roughly 10 times higher than urban. [evidence: moderate — Ellegood/Riley/Berg 2024: Rural School Bus Routing and Scheduling (arXiv)]
- Dismantling Hungary's post-2013 Klebelsberg Center district-school system (tankerület) and returning full financial autonomy to individual schools would require creating hundreds or thousands of new administrative posts and compliance structures, likely at higher unit cost than the current centralized model. [evidence: weak — Eduline: Pukli István, Nem azt kell először eldöntenünk (June 22, 2026)]
- A quasi-experimental Danish study of eight village school closures in 2011 found that local population would have been 7.6 percentage points more favorable over the subsequent decade if the schools had remained open, with the effect emerging after year six via social capital and housing market mechanisms. [evidence: moderate — ScienceDirect: Do rural school closures lead to local population decline? (Danish quasi-experimental study)]
- Hungary's Klebelsberg Center secured full state funding (1.91 billion HUF) for 46 electric school buses (43 mini + 3 large) by April 2027 under the Green Bus Program (Zöld Busz Program), signaling a partial shift toward centrally funded transport as an alternative to closure. [evidence: strong — Klebelsberg Center press release (June 7, 2024)]
- Hungarian territorial analysis found that villages retaining a primary school had somewhat better demographic outcomes but worse employment and welfare indicators than villages without a school, complicating simplistic 'school presence equals local prosperity' assumptions. [evidence: moderate — Academia.edu: Shrinking, growing or overburdened? Territorial patterns in child demographics (2021 data)]
- Polish large-N study (42,400 localities, 13,100 schools) found school presence closely tied to locality population, particularly in the 400–700 inhabitant range, with diseconomy-of-scale effects flattening above approximately 4,100 residents. [evidence: moderate — ResearchGate discussion: Do rural school closures lead to local population decline? (Polish data)]
- The current Hungarian centralized tankerület system shifted school maintenance to the state but did not resolve the structural per-pupil cost disadvantage of small village schools. [evidence: moderate — Eduline opinion analysis; PPKE finanszírozás reform]
- School transport is the largest variable cost in rural education access and cannot be reliably reduced without either closing schools or requiring families to bear uncompensated travel burdens (walking, unpaid caregiver time). [evidence: moderate — TASZ transport documentation; Index Délegyháza case; Ellegood/Riley/Berg 2024]
- Small school closures impose unequally distributed costs on rural families: children in outlying (tanya) settlements face the largest transport burdens, while closure decisions are made by district or national authorities who do not bear local consequences. [evidence: moderate — TASZ housing/education access analysis; Index Délegyháza reporting]

## Interpretation
Rural primary school viability in Hungary is constrained by structural cost diseconomies (fixed teaching staff regardless of enrollment) and unfunded transport obligations. Closing schools does not reliably yield net savings once capital construction, pupil transport, and lagged demographic costs are counted. The post-2013 tankerület system concentrated maintenance funding but preserved the small-school cost problem. School-bus services, critical to rural access, are municipal-level obligations without state subsidy and have collapsed in several regions due to rising fuel costs. International evidence (Danish, US, Polish) and emerging Hungarian demographic data suggest that school closures impose costs on local communities (population decline, social capital loss, housing depreciation) that exceed budgetary savings. Centralized transport investment (Klebelsberg Green Bus Program) signals partial recognition of the problem but serves only a small fraction of routes. The economic case for wholesale closure is therefore weak; sustainability depends on reform of either per-pupil funding formulas, decentralized transport financing, or school-network designs that preserve educational access without requiring full cost-shifting to families.

## Assumptions
- The per-pupil cost figures from the PPKE reform analysis and ScienceDirect study accurately reflect Hungarian school finance realities circa 2000–2005; the ratio of state to municipal funding has not fundamentally shifted since the tankerület centralization. [assumption]
- Transport costs reported in recent Hungarian cases (Délegyháza, TASZ documentation) are representative of the cost and service disruption patterns in other small-village regions, not outliers. [assumption]
- International evidence (Danish population study, US transport research, Polish school-size analysis) is sufficiently transferable to Hungarian village demographics and institutional context to inform policy inference. [assumption]
- The lagged demographic effect documented in the Danish study (population decline emerging in years 6–10 after closure) operates similarly in the Hungarian rural context, though not definitively proven. [assumption]
- School closures are currently treated in Hungarian policy discourse as primarily budgetary problems, not as embedded in broader territorial development and social cohesion frames; this framing assumption may limit the policy options considered. [assumption]

## Position
The financial case for closing Hungarian rural primary schools is weak because transport costs, capital construction, and lagged demographic losses exceed the salary savings from consolidation, and these aggregate costs are typically borne by families and communities rather than the education budget.

## Uncertainties
