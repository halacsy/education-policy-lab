# Expert analysis: school_network_planning

## Findings (evidence)
- Primary school enrollment in Hungary declined 17.3% over 20 years (859,000 students in 2005/2006 to 710,000 in 2024/2025), with first-graders falling 1.9% year-on-year and the number of primary school sites shrinking by 88 in just three years (2021/2022 to 2024/2025). [evidence: strong — KSH (Central Statistical Office); Népszava, July 2025]
- Birth cohorts are contracting sharply: births fell from 110,800 in 2015 to 77,600 in 2025 (–30% in one decade), and total fertility rate dropped below 1.3 by August 2025, guaranteeing continued primary enrollment decline in the short to medium term. [evidence: strong — KSH; Economx, May 2026]
- Some rural primary schools already cannot enroll a single first-grade student in a given year, forcing consolidation or forced mergers. [evidence: moderate — PSZ (Teachers' Union); Népszava, July 2025; Economx, July 2025]
- Hungarian education maintainers (tankerületek) employ a 'phase-out' method: rather than closing schools abruptly, they halt new first-grade/kindergarten enrollment and allow institutions to empty gradually over 3–8 years while reducing staff. [evidence: strong — Válasz Online, October 2025; Government Commissioner Balatoni Katalin statement]
- Hungarian law permits multi-grade classes (összevont osztály) combining 2–4 grades in lower primary (grades 1–4), with maximum class size of 27 students for grades 1–4 (27 × 1.2 override factor permitted by maintainer). [evidence: strong — Nkt. 2011. évi CXC, 4.§(24); Oktatási és Kultúrális Biztoshelyettes official reports]
- An informal, widely-cited but legally unconfirmed threshold for launching a single-grade class in Hungary is approximately 14 students per cohort; below this, schools typically face merged-class requirements. [evidence: weak — Community Q&A forum (gyakorikerdesek.hu), 2019; not law-text confirmed]
- One-half of all Hungarian teachers in 2024/2025 are age 50 or older, only 7.2% are under 30, and unqualified teacher proportions in disadvantaged (including rural) schools are 2–3 times higher than in advantaged schools. [evidence: strong — EU Education and Training Monitor 2025 country report (Hungary)]
- Three-quarters of families with children report that temporary or permanent closure of kindergartens or schools would cause serious difficulty; 45% say they could not arrange childcare in the short term, and 78% of the most-affected households live in small rural towns or villages. [evidence: moderate — Ipsos survey (2025, underlying data appears COVID-era; ambiguous dating)]
- Major EU-co-financed energy retrofit programs continue nationwide: approximately 24 billion HUF invested across 68 educational institutions under Széchenyi 2020, and similar large-scale projects in individual counties (e.g., 1.5 billion HUF in Heves), creating a stranded-asset tension when buildings face shrinking or zero enrollment. [evidence: strong — Klebelsberg Központ; Heves Online; Hírradó, December 2023]
- A Finnish difference-in-differences study (2010–2018) found that after primary school closure, surrounding community population declined more steeply than before closure, but Saxon evidence contradicts this, finding no localized depopulation effect from primary closures. [evidence: contested — Science Direct (Finnish study); ResearchGate (Saxon counter-study)]
- Specific micro-regions in Hungary show critically low annual birth cohorts (e.g., Bélapátfalva 55, Tab 67, Szentgotthárd, Bóly, Hegyhát under 80 births/year in 2024), making single-grade class viability impossible without cross-settlement consolidation. [evidence: moderate — PSZ president Totyik Tamás; Economx, May 2026]
- When local schools close, public transport operators sometimes prohibit unaccompanied children from regular bus routes, forcing parents to organize rotating supervision duty (busz-őrszolgálat), adding a hidden logistic burden beyond distance. [evidence: moderate — PSZ president Totyik Tamás statement; Válasz Online, October 2025]
- Slovakia is implementing explicit school-network rationalization with financing rule changes proposed from 2030, offering options such as school mergers, clusters, and satellite branches as non-mandated regional alternatives rather than top-down closures. [evidence: moderate — Slovak Education Minister Tomáš Drucker statement; Felvidék.ma, July 2025]
- Hungary-specific peer-reviewed research (2025) identifies settlements in Western Transdanubia, Southern Baranya, and Northern Hungary where child populations contracted 10–30% and where school closures or downgrades coincided with broader depopulation, with extreme disadvantage concentrations (>92%) in settlements of 50–70 children. [evidence: moderate — ResearchGate; 2025 peer-reviewed study on Hungary's public education post-2021]
- Government Commissioner Balatoni Katalin states that the goal is not closure but protection of small settlements and small schools while demand exists, and that the government pursues educational continuity through consolidations and local solutions. [evidence: moderate — Government Commissioner statement; Válasz Online, October 2025]

## Interpretation
Rural primary school viability in Hungary faces a structural, not cyclical, demographic crisis. Enrollment decline (17.3% over 20 years, accelerating) combined with birth-rate collapse (–30% in one decade) creates a pipeline problem: within years, many villages will have cohorts too small to sustain single-grade classes even under multi-grade clustering rules. Government policy favors 'phase-out' over abrupt closure—a pragmatic response that reduces immediate conflict but extends uncertainty for affected families and staff. Teacher shortage and rural recruitment difficulties compound the problem. Evidence from building investment, stranded-asset tensions, and comparative Nordic/Central European studies suggests closures and depopulation may reinforce each other, though causality remains contested. Transport friction (bus-duty rotations, unaccompanied-child restrictions) adds hidden household costs beyond distance. Specific micro-regions (Bélapátfalva, Tab, Hegyhát) already face viability thresholds. Slovakia's emerging policy framework—mergers, clusters, satellite branches as options rather than mandates—offers a design reference. The core tension is real: efficient resource allocation vs. community infrastructure and household logistics.

## Assumptions
- The statutory maximum class size (27 students, grades 1–4) and the informal ~14-student threshold for single-grade class launch remain stable regulatory anchors and are applied consistently by maintainers. [assumption]
- Phase-out policy will not be reversed; closures will not be abrupt or centrally mandated, avoiding the social conflict of the mid-2000s closure wave. [assumption]
- Public transport operators will continue to restrict unaccompanied children from regular routes when local schools close, making parent-organized supervision a necessary household adaptation. [assumption]
- Economic externalities of school closures (e.g., reduced housing demand, slower tax base, delayed municipal service recovery) are not captured in current budget analyses and thus remain invisible in closure decisions. [assumption]

## Position
In Hungarian villages under 2,000 population with annual birth cohorts below 50–70 children, phase-out closures of primary schools are economically and demographically inevitable within 5–10 years unless service delivery is radically redesigned through multi-settlement clusters, satellite branches, or distance-learning hybrids backed by transport subsidies and teacher-deployment flexibility.

## Uncertainties
- No authoritative statutory minimum class-launch enrollment threshold exists in Hungarian education law; the widely-cited '~14 students' figure is from unverified community sources, not legal text. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Official written guidance from the Ministry of Education or the State Secretariat clarifying the minimum enrollment rule for launching a single-grade class.)
- Hungary-specific quantitative research isolating the causal local depopulation effect of primary school closures is absent; comparative evidence (Finland, Saxony, Austria) is mixed and may not transfer to Hungarian rural settlement patterns. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: A matched-pair or difference-in-differences regression study comparing closure cohorts (treated) with matched non-closure villages (control) in Hungary, tracking birth rates, housing prices, and tax revenues 3–7 years pre- and post-closure.)
- Current national building-utilization and vacancy rates for primary schools in villages under 2,000 population are not published in searchable form; exact per-pupil cost curves and optimal school-size thresholds for Hungarian rural schools are unknown. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Publication by KSH or the State School Maintenance Authority (Klebelsberg Központ) of building-stock inventory data (occupancy rates, condition assessments, annual maintenance/utility costs) disaggregated by settlement population and region.)
- No empirical Hungary-specific evidence exists on the effectiveness or cost of distance-learning hybrids, multi-settlement clusters, or satellite-branch models in maintaining educational access and community engagement in depopulating villages. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Pilot program evaluation reports (qualitative and cost-benefit analysis) from 3–5 Hungarian municipalities testing cluster, satellite, or hybrid models for 2–4 years, measuring student outcomes, teacher retention, parent satisfaction, and net fiscal cost vs. closure.)
