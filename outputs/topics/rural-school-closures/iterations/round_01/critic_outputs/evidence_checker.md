# Critique: evidence_checker

## S2.mechanism
Objection: The scenario labels the claim 'Delayed secondary tracking is associated ... with reduced achievement gaps' as [evidence: strong]. That overstates the literature: most comparative studies report an association between later tracking and lower between-school/within-country inequality, but causal identification is mixed, effect sizes vary by context, and Poland's PISA gains after 1999 are confounded by many concurrent reforms. The cited 'strong' tag therefore misrepresents what the international literature actually supports (correlational evidence with important heterogeneity and identification concerns).
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Downgrade the evidence grade to 'moderate/contested' and explicitly state that the international literature primarily shows an association (not conclusive causation). Recommend adding: a request for quasi-experimental studies isolating tracking-timing effects (e.g., difference-in-differences or RD designs) and a Poland-specific causal-attribution caveat noting concurrent reforms.

## S5.evidence_status
Objection: The scenario asserts 'No current national registry ... exists in Hungary' with [evidence: strong]. Given the CURATED SOURCE REGISTRY is empty for this topic and the synthesis elsewhere describes data gaps, labeling this as 'strong' is unjustified. The claim is plausible but not supported at the 'strong' level without direct confirmation from Oktatási Hivatal or KSH publications.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Change the evidence grade to 'moderate' or 'weak' and replace the categorical statement with a verifiable action: 'No publicly accessible, comprehensive registry was found in the project's document review; verify by querying Oktatási Hivatal and KSH and cite the results.' Add a recommended data-validation step (contact KSH/Oktatási Hivatal or FOI search) before asserting 'no registry exists' as a strong fact.

## S4.mechanism
Objection: The scenario reports a numeric range for 'Post-closure transportation costs typically range 800-1,500 EUR per pupil per year in rural Central Europe' and tags it [evidence: moderate]. This precise range is presented without Hungary-specific sourcing and ignores major variation by distance, vehicle type, contract procurement, and subsidy regimes. Treating this cross-country estimate as 'moderate' evidence for Hungarian policy decisions is misleading.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Downgrade the evidence tag to 'weak' or mark as 'speculative' for Hungary; remove the precise euro-range or present it explicitly as an illustrative, region-level estimate with stated variance. Recommend commissioning an empirical costing exercise: collect recent Hungarian rural pupil-transport contracts or modeled per-pupil transport costs (distance bands, vehicle/fuel/driver costs) to produce a context-specific estimate.
