# Critique: assumption_checker

## S1.assumptions
Objection: Assumes local decision‑makers ‘have comparable financial and demographic data to negotiate fair, transparent retention arrangements.’ This conflicts with the synthesis finding that no national closure/outcome registry or standardized per‑settlement data exists and that evidence about municipal criteria is unknown—i.e. data availability and comparability vary and are not a reliable precondition for equitable ad hoc negotiation. The scenario therefore rests on an unverifiable and consequential data-availability assumption. 
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Replace the blanket data-availability assumption with an explicit precondition: require the Oktatási Hivatal/KSH to publish standardized, settlement‑level enrollment and operating‑cost datasets and provide a negotiable template for retention arrangements. Alternatively, model S1 as conditional on a short-term central data‑provision step (audit/registry) and describe negotiation rules that do not rely on locally comparable pre-existing datasets.

## S3.assumptions
Objection: Assert that Polish association‑run publicly‑subsidized rural schools ‘have achieved genuine operational sustainability’ — but both the scenario and the synthesis note that Polish counts, funding flows, and sustainability outcomes are unverified and no registry/longitudinal evidence was found. The claim of sustainability is therefore unsupported by the presented evidence and may conflate legal permissibility with durable financial/administrative viability. 
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Recast the assumption as a hypothesis to be tested rather than a fact. Require a documented empirical audit of Polish association‑run cases (counts, budgets, enrollment trends, exit/closure rates) and mandate a Hungarian pilot program with predefined financial‑viability metrics (3–5 year horizon), governance safeguards, and contingency funding before recommending wide-scale adoption.

## S2.assumptions
Objection: Takes for granted that Hungary’s institutional and municipal governance context is ‘sufficiently similar to Poland’s’ to transfer Poland’s 1999-style structural reform and that PISA gains are causally attributable to delayed tracking. The synthesis explicitly flags transferability and causal attribution as contested or unresolved, so the scenario overstates generalizability and causal inference from Polish evidence to Hungary. 
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Amend the assumption to require an explicit institutional comparability and causal‑identification precondition: (a) commission a feasibility audit mapping Hungarian municipal/state governance differences relative to Poland (funding flows, maintainer plurality, transport capacity); and (b) commit to a rigorous pilot and causal-evaluation design (difference‑in‑differences or regression‑discontinuity) isolating the effect of structural grade reorganization from concurrent reforms before scaling national implementation.
