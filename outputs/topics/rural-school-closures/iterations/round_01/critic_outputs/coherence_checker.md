# Critique: coherence_checker

## S1.assumptions
Objection: The scenario assumes "Teacher supply for multigrade classrooms is adequate without systemic training reform," yet the Implementation steps explicitly schedule multi-year, recurring in-service multigrade-pedagogy training for school staff. This is a direct internal contradiction: the model both assumes no systemic training reform is needed and depends on sustained training to be viable. Leaving the assumption as written risks underestimating the implementation cost, timeline, and feasibility if teacher supply/skills are in fact inadequate.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Revise the assumption to acknowledge current teacher-skill shortfalls for multigrade pedagogy and treat in-service training as a necessary systemic input (not optional). Add concrete metrics (e.g., % of local teachers needing upskilling, cost per teacher, minimum training completion timeline) and incorporate corresponding budget and staffing contingencies into Cost categories and Implementation steps. Commission a rapid supply-and-skill audit to parameterize these metrics before committing to village-level retention plans.

## S2.mechanism
Objection: The mechanism text asserts that "delayed secondary tracking is associated in the international literature with reduced achievement gaps" and tags that claim [evidence: strong], while the scenario's Evidence status explicitly labels attribution as contested (noting Poland's PISA gains may be due to concurrent reforms). This overstates causal certainty: the international literature supports an association, but the Polish case in particular does not provide strong causal identification that delayed tracking alone produced gains.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Downgrade the 'strong' evidence tag to 'moderate/contested' for the claim linking delayed tracking causally to reduced gaps. Clarify that observed international associations do not imply transferability to Hungary without isolating concurrent reforms. Add an explicit requirement for causal evaluation (e.g., a pilot with pre/post measures and a difference-in-differences design) before treating delayed-tracking as a primary justification for wide structural reorganisation.

## S3.assumptions
Objection: The scenario assumes that Poland's association-run, publicly-subsidized rural schools "have achieved genuine operational sustainability," but the Evidence status already concedes no Hungarian pilot data and explicitly states Polish association-run-school sustainability is an 'unverified assumption.' This is a concrete unsupported empirical claim being used to motivate policy transfer, risking policy design on an unvalidated precedent.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Replace the asserted sustainability claim with an explicit uncertainty: treat Polish association-run school sustainability as unverified. Add a required evidence-gathering step: obtain a registry/survey of Polish association-run schools (counts, budgets, survival rates, student outcomes) and run a small-group Hungarian pilot with pre-defined financial and governance viability metrics before scaling. Update Expected benefits to conditional language contingent on demonstrated Polish/Hungarian pilot outcomes.

## S4.uncertainties
Objection: The Mechanism claims post-closure transport costs "typically range 800-1,500 EUR per pupil per year" and often offset consolidation savings ([evidence: moderate]), yet the Uncertainties state that the "true net financial savings of consolidation once all transport and indirect costs are counted for Hungarian villages specifically are not quantified" (confidence: high). This is inconsistent: the scenario treats transport costs as a decisive typical offset but simultaneously asserts net savings are not quantified for Hungary, leaving the reader unclear about whether transport costs are a demonstrated offset or only a plausible possibility.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Reconcile these statements by distinguishing general Central-European cost ranges from Hungarian-specific totals. Keep the transport-cost range as international context but explicitly state it is not a Hungary-specific estimate. Add a concrete action: commission a Hungary-specific cost accounting exercise (per-settlement transport and consolidation budget model) to quantify net savings/losses before closure decisions are recommended, and reflect preliminary sensitivity ranges in Cost categories and Expected benefits rather than definitive offsets.
