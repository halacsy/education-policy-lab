# Critique: political_risk_checker

## S2.political_risks
Objection: The scenario acknowledges Poland's 1999 reform was politically reversible (reversed after 17 years) yet its political_risks section does not propose any concrete entrenchment or cross-party buy-in strategies to reduce reversal risk. Concretely: it treats political durability as an observation rather than a design constraint — there is no prescription for legal safeguards, mandated transition protections for affected teachers, ring-fenced multi-year funding for the new ISCED2 centers, or institutional mechanisms (cross-party oversight, statutory consultation minimums) that would make the reorganization politically durable. Without these, the scenario underestimates the likelihood of ideologically-driven reversal and the policy’s fragility. (Speculative risk: political opposition patterns could differ, but Poland’s reversal is direct evidence that structural reforms without entrenchment are vulnerable.)
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add an explicit entrenchment package to the political_risks/implementation design: (a) require cross-party legislative resolution or supermajority endorsement for network-level structural change; (b) include guaranteed multi-year, ring‑fenced center-school operating funding and indexed transport subsidies (5–10 year commitments) legislated at reform outset; (c) mandate statutory transition rules protecting teacher employment (redeployment, severance, retraining) and phased implementation timelines tied to objective milestones; and (d) require an independent, public evaluation at 3 and 7 years with automatic continuation clauses conditional on meeting pre-specified outcome benchmarks.

## S4.political_risks
Objection: The scenario correctly flags that compensatory measures (transport guarantees, TEIP-style support) require sustained multi-year funding, but its political_risks entry fails to specify how those funding commitments would be insulated from future budget cuts or political reversal. Concretely: there is no proposal for legal entrenchment of compensatory funding, nor institutional accountability (independent trustee, multi-year budget lines, or conditional grants) to prevent compensatory measures from being starved after closures occur — making closure effectively irreversible but without durable mitigation, which intensifies political backlash and undermines equity promises. The scenario therefore understates the practical risk that mitigations will be withdrawn and that communities will perceive closures as permanent losses.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Specify binding financing and oversight mechanisms for compensatory measures: (a) legislate multi-year, ring-fenced transport and TEIP-equivalent grant lines indexed to enrolment for a minimum 5–7 year transition period; (b) create an independent oversight board (multi-stakeholder, including parent/community reps) with authority to audit disbursements and publish quarterly compliance reports; (c) build automatic contingency funding triggers (e.g., if transport budgets fall below X% real value) that release reserve funds; and (d) attach a statutory re-appraisal clause requiring re-evaluation of closures only after a transparent review process, preventing ad-hoc reversal pressure from producing worse outcomes.

## S5.political_risks
Objection: S5 proposes a national, non-binding transparency/registry framework but treats its advisory status and political neutrality as given; it does not confront the credible risk that the registry or thresholds will be politicized (used as de facto mandates) or will be resisted as recentralization. Concretely: the political_risks discussion omits institutional design to (i) secure cross-party governance of the registry, (ii) legally limit how data/thresholds may be used (prevent unilateral closure mandates), and (iii) guarantee multi-year funding for the evidence infrastructure — all key to preventing drift toward binding mandates or instrument capture by successive administrations. The absence of such design both understates opposition risk and leaves entrenchment unaddressed.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Redesign the political governance of the framework: (a) establish the registry under a statutory multi-party oversight board (parliamentary representation across parties, local government and civil-society seats) with protected appointment rules to prevent unilateral capture; (b) include explicit legal clauses that the published thresholds are advisory unless ratified by a supermajority or implemented via separate enabling regulation; (c) require stakeholder co-design (municipalities, tankerületek, unions, parent groups) in pilot phases and publish impact assessments before national rollout; and (d) secure multi-year baseline funding in law or via a dedicated trust to ensure continuity across electoral cycles.

## S3.political_risks
Objection: The scenario notes possible perception of association-run models as privatization and union resistance, but its political_risks section understates organized opposition and does not propose political or legal safeguards to secure public subsidy continuity for association-run or hub models. Concretely: it assumes association-run schools can access public funds long-term, yet offers no contractual length limits, clawback protections, or statutory maintainer-status guarantees — leaving these schools vulnerable to derecognition or defunding if political winds change or unions mobilize. This omission risks destabilizing networked retention models after initial pilots, especially in less-organized communities.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Strengthen political risk mitigation for association-run/hub models: (a) require enabling national legislation that defines association-run schools’ public-finance entitlements and minimum contract durations (e.g., 5–10 years) with renewal criteria; (b) include collective-bargaining clarity with teachers’ unions (guaranteed employment terms, payroll transfer rules) to reduce union opposition; (c) fund capacity-building grants to ensure administrative robustness in weaker communities; and (d) pilot with legally-binding subsidy contracts and independent evaluation before scaling, with contingency plans for municipal takeover if associations fail to sustain operations.
