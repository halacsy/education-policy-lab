# Critique: feasibility_checker

## S1.implementation_steps
Objection: The implementation timeline (school leaders & teaching staff: in-service multigrade-pedagogy training year 1-3, recurring) understates the lead time and delivery capacity needed to upskill sufficient teachers. The scenario assumes teacher supply is adequate without systemic reform, yet provides only routine in-service training over three years. In practice: (a) teacher-education institutions and regional in‑service providers currently lack standardized multigrade curricula or sufficient trainer capacity; (b) hiring or re-deploying teachers with multigrade expertise cannot be achieved solely by short in-service courses if initial pre-service programs lack the module; and (c) schools will face coverage gaps during training (substitute teacher shortages). The plan omits concrete targets (how many teachers per district), funding for substitutes, and incentives to retain trained teachers in low-pay rural posts.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Commission a teacher-supply and training-capacity audit in month 0–6 (measure current multigrade-qualified headcount by tankerület). Based on that audit: (1) extend the training rollout to a 4–5 year phased program with annual numeric targets (e.g., X multigrade-certified teachers per tankerület); (2) fund short-term substitute pools and retention incentives (salary top-ups or housing allowance) to cover release time; (3) develop an accredited short-cycle 'multigrade specialist' certification fast-track with approved provider list and guaranteed cohort intakes, and include metrics for retention and classroom coverage in the implementation milestones.

## S2.implementation_steps
Objection: The timeline and sequencing for designating centrally-sited ISCED2 schools and establishing statutory transport (year 1 to year 2-3) is unrealistically compressed given real-world administrative, capital, and procurement lead times. The steps assume a Ministry/tankerületi can 'designate candidate towns and assess capacity' and then, within two years, establish funded transport arrangements and expand capacity. In reality: (a) capacity audits, planning approvals, capital expansion, and construction/renovation typically require 24–48 months; (b) statutory transport guarantees require inter-ministerial budget approval, vehicle procurement or contracting, and route/timetabling studies that often take a full procurement cycle (12–18 months) after budget allocation; and (c) redeployment or recruitment of specialist subject teachers for centralized ISCED2 schools will trigger collective-bargaining and HR processes not reflected in the timeline.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Replace the single rapid timeline with a staged, cluster-based roll-out: (1) months 0–6: rapid feasibility scan to identify 3–5 pilot clusters using pre-defined distance/time criteria; (2) months 6–18: detailed capacity audits, stakeholder consultation, and capital/transport costings for pilots; (3) months 18–48: phased capital works and transport procurement for pilots, with legal guarantee of transport funding in the multi-year budget and transitional bussing contracts; (4) parallel HR plan to recruit/transfer specialist teachers with negotiated timelines and compensations. Make pilot outcomes the trigger for broader roll-out rather than calendar-year targets.

## S3.implementation_steps
Objection: The plan to 'establish or clarify a legal mechanism analogous to Poland's association-run school amendment' on a year 1-2 timeline ignores two concrete barriers: (a) changing maintainer law or regulatory practice to permit association-operated, publicly-subsidized schools typically requires substantial legislative drafting, stakeholder negotiation (mayors, unions, church maintainers), and budgetary rule changes — processes that commonly take multiple parliamentary sessions; and (b) it assumes small village communities have the administrative bandwidth to form associations, apply for maintainer status, and manage payroll/accounting. The scenario treats the Polish amendment as directly exportable despite its own 'unverified sustainability' note, and offers no interim administrative support mechanism for communities.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: (speculative) Recast this step as a two-track approach: (A) short-term (year 0–2) 'regulatory sandbox' and pilot pathway using existing legal plurality — select 5–10 villages and provide a regional administrative backbone (tankerületi or NGO partner) to act as fiscal/HR agent while associations learn to manage operations; (B) medium-term (year 1–4) legislative review and draft amendment with mandated consultation windows, accompanied by a small grants programme to build municipal/association administrative capacity. Require pilot performance and a legal impact analysis before national rollout.

## S5.implementation_steps
Objection: The proposed timeline for a national closure/outcome registry and standardized teacher-training rollout (design/publish thresholds year 1-2; registry year 1-3; training year 2-4) underestimates the technical, legal (data-sharing/GDPR), and procurement lead times for KSH/Oktatási Hivatal IT systems and inter-agency agreements. The scenario claims strong evidence that no registry exists but assumes rapid creation is straightforward; in practice, establishing a national registry that links enrollment, closures, and community outcomes requires MoUs across ministries, data cleaning/mapping, IT procurement (often 12–24 months), and staffing — plus political clearance for what data are published. The plan omits interim transparency steps and budget lines for the registry build.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Adopt a staged delivery plan: (1) months 0–6: governance design, stakeholder MoUs, and scope (minimum dataset) to enable a limited public dashboard using existing KSH/Oktatási Hivatal datasets; (2) months 6–18: pilot registry for a representative sample of settlements (technical prototype, GDPR review, user-testing); (3) months 18–36: full procurement and rollout with dedicated data-team hiring and maintenance budget; (4) concurrently publish an interim 'cost-visibility' dataset compiled from currently available records to improve transparency while the registry is built.
