# Policy scenarios

## S1 — Status quo plus incremental adaptation
**Goal**
Keep the existing municipal/state-maintained village school open as long as feasible, using multigrade classrooms, felmenő rendszer phase-out, and ad hoc local negotiation without a national framework.

**Mechanism**
- Multigrade composite classes (összevont osztály) allow a school to remain open below the enrollment level that would sustain single-grade classes. [evidence: moderate]
- The felmenő rendszer permits gradual phase-out of class cohorts rather than immediate closure, reducing abrupt community disruption. [evidence: moderate]
- Since 2013 state-level operating funding removes the immediate closure incentive from cash-strapped municipalities but does not resolve underlying enrollment-driven cost pressure. [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
weak — No published Hungarian pilot data or outcome registry documents whether ad hoc, non-standardized local adaptation actually sustains schools long-term.

**Assumptions**
- Local decision-makers have comparable financial and demographic data to negotiate fair, transparent retention arrangements.
- Enrollment decline is a reliable proxy for school viability, without significant confounding factors.
- Teacher supply for multigrade classrooms is adequate without systemic training reform.

**Expected benefits**
- Preserves the school as community anchor infrastructure (gathering space, local employment) for as long as possible. [evidence: weak]
- Avoids abrupt travel-burden increase for young children by delaying reassignment to a center school. [evidence: weak]
- Low upfront administrative and legal cost since it relies on existing statutory flexibility (Nkt.) rather than new structures. [evidence: moderate]

**Equity impact**
Likely reduces short-term travel-burden inequity for lower-SES families who cannot absorb commuting costs, but risks compounding disadvantage over time if multigrade teaching quality and teacher supply are not secured, and provides no systematic mitigation for compositional/peer effects as higher-SES families opt out.

**Cost categories**
- Ongoing state-funded operating costs (salaries, utilities, maintenance) for below-threshold enrollment
- In-service multigrade-pedagogy upskilling costs for teachers
- Local administrative time for ad hoc negotiation with tankerület/county authorities

**Implementation steps**
- Village local government (önkormányzat) — Initiate consultation with tankerületi központ on retaining multigrade operation and document enrollment projections. (timeline: year 1)
- Tankerületi Központ (county education district) — Approve or negotiate felmenő rendszer phase-out schedule for at-risk cohorts, aligned to school-calendar timelines. (timeline: year 1-2)
- School leadership and teaching staff — Undertake in-service multigrade-pedagogy training to address the training gap identified in mainstream pre-service education. (timeline: year 1-3, recurring)
- Ministry of Education / Oktatási Hivatal — Monitor enrollment trajectory annually to flag when incremental adaptation approaches non-viability. (timeline: ongoing, year 1 onward)

**Political risks**
- Quiet, uncoordinated attrition may be politically convenient short-term but risks sudden non-viability crises with no transition plan.
- Absence of a national framework leaves outcomes dependent on individual mayors' negotiating capacity, risking inequitable treatment across villages.
- Rural electoral base sensitivity may block even necessary future consolidation, prolonging unviable operation.

**Uncertainties**
- What minimum viable enrollment thresholds do municipalities currently use, and how consistently, is unknown. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A systematic registry or survey of municipal school operation criteria published by the Oktatási Hivatal.)
- The felmenő rendszer's current utilization rate and the barriers preventing wider adoption are not documented. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A retrospective analysis of school reorganization filings with regional education authorities over the past 5-10 years.)
- Whether ad hoc, non-standardized adaptation produces worse or better long-term outcomes than a coordinated framework is untested. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Pilot programmes comparing coordinated versus ad hoc adaptation models across matched villages with published outcome tracking.)

## S2 — Structural grade reorganization (lower grades stay, upper grades centralize)
**Goal**
Restructure the school system so only lower grades (1-4/1-6) remain in the village while upper grades or a new ISCED2-type school are consolidated in a larger town, following the Polish 1999 structural-reform pattern of shortened primary plus centrally sited later-stage schooling.

**Mechanism**
- Delaying secondary tracking/centralization by keeping only lower grades local reduces the age at which children must commute, while still consolidating upper-grade resources centrally. [evidence: moderate]
- Central siting of upper-grade/ISCED2 schools lengthens commutes for ages 10-15 even where the local lower-grade primary remains open, a documented side effect from the Polish 1999 reform. [evidence: moderate]
- Delayed secondary tracking is associated in the international literature with reduced achievement gaps, particularly benefiting lower-performing students. [evidence: strong]

**Evidence status**
contested — PISA gains following Poland's 1999 reform are strongly documented, but attribution to the structural delay itself versus concurrent reforms is an assumption, and the reform's political durability was contested enough to be reversed after 17 years.

**Assumptions**
- Hungary's institutional and municipal governance context is sufficiently similar to Poland's for the 1999 structural pattern to transfer meaningfully.
- A suitable centrally-sited town with capacity to host consolidated upper-grade/ISCED2 schooling exists within reasonable travel distance of most candidate villages.
- PISA score gains attributed to delayed tracking are not primarily artifacts of concurrent unrelated reforms or measurement changes.

**Expected benefits**
- Preserves local presence for the youngest, most travel-vulnerable children (ages 6-10/12) while consolidating specialist resources for older students. [evidence: moderate]
- May reduce achievement gaps by delaying selective tracking, per international delayed-tracking literature. [evidence: strong]
- Concentrates specialist subject-teacher resources where they are hardest to recruit for small rural sites, addressing teacher-supply constraints. [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
Mixed: protects youngest and most travel-vulnerable children from long commutes, likely benefiting lower-SES families disproportionately dependent on local transport, but shifts the travel and selection burden onto ages 10-15, a group also sensitive to Hungary's existing early-tracking SES gradient; net equity effect depends on whether upper-grade consolidation is paired with transport guarantees.

**Cost categories**
- Capital investment in a new or expanded ISCED2-type central school
- Transport infrastructure and subsidy for ages 10-15 commuting to the central school
- Retained lower-grade building maintenance and multigrade staffing in villages
- Administrative and legal cost of restructuring the school network and maintainer arrangements

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education / Klebelsberg Center (Tankerületi Központ) — Designate candidate town(s) for centrally sited ISCED2-type schools and assess capacity and commuting-distance feasibility for surrounding villages. (timeline: year 1)
- Regional/county authorities — Consult with affected village governments and parent communities on the restructuring plan, per statutory consultation requirements. (timeline: year 1-2)
- Village local governments — Retain and adapt lower-grade school buildings, coordinating with tankerület on multigrade staffing. (timeline: year 2-3)
- Ministry of Education / county transport authority — Establish and fund statutory transport arrangements for upper-grade pupils commuting to the central school. (timeline: year 2-3)

**Political risks**
- Rapid, top-down network restructuring (as in Poland's 2016-2019 reversal) can cause teacher layoffs and consolidation strain, damaging trust regardless of pedagogical merit.
- Structural reforms lacking cross-party consensus are vulnerable to ideologically-driven reversal, as Poland's 1999 reform was reversed after 17 years despite documented gains.
- Parents of upper-grade pupils may resist longer commutes for their children, generating localized political opposition distinct from lower-grade retention support.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether Hungary's institutional and municipal capacity can support the central-siting logistics that Poland's reform required is unverified. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A feasibility audit of candidate central towns' capacity and transport network for a representative sample of village clusters.)
- Whether PISA gains after Poland's 1999 reform are causally attributable to delayed tracking itself, versus concurrent reforms, remains an open empirical question. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A difference-in-differences or regression-discontinuity study isolating the tracking-delay effect from other concurrent Polish reforms.)
- Political durability of a Polish-pattern reform in Hungary without cross-party consensus is unknown and may face reversal risk similar to Poland's 2016-2019 experience. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Cross-party consultation records and durability tracking of similar structural reforms over a 10+ year horizon.)

## S3 — Networked retention: clustering, association-run and multi-service hub models
**Goal**
Retain village primary schools through inter-municipal pedagogical networks (French RPI-style clustering), community/association-run publicly-subsidized schools (Polish model), or multi-service hubs combining school with other civic functions.

**Mechanism**
- France's RPI model clusters several small rural municipalities' schools into a shared pedagogical network rather than closing them, with grade-splitting across sites or inter-village transport. [evidence: moderate]
- Poland's mid-2000s amendment allows municipalities to transfer very-low-enrollment village schools to parent/community associations to operate as publicly-subsidized non-public schools instead of closing them. [evidence: moderate]
- Multi-service hub models (school plus health/civic functions) are proposed in European policy but lack robust cost-benefit data and sustained uptake evidence in comparable rural contexts. [evidence: weak]
- Hungary's maintainer plurality (municipal, church, private foundation, state) creates existing legal pathways for non-municipal continuity that networked models could activate. [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
weak — Component mechanisms (RPI, association-run schools) are moderately documented abroad, but no Hungarian pilot data exists on adaptation success, and association-run school sustainability in Poland itself is an unverified assumption rather than confirmed outcome.

**Assumptions**
- Association-run publicly-subsidized rural schools in Poland have achieved genuine operational sustainability rather than merely delaying eventual closure.
- Hungarian communities under 2,000 population possess or can develop sufficient administrative capacity to organize and sustain an association-run school or hub model.
- Inter-municipal cooperation (RPI-style) is politically and administratively achievable across Hungarian village governments without excessive coordination overhead.
- Multi-service hub models can achieve utilization and cost-effectiveness in Hungarian villages despite the absence of local demonstration data.

**Expected benefits**
- Preserves local school presence and community-anchor function without requiring full municipal-scale enrollment. [evidence: weak]
- Offers a politically more durable middle path than binary open/close decisions, reducing reversal-cost risk from abrupt closure. [evidence: moderate]
- Multi-service hubs could offset fixed per-pupil costs by sharing overhead with other civic functions, though this is unproven in Hungarian contexts. [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
Potentially positive for equity by avoiding both the travel burden of full consolidation and the compositional/peer-effect risks of unmanaged decline, but administrative capacity requirements may disadvantage the poorest, least-organized villages, creating a new form of inequity between communities able versus unable to organize networked or association-run models.

**Cost categories**
- Inter-municipal coordination and administrative overhead for pedagogical network management
- Transitional legal and financial setup costs for association-run school registration and public subsidy access
- Capital and operating costs for multi-service hub facility conversion and shared staffing
- Rotating specialist-teacher travel and coordination costs across networked sites

**Implementation steps**
- Cluster of neighbouring village local governments — Form an inter-municipal pedagogical network agreement (RPI-style) defining shared staffing and grade-splitting arrangements. (timeline: year 1)
- Ministry of Education / national legislature — Establish or clarify a legal mechanism analogous to Poland's association-run school amendment, permitting parent/community associations to operate publicly-subsidized rural schools. (timeline: year 1-2)
- Parent/community associations — Apply for maintainer transition and secure public subsidy under the new legal mechanism where municipal operation is unviable. (timeline: year 2-3)
- Village governments and regional development agencies — Pilot multi-service hub conversion in 3-5 representative villages, combining school with health/civic functions and tracking cost and utilization outcomes. (timeline: year 2-4)

**Political risks**
- Association-run models may be perceived as a step toward privatization or reduced state responsibility, generating opposition from teachers' unions and local-government associations.
- Inter-municipal cooperation requires sustained political will across multiple village governments, which may fracture over time due to local rivalries or asymmetric costs/benefits.
- Multi-service hub models lack demonstrated Hungarian precedent, risking public skepticism or under-resourcing if piloted without adequate evaluation infrastructure.

**Uncertainties**
- The current count, average enrollment, and budget sustainability of association-run schools in Poland is not verified, so the model's replicability is uncertain. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A registry or statistical survey of Polish gminas documenting association-run school counts, funding flows, and operational outcomes.)
- Whether Hungarian village governments and communities have the administrative capacity to sustain inter-municipal networks or association-run schools without external support is unknown. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A structured audit of a representative sample of municipalities under 2,000 inhabitants measuring readiness to activate maintainer alternatives.)
- Cost-benefit outcomes and sustained uptake of multi-service hub models in comparable Hungarian villages are entirely undocumented. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Inventory and evaluation of active hub programmes in Hungary, including baseline costs, staffing, utilization rates, and parental/community satisfaction surveys.)

## S4 — Consolidation with mitigation (closure as default, compensatory measures)
**Goal**
Close low-enrollment village schools and reassign pupils to a consolidated center school, paired with statutory transport guarantees, targeted support (TEIP-style), and transparent cost/consultation rules to offset travel and equity harms.

**Mechanism**
- Portugal applied a minimum-enrollment threshold (~21 students) that consolidated isolated 1st-cycle schools into larger centros escolares between 2005-2013. [evidence: moderate]
- TEIP-style targeted support (extra resources, pedagogical autonomy, social-work services) is associated with reduced dropout and grade-repetition rates in disadvantaged and rural schools. [evidence: moderate]
- Statutory transport guarantees convert the closure decision into an explicit, budgeted cost shift, making trade-offs visible and comparable rather than hidden. [evidence: moderate]
- Post-closure transportation costs typically range 800-1,500 EUR per pupil per year in rural Central Europe, often offsetting or exceeding apparent per-pupil consolidation savings. [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
contested — Portugal's PISA gains are documented but attribution to consolidation itself versus concurrent TEIP/curriculum reform is unresolved, and consolidation's causal link to accelerated rural depopulation is contested in the literature.

**Assumptions**
- Centros escolares-type consolidated cost-per-student and quality-of-service data reliably reflect actual educational and social benefit.
- TEIP's observed correlation with reduced dropout reflects genuine pedagogical improvement rather than selection/creaming effects into the programme.
- Current commuting infrastructure and subsidized public transport can sustain daily trips of 20+ km for primary-age children without unacceptable welfare cost.
- A nearby center school with spare capacity exists within plausible bussing distance for most, though not all, closure candidates.

**Expected benefits**
- Concentrates pedagogical and specialist resources, potentially improving instructional quality and subject-teacher access. [evidence: moderate]
- TEIP-style targeted support measurably associated with reduced dropout and repetition in comparable disadvantaged/rural contexts. [evidence: moderate]
- Transparent cost/consultation rules make trade-offs visible to stakeholders, potentially improving accountability relative to ad hoc closure. [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
Net financial savings are frequently overstated once transport and indirect costs are counted (falling to 5-15% of gross reduction), and lower-SES families bear disproportionate travel burden and opportunity cost; without robust, well-funded compensatory measures this scenario risks worsening equity for the families least able to absorb commuting costs, even as it may improve average resource concentration for those who remain.

**Cost categories**
- Statutory transport provision and subsidy (busing, fuel, driver wages, infrastructure)
- TEIP-style targeted support funding (extra staff, social-work services, pedagogical autonomy resources)
- Consultation and transparency infrastructure (published cost data, consultation process administration)
- Capital cost of expanding capacity at the consolidated center school

**Implementation steps**
- Tankerületi Központ / Ministry of Education — Publish transparent per-settlement cost and enrollment data to inform closure candidacy and enable statutory consultation. (timeline: year 1)
- Regional/county authorities and affected village communities — Conduct statutory consultation on proposed closure, documenting community input and alternatives considered. (timeline: year 1-2)
- National/regional transport authority — Establish and legally guarantee free/subsidized transport for reassigned pupils, with defined maximum commute-time limits. (timeline: year 2)
- Consolidated center school and Ministry of Education — Deploy TEIP-style targeted support (extra staff, social-work services) for reassigned pupils, particularly disadvantaged and Roma families. (timeline: year 2-3)

**Political risks**
- Closure is politically risky if framed as central-state efficiency imposed against community will, particularly given rural areas' electoral significance.
- Reversal is highly costly once teachers disperse and buildings are repurposed, making this scenario largely irreversible if demographic or policy assumptions change.
- Compensatory measures (transport, TEIP-style support) require sustained multi-year funding commitments that may be cut in future budget cycles, undermining the mitigation promise.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether school closure causally accelerates village out-migration or merely ratifies decisions already made by prior demographic decline is empirically unresolved. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Longitudinal village-level data linking school closure timing to household migration flows, controlling for prior population trends.)
- The true net financial savings of consolidation once all transport and indirect costs are counted for Hungarian villages specifically are not quantified. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Comparative budget data from a matched pair or cohort of Hungarian villages with and without school consolidation.)
- The magnitude of peer-composition effects on remaining/reassigned pupils' outcomes after consolidation is unknown for the Hungarian context. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Matched cohort study comparing learning trajectories of pupils in closed vs. open village schools, controlling for baseline SES and prior achievement.)

## S5 — National systemic framework: transparency, thresholds and evidence infrastructure
**Goal**
Establish a national framework of transparent minimum-viability thresholds, cost-visibility rules, teacher-training standardization, and a closure/outcome registry to inform (not dictate) local decisions across any of the above models.

**Mechanism**
- Finland's post-1972 reform bundled structural unification, uniform teacher training, transparent municipal cost-sharing, and statutory transport/support guarantees rather than deploying separate policy levers. [evidence: moderate]
- Finland's university-based teacher-education reform (1979) created a uniformly high-skill teaching workforce across rural and urban schools, associated with low between-school outcome variance. [evidence: moderate]
- Statutory school-transport guarantees convert closure decisions into explicit, comparable cost trade-offs rather than hidden or locally opaque calculations. [evidence: moderate]
- No current national registry of rural school closure decisions and community outcomes exists in Hungary, preventing evidence-based comparison of long-term impacts across models. [evidence: strong]

**Evidence status**
moderate — Finland's bundled-reform outcomes are moderately documented internationally, but the causal necessity of bundling (versus any single lever) is an explicit assumption, and Hungary's institutional capacity to replicate Finnish-style reform is unverified.

**Assumptions**
- Transparency and cost visibility enable better stakeholder decision-making, though no direct causal mechanism beyond logical inference is claimed.
- Hungary's institutional context admits the possibility of system-wide teacher-education reform and municipal cost-sharing mechanisms analogous to Finland's, despite unmodelled specific institutional barriers.
- A national threshold/registry framework will inform rather than override local decision-making authority, avoiding a return to rigid centralized mandates.
- Sufficient political will exists to fund and maintain a long-term evidence infrastructure (registry, thresholds) across changing government administrations.

**Expected benefits**
- Provides the missing evidence base (enrollment thresholds, closure outcomes, cost comparisons) identified as absent across all other scenarios' expert analyses. [evidence: strong]
- Standardized teacher training (multigrade pedagogy modules) could address the documented supply-quality gap distinct from headcount shortages. [evidence: weak]
- Cost-visibility rules make the currently hidden incentive misalignment (state captures efficiency savings, communities bear community-loss costs) explicit and debatable. [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
Primarily an enabling/procedural scenario rather than a direct service-delivery model, so equity impact depends entirely on how thresholds and registries are subsequently used; if paired with any of S1-S4, it could improve equity by making disproportionate travel/compositional burdens on lower-SES families visible and subject to policy correction, but on its own changes no immediate outcome for any village.

**Cost categories**
- Design and maintenance of a national closure/outcome registry (Ministry of Education / KSH data infrastructure)
- Standardization and rollout of multigrade-pedagogy teacher-training modules across teacher-education institutions
- Cost-visibility rule development and publication infrastructure for municipal/state school-operation budgets
- Ongoing data collection and analysis capacity (KSH/Oktatási Hivatal staffing)

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education / Oktatási Hivatal — Design and publish transparent, evidence-based minimum-viability threshold guidelines (non-binding) for school operation, informed by settlement-level cost and demographic data. (timeline: year 1-2)
- KSH (Central Statistical Office) and Ministry of Education — Establish a national closure/outcome registry tracking settlement-level enrollment, closure decisions, and post-closure community outcomes. (timeline: year 1-3)
- Teacher-education institutions and Ministry of Education — Develop and standardize mandatory multigrade-pedagogy training modules across pre-service and in-service teacher education. (timeline: year 2-4)
- Tankerületi Központok and municipal governments — Publish standardized, comparable per-settlement operating and transport-cost data as part of the cost-visibility rule rollout. (timeline: year 2-3)

**Political risks**
- A national framework risks being perceived as recentralization or a precursor to mandated closures, triggering resistance from rural constituencies despite its stated non-binding, informational purpose.
- Sustained multi-year investment in registry and training infrastructure is vulnerable to budget cuts or political deprioritization across election cycles.
- Absence of consensus on what counts as a 'minimum-viability threshold' could stall the framework in prolonged technical/political negotiation before any registry produces usable data.

**Uncertainties**
- What minimum viable enrollment thresholds Hungarian municipalities currently use, and how they vary regionally, remains unknown absent this registry. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A systematic registry or survey of municipal school operation criteria, collected and published by the Oktatási Hivatal.)
- Whether Finland's bundled-reform causal logic (structural + curricular + financing + transport guarantees together) is causally necessary, versus any single component being sufficient, is unverified by direct study. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A longitudinal study comparing school-quality outcomes before and after the 1979 teacher-education reform, controlling for other concurrent Finnish policy changes.)
- Whether a Hungarian national threshold/registry framework would in practice remain advisory or drift toward binding mandates over time is politically and institutionally uncertain. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Comparative case studies of similar advisory frameworks in other countries, tracking their evolution toward or away from binding status over a 10+ year horizon.)
