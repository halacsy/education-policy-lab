# Expert analysis: education_finance

## Findings (evidence)
- Fixed operating costs (building maintenance, utilities, administration, baseline teacher salary) constitute 60–75% of a small rural primary school's budget regardless of enrollment, creating a steep per-pupil cost curve as student numbers decline. [evidence: moderate — general domain knowledge; OECD rural school finance literature]
- Multigrade teaching (combined-year classrooms) is the established low-cost adaptation in European and Hungarian small schools before closure, reducing per-pupil costs by 15–25% without capital investment. [evidence: weak — professional practice reports; specific Hungarian cost data not retrieved this round]
- Since 2013, Hungary has centralized primary school operating costs to the state budget, removing the closure decision incentive from cash-strapped municipalities and shifting it to regional/national authorities. [evidence: moderate — Hungarian education financing reforms (KIR-based; source not live-verified this round)]
- Post-closure transportation costs (busing, fuel, driver wages, infrastructure) typically range from 800–1,500 EUR per pupil per year in rural Central Europe, often offsetting or exceeding the per-pupil savings from consolidation. [evidence: moderate — European transport economics; exact Hungarian coefficients not sourced this round]
- Young children (grades 1–4) in closed-school catchments experience documented increases in travel time, fatigue, and absenteeism, with disproportionate burden on disadvantaged and Roma families lacking private transport. [evidence: weak — international comparative studies; Hungary-specific microdata not available this round]
- School consolidation financial savings are frequently overstated in policy estimates; once transport, capital repair backlogs, and lost local economic multipliers are counted, net savings fall to 5–15% of gross per-pupil cost reduction. [evidence: moderate — OECD/EI rural consolidation meta-analyses]
- Empirical causality between school closure and village out-migration is contested: some studies find closure accelerates decline; others attribute both to prior demographic collapse, making the causal direction unclear. [evidence: contested — international rural sociology literature; Hungary-specific panel data not retrieved this round]
- Multi-service hub models (combining school, health clinic, civic centre, library) are proposed in European policy but lack robust cost–benefit data and sustained uptake in comparable Hungarian villages. [evidence: weak — EU rural policy briefs; implementation data for Hungary absent]
- Hungary's school-age cohort (ages 6–14) has declined by approximately 35–40% since 2000 due to low fertility and emigration, creating structural (not solely migration-driven) pressure on rural school viability. [evidence: moderate — KSH (Hungarian Central Statistical Office); data not live-verified this round]

## Interpretation
Rural primary school viability in depopulating villages hinges on the interplay of fixed-cost floors, transport economics, and demographic trends. The sharp rise in per-pupil operating cost as enrollment falls is partly mitigable through multigrade teaching, but the true post-closure saving is often smaller than policy assumes once transport and indirect costs are factored in. Hungary's 2013 shift to state funding removed local closure incentives but did not resolve the underlying question: whether consolidation genuinely frees resources for better education elsewhere or simply relocates costs and harms (travel burden, community fragmentation) without real savings. The causal link between school closure and village decline is empirically murky—closure may accelerate already-failing communities or merely ratify decisions already made by out-migrating families. Hub models and multigrade adaptation are theoretically promising but lack robust outcome data in Hungarian contexts.

## Assumptions
- Current Hungarian budgetary structures and teacher-deployment rules remain stable over the 5–10 year horizon relevant to school closure decisions. [assumption]
- Parents and students in depopulating villages would accept multigrade teaching and consolidated catchment areas as legitimate substitutes for single-grade, single-site schooling. [assumption]
- The educational outcomes and social cohesion benefits of maintaining a village school are comparable across communities; no unmeasured heterogeneity in village capacity to sustain hub models or alternative governance. [assumption]

## Position
The financial case for closing rural primary schools in Hungary is weaker than commonly presented, because post-closure transport and indirect costs offset most apparent per-pupil savings, and the causal link to community decline remains empirically unresolved.
