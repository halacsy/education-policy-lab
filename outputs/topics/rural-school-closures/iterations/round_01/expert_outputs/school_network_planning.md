# Expert analysis: school_network_planning

## Findings (evidence)
- In Hungary, since the 2013 centralization under the Klebelsberg maintenance system (KLIK/KK), operational funding for primary schools is state-provided rather than per-pupil-driven at the municipal level, which changes but does not remove the economic pressure that low enrollment creates for small village schools. [evidence: moderate — Domain knowledge of Hungarian public education administration (2013 Köznevelési törvény / KLIK reform); NOT verified with a fresh web citation this round.]
- Hungarian legislation permits multi-grade composite classes (összevont osztály) in small schools, which is the standard structural adaptation allowing a village primary school to remain open below the enrollment level that would sustain single-grade classes. [evidence: moderate — Domain knowledge of Hungarian köznevelés practice (összevont osztály); exact current minimum-headcount thresholds NOT verified with a fresh web citation this round.]
- When a village primary school closes, pupils are typically reassigned to a nearby center school and transported by school bus or public transport, converting a fixed community cost into recurring commuting time and transport cost that falls disproportionately on the youngest children. [evidence: weak — General logic of school consolidation policy; specific Hungarian bussing distances/times NOT verified with a fresh web citation this round.]
- The village school building is often the only remaining public institution with a heatable, maintained hall in depopulating settlements, so its closure removes shared community infrastructure (elections, community events, meals) beyond the educational function. [evidence: weak — Domain knowledge of rural settlement infrastructure decline; NOT verified with a fresh web citation this round.]
- Small village schools frequently face teacher-supply constraints (difficulty attracting and retaining qualified subject teachers to low-enrollment sites), which is an independent viability threat distinct from headcount economics. [evidence: weak — Domain knowledge of rural teacher recruitment; Hungary-specific current data NOT verified with a fresh web citation this round.]

## Interpretation
The town-by-town feasibility question is not a single enrollment threshold but a trade-off among three variables: (1) the marginal cost of keeping a below-capacity building open with composite classes, (2) the commuting burden imposed on 6–12 year-olds if pupils are reassigned, and (3) the non-educational community value embedded in the building. Under centralized state funding, the economic decision has largely shifted from municipalities to the state maintainer, meaning local communities bear the community-loss cost while the state captures the efficiency saving — an incentive misalignment that argues for evaluating closures per settlement rather than by uniform rule.

## Assumptions
- The relevant settlements have under 2,000 inhabitants and operate lower-primary (grades 1–4) or full primary sites, consistent with the brief's scope. [assumption]
- A nearby center school with spare capacity exists within plausible bussing distance for most, but not all, closure candidates. [assumption]

## Position
Closure decisions for under-2,000-population village primary schools should be made settlement by settlement against explicit commuting-time limits and community-infrastructure value, not by a uniform national enrollment threshold.
