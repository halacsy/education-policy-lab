# Expert analysis: international_comparison

## Findings (evidence)
- Hungary operates parallel primary education tracks with selective 4- and 6-year gymnasia drawing higher-performing and higher-socioeconomic-status students at ages 10–12, earlier than typical OECD lower-secondary tracking age. [evidence: moderate — OECD/Eurydice country profiles (system feature; 2024 verification pending)]
- Hungary exhibits one of the OECD's steepest socioeconomic gradients in PISA performance across multiple cycles (2012–2018), meaning student achievement is highly stratified by family socioeconomic status. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA reports (2012, 2015, 2018 cycles; 2022–2024 update required)]
- Rural Hungarian 8-year primary schools retain a residual, negatively selected student population after higher-socioeconomic-status families opt into early-entry gymnasia, compounding enrollment decline with compositional disadvantage. [evidence: weak — logical inference from system structure (not empirically verified for rural cohorts this round)]
- School consolidation economics literature (US-focused) generally finds modest cost savings from merging small schools and diminishing or negative returns below certain enrollment thresholds once transportation and administration are included. [evidence: moderate — school consolidation economics literature (Duncombe & Yinger stream; specific DiD study verification needed)]
- Scotland's Schools (Consolidation and Parental Involvement) Act 2010 establishes a statutory presumption against rural school closure, requiring local authorities to conduct rigorous consultation and demonstrate no viable alternative before closure. [evidence: moderate — Scottish legislative text (2010; implementation record 2010–2025 not verified this round)]
- France's regroupement pédagogique intercommunal (RPI) model clusters several small rural municipalities' schools into a shared pedagogical network rather than closing them, with grade-splitting across sites or inter-village transport. [evidence: moderate — French education policy (established practice; quantitative uptake and outcomes not verified this round)]
- Norway maintains many small rural schools as explicit regional/settlement policy, treating school retention as an instrument of rural viability rather than purely as an education efficiency decision. [evidence: weak — Norwegian regional policy (general knowledge; specific statute and outcome data not verified this round)]

## Interpretation
Hungary's early tracking system (age 10–12 gymnasium entry) and steep PISA socioeconomic gradient mean that rural primary schools face a double enrollment squeeze: structural outflow of higher-SES students to urban/selective tracks, plus absolute demographic decline in villages. International comparison reveals two broad policy responses: (1) consolidation-as-default (US pattern, parts of Finland) with modest cost savings but transport/administrative risks, and (2) retention-with-alternative-structure (Scotland's statutory safeguard, France's RPI clustering, Norway's regional policy). Evidence on achievement effects of consolidation vs. smaller-school alternatives in similar (Central European, early-tracking) contexts is not yet located this round. Transferability to Hungary depends on whether rural school closure targets efficiency or serves as a symptom of abandoned rural settlement policy.

## Assumptions
- Hungarian rural primary schools serve settlements with chronic out-migration, making enrollment decline irreversible under status quo (no counterfactual policy intervention). [assumption]
- School closure decisions in Hungary reflect primarily resource efficiency logic rather than explicit rural development strategy. [assumption]
- Transferability of international models (Scotland, France, Norway) to rural Hungarian contexts requires compatible municipal governance structures and funding regimes. [assumption]
- PISA gradient and early-tracking patterns persist in current Hungarian education policy such that rural schools continue to experience negative selection. [assumption]

## Position
Rural Hungarian primary school closures should be preceded by an explicit rural development assessment and comparison of achievement/equity outcomes under consolidation versus retention-with-structural-alternatives (RPI-type clustering, multi-school governance, or scaled pedagogy), not decided on cost grounds alone.

## Uncertainties
- Current (2023–2025) enrollment and closure statistics for Hungarian rural primary schools: exact number of villages with <100 students, closure rate since 2015, post-closure student redistribution outcomes. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Hungarian Central Statistical Office (KSH) or Ministry of Education village-level school register with annual closure and enrollment time-series.)
- Causal estimates of consolidation effects on student achievement and equity in Central European (Hungarian, Slovak, Czech) or dual-tracking early-exit contexts: do students in consolidated or retained small schools differ in attainment? (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Difference-in-differences or regression-discontinuity study of school consolidation in a Central European country with matched pre/post enrollment and test-score data, ideally with subgroup analysis (rural vs. urban, SES quintiles).)
- Implementation and outcome evidence for Scotland's rural school closure presumption act (2010–2025): how many proposed closures were blocked, what happened in affected communities, and whether student outcomes improved or deteriorated under the statutory framework. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Scottish Government education impact reports, local authority closure decision logs, and PISA/national assessment data for schools in retained vs. consolidated rural areas.)
- Long-term community and settlement outcomes in villages where primary schools were closed: population migration, economic activity, housing values, and perceived social cohesion relative to villages retaining schools. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Longitudinal community-level dataset (10–20 years post-closure) linking school closure decisions to demographic, economic, and social indicators in Hungarian and/or Central European rural areas.)
