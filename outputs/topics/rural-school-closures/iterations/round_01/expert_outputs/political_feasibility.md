# Expert analysis: political_feasibility

## Findings (evidence)
- Hungary's education infrastructure maintenance shifted from municipal to central state authority following the 2013 establishment of the Klebelsberg Центр (KLIK), concentrating school-closure decisions at the national level rather than in village governments. [evidence: moderate — Hungarian education governance structure; public institutional records (KLIK founding decree 2013)]
- Church-maintained primary schools in rural areas serve as an alternative governance path that may preserve small-village schools when secular municipal operation becomes financially unviable. [evidence: moderate — Hungarian Catholic education expansion programme (observable institutional cases); structure of church-state education partnerships post-2010]
- Teachers' unions and local-government associations represent the primary organized constituencies historically mobilized against centralized school-closure policies in Hungary. [evidence: weak — Observed pattern in Hungarian education-policy discourse; not verified by current archival data or recent-round research]
- Rural depopulation creates reversal risk: once teachers disperse and school buildings are converted to other uses, reopening a closed primary school requires rebuilding human and physical capital simultaneously, making reversal politically and economically costly. [evidence: moderate — General institutional economics of school infrastructure; village-level case knowledge (not fresh-round sourced)]
- Rural villages form a core electoral constituency in Hungarian national politics, creating incentives for central government to avoid visible, concentrated school closures and instead pursue gradualist attrition or reclassification strategies. [evidence: weak — Structural political-economy knowledge; not independently verified in current round]
- The Magyar Falu Program (2018–present) includes school-related infrastructure components, but the causal link between programme funding and rural primary school viability is not empirically documented. [evidence: weak — Magyar Falu Program published framework documents; causal claim is unverified]
- Political durability of rural school closure decisions depends on whether closure is framed as demographic necessity (rural development) or as central-state efficiency imposed against community will, with the former generating less organized reversal pressure. [evidence: weak — Frame-analysis theory applied to Hungarian policy discourse; not empirically tested in current round]

## Interpretation
The political feasibility of rural school closure in Hungary is constrained by three structural factors: (1) centralized authority (KLIK) reduces local resistance capacity but increases national-level visibility and electoral risk; (2) alternative models (church maintenance, hybrid governance) exist but are not scaled or systematized; (3) reversal risk is high because dispersed teachers and repurposed buildings create path-dependency. The absence of publicly visible, large-scale closures suggests current policy relies on quiet attrition and reclassification—politically less costly but slower and harder to monitor. Durability favours framing closure as community-led or demographic rather than state-imposed efficiency, but this framing is vulnerable if villages mobilize electoral pressure. Church-maintained and mixed-governance models merit investigation as politically durable alternatives, but their scalability and cost-effectiveness remain uncharacterized.

## Assumptions
- Church-maintained schools in rural Hungary have access to sufficient capital and trained labour to operate independently of municipal subsidy; no data confirms this operational viability across village size and infrastructure condition. [assumption]
- Electoral incentives for rural-base protection are sufficient to prevent systematic, large-scale announced closures; however, quiet attrition and reclassification may achieve the same outcome without triggering mobilization. [assumption]
- The Magyar Falu Program is operationally linked to school-sustainability outcomes; this connection is assumed but not independently verified. [assumption]

## Position
Rural primary school closures in Hungary are politically more durable if framed as community-driven demographic adaptation rather than central-state efficiency, and if coupled with scalable alternative governance models (church maintenance, pedagogical networks) that reduce visibility and reversal risk.
