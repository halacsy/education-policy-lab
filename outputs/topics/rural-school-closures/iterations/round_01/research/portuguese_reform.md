1. **School-network consolidation ("reordenamento da rede escolar"):** From the mid-2000s (notably under Minister Maria de Lurdes Rodrigues, 2005–2009), Portugal closed thousands of small, isolated 1st-cycle (grades 1–4) rural primary schools, applying a minimum-enrollment rule (commonly cited as ~21 students) below which a school was merged into a larger "centro escolar." The number of standalone primary schools reportedly fell from well over 10,000 in the early 2000s to a few thousand within under a decade. **Evidence: moderate (recalled, not source-verified this round).**

2. **Centros escolares model:** Consolidation replaced village schools with larger, better-equipped multi-grade "school centers," intended to improve teacher specialization, peer-group size, and access to extracurricular/support services, but increased bus travel time for young children and was criticized for eroding the school-as-community-hub function. **Evidence: moderate; contested on community-impact side.**

3. **TEIP program (Territórios Educativos de Intervenção Prioritária):** Targeted-support scheme (since 1996, expanded as "TEIP2/TEIP3" from 2006–2012) channels extra resources, autonomy, and social-work/psychology support to schools in disadvantaged territories (many overlapping with declining rural/peri-urban areas). Associated with reduced dropout and some retention decline, though achievement gaps versus non-TEIP schools reportedly persisted. **Evidence: moderate.**

4. **Mega-agrupamentos (cluster mergers):** From ~2012–2013 onward, small school clusters were merged into larger "mega-agrupamentos" spanning pre-school through secondary, framed as enabling pedagogical continuity/vertical articulation and administrative efficiency, but criticized for increasing bureaucratic distance from individual village schools. **Evidence: weak-moderate.**

5. **Retention (grade repetition) reduction:** Portugal had among the highest OECD grade-repetition rates in the 2000s. Curriculum-flexibility reforms (Decree-Law 55/2018, "Autonomia e Flexibilidade Curricular," plus earlier "Projeto MAIA"-type measures) discouraged automatic retention in favor of individualized recovery plans; national retention rates reportedly fell substantially through the 2010s. Debate exists over whether this reflects genuine learning gains or grade-inflation/administrative pressure. **Evidence: moderate; contested interpretation.**

6. **PISA trajectory:** Portugal's PISA scores moved from below-OECD-average in the early 2000s to above-average by 2015, then dipped in 2022 alongside most countries (pandemic effect) — often cited as evidence that standards/curriculum/TEIP-type reforms improved outcomes without wholesale structural overhaul (no vouchers, no mass privatization). **Evidence: moderate (trend widely reported; exact 2022 figures not re-verified this round).**

7. **Municipalization of school-network decisions:** Transfer of 1st-cycle/pre-school competences to municipalities (generalized under Lei n.º 50/2018) gave local governments leverage to negotiate exceptions for isolated schools, sometimes topping up funding to keep small rural schools open as multi-service community hubs. **Evidence: weak (recalled framework, specifics uncertain).**

8. **Community-impact literature:** Portuguese rural-sociology/education-geography research (associated with bodies like CES-Coimbra or CNE opinions) has argued school closures accelerated village depopulation by removing a key anchor institution and young-family draw. **Evidence: weak-moderate, qualitative.**
