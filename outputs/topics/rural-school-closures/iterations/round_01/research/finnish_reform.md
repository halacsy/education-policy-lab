### Findings (prose, unverified this round)

1. **Peruskoulu reform (1972–1977)** replaced the parallel-track system with a unified 9-year comprehensive school, rolled out north-to-south starting deliberately in sparsely populated regions first — bundling curriculum unification, municipal maintenance duties, and free meals/transport, not just a structural merger.

2. **1979 teacher-education reform** moved all class-teacher training to research-based university master's degrees — widely regarded as the load-bearing carrier of later quality/equity outcomes, meaning even single-teacher village schools get uniformly highly-trained staff, not a lower rural tier.

3. **Municipal ownership + national equalization**: schools are run by municipalities (kunta), but state per-pupil transfers have historically been augmented for sparsely populated municipalities to offset small-school diseconomies of scale.

4. **Statutory school-transport guarantee**: free transport/accommodation is guaranteed once a pupil's journey exceeds a set threshold — converting "close the small school" into an explicit cost-shift to municipal transport budgets, a mechanism directly relevant to the Hungarian trade-off.

5. **Three-tier support model** (yleinen/tehostettu/erityinen tuki), formalized 2010–2011, was designed partly to keep pupils with additional needs in their local (often small) school via itinerant specialists and shared multi-professional teams rather than one specialist per building.

6. **Long-run school-network consolidation**: the number of comprehensive schools has declined substantially since the 1990s via municipal mergers and demographic decline — a decades-long trend, not a recent shock. *No verified current count available this round.*

7. **Equity literature attributes Finland's low between-school variance to system-wide uniformity** (curriculum, teacher training, comprehensive structure) rather than to preserving individual small-school buildings — suggesting the transferable lesson for Hungary may be systemic uniformity, not building-level preservation.

8. **Transferability caveat (interpretive, not sourced)**: Finland's reform relied on compact, low-corruption municipal governance, high teacher trust, and a settlement pattern with fewer extreme-periphery villages than some Hungarian regions — structural preconditions, not directly importable as a discrete policy instrument.
