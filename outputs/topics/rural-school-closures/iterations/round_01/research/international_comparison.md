## Research Notes (Round 1) — International Comparison Domain

**1. Hungary's early/dual tracking structure.** Hungary operates parallel primary tracks: standard 8-grade general schools alongside selective 4- and 6-year gimnáziums that begin drawing off higher-performing/higher-SES students at ages 10–12, well before the OECD-typical lower-secondary tracking age. This structural feature (documented repeatedly in OECD/Eurydice country profiles) is directly relevant to rural viability: rural 8-year schools tend to retain a residual population after urban/higher-SES families opt into early academic tracks, compounding rural enrollment decline with negative selection. Evidence: moderate (well-documented system feature, not a fresh statistic).

**2. Hungary's PISA socioeconomic gradient.** Across multiple PISA cycles (2012, 2015, 2018), OECD reporting has consistently placed Hungary among the OECD countries with the steepest relationship between socioeconomic status and student performance — i.e., one of the strongest "PISA gradients." This is a repeated cross-cycle finding, not a one-off statistic, so I grade it strong in direction though I cannot cite the exact 2022-cycle coefficient without verification. Evidence: strong (recurring OECD finding); source needs re-verification for latest cycle.

**3. School consolidation economics literature (US-focused).** The education-economics consolidation literature (associated with work such as Duncombe & Yinger's "economies of size" research stream) generally finds that cost savings from consolidating already-small schools are modest and that below a certain size threshold, further consolidation yields diminishing or even negative returns once transportation and administrative overhead are counted. This is a general literature pattern, not a single verified citation. Evidence: moderate/contested — needs a specific up-to-date DiD study to firm up.

**4. Finland's rural school network contraction.** Finland is widely cited in comparative education discussions as having closed a large share of its small comprehensive schools over recent decades amid municipal restructuring, making it a frequently invoked comparison case for "what happens after consolidation." Evidence: weak as stated here (recalled pattern, no current figure verified).

**5. Scotland's statutory presumption against rural school closure.** Scotland's Schools (Consolidation and Parental Involvement) Act 2010 is a real, specific statute requiring local authorities to follow a rigorous consultation process and demonstrate no viable alternative before closing a rural school — a concrete transferable policy model for Hungary. Evidence: moderate (recalled specific law; exact current implementation record not verified this round).

**6. France's RPI (regroupement pédagogique intercommunal) model.** France has a long-standing practice of clustering several small rural municipalities' schools into a shared pedagogical network (RPI) rather than closing them outright, splitting grade-levels across sites or bussing between a small number of linked villages. This is a well-known, transferable alternative-to-closure model. Evidence: moderate (established policy practice; no fresh statistic).

**7. Norway's small-school tradition tied to regional policy.** Norway maintains many very small rural schools as a matter of explicit regional/settlement policy (geography-driven, given fjords/islands), treating school retention as an instrument of rural-viability policy rather than purely an education-efficiency decision. Evidence: weak/assumption-leaning (general knowledge, not a specific verified source).
