# Rejected framings

Candidate framings generated per scenario; one selected, the rest recorded here with the reason for rejection.

## S1
- CHOSEN: Status quo plus incremental adaptation with targeted teacher-training infrastructure and transparent local-negotiation standards
- REJECTED: Status quo plus incremental adaptation with minimal intervention — reason: The received devil_advocate and coherence_checker feedback expose a critical internal contradiction: the scenario assumes 'teacher supply for multigrade classrooms is adequate without systemic training reform,' yet explicitly schedules multi-year recurring in-service training in the implementation steps. A framing that minimizes intervention would deepen this incoherence. The chosen framing acknowledges that multigrade pedagogy requires deliberate, structured teacher-preparation investment, not passive adequacy.
- REJECTED: Status quo plus incremental adaptation relying on ad hoc case-by-case local negotiation without data infrastructure — reason: The assumption_checker feedback notes that local decision-makers lack 'comparable financial and demographic data to negotiate fair, transparent retention arrangements' because no national registry exists. A framing anchored to ad hoc negotiation would perpetuate the data-accessibility problem and risk widening equity gaps between well-organized and poorly-organized settlements. The chosen framing includes explicit transparency and data-disclosure requirements to offset this structural disadvantage.

## S2
- CHOSEN: Structural grade reorganization with cross-party entrenchment safeguards and rigorous transport-welfare validation
- REJECTED: Structural grade reorganization modeled closely on Poland's 1999 pattern without amendment — reason: The received political_risk_checker feedback identifies that Poland's 1999 reform was reversed after 17 years and notes Hungary lacks any documented cross-party consensus mechanism. A direct-transfer framing would ignore this reversibility risk and transplant a proven-unstable model without addressing the political-entrenchment gap. The chosen framing insists on explicit cross-party safeguards absent from the original Polish case and absent from the S2 as received.
- REJECTED: Structural grade reorganization paired with existing transport subsidies, assuming current infrastructure is adequate — reason: The received assumption_checker and equity_checker feedback highlight documented informal parent-organized escort shifts because regional buses refuse unaccompanied minors, indicating current transport safeguards are already strained. A framing relying on 'existing' transport would ignore welfare data that demonstrates current arrangements are inadequate. The chosen framing mandates route-specific safety and capacity audits before reassignment, treating current arrangements as a failure point rather than an adequate baseline.

## S3
- CHOSEN: Networked retention via association-run and multi-service models with dedicated capacity-building support for low-administrative-capacity villages
- REJECTED: Networked retention via association-run and multi-service models as a universally applicable alternative — reason: The received equity_checker feedback identifies a risk that administrative-capacity requirements 'will systematically disadvantage the poorest, least-organized villages' without targeted support. A universally applicable framing would replicate existing inequalities by allowing well-organized settlements to access the model while excluding the most vulnerable. The chosen framing insists on dedicated capacity-building grants, model-contract templates, and legal/accounting support targeted at low-capacity municipalities to prevent this systematic exclusion.
- REJECTED: Networked retention via association-run models, assuming Polish-style sustainability is transferable directly to Hungary's post-2013 tankerület centralization — reason: The received assumption_checker feedback notes Poland retained gmina-level local school-ownership authority that Hungary's municipalities lost after 2013 under tankerület/KLIK centralization. A framing assuming direct transferability would ignore a fundamental structural difference in governance autonomy. The chosen framing requires explicit legal-comparative analysis and legislative amendment to clarify whether the association-run mechanism is genuinely available under current Hungarian law, treating transferability as contingent rather than assumed.

## S4
- CHOSEN: Consolidation with binding equity thresholds, disaggregated outcome monitoring, and enforceable transport-safety standards
- REJECTED: Consolidation with TEIP-style compensatory measures as typically implemented (aggregate, equity-blind outcome reporting) — reason: The received equity_checker feedback notes that S4 'omits any requirement that compensatory measures capture and publish disaggregated equity data' by ethnicity and income. The scenario's own evidence (Portugal's 15-year TEIP outcomes) shows that aggregate compensatory programmes failed to close performance gaps. A framing based on typical TEIP implementation would replicate that failure pattern. The chosen framing mandates disaggregated monitoring by ethnicity, income, and settlement size, with binding enforcement of compensatory-measure funding, treating equity-blind implementation as insufficient.
- REJECTED: Consolidation with cost mitigation paired with advisory (non-binding) transport and support standards — reason: The received cost_checker and political_risk_checker feedback highlight that 'compensatory measures (transport guarantees, TEIP-style support) require sustained multi-year funding' and risk being 'cut in future budget cycles once the initial closure has already occurred.' A framing relying on advisory standards would leave these protections vulnerable to discretionary abandonment. The chosen framing specifies binding, ring-fenced budget allocation and enforceable thresholds (e.g., maximum travel time modeled on Portugal's 25-minute rule) to prevent post-closure defunding.

## S5
- CHOSEN: National systemic framework with mandatory disaggregated equity-data reporting, explicit anti-instrumentalization safeguards, and cross-party funding commitment mechanisms
- REJECTED: National systemic framework as a procedurally neutral, aggregate transparency mechanism — reason: The received equity_checker feedback warns that 'if it is implemented as an aggregate, ethnicity-blind system, it risks providing a veneer of evidence-based legitimacy to decisions that remain as inequitable as under the status quo.' The political_risk_checker feedback identifies that the registry 'may be politically instrumentalized: government or tankerület actors could selectively cite thresholds to justify predetermined closure decisions while ignoring them when politically inconvenient.' An aggregate, neutral framing would enable exactly this instrumentalization. The chosen framing requires disaggregated reporting by ethnicity, income, and settlement size as mandatory from inception, and includes explicit process-tracing requirements and published decision-rule algorithms to prevent selective citation.
- REJECTED: National systemic framework relying on sustained political will and administrative continuity across government administrations — reason: The received political_risk_checker feedback notes the 'documented absence of cross-party consensus mechanisms' and 'chronic under-budgeting pattern' in the tankerület system. A framing anchored to sustained political will would rely on a motivation documented to be absent. The chosen framing treats political will as unreliable and instead specifies binding funding-commitment mechanisms (ring-fenced budget lines, multi-year statutory allocations, possibly constitutional-level protection) to insulate the registry and training infrastructure from budget-cycle and administration-change vulnerability.
