# Expert analysis: school_network_planning

## Findings (evidence)
- Hungary's primary-school enrollment declined by 0.3% annually (2023/24 to 2024/25), representing a 17.3% cumulative fall over 20 years from 859,000 students in 2005/06 to 710,000 in 2024/25. [evidence: strong — KSH (Central Statistical Office) via Népszava, 2025]
- The number of school locations operating primary education fell from 3,550 in 2021/22 to 3,537 in 2024/25, a loss of 13 sites in a single year and 88 over three years. [evidence: strong — KSH via Népszava, 2025]
- Hungary's total fertility rate fell below 1.3 by August 2025, down from 1.55 in 2023, far below the 2.1 replacement-level threshold. [evidence: strong — Economx, 2025]
- Five counties experienced population loss exceeding 1% in 2024 alone (one resident per hundred disappearing annually), concentrated in five regions: three in eastern Hungary and two in western regions (Vas and Zala counties). [evidence: strong — Válasz Online, 2025]
- As of 2020, 44% of Hungarian settlements had no lower-primary school (grades 1–4) and 52% had no upper-primary school (grades 5–8) available locally. [evidence: moderate — Edunext citing official education data, 2020]
- The share of primary-school students attending schools with fewer than 100 pupils grew from 5.5% nationally in 2010 to 8.5% in 2020, reaching 15–22% in Békés, Nógrád, and Tolna counties, versus under 5% in Budapest and Pest county. [evidence: strong — Edunext citing official national statistics, 2020]
- Hungary's National Public Education Act sets a minimum class size of 14 pupils for grades 1–4; falling below this threshold forces grade merging or student transfer, creating a hard legal boundary for stand-alone school viability. [evidence: strong — Eduline citing National Public Education Act]
- Between 1988 and 2005, Hungary lost nearly 4,000 classrooms nationally while the count of school sites did not fall proportionately, indicating that consolidation-without-closure (satellite-campus downgrading) has been the dominant adjustment mechanism rather than outright shutdowns. [evidence: moderate — Bódi Ferenc – Fekete Attila, institutional research, cited in ELTE, 2000s]
- Finnish difference-in-differences analysis (2010–2018) and German (Saxony) studies found that school closures precede and may accelerate population decline, with lagged effects becoming statistically significant 6+ years after closure, driven by lock-in effects in social capital and housing markets. [evidence: moderate — ResearchGate: Finnish and Saxony comparative studies cited in Hungarian rural-education analysis]
- Since 2021, the nationalization of schools shifted control from local municipalities to centralized authorities, significantly reducing municipalities' autonomy in responding to local educational needs, particularly in Western Transdanubia and low-density Northern Hungarian regions. [evidence: moderate — ResearchGate preprint on post-2021 territorial patterns in Hungarian education, 2025]
- Small schools (under 150 pupils) comprise nearly one-third of municipal primary schools yet enroll fewer than 10% of all students; below 100 pupils, class sizes are half and student-teacher ratios two-thirds of urban-school values, but this difference does not alone justify treating small-settlement schools as a serious efficiency problem. [evidence: moderate — OFI (Institute for Educational Research) analysis of unit-cost economics based on Hermann (2007) data]
- Teachers' Union president warned that within five to ten years population decline could force serious systemic rethinking, with several general schools already recording zero first-grade enrollments. [evidence: strong — Totyik Tamás (PSZ), Népszava interview, 2025]
- In a post-COVID survey (Ipsos Omnibusz, n=1,000), three-quarters of families with children reported that temporary or permanent school/kindergarten closure would create significant difficulty, and 45% said childcare would be unsolvable even short-term; however, the survey context and COVID-era republication raise questions about applicability to current depopulation-driven scenarios. [evidence: weak — Ipsos Hungary, republished but COVID-era context]
- Expert disagreement exists on commuting-tolerance thresholds by grade level: Trencsényi László (Hungarian Pedagogical Society) stressed the burden of half-asleep first-graders on 6 a.m. buses, while Setényi János (Mathias Corvinus Collegium) argued that lower-primary should remain local but upper-primary (grades 5–8) students should commute to large, well-equipped district centers. [evidence: contested — Edunext, citing Trencsényi and Setényi expert statements]
- Government commissioner Balatoni Katalin stated the goal is not closure but 'protecting small schools as long as there is demand,' implicitly acknowledging that major restructuring via mergers and local solutions is forthcoming. [evidence: moderate — Válasz Online, ministerial statement, 2025]

## Interpretation
Hungary faces a convergent demographic and institutional crisis: fertility collapse (below 1.3) and regional population loss exceeding 1% annually are shrinking the primary-school population by 17.3% over two decades, while legal class-size floors (14 pupils) and operational economics are forcing closure or radical restructuring of small schools. The adjustment so far has been silent consolidation (classrooms disappearing faster than school counts), but teacher unions and government signals indicate that major systemic rethinking is imminent. Expert disagreement persists on where commuting tolerances should lie by grade level, and comparative evidence from Finland and Germany suggests that school closures may accelerate rather than merely reflect population decline, creating lock-in effects that further weaken rural settlement attractiveness. Municipalities' loss of autonomy post-2021 has reduced their capacity to adapt locally. The policy tension is genuine: small schools entail higher per-pupil costs and staffing ratios, yet serve as community anchors, and their closure risks triggering secondary depopulation.

## Assumptions
- The 2020 settlement-level schooling-access data (44% without lower-primary, 52% without upper-primary locally) accurately represents the current baseline, though demographic shifts since 2020 may have worsened the pattern. [assumption]
- The comparison of classroom and school-site shrinkage (classrooms fell ~4,000 versus school sites' stable/minimal decline) accurately captures the scale of historical consolidation-without-closure; alternative explanations (e.g., larger average class sizes in remaining schools) have not been ruled out. [assumption]
- The Finnish and German lagged-population-decline findings apply to the Hungarian context with sufficient methodological fidelity, despite differences in education-governance structure and rural demographics. [assumption]
- The 2021 centralization of school governance materially reduced municipal autonomy in a way comparable to the shift being analyzed; the extent of this reduction is inferred rather than quantified. [assumption]
- Commuting-tolerance thresholds (expert disagreement between grades 1–4 local vs. 5–8 bussed) meaningfully vary by child age and family circumstance in ways that a single policy rule cannot optimize. [assumption]
- Survey-based estimates of family hardship from school closure (45% childcare unsolvable) reflect sentiment rather than binding constraint; actual behavior under closure might differ from survey response. [assumption]

## Position
Rural primary-school closures in demographically collapsing villages of under 2,000 residents risk accelerating population decline by severing community cohesion and family-settlement decisions, and are justified primarily by per-pupil cost differences that do not constitute a serious system-wide efficiency problem, making alternative models (satellite campuses, mixed-grade instruction, or controlled consolidation with local hub-school retention) preferable to outright closure.

## Uncertainties
- The causal direction between school closure and population decline remains incompletely resolved: does closure primarily follow depopulation (selection effect), or does it trigger secondary out-migration (treatment effect)? Finnish and Saxony studies suggest the latter with a 6+ year lag, but the mechanism and applicability to Hungarian administrative and cultural context are not definitively established. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Longitudinal matched-pair or synthetic-control panel data tracking settlements with and without closures (controlling for pre-closure population trends, economic structure, and distance to urban centers) over 10+ years in Hungary would isolate the causal impact.)
- The economic sustainability threshold—the minimum school size or village population below which per-pupil costs become budgetarily unmanageable—is not precisely defined in current Hungarian policy or research; this threshold varies by property condition, staffing flexibility, and central-subsidy design. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Detailed micro-level financial audits of a stratified sample of small schools (50–150 pupils) in different regions, with transparent accounting of building maintenance, teacher-salary spreading, and realistic alternative arrangements (mergers, satellite campuses, multi-grade classes), would establish the true sustainability boundary.)
