# Expert analysis: international_comparison

## Findings (evidence)
- In OECD countries, the rural-urban achievement gap in PISA largely disappears after controlling for socio-economic status; in several countries (Estonia, Finland, Lithuania, Spain, US), rural students would outperform urban peers if socio-economic profiles were equalized. [evidence: strong — OECD Education Working Paper No. 196 (2019), Learning in Rural Schools: Insights from PISA, TALIS and the Literature]
- Rural students maintain lower university completion expectations than urban peers even at equal socio-economic status, indicating an aspirations gap distinct from an achievement gap. [evidence: moderate — OECD Working Paper 196 (2019); ERIC ED594134]
- Difference-in-differences evidence on rural school closures and local population decline is contested: Finnish data (2010–2018) shows closures precede depopulation, but studies from Saxony, Sweden, and Denmark find no significant population effect. [evidence: contested — Journal of Rural Studies (2021) — Finnish study; Barakat (2015) Saxony; Amcoff (2012) Sweden]
- In Hungary, the 2004 Multi-Functional Micro-Regional Local Government Associations (MLGAs) reform rechanneled state incentives, causing the number of villages maintaining primary schools to drop by approximately half within four years; prior to 2004, closures were rare despite lower per-pupil state funding in small schools. [evidence: strong — Kovács, K. (2012), Journal of Rural Studies 28; Hungarian education policy analysis]
- Between 2021 and 2024, Hungarian primary-education school sites fell from 3,609 to 3,521; geographical concentrations of disadvantaged students exceeding 70–92% have emerged in Northern Hungary and Southern Transdanubia, linked to closures in peripheral areas and residential mobility toward advantaged localities. [evidence: moderate — Shrinking, growing or overburdened? Territorial patterns in child demographics and institutional burden in Hungary's public education system after 2021 (2025); KIR data]
- Czech residents of villages with operating schools report they would be hard-pressed to lose them, but only 17% of inhabitants in municipalities without a primary school consider its absence a major shortcoming, suggesting attachment to schools is partly retrospective and symbolic rather than reflecting current practical necessity. [evidence: weak — Bernard, Joukl, Vítková (2022), Czech empirical study cited in Barakat (2015) Saxony review]
- In Hungary and Romania, rural communities with operating primary schools report higher average welfare levels than those without; both countries document negative community-level impacts from closures, though causality remains unclear and closure communities do not necessarily have the worst objective living conditions. [evidence: moderate — Bulletin UASVM Cluj-Napoca (2016), Presence or Absence of Schools: What Impact for Rural Communities in Hungary And Romania?]
- Multigrade teaching models and small-school pedagogies based on peer learning and individualized instruction, long established in rural Nordic schools, are increasingly adopted in urban systems as deliberate didactic tools rather than mere resource constraints. [evidence: moderate — OECD Working Paper 196 (2019); Springer chapter Multi-grade Teaching in a Small Rural School in Northern Norway (2022)]
- US research on school closure and student achievement is mixed; negative effects can be reduced or eliminated if displaced students transition to high-quality receiving schools, but few studies focus explicitly on rural or suburban closure contexts. [evidence: moderate — Research for Action (2024), Revisiting Research on School Closings: Key Learnings for District and Community Leaders]

## Interpretation
International evidence on rural school closures reveals a complex landscape: achievement gaps between rural and urban students largely reflect socio-economic disparities rather than school quality, yet rural students face distinct aspirations deficits. The causal link between closure and population decline is contested—Finland shows a closure-depopulation association, but Saxony, Sweden, and Denmark do not. Hungary experienced dramatic consolidation after its 2004 policy shift, with recent data confirming ongoing geographic concentration of disadvantage. Community attachment to schools appears partly symbolic; residents of villages without schools report fewer practical losses than residents in villages with schools predict they would suffer. Multigrade and small-school pedagogies are effective tools, not mere stopgaps. The evidence does not support a simple closure-as-depopulation narrative, but neither does it vindicate closures as neutral on community welfare or equity outcomes.

## Assumptions
- School availability and local community welfare are linked through mechanisms other than purely demographic retention—e.g., symbolic anchor, local services clustering, fiscal stimulus—whose strength varies by national context. [assumption]
- Rural disadvantage in Hungary reflects both closure-driven consolidation and broader economic restructuring; isolating the closure effect requires careful causal inference not fully available in current descriptive studies. [assumption]
- Multigrade and small-school pedagogies can be implemented and scaled with adequate teacher training and curricular support; without these, they risk becoming low-floor, low-ceiling delivery systems for disadvantaged students. [assumption]
- Transport distance, travel time, and care-work burden for rural families are material constraints that differ qualitatively from urban consolidation scenarios; their impact on educational equity cannot be assumed to mirror urban contexts. [assumption]

## Position
Rural school closure in Hungary is not a demographic inevitability with uniform effects; its equity and community impact depends on the receiving school's quality, transport accessibility, and whether alternatives like multigrade teaching are actively resourced rather than passively imposed.

## Uncertainties
- What is the Hungarian-specific causal effect of school closure on local population retention, fertility, or outmigration, isolated from broader rural economic decline? (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A matched difference-in-differences study comparing villages with and without school closures, controlling for pre-closure demographic and economic trends, 2000–2025.)
