# Expert analysis: portuguese_reform

## Findings (evidence)
- Portugal closed 4,197 primary schools between 2005/2006 and 2012/2013, initially targeting schools with fewer than 10 students (2005), then expanding to schools with fewer than 21 students (2010). [evidence: strong — PÚBLICO: Escolas primárias encerradas desde 2005]
- In Portugal's Central Region, 90% of all school closures (1,981 establishments) occurred in 2005–2014, with the process stabilizing after 2015; the Viseu Dão Lafões inter-municipal area alone lost 527 establishments (2005–2022). [evidence: strong — DGEstE (2024): Espaços e práticas educativas em transformação — 20 anos da Rede Escolar da Região Centro]
- In Trás-os-Montes (Northern Portugal), primary schools fell from 651 to 161 between 2000 and 2006/2007, representing one of the most affected regions under the sub-10-student closure threshold. [evidence: strong — Diário de Notícias (2008): Quatro mil escolas fechadas em 8 anos]
- Of nearly 4,000 closed Portuguese schools surveyed, approximately 20% remain unused; the remainder have been converted into museums, libraries, theatres, cafés, restaurants, police posts, or chapels, with about 40 schools in cultural venues and 100+ nationally in choirs/bands. [evidence: moderate — PÚBLICO: Escolas primárias encerradas desde 2005]
- Sociologist Rui d'Espiney claims that removing children from villages accelerated rural desertification and village isolation, though this is an advocacy expert assertion rather than a controlled empirical study. [evidence: contested — PÚBLICO and Diário de Notícias (D'Espiney, Instituto de Comunidades Educativas)]
- The largest Portuguese teachers' union (FENPROF) does not demand blanket preservation of small schools but requires closures to have community consensus, safeguards on travel duration/comfort/safety, and negotiated timing so replacement schools have proper facilities before transfer. [evidence: moderate — FENPROF: Encerramento de escolas e reorganização escolar]
- Portugal's TEIP (Territórios Educativos de Intervenção Prioritária) compensatory programme, serving 17% of school clusters in disadvantaged areas, reduced dropout and failure rates but failed over 15 years to reduce the performance gap in national exams between TEIP and non-TEIP schools. [evidence: strong — MDPI Education Sciences (2019) and Procedia Social and Behavioral Sciences (2014)]
- Portugal's Programa Nacional de Promoção do Sucesso Escolar (PNPSE, launched 2016) achieved a 29% reduction in basic-education retention rates by the end of 2016–2018 without closing or merging schools, using locally designed action plans instead. [evidence: strong — PNPSE official site (pnpse.min-educ.pt) and 2019 Moncão conference document]
- About 70% of Portuguese students travel to school by motorized modes (car/bus), rising to approximately 83% among rural students specifically, indicating that school consolidation increases motorized transport dependency for rural children. [evidence: moderate — Universidade do Minho repository (2015) thesis on school transport]
- Fundão municipality explicitly set a 25-minute travel-time threshold in its closure-decision matrix, preventing school closure beyond that limit and forcing broader deliberation on school consolidation in low-density territories. [evidence: moderate — Câmara Municipal do Fundão: Reorganização da Rede Escolar em Territórios de Baixa Densidade]
- Portugal's 2005/2006 school network reorganization paired the closure of 1,418 schools with construction of 800 larger, better-equipped 'centros escolares' (consolidated schools), while some rural schools retained multigrade classrooms as a lower-cost alternative. [evidence: moderate — Diário de Notícias (2008) and Fundão municipal documentation]
- Portuguese national standardized retention-reduction interventions like PNPSE demonstrated measurable success without structural school network changes, suggesting non-structural pedagogical reforms can address educational outcomes in low-density areas. [evidence: moderate — PNPSE official site and research papers cited]
- Portugal's 20-year experience shows that mass school closures in low-density regions (2005–2014) were followed by policy stabilization and shift toward targeted interventions and transport safeguards, not further consolidation. [evidence: moderate — DGEstE (2024) regional analysis and FENPROF policy documents]
- In Portugal's Alcoutim municipality, some closed school buildings were repurposed as chapels, demonstrating local agency in redefining community use of former school facilities in depopulating areas. [evidence: weak — PÚBLICO: Escolas primárias encerradas desde 2005]
- Teacher union position in Portugal distinguishes between structural school closures and operational safeguards (travel time, facilities timing, community voice), implying that closure itself is not the primary teacher concern but rather the conditions and pace of implementation. [evidence: moderate — FENPROF public statements and policy documents]
- Portuguese TEIP research shows a discrepancy: dropout/failure rates fell but national exam performance gaps between TEIP and non-TEIP schools persisted, suggesting that access and retention alone do not guarantee learning outcome equity. [evidence: strong — MDPI Education Sciences (2019) and Procedia Social and Behavioral Sciences (2014)]

## Interpretation
Portugal's two-decade experience with rural primary school consolidation reveals a complex trade-off: mass closures (4,197 schools in 2005–2013, concentrated heavily in 2005–2014) were justified on efficiency grounds but generated contested claims about community impact and did not demonstrably improve learning outcomes. The TEIP program, which addressed disadvantage in remaining schools, reduced dropout but failed to close the performance gap, indicating that structural closure alone—nor compensatory resource allocation in consolidated sites—guarantees educational equity. Conversely, PNPSE (non-structural, locally designed retention-reduction plans) achieved measurable success without network changes, suggesting that pedagogical reform and teacher agency can move outcomes in low-density areas. Repurposing of closed buildings (museums, cafés, chapels) shows local communities exercise agency in redefining civic space, though 20% remain unused, indicating incomplete social integration. Recent Portuguese policy (2015 onward) moved from aggressive closure to safeguarded thresholds (25-minute travel time), multigrade alternatives, and paired investment in larger, better-equipped centers—a model that acknowledges both fiscal and community-cohesion concerns. For Hungary, the key insight is that closure intensity does not automatically drive learning gains; instead, conditions of consolidation (travel burden, stakeholder voice, pedagogical support, facility quality, pacing) and non-structural reforms targeting retention and teaching quality appear to account for measurable outcomes. The transfer risk is assuming closure + consolidation alone will solve resource constraints without implementing accompanying safeguards, teacher retraining, and local deliberation.

## Assumptions
- That Hungary's depopulating villages and low-density rural regions present demographic and administrative conditions sufficiently similar to Portugal's Trás-os-Montes, Viseu, and Alcoutim regions to permit transfer of findings. [assumption]
- That outcome measures (retention rate, learning gaps, test scores) used in Portuguese studies are comparable to the measures Hungary would use to evaluate rural school success. [assumption]
- That absence of large-scale controlled trials on closure impact in Portugal does not mean the PNPSE and TEIP quantitative findings are invalid for Hungary; transfer is credible if mechanisms (teacher agency, local planning, pedagogical focus) are system-invariant. [assumption]
- That the 2005–2014 Portuguese closure policy represented a policy choice, not an inevitable demographic necessity, and therefore lessons about safeguards and alternatives are actionable in Hungary. [assumption]
- That village repurposing, as evidenced in Portuguese closures (museums, chapels, cafés), reflects genuine community investment, not merely post-hoc rationalizations of facility loss. [assumption]
- That teacher union positions (FENPROF) reflect educator ground-truth about implementation risk and safeguard adequacy, not mere defensive institutional interests. [assumption]
- That PNPSE's success was driven by local agency and pedagogical redesign, not by unmeasured contextual factors (e.g., economic recovery, teacher cohort changes) that may not be replicable. [assumption]
- That rural transport infrastructure and vehicle availability in Hungary are comparable enough to Portuguese conditions that the 70–83% motorization finding and travel-time thresholds are meaningful analogues. [assumption]

## Position
Portugal's experience shows that school closure intensity from 2005–2014 did not automatically improve learning outcomes, and conditions of consolidation (travel burden, facility quality, community voice, pedagogical support) matter more for rural student access and equity than closure decisions alone.

## Uncertainties
- To what extent did Portugal's school closures directly cause village depopulation, versus depopulation driving closure demand? The causal direction is contested in existing sources (sociologist advocacy claims versus no quantitative longitudinal study). (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A quasi-experimental study comparing closure timing with village demographic trajectories; or a before–after panel of migration rates in villages with and without closed schools, controlling for economic shocks.)
- What proportion of Hungarian depopulating villages have the demographic and geographic characteristics (sub-2,000 population, travel distance > 25 minutes to next school) that would align with Portuguese closure scenarios? No baseline census data on rural school catchment distance has been cited. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Hungarian Central Statistical Office (KSH) or Ministry of Education spatial analysis of rural school locations, catchment populations, and inter-school distances in target depopulating regions.)
