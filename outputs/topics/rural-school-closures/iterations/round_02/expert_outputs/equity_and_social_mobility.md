# Expert analysis: equity_and_social_mobility

## Findings (evidence)
- More than 1,200 of 3,155 Hungarian settlements (38%) had no public educational institutions by 2024, creating territorial exclusion from primary education access. [evidence: strong — Shrinking, growing or overburdened? Territorial patterns in child demographics and institutional burden in Hungary's public education system after 2021 (2025), https://www.researchgate.net/publication/396483025]
- Disadvantaged Roma children are increasingly concentrated in rural settlements (up to 94% in some areas), while rural schools face disproportionate teacher shortages with 2–3 times higher rates of unqualified teachers than non-disadvantaged schools. [evidence: strong — EU Education and Training Monitor 2025 – Hungary, https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html; Shrinking, growing or overburdened? (2025)]
- Nearly 300 'ghetto schools' with over 50% Roma enrollment exist in Hungary; three-quarters operate in villages, with nearly half concentrated in Northern Hungary, where school-choice restrictions historically limited segregation. [evidence: strong — Education of Roma and Educational Resilience in Hungary, Springer (2020), https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-52588-0_6; CEU Working Paper, The Wider Context of the Segregation of Roma Pupils in Hungary (2020)]
- Roma student dropout rates (58.7% for 18–24-year-olds) are nearly 6 times higher than non-Roma rates (9.3%), with rural residence, Roma ethnicity, and disability constituting the most vulnerable early-leaver profile. [evidence: strong — National Policies Platform YouthWiki Hungary 4.1, https://national-policies.eacea.ec.europa.eu/youthwiki/chapters/hungary/41-general-context]
- In a Chilean natural experiment, school closures increased high-school dropout probability by 49–68 percent (1.8–2.5 percentage points), with larger effects on grade repetition and long-term earnings losses especially for low-income students. [evidence: moderate — Grau, Hojman & Mizala (2018), cited in arXiv:2203.17101, https://arxiv.org/pdf/2203.17101; Rural Schools Open, https://ruralschoolsopen.org/understand-closure/]
- Using Serbian war-shock school closures as a natural experiment, Kóczán (2024) found first-graders at closure experienced 7–9 percent earnings reductions 20 years later, with larger losses for those in the bottom half of income distribution, indicating persistent long-run inequality effects. [evidence: moderate — Lasting scars: The long-term effects of school closures on earnings, World Development 176 (2024), https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0305750X23003327]
- Disadvantaged pupils in small classes (Tennessee STAR experiment) outperformed regular-class peers by 0.22 standard deviations; benefits were largest for Black students, economically disadvantaged students, and boys. [evidence: moderate — Brookings, Class Size: What Research Says and What It Means for State Policy, https://www.brookings.edu/articles/class-size-what-research-says-and-what-it-means-for-state-policy/]
- In Hungary, residential segregation does not mechanically produce educational segregation: free parental school choice allows non-Roma and wealthier families to exit consolidated rural hub schools, reproducing segregation at a different spatial scale ('white flight'). [evidence: moderate — CEU Working Paper, The Wider Context of the Segregation of Roma Pupils in Hungary (2020), https://cps.ceu.edu/sites/cps.ceu.edu/files/attachment/publication/3258/cps-working-paper-educ-social-selection-education-hungary-2020.pdf]
- Historically, Hungarian local governments managed population decline in small rural schools by reducing parallel classes (multi-grade consolidation) rather than full closure, which maintained small-school infrastructure but limited within-school separation options for minority pupils. [evidence: moderate — CEU Working Paper, The Wider Context of the Segregation of Roma Pupils in Hungary (2020)]

## Interpretation
Rural primary school closures in Hungary pose a compounded equity and social mobility risk. The existing landscape already concentrates disadvantage: 38% of settlements lack any school, disadvantaged Roma children are over-represented in rural areas, and rural schools suffer acute teacher shortages and lower qualification levels. Evidence from Chile and Serbia shows closures increase dropout risk and produce long-term earnings losses concentrated among low-income students—effects that would compound the existing vulnerability of rural Roma pupils facing 58.7% dropout rates. Small classes benefit disadvantaged learners disproportionately, suggesting retention of small rural schools may protect this population. However, a consolidation strategy risks triggering 'white flight' (non-Roma parental exit), leaving rural hub schools more segregated and under-resourced than before. The historical preference for multi-grade organization over closure suggests feasible alternative models. The policy dilemma is not closure vs. open—but rather what form of rural school organization best sustains equity in a depopulating context.

## Assumptions
- Rural primary school closures are centralized policy decisions rather than autonomous community choices; the analysis assumes exogenous pressure to close, not organic voluntary consolidation. [assumption]
- The transfer/commute distance from closed schools is material enough to affect attendance and well-being; the analysis does not fully account for school-transport provision or parental logistics as moderating factors. [assumption]
- Educational segregation in Hungary is a policy-relevant inequality dimension; the analysis privileges this over other school-choice externalities (e.g., peer effect sorting by ability). [assumption]
- Long-run earnings effects from school disruption (Serbia study) are transferable to planned closures in the Hungarian rural context, despite methodological differences (war shock vs. policy choice). [assumption]

## Position
Rural primary school closures in Hungary would disproportionately harm low-income and Roma students through increased dropout risk and long-term earnings loss, unless accompanied by carefully designed consolidation (multi-grade organization, not exodus-inducing hub schools) and genuine transport/resource equity safeguards.

## Uncertainties
- The direct causal effect of rural school closures on social mobility in Hungary has not been empirically quantified; evidence is drawn from Chile and Serbia (non-Hungarian contexts) or correlational Hungarian data. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: A Hungarian natural experiment (e.g., variation in closure timing by municipality) or quasi-experimental matching study on cohorts before/after school closures, linked to adult earnings/educational outcomes via tax or administrative data.)
- The degree to which small-class peer effects (which favor disadvantaged pupils) versus economies of scale (which favor consolidation) net out in Hungarian rural contexts is unknown; no cost-benefit or randomized trial exists for this trade-off in Hungary. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Comparative cost-effectiveness research on multi-grade rural school models vs. consolidated hub-school models in Hungary, ideally with value-added test data or matched cohort outcomes; or a randomized pilot program allowing some municipalities to maintain small schools while others consolidate.)
