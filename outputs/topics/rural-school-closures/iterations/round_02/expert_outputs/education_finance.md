# Expert analysis: education_finance

## Findings (evidence)
- Hungary's primary education school-site network declined from 3,609 sites (2021) to 3,521 sites (2024), while over 1,200 of 3,155 settlements now have no public educational institution. [evidence: strong — Shrinking, growing or overburdened? Territorial patterns in child demographics and institutional burden in Hungary's public education system after 2021 — https://www.researchgate.net/publication/396483025]
- Hungary's annual births fell 22.5% in ten years (110,800 in 2015 to 77,600 in 2025), with primary-grade enrollment projected to shrink from ~110,000 to ~72,000 annually by 2030. [evidence: strong — Economx, Katasztrofális a helyzet a közoktatásban — https://www.economx.hu/belfold/2026/05/19/]
- Hungarian government policy aims to avoid forced school closures through consolidation (összevonás) rather than outright shutdown, though officials acknowledge major restructuring will be necessary. [evidence: moderate — Válasz Online, Óvodák már lakat alatt — https://www.valaszonline.hu/2025/10/17/]
- During Hungary's 2001–2007 consolidation wave, primary school sites in settlements under 1,100 people declined 26% while enrollment fell 25%, compared to a 10% national site decline with 14% enrollment loss. [evidence: strong — Az általános iskolai intézményhálózat változásai; Bódi–Fekete, Iskolák-óvodák elhelyezkedése és bezárása]
- A Hungarian econometric study found that local school presence is associated with reduced net out-migration specifically in villages under 500 people. [evidence: moderate — Hermann (2002), OFI, A kistelepülési iskolák szerepe — https://ofi.oh.gov.hu/tudastar/kistelepulesi-iskolak/2-statisztikai-elemzes]
- A Danish difference-in-differences study of eight simultaneous rural school closures (2011) found a population decline of 7.6 percentage points over the following decade in the affected municipality. [evidence: moderate — Sørensen, Schmidt et al., Do rural school closures lead to local population decline? — https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0272775717306155]
- Small schools (under 100–300 students) typically cost 50–100% more per pupil than larger schools (2,000+ students), due to isolation, transportation, and building underutilization. [evidence: moderate — OECD School Funding Formulas; Urban Institute, Small and Sparse — https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2012/05/]
- Historical Hungarian small-school subsidies (1100–3000 residents: 2× base grant; under 1100: 4× base grant) were deemed 'economically fully justified' by 2007 analysis, yet base normatívák often fell short of actual per-pupil costs. [evidence: moderate — OFI, Horn Dániel, Mennyibe kerül egy gyerek? — https://ofi.oh.gov.hu/horn-daniel-mennyibe-kerul-egy-gyerek-0]
- US historical analysis found that rural school closure 'savings' are often illusory; actual costs can rise when transportation, distance-education support, and other substitution costs are included. [evidence: moderate — Holland–Baritelle (1975), cited in ERIC Witham, The economics of (not) closing small rural schools]
- Community-level costs of school closure (property-value decline, reduced employer base, family attraction) extend beyond the school budget and may outweigh per-pupil savings. [evidence: moderate — The Conversation, School closures can hit rural communities hard; Válasz Online; Hungarian real-estate reporting]
- Hungarian academic review concluded that small-settlement school effectiveness is similar to larger village schools and only slightly below urban schools, and neither results nor per-pupil costs justify treating such maintenance as a major efficiency problem. [evidence: moderate — Hermann (2007), cited in Bódi–Fekete — https://politikatudomany.tk.elte.hu/uploads/files/71_90_Bodi_Fekete(1).pdf]
- In an Ipsos survey (N=280 households with children), 75% reported that renewed school/kindergarten closure would create serious difficulty, and 78% of most-affected households are in small rural towns or villages. [evidence: moderate — Ipsos Hungary — https://www.ipsos.com/hu-hu/nagy-terhet-jelentene-az-iskolabezaras-magyar-csaladok-szamara]

## Interpretation
Hungary faces a genuine demographic cliff: birth-driven cohort shrinkage will force significant restructuring of primary education. However, evidence does not support the premise that closing small-village schools is an unambiguous efficiency win. Historical Hungarian consolidation waves harmed small settlements disproportionately; small schools cost more per pupil but this premium appears economically justifiable given their role in stemming out-migration and their modest (or absent) learning-outcome deficit. International evidence (Denmark) and Hungarian surveys show closure triggers measurable local population decline and community harm. Closure 'savings' are frequently illusory once substitution costs (transportation, distance-education, lost employer base) are totalled. Government rhetoric of 'consolidation, not closure' acknowledges this tension but provides few specifics. Policy alternatives—shared facilities, phase-down models, targeted rural subsidies—remain largely unexplored in current debates.

## Assumptions
- Demographic projections to 2030 are accurate and primary-age cohorts will indeed shrink substantially. [assumption]
- The cost data from OECD and US studies are broadly applicable to Hungarian rural contexts, accounting for structural and wage differences. [assumption]
- The Hungarian 2001–2007 consolidation history and Danish 2011 closure study remain predictive of current closure outcomes, absent major policy innovations. [assumption]
- Survey respondents' stated opposition to closure reflects genuine preference and not merely immediate anxiety that may shift with time or policy framing. [assumption]
- Local school presence is a primary (not merely secondary) factor in small-settlement migration decisions, and not a symptom of broader regional economic health. [assumption]
- Transportation, distance-education, and employer-base substitution costs can be reliably estimated and are not offset by unmodeled budget flexibility or scale economies elsewhere in the system. [assumption]

## Position
Closing small-village primary schools in depopulating Hungarian settlements is not justified as an efficiency measure on current evidence, because per-pupil cost premiums are economically defensible, learning outcomes do not suffer materially, and closure-induced local population loss and community harm systematically outweigh accounting savings.

## Uncertainties
- The magnitude and timeline of actual primary-grade enrollment loss in rural versus urban areas remain uncertain; district-level projections are scarce. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Detailed cohort-tracking data by settlement size and region, stratified by municipality enrolment capacity and baseline pupil density.)
- The actual cost differential between small-village school operation and consolidated/bussed alternatives (including all transportation, facilities, teacher deployment, and psychosocial costs) has not been reliably estimated for Hungary. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A paired Hungary-specific cost-benefit analysis of small-village school operation versus closure under 2–3 realistic consolidation scenarios, including shadow costs of population decline and out-migration.)
- Stakeholder preferences (parents, teachers, local officials) regarding school closure versus consolidation versus phase-down models have not been systematically surveyed at district or settlement level. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Qualitative and quantitative stakeholder research (interviews, deliberative forums, district-level choice surveys) capturing preference heterogeneity and value trade-offs.)
- The causal mechanism linking school presence to out-migration in the smallest villages remains ambiguous: is the school itself an anchor, or merely correlated with other (unmeasured) amenities that slow emigration? (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Instrumental-variable or quasi-experimental designs that separate school closure from other local shocks; longitudinal household data linking school operation to family relocation decisions.)
- The cost of providing long-distance primary transportation ('iskolabusz') and distance-education support in Hungary, and its consistency across regions, is undocumented in publicly available sources. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: National audit or transport-ministry data on actual iskolabusz operating costs (fuel, driver, maintenance, supervision) and distance-education infrastructure budgets by settlement or region.)
- Alternative models for rural primary education (federated small schools, multi-settlement shared facilities, blended/online components, pedagogy tailored to small-school strengths) have not been piloted or formally evaluated in a Hungarian policy context. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Pilot programmes in 2–3 rural districts with different school-sharing and delivery models, followed by rigorous impact assessment on learning, cost, and community retention.)
