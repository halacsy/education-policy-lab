# Synthesis

## Overview
Ten expert analyses converge on an undisputed demographic core—Hungary's fertility collapse (from 1.61 in 2021 to below 1.3 by 2025) and shrinking rural cohorts are eroding the pupil base for which the primary school network was built, forcing some form of consolidation—but diverge sharply on causation, mechanism, and normative response. The legal/governance and political-feasibility experts show that closure is not automatic: Hungary's centralized tankerület system retains broad discretion, procedural safeguards (May deadlines, mandatory consultation, phase-in protections) slow implementation, and maintainer-change to church operators has become the dominant practical escape valve, itself contingent on political favor (as the MET case shows). The finance, demography, and school-network-planning experts document that small-school cost premiums, once treated as a settled efficiency argument for closure, are contested: Hungarian and OECD research suggests these premiums are economically defensible once community and out-migration effects are counted, and comparative Nordic/Iberian/Polish evidence is genuinely split on whether closures cause depopulation or merely follow it. The equity expert adds that Roma and low-income rural pupils face compounding risk from closure-driven dropout and long-run earnings loss, while the implementation-planning expert notes that current closure debates misdiagnose the teacher-shortage problem, which is concentrated in cities, not small rural schools. Across all ten records, uncertainty is preserved rather than resolved: causal direction between school presence and settlement viability, the true magnitude of cost differentials, and the feasibility of alternative models (multigrade classrooms, community hubs, association-schools) all remain empirically underdetermined despite policy urgency.

## Disagreement map
### Does school closure cause local depopulation, or merely follow it?
- **finnish_reform, education_finance, school_network_planning, demography**: School closure triggers or accelerates local population decline through a self-reinforcing mechanism (loss of social capital, housing-market lock-in, reduced settlement attractiveness), based on Finnish (2010–2018), Danish (2011), and Italian (2024) natural-experiment evidence. Why: Difference-in-differences studies in Finland, Denmark, and Italy find measurable population and income decline in the years following closure, beyond pre-existing demographic trends.
- **international_comparison, portuguese_reform** (minority): The causal link between closure and depopulation is contested and not universally supported; studies from Saxony, Sweden, Denmark (in the international_comparison record), and Portugal find no significant or only ambiguous population effect from closures. Why: Multiple national contexts show no significant closure-depopulation link once other confounders are considered, and Portuguese sociologist claims of causation are explicitly flagged as contested advocacy rather than controlled empirical findings.

### Are small rural schools an efficiency problem justifying closure?
- **education_finance, school_network_planning, hungarian_education_system**: Per-pupil cost premiums of small schools (50–100% higher) are economically defensible once out-migration prevention, community-hub value, and comparable learning outcomes are counted; closure is not justified as a pure efficiency measure. Why: Hungarian academic review (Hermann 2007) finds small-school effectiveness comparable to larger village schools; OFI research concludes cost differentials do not constitute a serious system-wide efficiency problem.
- **finnish_reform, polish_reform** (minority): Cost-per-pupil in small schools is roughly double that of larger schools and is cited by municipal administrators as the primary driver of closure decisions, though empirical verification of actual realized savings is scarce. Why: Finnish and Polish municipal decision-makers explicitly invoke cost rationale for closures, even though follow-up audits confirming real savings are largely absent, leaving the claim asserted rather than verified.

### What is the appropriate commuting/travel-time threshold by grade level for consolidated schooling?
- **school_network_planning (citing Trencsényi László)** (minority): Lower-primary pupils (grades 1–4) should not be subjected to long commutes; early-morning bus travel imposes disproportionate burden on young children. Why: Pedagogical and child-welfare concerns about fatigue and developmental appropriateness for the youngest pupils.
- **school_network_planning (citing Setényi János)**: Lower-primary should remain local, but upper-primary (grades 5–8) students should commute to larger, better-equipped district centers to access specialist resources. Why: Resource and specialist-staffing constraints in small schools make upper-primary commuting an acceptable trade-off for educational quality.

### Is the current teacher shortage a valid rationale for rural school consolidation?
- **implementation_planning** (minority): Small rural schools are not the cause of the national teacher shortage; the shortage is concentrated in Budapest and large cities, and small schools represent barely 1% of the system, so consolidation framed as a shortage remedy misdiagnoses the problem. Why: Economist Júlia Varga's research shows shortage growth is fastest in large urban centers, not small rural schools.
- **demography, equity_and_social_mobility**: Rural schools face disproportionate teacher shortages (2–3x higher unqualified-teacher rates) and an aging workforce, which compounds capacity constraints even where enrollment might otherwise justify keeping a school open. Why: EU Education and Training Monitor and demographic workforce data show structural staffing vulnerability specifically concentrated in rural and disadvantaged schools.

### Does maintainer-change to church operators represent a genuine, durable alternative to closure?
- **legal_and_governance, hungarian_education_system**: Maintainer-change is a real, operationally available 'exit option' for schools facing demographic pressure, evidenced by documented 2025 Budapest cases and near-unanimous local support where it prevents closure. Why: Documented cases show maintainer-change functioning as a lived governance reality with community buy-in, not merely a theoretical legal category.
- **political_feasibility** (minority): Maintainer status is politically contingent and reversible by state coercion, as shown by the forcible closure/transfer of Hungarian Evangelical Fellowship (MET) schools in 2023–2024 despite parental opposition, undermining claims of durable protection. Why: The MET case demonstrates that the state can and has withdrawn subsidies and seized assets to reverse maintainer arrangements it disfavors politically.

## What the experts agree on
- Hungary's fertility collapse and shrinking primary-age cohorts (from ~110,000 to a projected ~72,000 annual entrants by 2030) make some form of network contraction or restructuring unavoidable within the next 5–10 years. [evidence: strong]
- The primary school site network is already contracting (from ~3,609 sites in 2021 to ~3,521–3,537 by 2024/2025), and over 1,200 of Hungary's 3,155 settlements have no public educational institution at all. [evidence: strong]
- Small rural schools produce and maintain local social capital and function as community hubs and place-identity anchors beyond their strictly educational role. [evidence: moderate]
- Once family socioeconomic background is controlled for, rural small-school pupil achievement is broadly comparable to larger schools, undermining a purely quality-based rationale for closure. [evidence: moderate]
- Multigrade (composite-class) teaching models are a legally available, pedagogically viable alternative to closure, though they require dedicated teacher preparation and resourcing to function well. [evidence: moderate]
- Rural and disadvantaged-area schools face disproportionately severe teacher shortages and higher rates of unqualified staff compared to non-disadvantaged and urban schools. [evidence: strong]
- Closure-cost-savings claims are frequently asserted by administrators but rarely empirically verified through rigorous audit; substitution costs (transport, distance-education, facility investment) are often omitted from savings calculations. [evidence: moderate]
- Current Hungarian political rhetoric explicitly favors 'managed merger' and consolidation framing over overt 'closure' language, reflecting political sensitivity around the issue. [evidence: moderate]
- No rigorous, Hungary-specific causal study currently isolates the effect of rural school closure on local population retention, social mobility, or long-term economic outcomes; existing evidence relies on international analogues (Finland, Denmark, Italy, Chile, Serbia, Portugal) of uncertain transferability. [evidence: contested]
