# Critique: assumption_checker

## S1.assumptions
Objection: The scenario assumes teacher supply for multigrade classrooms is adequate without systemic training reform, yet the scenario's own text cites contradictory evidence: half of Hungarian teachers are 50+ and rural vacancy postings are rising ~25% year‑on‑year. That directly undermines the 'adequate supply' claim and ignores the documented Finnish finding that initial teacher-education programmes historically do not prepare teachers for multigrade pedagogy. The assumption is therefore unsupported and internally inconsistent.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Replace the blanket adequacy assumption with an explicit, evidence‑based staffing contingency: require a district‑level audit of multigrade‑qualified teacher supply and pipeline projections (by year and grade-band) as a prerequisite for adopting S1 at scale; add funded multi‑year initial- and in‑service training targets (numbers trained per year) and a contingency staffing/ recruitment plan (e.g., regional incentive pay, temporary itinerant specialist pools) that must be met before any village school is retained under multigrade arrangements.

## S2.mechanism
Objection: The scenario leans on an asserted learning benefit from 'delayed secondary tracking' (shortened primary + centralized ISCED2) despite flagging the international literature as contested. It does not specify which comparative studies support a causal effect, nor does it address the major caveat that Poland's result may not transfer to Hungary. The mechanism therefore overstates expected pedagogical gains without a Hungarian causal basis and without accounting for known transport/safety constraints (regional buses refusing unaccompanied minors).
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Recast the mechanism to foreground uncertainty: state explicitly that delayed-tracking benefits are contested and conditional on context; require a Hungarian pilot (quasi‑experimental or matched‑comparison) that measures achievement gaps, attendance, and welfare impacts before national rollout. Add a mandatory transport‑welfare audit (unaccompanied‑minor policy, route times, supervision costs) as part of any ISCED2 centralization plan and make continued centralization contingent on pilot outcomes and transport-safety remediation.

## S3.assumptions
Objection: S3 assumes Polish-style association-run or inter‑municipal network models are transferable to Hungary, but ignores the concrete governance barrier the scenario itself raises: Hungary's post‑2013 centralization (tankerület/KLIK) removed municipal maintainer authority that Polish gminas retain. This is not a murky difference — it is a structural legal constraint that makes simple policy transfer implausible without legislative change. The assumption of easy transferability is therefore incorrect.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Make transferability conditional and practical: add a required formal legal comparative analysis by Hungarian and Polish education‑law experts to identify which statutory changes are necessary to allow association maintainers under current Nkt. If legislative change is needed, specify the legislative path, timeline, and minimum guarantees (state funding continuity, teacher-employment protections). Pilot one or two association-run schools with explicit state backstops and capacity‑building grants before scaling, and publish model contract templates and accounting standards to lower administrative barriers for low‑capacity municipalities.

## S4.assumptions
Objection: The scenario assumes TEIP‑style targeted support and statutory transport guarantees will meaningfully offset closure equity harms, but the scenario's own evidence notes that Portugal's TEIP reduced dropout/failure yet 'failed over 15 years to close the performance gap' on national exams. Treating TEIP-style measures as likely sufficient is therefore unsupported and contradicts the cited long‑term Portuguese outcome.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Adjust the assumption to reflect limited effectiveness: acknowledge that TEIP-style supports may reduce immediate access/dropout but not automatically equalize learning outcomes, and build stronger, explicit compensatory measures into the design — e.g., ring‑fenced multi‑year funding for intensive remedial instruction, mentor/teacher‑exchange programs between sending and receiving schools, disaggregated monitoring (ethnicity/income) with pre‑specified remedial triggers, and statutory multi‑year funding commitments (not one‑off allocations). Require a Hungary‑specific pilot evaluation with outcome targets (attendance, standardized achievement, segregation indices) before declaring consolidated closure+mitigation a net-equity solution.
