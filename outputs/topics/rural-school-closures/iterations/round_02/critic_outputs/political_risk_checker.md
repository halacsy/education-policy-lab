# Critique: political_risk_checker

## S2.political_risks
Objection: The scenario acknowledges Poland's 1999 reform was politically reversible but proposes no concrete entrenchment mechanisms; it therefore relies on an unstated, politically fragile assumption that Hungary can avoid the same reversal. Given the synthesis's explicit evidence that Poland's reform was later reversed and that Hungary lacks cross-party consensus mechanisms, the scenario's core structural-change claim is vulnerable—nothing in S2 minimizes or mitigates the demonstrated reversal pathway (legislative reversal, funding reallocation, or executive undoing).
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add a mandatory 'entrenchment package' to the mechanism and implementation steps: (a) specify legal safeguards (e.g., statutory supermajority or a constitutional clause protecting the new ISCED2 structure), (b) include a binding, multi-year, ring-fenced funding commitment for capital and transport tied to the reform (statute or escrow), (c) require a formal cross-party consensus process documented before rollout (timebound inter-party agreement or parliamentary committee report), and (d) build staged pilots with external evaluation and explicit legislative ‘pause/review’ triggers rather than one-off national implementation. Include these items in timelines and political-risk mitigation so reversal pathways are directly constrained.

## S3.political_risks
Objection: The scenario flags union and political perception risks around association-run models but fails to confront the state's coercive reversal capacity evidenced by the MET case: it contains no binding legal protections for association maintainers, teacher employment continuity, or asset security. In practice that omission means association-run schools could be established and then de-funded, re-assigned, or asset-seized for political reasons—precisely the reversal risk S3 purports to avoid.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Specify and legislate concrete safeguards for association-run maintainers: (1) multi-year funding contracts with automatic disbursement clauses and penalties for unilateral termination; (2) statutory teacher-transfer protections (guaranteed retention or compensated redeployment, TUPE-style clauses) to prevent union mobilization; (3) asset-ownership or escrow mechanisms (third-party trusteeship) to prevent state seizure; (4) an independent appeals body with expedited judicial review for maintainer-funding withdrawal; and (5) pilot agreements co-signed by national ministry and international funder (or EU instrument) to provide an initial external financial/legal backstop. Add these to implementation_steps and political_risks mitigation.

## S5.political_risks
Objection: S5 assumes a non-binding, advisory registry and thresholds will be used neutrally, but offers no institutional design to prevent selective instrumentalization (using thresholds to justify pre-determined closures) or political shutdown of the registry. The synthesis documents the very real risk that transparency tools are weaponized absent independent governance and protected funding—S5's current design therefore leaves the framework liable to the exact political misuse it seeks to prevent.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Transform the advisory design into a legally-guarded evidence infrastructure: (a) create an independent statutory agency (or place the registry under KSH with a guaranteed multi-year appropriation law) governed by a multi-party board with civil-society representation; (b) codify rules on how thresholds may be applied (e.g., thresholds trigger mandatory procedural steps, not automatic closures) and require written, public justification when deviating; (c) enshrine registry funding in statute (ring-fenced funds) and specify data-disaggregation fields and publication cadence; (d) mandate external audits and provide standing legal remedies for communities alleging misuse of thresholds. Add these institutional protections to implementation_steps and political_risks.

## S1.assumptions
Objection: S1 assumes teacher supply for multigrade classrooms is adequate without systemic training reform, yet its own assumptions and cited Finnish and domestic evidence indicate teacher-education gaps and rising rural vacancies (~25% year-on-year). This is a direct contradiction: operationalizing multigrade teaching at scale is implausible without addressing teacher training, recruitment and retention—S1 offers no credible contingency if supply and competency do not materialize.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Replace the 'no systemic reform' assumption with a conditional implementation plan: conduct a district-level multigrade-capacity audit before committing to retention-by-default; mandate and fund a certified multigrade pedagogy upskilling programme (minimum hours, accreditation, and staggered cohort timeline); add targeted rural recruitment and retention incentives (salary top-ups, housing, fast-track certification) tied to measured staffing benchmarks; and include a contingency closure/centralization trigger if defined staffing and quality metrics are not met within a specified multi-year window. Document these steps and budget lines in implementation_steps.
