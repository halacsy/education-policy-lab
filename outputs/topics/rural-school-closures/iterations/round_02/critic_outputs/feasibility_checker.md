# Critique: feasibility_checker

## S1.implementation_steps
Objection: The implementation steps assume tankerület/municipal capacity and an available teacher pipeline sufficient to deliver multi-year in‑service multigrade upskilling (timeline: year 1–4) and transparent retention negotiations (year 1–2). This is concretely flawed: the scenario's own assumptions/evidence state half of Hungarian teachers are 50+ and rural vacancy postings are rising ~25% year‑on‑year, and it also notes chronic under‑budgeting of tankerület administration. Those facts make the proposed timelines and reliance on voluntary local negotiation unrealistic — upskilling cannot substitute for a shrinking, ageing workforce, and under‑resourced tankerülets lack analytic/staff time to manage case‑by‑case negotiated retention quickly. The plan contains no substitute‑teacher pool, no targeted recruitment/retention incentives, no funded teacher-release time, and no district capacity audit to validate baseline staffing. (This is not merely stylistic — it undercuts feasibility of the core mechanism.)
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Before training rollout or negotiation deadlines, require and budget for a district‑level multigrade‑capacity audit (deliverable within 6 months) that maps vacancies, retirements, and qualified‑for‑multigrade counts through 2030. Based on the audit: (a) extend the training timeline to year 1–6 and modularize it into certification milestones tied to staffing targets; (b) fund regional multigrade teacher coordinator posts (1 per tankerület) and a centrally financed substitute/relief pool to cover release time during training; (c) create targeted recruitment/retention incentives (temporary salary top‑ups, relocation supports, accelerated certification stipends) for rural posts and budget them as an explicit cost category; (d) require tankerület capacity‑building grants (administrative FTEs and data dashboards) as a precondition for any negotiated retention arrangement. Add KPIs (vacancy rate <X% before year 3) and contingency triggers (if unmet, pause retention‑only approvals until pipeline remedies are in place).

## S2.implementation_steps
Objection: The specified timelines and sequencing materially understate legal, procurement, transport‑safety and labour‑relations lead times. Designating and constructing/expanding ISCED2 sites with 'realistic multi‑year procurement and construction timelines (year 1–5)' overlooks Hungarian public‑works and school‑procurement realities (planning, environmental/permit reviews, tender protests and construction typically add 2–4 years beyond initial design), so year 1–5 is optimistic for significant capacity increases. The transport step (year 2–4) is sequenced too late relative to pupil transfers and omits an operational fix for the documented current problem that regional buses often refuse unaccompanied minors — a safety/policy gap that will block lawful daily commutes unless resolved before transfers. The teacher redeployment timeline (year 2–3) compresses complex negotiations under Act LII, risks triggering union mobilization or litigation, and contains no funded compensation/redeployment package. These gaps make an on‑schedule, mass transfer of upper‑grade pupils infeasible and politically and legally risky.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Reframe as a phased pilot with explicit procurement/transport/legal milestones: (1) Year 0–1: launch legislative/administrative workstream to fix unaccompanied‑minor transport rules and require transport‑operator safety protocols; secure temporary supervised‑escort funding for immediate interim transfers. (2) Year 0–2: run 1–2 regional pilots using leased or modular classroom space (temporary ISCED2 capacity) so students can be centralized without waiting for full construction. (3) Procurement: build formal procurement and environmental/permit buffers into capital timelines and expect capital completion in year 3–7; specify contingency modular capacity funded for years 1–4. (4) Labour relations: negotiate pre‑agreed redeployment/compensation protocols with teacher unions and include a funded voluntary early‑retirement/transition package and retraining stipends; schedule union sign‑offs before any reassignment commencement. (5) Move transport planning earlier (year 0–2) with mandatory safety audits, funded daily supervised‑escort pilots, and operator contractual clauses covering unaccompanied minors. Make phased triggers explicit (e.g., only transfer if transport safety audit and union redeployment agreement both signed).

## S5.implementation_steps
Objection: The registry and training implementation steps (registry timeline year 1–4; training rollout year 2–5) understate the legal and administrative lead time needed to collect and publish disaggregated closure/outcome data in Hungary, especially sensitive fields such as ethnicity and income. The scenario itself flags that 'no current national registry exists' and labels evidence weak; it does not account for the need for primary legislation or clear legal basis to collect ethnicity data, the mandatory Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA), interoperability work with KSH/tankerület systems, or the staffing and budget increase KSH/EMMI will need. Given chronic under‑budgeting of tankerület analytic capacity and the politically contested nature of ethnicity data, the proposed timeline is likely infeasible and politically vulnerable.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Insert preparatory legal and capacity steps and extend the timeline: (a) Year 0–1: draft and pass enabling legislation (or explicit legal basis) authorizing collection of the registry's disaggregated fields, and mandate a published technical specification; (b) Year 1: complete a DPIA and public consultation on the registry schema before any data collection; (c) Year 1–2: run a 12‑month pilot in 2–3 tankerülets collecting non‑sensitive fields and testing interoperability with KSH, publish pilot results; (d) Year 1–4: recruit/second an analytic unit (4–6 FTE analysts + data‑engineer) jointly funded by EMMI and KSH on a ring‑fenced budget to operate the registry; (e) Year 2–6: phased rollout of sensitive disaggregations (ethnicity/income) contingent on legal clearance and DPIA outcomes. Require multi‑year ring‑fenced funding and an independent governance board (including civil‑society representation) to reduce politicization risks.
