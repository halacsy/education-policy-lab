# Policy brief — The Future of Rural Primary Schools

This brief synthesizes ten expert analyses and a stakeholder stress-test on the future of small village primary schools in Hungary. Five policy scenarios are compared against what evidence actually supports, what remains genuinely uncertain, and what forty distinct argument clusters raised by modeled stakeholder interests demand in response. No scenario is recommended as 'the answer' — disagreement among experts and stakeholders is preserved throughout, because it reflects real, unresolved value conflicts and evidence gaps rather than a problem to be smoothed over.

## Scenario key (full detail: scenarios.en.md)
- S1 — S1 — Status quo plus incremental adaptation: keep the village school open via multigrade classrooms and ad hoc local negotiation, without a national framework.
- S2 — S2 — Structural grade reorganization: lower grades stay local, upper grades centralize into a larger ISCED2-type school (Polish 1999 pattern).
- S3 — S3 — Networked retention: inter-municipal clustering, association-run schools, or multi-service community hubs.
- S4 — S4 — Consolidation with mitigation: closure as default, paired with statutory transport guarantees and TEIP-style targeted support.
- S5 — S5 — National systemic framework: transparent thresholds, cost-visibility rules, teacher-training standards, and a closure/outcome registry to inform (not dictate) local decisions.

## What we know
- Hungary's fertility collapse and shrinking primary-age cohorts (from roughly 110,000 to a projected ~72,000 annual entrants by 2030) make some form of network contraction or restructuring unavoidable within the next 5-10 years. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- The primary school site network is already contracting (from roughly 3,609 sites in 2021 to roughly 3,521-3,537 by 2024/2025), and over 1,200 of Hungary's 3,155 settlements have no public educational institution at all. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Multigrade (összevont) classrooms combining up to three, exceptionally four, consecutive grades are legally available and administratively operational today, used in an estimated 200-300 schools nationally. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Once family socioeconomic background is controlled for, rural small-school pupil achievement is broadly comparable to larger village schools and only moderately below urban schools, undermining a purely quality-based rationale for closure. [fact] (evidence: moderate)
- Small schools (under 150 pupils) comprise nearly one-third of municipal primary schools yet enroll fewer than 10% of all students; researchers argue the cost differential alone does not constitute a serious system-wide efficiency problem. [fact] (evidence: moderate)
- Rural and disadvantaged-area schools face disproportionately severe teacher shortages and higher rates of unqualified staff (2-3 times higher) compared to non-disadvantaged and urban schools. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Portugal's TEIP compensatory programme reduced dropout and failure rates in disadvantaged school clusters but failed over 15 years to close the performance gap between TEIP and non-TEIP schools on national exams. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Poland introduced a legal framework allowing small schools (up to 70 students) to remain open under non-governmental (association, foundation, natural-person) management while retaining public funding and curriculum. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- No bright-line statutory minimum enrollment threshold currently exists in Hungarian education law (unlike, e.g., Ukraine's 45-student rule); closure decisions operate on a discretionary, case-by-case basis. [fact] (evidence: moderate)
- Documented Roma dropout rates stand at 58.7% versus 9.3% for non-Roma pupils nationally. [fact] (evidence: strong)

## What we consider likely
- Ad hoc local negotiation without a national framework likely produces uneven outcomes across settlements, systematically favoring better-organized or wealthier municipalities over poorer, less-organized ones, though no comparative study yet confirms this. [estimate]
- Centralizing upper grades to a distant hub site would likely shift substantial daily commuting burden and safety risk onto 10-14-year-olds, disproportionately affecting Roma and low-income families already relying on informal escort arrangements. [estimate]
- Association-run and networked retention models likely risk excluding the poorest, least-organized municipalities from participation unless dedicated capacity-building grants and technical assistance are guaranteed. [estimate]
- A national closure/outcome registry, if built without mandatory disaggregation by settlement size, ethnicity and income, would likely provide a veneer of evidence-based legitimacy to decisions that remain as inequitable as under the status quo. [estimate]
- Statutory transport guarantees, absent a binding maximum travel-time rule and audited unaccompanied-minor safety measures, would likely remain declarative rather than protective in practice. [estimate]

## Where experts disagree
### Does school closure cause local depopulation, or merely follow it?
- **finnish_reform, education_finance, school_network_planning, demography**: School closure triggers or accelerates local population decline through a self-reinforcing mechanism (loss of social capital, housing-market lock-in, reduced settlement attractiveness), based on Finnish (2010-2018), Danish (2011), and Italian (2024) natural-experiment evidence. [value] Why: Difference-in-differences studies in Finland, Denmark, and Italy find measurable population and income decline in the years following closure, beyond pre-existing demographic trends.
- **international_comparison, portuguese_reform** (minority): The causal link between closure and depopulation is contested and not universally supported; studies from Saxony, Sweden, Denmark, and Portugal find no significant or only ambiguous population effect from closures. [value] Why: Multiple national contexts show no significant closure-depopulation link once other confounders are considered, and Portuguese sociologist claims of causation are explicitly flagged as contested advocacy rather than controlled empirical findings.
### Are small rural schools an efficiency problem justifying closure?
- **education_finance, school_network_planning, hungarian_education_system**: Per-pupil cost premiums of small schools (50-100% higher) are economically defensible once out-migration prevention, community-hub value, and comparable learning outcomes are counted; closure is not justified as a pure efficiency measure. [value] Why: Hungarian academic review (Hermann 2007) finds small-school effectiveness comparable to larger village schools; OFI research concludes cost differentials do not constitute a serious system-wide efficiency problem.
- **finnish_reform, polish_reform** (minority): Cost-per-pupil in small schools is roughly double that of larger schools and is cited by municipal administrators as the primary driver of closure decisions, though empirical verification of actual realized savings is scarce. [value] Why: Finnish and Polish municipal decision-makers explicitly invoke cost rationale for closures, even though follow-up audits confirming real savings are largely absent, leaving the claim asserted rather than verified.
### What is the appropriate commuting/travel-time threshold by grade level for consolidated schooling?
- **school_network_planning (Trencsényi László)** (minority): Lower-primary pupils (grades 1-4) should not be subjected to long commutes; early-morning bus travel imposes disproportionate burden on young children. [value] Why: Pedagogical and child-welfare concerns about fatigue and developmental appropriateness for the youngest pupils.
- **school_network_planning (Setényi János)**: Lower-primary should remain local, but upper-primary (grades 5-8) students should commute to larger, better-equipped district centers to access specialist resources. [value] Why: Resource and specialist-staffing constraints in small schools make upper-primary commuting an acceptable trade-off for educational quality.
### Is the current teacher shortage a valid rationale for rural school consolidation?
- **implementation_planning** (minority): Small rural schools are not the cause of the national teacher shortage; the shortage is concentrated in Budapest and large cities, and small schools represent barely 1% of the system, so consolidation framed as a shortage remedy misdiagnoses the problem. [value] Why: Economist Júlia Varga's research shows shortage growth is fastest in large urban centers, not small rural schools.
- **demography, equity_and_social_mobility**: Rural schools face disproportionate teacher shortages (2-3x higher unqualified-teacher rates) and an aging workforce, which compounds capacity constraints even where enrollment might otherwise justify keeping a school open. [value] Why: EU Education and Training Monitor and demographic workforce data show structural staffing vulnerability specifically concentrated in rural and disadvantaged schools.
### Does maintainer-change to church operators represent a genuine, durable alternative to closure?
- **legal_and_governance, hungarian_education_system**: Maintainer-change is a real, operationally available 'exit option' for schools facing demographic pressure, evidenced by documented 2025 Budapest cases and near-unanimous local support where it prevents closure. [value] Why: Documented cases show maintainer-change functioning as a lived governance reality with community buy-in, not merely a theoretical legal category.
- **political_feasibility** (minority): Maintainer status is politically contingent and reversible by state coercion, as shown by the forcible closure/transfer of Hungarian Evangelical Fellowship (MET) schools in 2023-2024 despite parental opposition, undermining claims of durable protection. [value] Why: The MET case demonstrates that the state can and has withdrawn subsidies and seized assets to reverse maintainer arrangements it disfavors politically.

## What we don't know
- Whether ad hoc local negotiation without a national framework produces systematically fair outcomes across settlements, or advantages better-organized/wealthier villages — no comparative study of settlement-level negotiation outcomes currently exists. [fact]
- Whether teacher supply for multigrade classrooms can scale fast enough given that half of Hungarian teachers are 50+ and rural vacancy postings are rising roughly 25% year-on-year. [fact]
- Whether the achievement-gap benefit attributed to delayed tracking/centralization (S2) actually materializes in the Hungarian context, given contested causal attribution in the international literature and no Hungarian pilot data. [fact]
- Whether Hungary's post-2013 centralized tankerület/KLIK governance structure permits genuine legal transfer of Polish-style association-run school autonomy, given that Polish gminas retain local school-ownership authority that Hungarian municipalities lost. [fact]
- Whether post-closure transportation costs in the Hungarian context actually fall within the cited 800-1,500 EUR/pupil/year range, and whether the net financial effect of consolidation is positive or negative once transport, mitigation, and indirect community costs are included. [estimate]
- Whether a genuinely non-binding, advisory national framework (S5) can remain politically neutral in practice, or whether it will inevitably be used selectively to legitimize predetermined closure or retention decisions. [assumption]
- Whether the registry and thresholds (S5), once operational, will in fact capture and publish disaggregated equity data (settlement size, ethnicity, income), or default to aggregate reporting that obscures unequal downstream impacts — no technical specification has yet been published. [assumption]
- Whether association-run and multi-service hub models (S3) can achieve genuine long-term operational sustainability and educational quality over a 10+ year horizon, or whether they merely delay closure while quality erodes — no rigorous quasi-experimental study exists. [fact]

## What could be done
- **S1 — Status quo plus incremental adaptation**: Keep the village school open through existing multigrade classrooms and the felmenő rendszer phase-in mechanism, relying on ad hoc, case-by-case local negotiation between municipality and tankerület without a new national framework.
- **S2 — Structural grade reorganization**: Restructure so only lower grades remain in the village while upper grades or a new ISCED2-type school consolidate in a larger town, following the Polish 1999 structural-reform pattern.
- **S3 — Networked retention**: Retain village schools through inter-municipal pedagogical networks, community/association-run publicly-subsidized schools, or multi-service hubs combining school with childcare, pre-school and senior services.
- **S4 — Consolidation with mitigation**: Close low-enrollment village schools and reassign pupils to a consolidated center school, paired with statutory transport guarantees, TEIP-style targeted support, and transparent cost/consultation rules.
- **S5 — National systemic framework**: Establish a national framework of transparent minimum-viability thresholds, cost-visibility rules, teacher-training standardization, and a closure/outcome registry to inform, not dictate, local decisions across any of the above models.

## What each option costs
- **S1**: Recurring multigrade teacher-training and in-service pedagogy support costs, plus capital-maintenance backlog costs of under-enrolled buildings; these fall on an aging, shrinking rural teaching workforce unless systemic investment accompanies retention, effectively asking teachers to absorb coping costs. [estimate]
- **S1**: Absence of a bright-line statutory threshold leaves retention decisions to discretionary tankerület judgment, which is politically reversible year to year — poorer, less-organized villages risk drifting into de facto under-resourced status without transparent enrollment and cost disclosure. [estimate]
- **S2**: Statutory transport infrastructure and daily commuting costs shift travel burden onto 10-14-year-olds and their families; this disproportionately falls on lower-income and Roma families and risks triggering white-flight exit that re-segregates the centralized site — no compensatory transport-cost mechanism is built into the core design. [value]
- **S2**: Severance, compensation, or redeployment costs for redundant teachers under Act LII (Status Law) risk being experienced as involuntary displacement without binding consultation guarantees, potentially triggering union mobilization comparable to the Tanítanék movement. [estimate]
- **S2**: Poland's own 1999 reform was reversed after 17 years; without cross-party entrenchment mechanisms, Hungary risks a costly, disruptive structural change whose achievement-gap benefit remains contested and unproven locally. [estimate]
- **S3**: Legal drafting, negotiation, and ongoing administrative/accounting capacity costs for association-run schools risk systematically excluding the poorest, least-organized municipalities unless dedicated capacity-building grants and technical assistance are guaranteed — otherwise the model rewards already-advantaged villages. [value]
- **S3**: Teacher precarity documented in the comparable Polish model (roughly one in five rural teachers holding a part-time secondary job) risks transferring to Hungary absent statutory continuity-of-employment and pay-parity guarantees, especially given the state's demonstrated willingness to coercively reverse maintainer status (MET case). [estimate]
- **S4**: Portugal's 15-year TEIP record shows compensatory measures narrow but do not close performance and dropout gaps; closure-as-default risks legitimizing continued disadvantage for Roma and low-income pupils (dropout 58.7% vs 9.3%) under the cover of mitigation. [value]
- **S4**: Recurring transport service costs (unvalidated against Hungarian data, cited internationally at 800-1,500 EUR/pupil/year), plus indirect community-level costs (property-value decline, reduced local employer base) documented but rarely included in official budget accounting, may partly or wholly offset projected consolidation savings. [estimate]
- **S4**: Sustained multi-year funding for compensatory measures is vulnerable to budget-cycle discontinuity; without a ring-fenced, binding funding-commitment mechanism, mitigation risks being cut once the closure has already occurred and political attention has moved on. [estimate]
- **S5**: One-off design, legal-drafting, IT-build and data-management costs for the registry, plus recurring teacher-training rollout costs, carry the risk of remaining an aggregate, equity-blind system unless disaggregation by settlement size, ethnicity and income is mandated from inception. [value]
- **S5**: A non-binding, advisory framework may be politically instrumentalized: actors could selectively cite thresholds to justify predetermined closure decisions while ignoring them when politically inconvenient, absent explicit rules on how thresholds may be used. [estimate]

## What research could resolve
- A district-level capacity audit of multigrade-qualified teacher supply and pipeline projections through 2030, to test whether S1's status-quo retention can be sustained without systemic training reform.
- A comparative study of retention-negotiation outcomes across settlements of varying administrative capacity and wealth, to test whether ad hoc S1 negotiation systematically disadvantages poorer villages.
- A Hungarian pilot study comparing achievement gaps in villages that adopt S2's centralized ISCED2 structuring versus those retaining full 1-8 local schools, controlling for socioeconomic background.
- A transport-safety and welfare audit of existing school-bus routes and capacity in candidate S2/S4 centralization regions, including unaccompanied-minor policy compliance.
- A formal legal-comparative analysis assessing whether Hungary's Nkt. provisions and post-2013 tankerület centralization permit genuine legal transfer of Polish-style S3 association-run maintainer autonomy.
- A longitudinal quasi-experimental study (diff-in-diff or matched-pair) comparing S3 association schools vs. traditional public or closed schools on student outcomes, teacher retention, and institutional vitality over 10+ years.
- A Hungary-specific cost-benefit analysis of small-village school operation versus S4 closure under 2-3 realistic consolidation scenarios, including transport, mitigation, and indirect community costs.
- A Hungarian pilot evaluation comparing learning outcomes of displaced rural/Roma pupils at S4 consolidated sites with and without TEIP-style targeted support, tracked over 5-10 years.
- An explicit technical-specification review, published for public comment, of S5's planned registry data schema and disaggregation fields before system-build begins.
- A process-tracing case study of how similar advisory frameworks (e.g. EU pay-conditionality benchmarks, prior Hungarian education indicators) have actually been used in real decisions over a multi-year period, to test S5's risk of selective political use.

## What people must decide
- Whether the primary value priority is subsidiarity and local/community agency (favoring S1/S3) or system-wide efficiency and quality standardization through specialist staffing (favoring S2/S4) — these are not reconcilable by evidence alone since both rest on legitimate but conflicting values. [value]
- Whether the state is willing to accept irreversible institutional loss (closure) in exchange for compensatory measures whose own long-term evidence (Portugal's TEIP) shows they narrow but do not close equity gaps — a value trade-off between administrative efficiency and irreducible harm to displaced pupils. [value]
- How much weight to give to the documented risk that maintainer status (church, civic, association) is politically reversible by state action (the MET precedent) when deciding whether to expand non-state alternatives to closure — a political-trust judgment, not an empirical one. [value]
- Whether a national registry and threshold framework (S5) should remain strictly non-binding advisory infrastructure (preserving discretionary flexibility) or should be given binding force with enforceable equity-disaggregation requirements — this is a governance-design choice about how much central discretion versus rule-bound predictability the system should have. [value]
- Who bears the burden of proof and the cost of delay: should closure/consolidation proceed on currently available (contested, partly non-transferable) evidence, or should Hungary-specific pilot data be required first, even though the demographic pressure is already accelerating? [value]
