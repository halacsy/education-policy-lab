# Research Notes: International Comparison — Rural Primary School Closure, Selection Age & Equity

## Finding 1: PISA rural-urban achievement gap disappears after controlling for socio-economic status
OECD's analysis of PISA 2015 data across OECD countries found that the rural-urban academic performance gap "disappears completely after accounting for students' and schools' socio-economic profile." In several countries (Estonia, Finland, Lithuania, Spain, US), rural students would actually outperform urban students if socio-economic profiles were equalized. In Austria, Poland, Slovenia and others, the rural-urban gap actually favored rural schools.
Source: OECD Education Working Paper No. 196, "Learning in Rural Schools: Insights from PISA, TALIS and the Literature" (2019) — https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2019/03/learning-in-rural-schools_75ae062d/8b1a5cb9-en.pdf

## Finding 2: Rural students have lower educational aspirations even at equal SES
Even when accounting for socio-economic status, OECD found rural students remain less likely to expect completing a university degree than city students — a persistent aspirations gap unrelated to raw ability, distinct from an achievement gap.
Source: ERIC ED594134, OECD Working Paper 196 summary — https://eric.ed.gov/?id=ED594134

## Finding 3: Denmark — mixed evidence on population effects of rural school closures
A 2021 Journal of Rural Studies paper on Denmark notes that "many rural schools have been closed since 2000," generating heated local political debates, but that "previous research has found mixed evidence on the population effect of rural school closures."
Source: Sørensen, J.F.L. et al., "Do rural school closures lead to local population decline?" Journal of Rural Studies, Vol. 87 (2021) — https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0272775717306155 (citation embedded in related ScienceDirect listing)

## Finding 4: Finland — DID study finds closures DO precede local depopulation
A Finnish difference-in-differences study (2010–2018 data, matching methods) found that "in Finland after school closure, the population of the surrounding community decreased more than before school closure" — contradicting the Danish/Saxon null results and suggesting effect heterogeneity by national context.
Source: "Primary school closures and population development – is school vitality an investment in the attractiveness of the (rural) communities or not?" Journal of Rural Studies (2021) — https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0743016721000115

## Finding 5: Saxony (Germany), Sweden — no significant closure-depopulation link
Contrasting Finland, research in Saxony (Barakat, 2015) and Sweden (Amcoff, 2012) "find there is no significant connection between school closure and population decline," and Barakat (2014) explicitly concluded there is "no evidence of a localised depopulating effect of primary school closures," while noting the popular belief that closures are a "'recipe for depopulation.'"
Source: "A 'Recipe for Depopulation'? School Closures and Local Population Decline in Saxony" (ResearchGate summary) — https://www.researchgate.net/publication/262115739_A_'Recipe_for_Depopulation'_School_Closures_and_Local_Population_Decline_in_Saxony

## Finding 6: Czechia — residents value schools symbolically more than practically
A Czech study (Bernard, Joukl, Vítková 2022) found a paradox: in villages that still have a school, most residents consider it hard to do without, "but only 17% of inhabitants of municipalities without an elementary school consider its absence a major shortcoming" — suggesting attachment to schools is partly retrospective/symbolic rather than reflecting current felt need.
Source: cited in "A 'Recipe for Depopulation'?" literature review (ResearchGate) — https://www.researchgate.net/publication/262115739_A_'Recipe_for_Depopulation'_School_Closures_and_Local_Population_Decline_in_Saxony

## Finding 7: Hungary — 2004 policy shock halved villages with own primary schools within four years
Kovács (2012) documents that Hungary's 2004 Act on Multi-Functional Micro-Regional Local Government Associations (MLGAs) "rechanneled state incentives and promoted the amalgamation of rural schools," and that "within four years the number of villages maintaining primary schools dropped by a half," with an estimated 10% of small schools fully closed and 30–35% amalgamated under central administration. Prior to this, state co-financing had covered 80–90% of primary education costs nationally but only 50% in small village schools, yet closures had been rare until the 2004 reform.
Source: Kovács, K., "Rescuing a small village school in the context of rural change in Hungary," Journal of Rural Studies 28 (2012) — https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0743016712000216

## Finding 8: Hungary post-2021 — school-site numbers declining, disadvantage geographically concentrated
A 2025 study using KIR (Hungarian education administration) data reports that the number of school sites fell from 9,046 in 2021 to 8,590 by 2024, with primary-education (ISCED 1–2) sites falling from 3,609 to 3,521 over the same period; the study also finds settlement-level concentrations of disadvantaged students exceeding 70–92% in parts of Northern Hungary and Southern Transdanubia, linked to "school closures in peripheral areas, and residential mobility toward more advantaged localities."
Source: "Shrinking, growing or overburdened? Territorial patterns in child demographics and institutional burden in Hungary's public education system after 2021" (2025) — https://www.academia.edu/144471812/Shrinking_growing_or_overburdened_Territorial_patterns_in_child_demographics_and_institutional_burden_in_Hungarys_public_education_system_after_2021

## Finding 9: Hungary/Romania comparative — ambiguous welfare link, but negative community impact confirmed
A comparative Hungary–Romania study found that "rural communities with schools have a higher level of welfare" on average, and that "in both countries school closures had a negative impact on the community," though this did not necessarily mean closure communities had the objectively worst living conditions — evidence is descriptive/correlational, not causal.
Source: "The Presence or the Absence of Schools: What Impact for Rural Communities in Hungary And Romania?" Bulletin UASVM Cluj-Napoca (2016) — https://journals.usamvcluj.ro/index.php/agriculture/article/view/12020

## Finding 10: Nordic/international alternative models — multigrade teaching as a pedagogical asset, not just a stopgap
OECD notes that "urban" systems are increasingly borrowing rural pedagogical techniques: "alternative models based on small schools, multigrade teaching, moderate student-teacher ratios and close home-school relationships" show potential benefits transferable from rural contexts. Norway's multigrade small-school model uses peer-learning and individualized working plans as deliberate didactic tools, not merely a resource constraint.
Source: OECD Working Paper 196 (2019) — https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2019/03/learning-in-rural-schools_75ae062d/8b1a5cb9-en.pdf; and Springer chapter "Multi-grade Teaching in a Small Rural School in Northern Norway" (2022) — https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-97460-2_14

## Finding 11: US evidence on consolidation/closure achievement effects is genuinely mixed
A 2024 research synthesis states that "research on whether the strategy [consolidation] saves money or helps students is mixed," and that negative achievement effects of closure "can be reduced or eliminated if displaced students are able to attend a high-quality, high-performing school after closure" — but that most research has focused on urban contexts with "few research studies focused on student achievement for students experiencing school closures in rural or suburban areas."
Source: "Revisiting Research on School Closings: Key Learnings for District and Community Leaders," Research for Action (2024) — https://www.researchforaction.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/06/revisiting-research-on-school-closings-key-learnings-for-district-and-community-leaders.pdf
