# Research Notes: School-Size Economics, Building Stock & Commuting Tolerances in Rural Hungarian Primary Education

**1. National enrollment decline and shrinking site count.** 
The number of general-school students continued to fall in the 2024/2025 school year, with about two thousand fewer students in primary institutions than a year earlier, based on KSH (Central Statistical Office) data — 710,000 full-time general-school students versus 712,000 the year before, a 0.3% annual decline.
 
Compared to 20 years earlier, the decline reaches 17.3%: in 2005/2006 there were about 149,000 more students, i.e. 859,000 total.
 
The shrinking student population is also affecting the number of schools: in 2024/2025 a total of 3,537 school locations operated general schools, 13 fewer than the previous year, 38 fewer than in 2022/2023, and 88 fewer than in 2021/2022.
 Source: Népszava/KSH, "Egyre kevesebb az általános iskolás Magyarországon," https://nepszava.hu/3287108_altalanos-iskola-diakok-letszam-csokkenes-magyarorszag-ksh

**2. Union warning on inevitable school mergers.** 
Totyik Tamás, president of the Teachers' Union (PSZ), warned in an interview that within five to ten years population decline could cause serious challenges, and the current school system may need to be rethought.
 
He added that there are already several general schools where not a single first-grade student was enrolled.
 Source: Népszava (same article as above).

**3. Fertility collapse accelerating.** 
By August 2025 Hungary's total fertility rate had fallen below 1.3, down from 1.55 in 2023, far below the 2.1 replacement-level optimum.
 Source: Economx, "Demográfiai apokalipszis," https://www.economx.hu/magyar-gazdasag/kozoktatas-demografiai-valsag-pedagogusok-szakszervezete-iskola.817275.html

**4. Regional demographic collapse concentrated in specific counties.** 
Five counties experienced a combined population loss from excess deaths and out-migration exceeding 1% in 2024 alone — meaning one in every hundred residents disappeared from these counties within a single year.
 
Three of these regions are in eastern Hungary, but two are in western Hungary, in the sparsely-populated, aging Vas and Zala county areas.
 Source: Válasz Online, "Óvodák már lakat alatt, de itt lappang az iskolabezárási hullám is," https://www.valaszonline.hu/2025/10/17/ovodak-iskolak-bezarasa-demografia-oktatas/

**5. Government's stated policy stance.** 
Ministerial commissioner Balatoni Katalin stated that "the goal is not closure, but protecting small settlements and small schools as long as there is demand for them," adding that the government does everything to keep education running "to the last resort, even through mergers and local solutions."
 The article's author interprets the phrase "as long as there is demand" and the mention of mergers as an implicit government acknowledgment that major restructuring is coming. Source: Válasz Online (same as above).

**6. Small-school size distribution and unit-cost economics.** 
Small schools have higher unit costs, lower class sizes, and fewer students per teacher than larger schools; below 100 pupils, class size is half and the student-teacher ratio two-thirds of urban-school values.
 
Schools under 150 pupils make up nearly one-third of municipal general schools, yet fewer than 10% of all students attend them.
 Note: this OFI (Institute for Educational Research) analysis draws on older Hermann (2007)-era data but is methodologically still the most detailed unit-cost breakdown found. 
It concludes that these figures do not justify treating the maintenance of small-settlement schools as one of the serious efficiency problems of Hungarian public education.
 Source: OFI, "A falusi kisiskolák és a méretgazdaságossággal összefüggő hatékonyságveszteségek," https://ofi.oh.gov.hu/falusi-kisiskolak-es-meretgazdasagossaggal-osszefuggo-hatekonysagvesztesegek

**7. Growing local absence of grade-appropriate schooling.** 
As of 2020, 44% of Hungarian settlements had no lower-primary (grades 1–4) school, and 52% had no locally available upper-primary (grades 5–8) education.
 
Between 2010 and 2020 the share of general-school students attending schools with fewer than 100 pupils grew from 5.5% to 8.5% nationally, reaching 15–22% in Békés, Nógrád, and Tolna counties by 2020, versus under 5% in Budapest and Pest county.
 Source: Edunext, "A hazai településeken felén nincs felső tagozatos iskola," https://edunext.hu/cikk/a-hazai-telepulesek-felen-nincs-felso-tagozatos-iskola

**8. Commuting-tolerance debate among experts.** 
Trencsényi László, president of the Hungarian Pedagogical Society, contrasted a 7th-8th grader commuting into a "school-town" with putting a half-asleep first-grader on a school bus at 6 a.m.
 
By contrast, Setényi János (Mathias Corvinus Collegium) argued that instead of village small schools, large, well-equipped district-center institutions are needed, with kindergarten and lower-primary staying local but upper-primary students commuting.
 This is a direct expert disagreement on where the commuting-tolerance line should sit by grade level. Source: Edunext (same article as above).

**9. Legal class-size floor constrains stand-alone viability.** 
Hungary's National Public Education Act sets minimum class size at 14 pupils and maximum at 27 for general-school grades 1–4.
 Falling below the 14-pupil floor forces either merging classes/grades or transferring students to another school — a hard legal threshold directly relevant to which villages can keep a stand-alone school open. Source: Eduline, "A nap kérdése: maximum hány gyerek járhat egy osztályba," https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/20250303_A-nap-kerdese-maximum-hany-gyerek-jarhat-egy-osztalyba

**10. Historical classroom/building-stock erosion.** 
By 2005, compared to the 1988 baseline, Hungary had almost 4,000 fewer classrooms nationally, and compared to 1999, nearly 8,000 fewer — while the number of schools itself did not fall to either the 1988 baseline or the 1997 low point.
 This mismatch (classrooms shrinking faster than school count) suggests consolidation-without-closure ("tagiskola"/satellite-campus downgrading) has long been the dominant adjustment mechanism rather than outright shutdowns. Source: Bódi Ferenc – Fekete Attila, "Iskolák-óvodák elhelyezkedése és bezárása," https://politikatudomany.tk.elte.hu/uploads/files/71_90_Bodi_Fekete(1).pdf

**11. Comparative causal evidence: closures may accelerate depopulation rather than follow it.** From a Finnish difference-in-differences study (2010–2018 window) discussed in a comparative rural-Hungary paper: 
examining impacts of school closures on local population development using DID regression modelling combined with matching methods, results indicate that in Finland after school closure, the population of the surrounding community decreased more than before school closure.
 A German (Saxony) study found similar lagged effects: 
the population decline first becomes statistically significant from the sixth year following closures onward, with interview findings pointing to lock-in effects in social capital and housing markets that help explain the dominance of long-term population effects.
 Both papers are cited in a comparative piece that explicitly extends the discussion to Hungary: 
in recent years, due to changes in financing, the number of rural schools declined also notably in Hungary.
 Source: ResearchGate, "Rescuing a Small Village School in the Context of Rural Change in Hungary," https://www.researchgate.net/publication/257246275_Rescuing_a_Small_Village_School_in_the_Context_of_Rural_Change_in_Hungary ; and "A 'Recipe for Depopulation'? School Closures and Local Population Decline in Saxony," https://www.researchgate.net/publication/262115739_A_'Recipe_for_Depopulation'_School_Closures_and_Local_Population_Decline_in_Saxony

**12. Post-2021 territorial pattern study (Hungary-specific, most recent academic source found).** 
The nationalization of schools shifted control from local governments to centralized authorities, significantly reducing municipalities' autonomy in responding to local educational needs.
 
Numerous rural settlements in Western Transdanubia, Southern Baranya, and low-density regions of Northern Hungary recorded stagnation or decline, including areas where the child population contracted by 10–30% over the period, often coinciding with the closure or downgrading of institutions.
 Source: ResearchGate preprint, "Shrinking, growing or overburdened?: Territorial patterns in child demographics and institutional burden in Hungary's public education system after 2021," https://www.researchgate.net/publication/396483025_Shrinking_growing_or_overburdened_Territorial_patterns_in_child_demographics_and_institutional_burden_in_Hungary's_public_education_system_after_2021

**13. Cross-border comparator threshold model (Transcarpathia/Kárpátalja).** 
Preliminary data indicate that in some villages student numbers fall significantly short of the level required for state funding support, while in others (e.g., Halábor) the 45-pupil minimum is expected to be just met next school year, though demographic trends still raise concern.
 
The decision on whether municipalities themselves cover operating costs or organize busing of children to a larger school falls to the local government.
 Community-hub argument voiced locally: 
a district representative warned that if a village loses its school, its heritage-preservation groups, dance ensembles and "retention power" could also be endangered.
 Source: KISZó, "Veszélyben a kárpátaljai magyar kisiskolák?" https://kiszo.net/2026/07/12/kiszo-kerdes-veszelyben-a-karpataljai-magyar-kisiskolak/

**14. Household-level burden of closure (caution: survey context appears COVID-era, republished).** 
Based on an Ipsos Omnibusz survey of 1,000 adult Hungarian citizens, three-quarters of families with children said temporary or permanent closure of kindergartens and schools would create difficulty for them.
 
45% said childcare would not be solvable even in the short term.
 Flagged as **weak/dated evidence** — the survey text references COVID second-wave context, so its relevance to today's depopulation-driven (non-pandemic) closure scenario is uncertain. Source: Ipsos Hungary, "Nagy terhet jelentene az iskolabezárás a magyar családok számára," https://www.ipsos.com/hu-hu/nagy-terhet-jelentene-az-iskolabezaras-magyar-csaladok-szamara
