## Research Notes: Poland's Rural School Experience — Relevance to Hungary's Rural Primary School Question

**1. Scale of closures (2000-2016) driven by demographic decline and per-pupil financing.** A Lex Localis study found the main reasons for closures over 2000-2016 were declining fertility plus migration reducing pupil numbers, with economic reasons cited as the direct proximate cause.

The main reasons for around 3300 Polish schools to close over the 2000-2016 period were declining fertility plus migration, combining to reduce numbers of primary-school pupils, with economic reasons cited as the direct cause of school closures in the countryside.

Source: Lex localis – Journal of Local Self-Government, "Reflections on the Closure of Rural Schools in the Local Socio-economic Context - The Example of Poland" (2021) — https://lex-localis.org/index.php/LexLocalis/article/view/1637

**2. Mechanism: 1990s decentralization + per-pupil funding formula structurally disadvantaged small rural schools.** After municipalities took over school ownership in the 1990s, funding tied to enrollment made small schools financially unsustainable.

Because education financing was dependent on the number of students, the funds allocated to small rural schools were insufficient. Many of them were closed, and pupils had to be transported to larger establishments.

This is corroborated by HuffPost: 
In the early 1990s, Poland transferred ownership and management of the public schools to local authorities. But in many of the smaller, less densely populated areas, there wasn't enough money to keep the schools going, and thousands were closed.

Source: Springer, "Reforming Education in Poland" (2020 chapter) — https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-41882-3_7; HuffPost, "Rescuing Rural Schools in Poland" (2015) — https://www.huffpost.com/entry/rescuing-rural-schools-in_b_6678580

**3. Long-term social capital costs of closure — quasi-natural experiment.** A multiple-case study tracked four villages that protested closures in 2000 (two saved, two closed), interviewed in 2004 and 2016.

This paper presents a multiple-case study of four Polish villages which in the year 2000 organized protests against school closures; two schools were saved and two were closed... The study shows that the decline of structural social capital was significantly smaller in those villages where the school was saved. In the village in which the building of the school did not serve the local population due to the decision of the authorities, a significant and long-term decline in both structural and cognitive social capital was observed.

Source: DOAJ/European Countryside, "Long-Term Impact of Closing Rural Schools on Local Social Capital: A Multiple-Case Study from Poland" (2020) — https://doaj.org/article/945b8185ef7b4864a17f16e7f2c9f800 (also at https://reference-global.com/article/10.2478/euco-2020-0031)

**4. Legal innovation: transferring small-school management to associations/NGOs as an alternative to closure.** Poland enacted a specific legal mechanism allowing gminas to hand small schools to non-governmental entities rather than close them outright.

A gmina self-government may assign the management of a small school (up to 70 students) under an agreement to a legal entity that is not a self-government unit (e.g. an association, a foundation) or to a natural person. This solution protects small rural communities
. Practically, these "association-run schools" (szkoły stowarzyszeniowe) remain part of the public system: 
Many non-public schools with public credentials were created from a public school by a person, group of persons, or local community. These schools are normally in rural communities in Poland. They are free of charge and receive government funding. They also meet public educational goals, implement the national curriculum, organize exams based on the Ministry's guidelines, and are supervised by a government board.

Source: ResearchGate, "Local Government in Poland: Responses to Urban-Rural Challenges" (2021) — https://www.researchgate.net/publication/374659587_Local_Government_in_Poland_Responses_to_Urban-Rural_Challenges; OurKids.net, "Public versus non-public schools in Poland" — https://www.ourkids.net/pl-en/public-vs-nonpublic-schools.php

**5. 2024-2025 policy trend: repurposing shrinking schools as multi-service community hubs rather than closing them.** A current Ministry of Education draft law formalizes school buildings as community infrastructure once enrollment falls.

According to the draft law, small schools after completion of compulsory classes may be used to meet the needs of the local community. The Ministry of Education is keen to introduce such regulations as an alternative to the closure of small primary schools... will be able to guarantee education for younger children in grades I-III in a school as close to the family home as possible, even if the number of pupils is low, and... will be able to provide pupils in grades IV-VIII of primary school with an adequate quality of education, in a school employing qualified teachers
. The rationale explicitly frames schools as social infrastructure: 
Primary schools, especially those in rural areas, have been perceived as centres of social and cultural life for many generations. The draft law makes it possible to strengthen this, hitherto unregulated by law, role of schools, while at the same time giving local authorities the opportunity to broaden their offer of support to their residents.
 Proposed reuse includes 
using unused parts of educational buildings... to meet the needs of parents returning to the labour market by providing crèche care (e.g. in the form of a day-care centre) on the school's premises, and offering pre-school education... also a place for the school authority to implement activities for seniors
.
Source: Eurydice network, "National reforms in school education" — https://eurydice.eacea.ec.europa.eu/eurypedia/poland/national-reforms-school-education

**6. Community-school model survey evidence (regional, cross-Baltic/Poland).** A quantitative + qualitative study of rural municipalities examined whether "community schools" with extended functions can substitute for closure.

In the light of rural depopulation and a decreasing number of schoolchildren, municipalities with rural territories face a question regarding whether to close small rural schools or seek alternatives for school operation... The results of quantitative surveys are supplemented and explained by the data of 58 in-depth interviews with different stakeholders, living or working in rural areas. The main findings show that rural schools and municipalities positively evaluate the approach of community schools with extended functions, and partly it is an adaptation strategy for the diminishing number of pupils.

Source: Eastern European Countryside journal, "Community School Model: Is It an Alternative for School Closures in Rural Territories?" — https://apcz.umk.pl/EEC/article/view/49577

**7. Downside of keeping tiny rural schools open: teacher precarity and system strain (2023 reporting).** Very small schools cannot offer full-time hours to subject specialists, creating multi-school commuting and side-jobs for teachers.

In rural areas, the cost of living is lower, but the small schools do not have enough weekly hours, especially for subject teachers, to employ them full-time. So teachers have to commute from school to school. Last year, the case of a headmistress from Krynica Morska came to light, who drew up joint timetables with six other schools because they all employed the same teachers.
 Financial precarity is severe: 
every fifth teacher has a part-time job
 according to the teachers' professional journal.
Source: World Socialist Web Site, "Poland's school system on the brink of collapse" (Sept 2023) — https://www.wsws.org/en/articles/2023/09/04/7eca-s04.html

**8. Governance structure enabling local resistance/protection.** Polish gminas (rural municipalities, of which there were 1,464 as of Jan 2024) are legally responsible for establishing/running primary schools, and residents have constitutional referendum rights at gmina level, which historically has been the vehicle for anti-closure protests (see finding 3).

On 1 January 2024, there were 2,477 communes, including 1,464 rural, 711 rural-urban and 302 urban communes. The commune establishes and administers public nursery schools (przedszkole)... primary schools (szkoła podstawowa)
. 
Constitution of Poland guarantees residents of gminas, powiats and voivodeships the right to directly settle matters through the institution of a local referendum. A referendum on self-taxation of residents for public purposes is a special type of the local referendum. However, such a referendum can only be held at the gmina level.

Source: Eurydice, "Administration and governance at local and/or institutional level" — https://eurydice.eacea.ec.europa.eu/eurypedia/poland/administration-and-governance-local-andor-institutional-level; ResearchGate "Local Government in Poland" (as above)

**9. Note on relevance/boundary to Poland's 1999/2017 tracking reforms (my core domain).** The 1999 reform's creation of larger-catchment lower-secondary schools (gimnazja) directly caused rural primary-school size reduction and closures as a side effect, distinct from the pure demographic-decline closures above — worth flagging as a related but separate causal channel.

The reduction of primary schooling from 8 to 6 years also had ramifications for teachers and schools, including job losses and the threat of closure for some smaller rural schools.
 The 1999 reform's explicit equity aim for rural areas: 
The newly established lower secondary schools were intended to contribute to the implementation of the substantial national curriculum reform and raise the quality of education particularly in rural areas. The new schools were to have larger catchment areas than the previously existing primary schools.

Source: World Class Learning Systems, "The Power of System Reform: Evidence from Poland" — https://worldclasslearningsystems.com/the-power-of-system-reform-evidence-from-poland/; Springer chapter (as above)
