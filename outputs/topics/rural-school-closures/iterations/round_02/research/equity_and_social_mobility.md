# Research Notes: Equity & Social Mobility Dimensions of Rural Primary School Policy (Hungary)

**Finding 1 — Territorial exclusion from education is already severe and growing.** A 2025 spatial-clustering study of Hungarian settlement-level data (2021–2024) found that 
by 2024, more than 1,200 of 3,155 settlements had no public educational institutions, raising concerns about territorial equity
. The same study found 
a deepening divide between dynamic urban/suburban zones and disadvantaged rural peripheries, with disadvantaged children increasingly concentrated in rural areas (up to 94% in certain settlements)
, while 
rapidly growing urban settlements face rising child-teacher ratios (e.g., up to 24:1 in some areas)
. Source: "Shrinking, growing or overburdened? Territorial patterns in child demographics and institutional burden in Hungary's public education system after 2021" (2025), https://www.researchgate.net/publication/396483025

**Finding 2 — Teacher quality gap compounds rural disadvantage.** Per the EU's 2025 Education and Training Monitor for Hungary: 
teacher shortages are greatest in rural areas, with the proportion of unqualified teachers in disadvantaged schools 2 to 3 times higher than in schools not disadvantaged
. Also, 
half of all teachers in the 2024/2025 academic year were 50 or older, while only 7.2% of teachers were under 30
. Source: Education and Training Monitor 2025 – Hungary, https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html

**Finding 3 — Small rural schools structurally drive between-school ethnic segregation.** A CEU working paper explains that in Hungary 
segregation in education occurs in cities between schools, and in villages – due to the higher costs of commuting to a smaller extent – due to the enrolment of non-Roma pupils in the schools of other settlements (often called "white flight")
. Historically 
the deterioration in efficiency caused by a decline in pupil numbers was mitigated by local governments reducing parallel classes rather than closing schools, leading to a large offer of small schools but reducing latitude for within-school separation — this especially applies to small rural schools operating only one class per grade
. Source: CEU Working Paper, "The Wider Context of the Segregation of Roma Pupils in Hungary" (2020), https://cps.ceu.edu/sites/cps.ceu.edu/files/attachment/publication/3258/cps-working-paper-educ-social-selection-education-hungary-2020.pdf

**Finding 4 — "Ghetto schools" are overwhelmingly a rural, and specifically North-Hungarian, phenomenon.** Research using the NABC database identified 
nearly 300 'ghetto schools' (with a Roma rate above 50%) among school premises over recent years — nearly half located in Northern Hungary and three-quarters operating in villages
. The same chapter notes 
resilient schools are more likely to be located in rural areas and in localities where school-choice options are more restricted
 — i.e., closing rural options may remove one factor limiting "white flight"-driven segregation, or alternatively concentrate disadvantage further depending on design. Source: "Education of Roma and Educational Resilience in Hungary," Springer (2020), https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-52588-0_6

**Finding 5 — Roma students face starkly worse outcomes, concentrated where rural schools are most affected.** Latest national data (2024) show 
58.7% of Roma students (18–24-year-olds) were school dropouts, versus 9.3% among non-Roma
, and 
among early school leavers, the most vulnerable groups are those living in rural areas, Roma and people with disabilities
. Separately, one estimate (Rorke, 2016, cited by Humanium) states 
45% of Roma children in education are placed in segregated schools or classrooms
. Sources: National Policies Platform YouthWiki Hungary 4.1, https://national-policies.eacea.ec.europa.eu/youthwiki/chapters/hungary/41-general-context ; Humanium, https://www.humanium.org/en/school-segregation-of-roma-children-discrimination-in-education-in-hungary/

**Finding 6 — International causal evidence: school closures raise dropout risk, especially for disadvantaged/low-income students.** A Chilean study found 
school closure increases the probability of high-school dropout between 49 and 68 percent (1.8 and 2.5 percentage points)
 and identified large effects on grade repetition (Grau, Hojman & Mizala, 2018, cited in arXiv:2203.17101, https://arxiv.org/pdf/2203.17101). A U.S. rural-school-closure advocacy synthesis states 
closures also disproportionately affect low-income students and communities, and mass closures affect regions with declining economies and high poverty
, and cites Texas research showing 
students involved in a closure experience a drop in test scores... but also longer term reductions in obtaining a college degree, employment, and earnings, especially for students from low-income backgrounds
. Source: Rural Schools Open, https://ruralschoolsopen.org/understand-closure/

**Finding 7 — Educational disruption has large, persistent earnings effects, larger for the poor.** Kóczán (2024, World Development) using NATO-bombing-driven school closures in Serbia as a natural experiment found 
those in first grade at the time of the shock earn about 7–9 percent less 20 years after the shock than unaffected cohorts just younger than them, and impacts are larger for those in the bottom half of the income distribution
. While this is a disruption/war-shock study rather than planned rural consolidation, it is a relevant precedent for the magnitude of long-run inequality effects from interrupted schooling. Source: "Lasting scars: The long-term effects of school closures on earnings," World Development 176 (2024), https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0305750X23003327

"Rational cuts? The local impact of closing undersized schools," Regional Science and Urban Economics, 109, 104057
, is cited as a reference in the Hungarian settlement-pattern study above but I did not retrieve its findings directly. Flagging for further retrieval: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.regsciurbeco.2024.104057

**Finding 9 — Class-size/peer-effect research suggests disadvantaged pupils benefit disproportionately from small classes, an argument potentially favoring keeping small rural schools open for the pupils they currently serve.** Brookings summarizes Tennessee's STAR experiment: 
elementary school students randomly assigned to small classes outperformed their classmates who were assigned to regular classes by about 0.22 standard deviations after four years
, and 
the positive effects of class size were largest for black students, economically disadvantaged students, and boys
. This is US evidence, not Hungary-specific, and is about class-size reduction, not small-school survival per se — a caveat for transferability. Source: Brookings, "Class Size: What Research Says and What It Means for State Policy," https://www.brookings.edu/articles/class-size-what-research-says-and-what-it-means-for-state-policy/

**Finding 10 — Residential vs. educational segregation are not simply mechanically linked, complicating "just build one central school" solutions.** Citing Kertesi & Kézdi (2014): 
residential segregation is not directly associated with educational segregation because, due to free parental school choice, the mobility of pupils among school districts reduces the role played by residence
. This implies that consolidating rural schools into a central "hub" school does not automatically desegregate — non-Roma/wealthier parents may exercise choice to avoid the consolidated school, reproducing segregation at a different scale. Source: CEU Working Paper (as above), https://cps.ceu.edu/sites/cps.ceu.edu/files/attachment/publication/3258/cps-working-paper-educ-social-selection-education-hungary-2020.pdf
