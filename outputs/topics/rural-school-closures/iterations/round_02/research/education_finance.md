I'll research current evidence on rural primary school economics, demographic trends, and policy alternatives, focusing on Hungary and comparable contexts.
## Research Notes: Rural Primary Schools in Depopulating Hungarian Villages

**1. Hungary's school-site network is shrinking, but slower than child population.** The number of school sites (feladatellátási hely) fell from 9,046 (2021) to 8,590 (2024); primary-education (ISCED 1–2) sites fell from 3,609 to 3,521 over the same period. By 2024, more than 1,200 of Hungary's 3,155 settlements had no public educational institution at all, and disadvantaged children have become increasingly concentrated in rural peripheries (up to 94% in certain settlements).
Source: "Shrinking, growing or overburdened? Territorial patterns in child demographics and institutional burden in Hungary's public education system after 2021" — https://www.researchgate.net/publication/396483025_Shrinking_growing_or_overburdened_Territorial_patterns_in_child_demographics_and_institutional_burden_in_Hungary's_public_education_system_after_2021

**2. Sharp, recent acceleration of Hungary's birth decline is now hitting the school pipeline.** Births fell from 110,800 (2015) to 84,300 (2024) and 77,600 (2025) — a 22.5% ten-year drop. The teachers' union president (PSZ) projects the first major kindergarten enrollment shock around 2027 and a visible primary-grade (alsó tagozat) enrollment drop by 2030, with annual cohorts shrinking from a historical ~110,000 to ~72,000.
Source: Economx, "Katasztrofális a helyzet a közoktatásban..." — https://www.economx.hu/belfold/2026/05/19/robbanas-elotti-allapot-totyik-tamas-psz-demografiai-kozoktatas-gyermekszam-pedagogusok/

**3. Government's stated policy is to avoid forced closures via consolidation ("összevonás") rather than outright shutdown, but insiders say this signals inevitable restructuring.** A ministerial commissioner stated the goal is protecting small settlements' and small schools' education "as long as there is demand" for it, while acknowledging mergers and local solutions will be needed. Journalistic analysis notes this phrasing tacitly admits major changes are coming, learning lessons from the more brutal, sudden closures of the mid-2000s.
Source: Válasz Online, "Óvodák már lakat alatt, de itt lappang az iskolabezárási hullám is" — https://www.valaszonline.hu/2025/10/17/ovodak-iskolak-bezarasa-demografia-oktatas/

**4. Historical Hungarian evidence: small-settlement schools shrank far faster than the national average during the 2000s consolidation wave.** Between 2001–2007, nationally the number of primary institutions fell 10% (4,718→4,233) while enrollment fell 14%; but on small settlements the number of school sites fell 26% (813→604) and student numbers fell 25%. In 2003–2005 alone, 95 independent primary schools closed in settlements under 1,100 people, driven by both financing-rule changes and demographic decline.
Sources: "Az általános iskolai intézményhálózat változásai" — https://folyoiratok.oh.gov.hu/uj-pedagogiai-szemle/az-altalanos-iskolai-intezmenyhalozat-valtozasai-a-kisebbsegi-oktatas ; Bódi–Fekete, "Iskolák-óvodák elhelyezkedése és bezárása" — https://politikatudomany.tk.elte.hu/uploads/files/71_90_Bodi_Fekete(1).pdf

**5. A Hungarian econometric study found local school presence is associated with reduced net out-migration specifically in the smallest villages.** A positive relationship between school operation and migration was found for the smallest settlements, suggesting that in villages under 500 people, having a local school may genuinely reduce net emigration, whereas for 500–1,000-person villages the effect was ambiguous and needs further study (Hermann, 2002).
Source: OFI, "A kistelepülési iskolák szerepe a települések népességmegtartó erejében" — https://ofi.oh.gov.hu/tudastar/kistelepulesi-iskolak/2-statisztikai-elemzes

**6. International quasi-experimental evidence: rural school closures cause measurable local population decline.** A Danish study of the simultaneous 2011 closure of eight village schools in one peripheral municipality found clear evidence of a negative population effect; a difference-in-differences analysis showed a population decline of 7.6 percentage points over the following 10-year period.
Source: ScienceDirect, "Do rural school closures lead to local population decline?" (Sørensen, Schmidt et al.) — https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0272775717306155

**7. Small schools cost substantially more per pupil — an OECD/US consensus finding.** Small schools are typically more costly per student than larger ones, partly due to isolation, extra transportation, and inefficient building use. US research (not Hungary-specific) finds districts under 100 students may be twice as expensive to operate as those over 2,000, and roughly 50% more expensive than 100–300-student districts; doubling enrollment in a 300-pupil district may cut per-pupil operating costs by about 62%.
Sources: OECD, "School Funding Formulas" working paper — https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2012/05/school-funding-formulas_g17a213a/5k993xw27cd3-en.pdf ; Urban Institute, "Small and Sparse: Defining Rural School Districts for K–12 Funding" — https://www.urban.org/sites/default/files/2023-03/Small%20and%20Sparse-Defining%20Rural%20School%20Districts%20for%20K%E2%80%9312%20Funding.pdf

**8. Hungary's own funding formula historically built in a small-settlement subsidy multiplier, but researchers flagged it as possibly still below true minimum costs.** In the early-2000s normatíva (per-pupil grant) system, settlements of 1,100–3,000 people received double the base per-pupil subsidy, and settlements smaller than that received quadruple, explicitly to offset small-school diseconomies of scale; a 2007-era analysis concluded such supplements were "economically fully justified" (közgazdaságilag teljesen indokolt), while noting that base normatívák often fell short of actual per-pupil operating costs.
Source: OFI, Horn Dániel, "Mennyibe kerül egy gyerek?" — https://ofi.oh.gov.hu/horn-daniel-mennyibe-kerul-egy-gyerek-0

**9. Closure "savings" are often illusory — older literature warns costs can even rise.** Reviewing US rural closure economics, Holland and Baritelle (1975) concluded that in rural areas the savings possible from school closure are unlikely to achieve large expenditure reductions, and in some instances closures have actually increased costs when the goal was to reduce them (due to transportation, distance-education support, and other substitution costs).
Source: ERIC, Witham, "The economics of (not) closing small rural schools" — https://files.eric.ed.gov/fulltext/ED415036.pdf

**10. Community-level costs of closure extend beyond the school budget line.** US commentary literature (Tieken) notes schools are often the largest employer in rural communities and provide political cohesion; closures are linked to declining property values and, in some evidence (e.g., Arkansas), disproportionately affect poorer and minority communities. In Hungary, current reporting confirms villages without a local school see falling real-estate prices and reduced ability to attract young families, while long-distance child transport ("iskolabusz") remains logistically unresolved 20 years after mass consolidation began — supervision gaps have led some parents to organize informal rotating "bus duty."
Sources: The Conversation, "School closures can hit rural communities hard" — https://theconversation.com/school-closures-can-hit-rural-communities-hard-128837 ; Válasz Online (as above) — https://www.valaszonline.hu/2025/10/17/ovodak-iskolak-bezarasa-demografia-oktatas/

**11. Hungarian academic findings suggest small-village schools are not an efficiency scandal, and closing them is not clearly justified on performance grounds.** A review by Hermann (2007) cited in Bódi–Fekete concludes that small-settlement school effectiveness is similar to larger village schools and only slightly below urban schools — neither results nor higher per-pupil costs justify treating small-settlement school maintenance as a major efficiency problem for Hungarian public education.
Source: Bódi–Fekete, "Iskolák-óvodák elhelyezkedése és bezárása" — https://politikatudomany.tk.elte.hu/uploads/files/71_90_Bodi_Fekete(1).pdf

**12. Public opinion strongly opposes renewed school closures/interruptions.** In an Ipsos survey (households with children, N=280), three-quarters of Hungarian families with children said a renewed closure of schools/kindergartens would create serious difficulty, and 78% of the households most critically affected by such closures live in small rural towns or villages.
Source: Ipsos Hungary — https://www.ipsos.com/hu-hu/nagy-terhet-jelentene-az-iskolabezaras-magyar-csaladok-szamara
