## Research Notes — Finnish School System & Rural School Policy (for Hungary transferability analysis)

**1. Scale of school network contraction, historical.** Between roughly 2000 and 2018 the number of comprehensive schools in Finland fell sharply, while average school size grew: "Between then and 2018 the number of schools in Finland decreased by 42 percent," and "In 2018, Finland had 100 schools with over 700 students, up from 13 schools of that size in the year 2000," with average student body rising "from 145 to 236 pupils." Source: Yle, "Small schools in danger of closing as Finland continues to centralise education," 2020, https://yle.fi/a/3-11478615

**2. Future closure scenarios (EDUFI/Statistics Finland projections).** A national agency (EDUFI) report modeled three scenarios for further network shrinkage due to declining birth cohorts: a mild scenario of ~25% closures, a "third of schools" scenario, and a severe scenario citing ~37% network shrinkage; official Kari Nyyssölä judged the middle scenario ("a third of schools") most likely. Source: same Yle article, https://yle.fi/a/3-11478615

**3. Current (2025) demographic pressure update.** A newer report finds Finland's shrinking birth cohorts mean "some areas in Finland will no longer be able to offer elementary school education to local children, meaning that they will have to travel neighbouring municipalities," tied to Finland's fertility rate hitting "a historic low of 1.25 in 2024." Source: Yle News, "Report: Half of Finland's elementary schools face closure due to plummeting birth rate," Sept 2025, https://yle.fi/a/74-20182333

**4. Cost rationale and unresolved evidence gap.** Municipal decision-makers cite cost savings as the main driver: a principal states "The cost per pupil can be twice as high in Oulu's smaller schools." However, the EDUFI counsellor noted "Few follow-up studies have... been conducted to determine whether municipalities actually saved money by closing small schools" — the efficiency rationale is asserted more often than empirically verified. Source: https://yle.fi/a/3-11478615

**5. Causal evidence: closures accelerate depopulation.** A peer-reviewed difference-in-differences study (2010–2018 data) found "the population of the surrounding community decreased more than before school closure" after closure, with "impacts of school closure... slightly more negative" in rural communities, suggesting "a higher risk of self-reinforcing population decline." Source: Kalaoja et al./ScienceDirect, "Primary school closures and population development," 2021, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0743016721000115 (also indexed at https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0883035509000160)

**6. Community/social-capital function of village schools.** Urban geographer Venla Bernelius (Helsinki University) argues "Schools are central to the appeal of an entire area" beyond their educational function — echoed in academic literature describing the small rural school's "role as a producer and maintainer of local social capital." Sources: https://yle.fi/a/3-11478615 ; Autti & Hyry-Beihammer, "School Closures in Rural Finnish Communities," Journal of Research in Rural Education, 2014, https://jrre.psu.edu/sites/default/files/2019-08/29-1.pdf

**7. Historical school-network principle and its erosion.** Since 1921 Finland's system was built on the principle that "the schools must be located close to the pupils (the distance from home to school being less than 5 km)," meaning "almost every village has had its own school" — a norm now under pressure. Since the 1990s recession, "the network of small rural schools in Finland has been radically weakened." Sources: Kalaoja & Pietarinen, "Small rural primary schools in Finland: A pedagogically valuable part of the school network," 2009, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0883035509000160 ; https://jrre.psu.edu/sites/default/files/2019-08/29-1.pdf

**8. Pedagogy of small/multigrade schools — teacher-education gap.** Interviewed Finnish village-school teachers report having "learnt step by step to teach multigrade groups, although they were not prepared for it in their teacher education," and researchers recommend teacher education give "better tools for multigrade teaching which is the working environment" of small rural schools. Source: Springer chapter, "The Heart of the Small Finnish Rural School," https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-981-19-7985-9_4 ; ResearchGate, "What Is the Future of Village Schools?", https://www.researchgate.net/publication/266891253_What_Is_the_Future_of_Village_Schools_A_Case_Study_on_the_Life_Cycle_of_a_School_in_Northern_Finland

**9. Comprehensive school (peruskoulu) reform bundle, 1970s.** The reform "replaced the primary and grammar school system with the nine-year comprehensive school," implemented gradually region by region "starting with Northern Finland in 1972... and it reached the rest of the country by 1977." It coincided with upgrading teacher education "from short seminary programs to university-level master's degrees by the mid-1970s." Sources: thisisFINLAND, https://finland.fi/life-society/100-years-finnish-education-success-stories/ ; Centre for Public Impact, https://centreforpublicimpact.org/public-impact-fundamentals/education-reform-in-finland-and-the-comprehensive-school-system/ ; Grokipedia, https://grokipedia.com/page/Education_in_Finland

**10. Governance evolution: centralize then decentralize.** After the 1970s reform, administration was initially centralized; then "the late 1980s the school system was decentralised. Schools became more responsible for their own management," with municipalities (over 300, each with "considerable autonomy") now controlling school-network planning — which is precisely why closure decisions are locally driven and vary. Source: "A History of Educational Reforms in Finland," https://www.researchgate.net/publication/275634576_A_history_of_educational_reforms_in_Finland ; Oxford Academic, "The Finnish Comprehensive School," https://academic.oup.com/book/42635/chapter/358101849

**11. Multi-tiered pupil-support system (relevant to small-school viability).** Since 2010, Finland has used a multi-tiered ("tehostettu tuki"/intensified support) framework comparable to US Response-to-Intervention, requiring specialist staffing that small remote schools may struggle to provide. Source: Björn et al., "Response-To-Intervention in Finland and the United States," 2018, https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5996047/. The Pupil and Student Welfare Act (1287/2013) mandates multidisciplinary student welfare services. Source: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8475110/

**12. Hungary-specific comparative case.** A Hungarian case study on the village of Erdőkürt documents a 2007 wave of "closure and merger of rural schools as a consequence of top-down pressure on local governments," which triggered an NGO campaign ("Human Chain for Rural Schools") against closures; the study examines community mobilization to save a school. Source: Kovács, "Rescuing a small village school in the context of rural change in Hungary," 2012, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0743016712000216

**13. Hungary historical pattern (pre-1990).** Hungarian rural-school policy underwent its own centralizing shocks: the post-1948 Stalinist regime pursued "radical demolition of small settlements and their schools now considered as 'uneconomic,'" and later "regional administrative organs exploiting their own periphery" produced "mass closure of rural schools in the 1970s under the motto of 'rationalization.'" Source: Springer chapter, "Ideology, Spatial Planning, and Rural Schools: From Interwar to Communist Hungary," https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-18799-6_6

**14. Comparative Hungary/Romania finding.** A comparative study concludes "rural communities with schools have a higher level of welfare," though causality is ambiguous — closures correlate with but do not straightforwardly cause the "worst living conditions." Source: "The Presence or the Absence of Schools: What Impact for Rural Communities in Hungary and Romania?", https://journals.usamvcluj.ro/index.php/agriculture/article/view/12020

**15. Recent Hungarian data (2021+).** A 2025 working paper examines "territorial patterns in child demographics and institutional burden in Hungary's public education system after 2021," indicating active current research on shrinking/growing/overburdened school regions in Hungary. Source: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/396483025_Shrinking_growing_or_overburdened_Territorial_patterns_in_child_demographics_and_institutional_burden_in_Hungary's_public_education_system_after_2021
