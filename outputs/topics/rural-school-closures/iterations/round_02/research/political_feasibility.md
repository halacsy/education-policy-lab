## RESEARCH NOTES: Political Feasibility of Rural Primary School Reform in Hungary

### Finding 1 — Centralization removed local government control over school existence (structural precondition for durability analysis)
Since 2013, all municipal primary/secondary schools were nationalized: the state became the maintainer and employer of all pedagogical staff, run through KLIK (Klebelsberg Institution Maintenance Centre) across 198 school districts (tankerület). This means village mayors and municipalities NO LONGER have legal authority over whether their local school operates — decisions are made by state school-district centres. This is politically load-bearing: any reform (closure OR preservation) is decided centrally, so local mobilization must target the national government, not local councils.
Source: "Hungary - vocational schools under new administration from July 2015" (CEDEFOP) — https://www.cedefop.europa.eu/en/news/hungary-vocational-schools-under-new-administration-july-2015
Also: KLIK established 2013 to replace "uncertain local government funding" — https://2015-2019.kormany.hu/en/ministry-of-human-resources/news/klebelsberg-institution-maintenance-centre-receives-new-directorship
Evidence: strong (official/institutional sources).

### Finding 2 — Chronic KLIK under-budgeting drives closure/consolidation pressure
The recurring solvency problems of KLIK stemmed from systematic under-budgeting (Semjén et al. 2018), forcing school-district leaders to cut spending and undertake only minimum mandatory tasks. This is the fiscal engine behind consolidation pressure on small rural schools — the reversal risk is that budget cycles, not community need, determine school fate.
Source: "The Increasing Replication of Territorial and Social Inequalities in Public Education in Hungary" (Social Sciences, MDPI, 2022) — https://doi.org/10.3390/socsci11030095
Evidence: moderate (peer-reviewed, cites earlier empirical work).

### Finding 3 — Church takeover is the dominant "alternative to closure" mechanism, and it is politically durable because it aligns state + church interests
When municipalities could not afford school upkeep, churches took over schools; church-run schools receive substantially more per-pupil state money than municipal schools (reportedly nearly double historically). For many small villages with only one school, a church takeover can be the only way to keep a local school open — but critics argue it can violate rights to neutral education, and teachers may have to accept religious conditions to keep their jobs. The number of church-run educational institutions has doubled since 2010. Church-run schools can select students, whereas state schools must accept all in their catchment.
Sources: "Hungary: The Renaissance of Church-Owned Schools" (FutureChallenges) — https://blog.futurechallenges.org/local/hungary-the-renaissance-of-church-owned-schools/
"Settling for a 'good enough' school" (Dialectical Anthropology, 2024) — https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10624-024-09743-x
"Hungary Hands Over Public Schools to Religious Institutions" (TFP) — https://www.tfp.org/hungary-hands-over-public-schools-to-religious-institutions-2/
UN rapporteur Shaheed (2025): church schools enjoy higher state funding, attract better-qualified teachers, select higher-achieving students, leaving less-resourced public institutions with disadvantaged children — https://balkaninsight.com/2025/03/21/un-expert-on-right-to-education-criticises-desperate-conditions-in-hungarys-schools/
Evidence: strong for the mechanism and doubling; contested for the "double funding" figure (older/blog source).

### Finding 4 — Local consent for church takeover can be near-unanimous when it saves a village school
In Alsóörs, presented as a typical case, out of 96 families only 2 voted against transferring the school to the church — underscoring strong popular support when the alternative is closure. Roughly 80 schools had already been transferred by municipalities at that time. This is politically important: preservation-via-church can command broad local buy-in, which raises durability but also carries reversal risk on secular/pluralism grounds and for non-selected (Roma, disadvantaged) students.
Source: "Hungary Hands Over Public Schools to Religious Institutions" (TFP) — https://www.tfp.org/hungary-hands-over-public-schools-to-religious-institutions-2/
Evidence: weak (single illustrative case, advocacy outlet); treat percentages as anecdotal.

### Finding 5 — The state can and does REVERSE school control coercively (MET case) — demonstrating high reversal risk when a maintainer falls out of political favor
The Hungarian Evangelical Fellowship (MET) lost most of its school network to the government via forced closure and "voluntary" transfer in 2024, after the state withdrew statutory subsidies (July 2023) and seized bank accounts (July 2023). The government justified takeovers by claiming delay "would cause significant or irreparable harm to children and pupils," while the Helsinki Committee said the opposite was true. Parents marched in protest. This shows that maintainer status is politically contingent and reversible — a durability warning for any consolidation/preservation model resting on a specific operator.
Source: "End Game Nears for Hungarian Church at Odds with Orban" (Balkan Insight, Sept 2024) — https://balkaninsight.com/2024/09/05/end-game-nears-for-hungarian-evangelical-fellowship-at-odds-with-orban/
Evidence: strong (contemporaneous reporting with named sources).

### Finding 6 — Teachers are a proven, cross-ideological mobilizing bloc, but state repression has raised the cost of mobilization
2022–2023 saw some of Hungary's largest protests since 1989; teachers built a broad coalition of students, parents, and citizens cutting across political divides; religious/right-leaning schools also supported teachers. BUT: the right to strike was severely restricted; ≥14 teachers were fired for "civil disobedience"; police used tear gas at student-majority protests (May 2023). The Tanítanék movement's mailing list exceeds 90,000. Implication: rural-school mobilization has a real base but operates in a repressive, self-censoring environment.
Sources: "Teachers in Hungary Oppose Democratic Backsliding" (Horizons Project, 2025) — https://horizonsproject.us/teachers-in-hungary-oppose-democratic-backsliding/
"Lessons on challenging authoritarianism..." (Waging Nonviolence, 2024) — https://wagingnonviolence.org/2024/07/challenging-authoritarianism-orban-hungary-teachers-movement/

Evidence: strong (multiple independent sources).

### Finding 7 — The "Status Law" (Act LII of 2023) makes teacher relocation a government tool — directly relevant to consolidation
The 2023 law ("revenge/status law", in force 1 Jan 2024) allows employers/school-district centres to change a teacher's place of work; the only concession was that lower-primary (grade ≤4) teachers cannot be transferred, while teachers of older children can be transferred, even ad hoc. This is precisely the legal instrument by which consolidation would redistribute rural teachers — and it was passed without meaningful consultation, which is itself a durability/reversal liability. ~16,000 teacher shortage; ~5,000 threatened to resign over the bill.
Sources: "Hungary's 'revenge bill' against teachers..." (Heinrich Böll Stiftung, 2023) — https://cz.boell.org/en/2023/05/12/hungarys-revenge-bill-against-teachers
"Hungary passes controversial education law..." (ETUCE) — https://www.csee-etuce.org/en/news/member-organisations/5248-hungary-passes-controversial-education-law-despite-protest-from-education-trade-unions
Evidence: strong.

### Finding 8 — EU conditionality now underwrites teacher pay through 2030 — a stabilizing / durability lever
Hungary and the EU agreed a permanent teacher pay-rise programme: €1.8 billion in EU funds 2024–2030/31 (~HUF 735 billion), raising average pay to 80% of the average graduate wage, a level the government committed to maintain until at least 31 December 2030. In the first years, most of the increase is EU-financed. Meaningful social dialogue with unions was made part of the EU agreement. Government initially tied pay rises to release of frozen funds, then retracted that framing. Implication: EU conditionality can lock in commitments across electoral terms — a rare durability anchor — but also makes the sector hostage to the EU-Hungary rule-of-law dispute.
Sources: "Hungary raises teachers' wages with EU support" (European Social Fund Plus) — https://european-social-fund-plus.ec.europa.eu/en/projects/hungary-raises-teachers-wages-eu-support
"EU Funds Contribute to Rise in Teachers' Pay" (Hungary Today, Feb 2025) — https://hungarytoday.hu/eu-funds-contribute-to-teachers-pay-rise/
"Newly-arrived EU Funds..." (Hungary Today, May 2024) — https://hungarytoday.hu/newly-arrived-eu-funds-to-contribute-to-teachers-pay-rise/
Evidence: strong (EU + multiple reports); note 88% domestic / 12% EU framing appears in some Hungarian govt communications — https://hungarytoday.hu/teachers-pay-rise-to-continue-from-january/ (share-of-funding figures are contested between sources).

### Finding 9 — Rural/disadvantaged-area pay premium exists (a preservation incentive already in law)
Teachers in "catching-up"/beneficiary (disadvantaged) municipalities are to be paid 20% higher; those working with disadvantaged children get larger increases. This is a pre-existing policy lever that could be scaled to make rural retention politically and practically viable — relevant to the pedagogus_erdekvedo condition on rural-teacher parity.
Source: "Teachers' Pay Rise to Continue from January" (Hungary Today, Dec 2024) — https://hungarytoday.hu/teachers-pay-rise-to-continue-from-january/
Evidence: moderate (govt-sourced reporting).

### Finding 10 — Demographic collapse is accelerating, hardening the closure driver
2025 TFR fell to ~1.31 (from ~1.38–1.39 in 2024), the lowest in 14 years; only ~72,000 births in 2025 (down 7.1% y/y), ~22% below the 2021 peak of ~93,000; population fell below 9.5 million; natural decrease ~52,200. About 30% of Hungarians live in villages (2024). Analysts explicitly note declining births "may lead to school consolidations and teacher surplus in some regions." Implication: the enrollment squeeze on village schools will intensify through the 2030s regardless of policy — meaning "do nothing" is not a stable equilibrium and reform pressure is structural, not cyclical.

### Cross-cutting observations for later structured analysis (not new findings)
