# Expert analysis: education_finance

## Findings (evidence)
- Hungarian 6/8-year gimnázium students show roughly 10% larger competency gains than similar-ability, similar-background peers in 4-year gimnázium, per new statistical-model research controlling for family background. [evidence: moderate — Horn Dániel (Corvinus), G7 Dirik podcast, July 2026]
- Only about 8% of a school-age cohort attends 6- or 8-year gimnázium tracks, making this a small but disproportionately contested segment of the system. [evidence: moderate — Telex/G7 podcast summary, July 2026]
- Hungary has the highest socioeconomic segregation of students between schools in the EU, and a 53-point SES performance gap versus a 33-point EU average. [evidence: strong — EU Education and Training Monitor 2025, Hungary country report]
- Historical intake data show much stronger rejection rates at admission (~46%) in low-poverty-share classes versus high-poverty-share classes (~22%), indicating admissions gatekeeping tracks SES more than ability alone. [evidence: moderate — OFI (Oktatáskutató és Fejlesztő Intézet) study]
- Peer-reviewed and institute-level literature concludes early tracking at age 10-12 in Hungary exacerbates social inequality, with track access driven more by family background than test scores. [evidence: strong — Horn et al. 2016 chapter; Kertesi & Kézdi HLCS/NABC-based studies]
- Cross-country evidence indicates early tracking has modest-to-negative effects on average achievement while delaying tracking improves social mobility without a clear achievement trade-off. [evidence: strong — IZA World of Labor review of tracking literature]
- Some German states (Berlin, Brandenburg) delayed tracking to age 12 specifically to counter equity harms otherwise seen from age-10 tracking common elsewhere in Germany. [evidence: moderate — IZA Policy Paper on the PISA Shock (Hanushek & Woessmann 2006; Entorf & Lauk 2008)]
- The claim that church-maintained schools receive about four times more per-student state funding than state schools is contested; the Ministry and Catholic Bishops' Conference dispute the calculation methodology. [evidence: contested — Index.hu (2019) vs. Ministry/Oktatási Hivatal rebuttal]

## Interpretation
The evidence converges on a real tension rather than a simple verdict: Hungarian data suggest 6/8-year tracks may add real pedagogical value for admitted students, but admissions are strongly class-selective, and Hungary already has the EU's highest school segregation — so any value-added is purchased partly by concentrating advantage. International evidence suggests delaying tracking is a low-average-achievement-cost way to reduce inequality, but Hungary's specific 8%-of-cohort elite-track structure, entrenched parental demand, and contested church-school funding architecture make a German-Berlin-style delay or Spanish-style comprehensive reform politically costly, not merely technically straightforward.

## Assumptions
- The ~10% learning-gain estimate is treated as a single recent study result, not yet independently replicated or formally peer-reviewed in sources cited. [assumption]
- Funding comparisons across maintainer types assume normatíva and feladatfinanszírozás figures are being compared on a like-for-like per-student basis, which sources dispute. [assumption]

## Position
Given Hungary's already-highest-in-EU school segregation, any policy alternative to age 10-12 korai szelekció should be judged primarily by whether it reduces SES-based sorting at intake, not only by whether it preserves the apparent ~10% pedagogical value-added advantage for admitted students.

## Uncertainties
- Whether the ~10% value-added estimate reflects genuine school effect versus unmeasured peer-composition or teacher-sorting confounds remains unresolved. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Independent replication with richer teacher-quality and peer-composition controls, ideally using admission-cutoff regression discontinuity design.)
- The magnitude of church-school funding advantage (if any) relative to state schools remains contested and unresolved by available sources. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A transparent, methodologically agreed per-student cost audit across all maintainer types published by an independent fiscal authority.)
- No Hungary-specific costed estimate exists for what a Berlin-style or Spain-style delay of korai szelekció to age 12-14 would cost in transition (teacher retraining, curriculum redesign, building capacity). (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A dedicated costing study or pilot-programme budget analysis for a delayed-tracking scenario in the Hungarian context.)
