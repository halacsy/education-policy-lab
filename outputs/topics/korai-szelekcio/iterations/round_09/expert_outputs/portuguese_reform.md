# Expert analysis: portuguese_reform

## Findings (evidence)
- Portugal maintained a comprehensive (non-selective) school structure through compulsory basic education and improved PISA results by approximately 31 points (0.3 standard deviations) between 2000 and 2015–2018 without introducing early tracking. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA trend reports; Marôco, Portugal: The PISA Effects on Education, Springer]
- Portugal's documented gains in learning outcomes resulted from curriculum standards clarification, increased school autonomy, regular student assessment, and vocational pathway development—not from changes to selection age. [evidence: strong — Springer, Curriculum and Educational Reforms in Portugal; Crato 2020]
- Hungary's early selection system (at ages 10 and 12 into 8-year and 6-year grammar schools) amplifies achievement variance and inequality of opportunity without raising average performance. [evidence: strong — Terrin & Triventi, The Effect of School Tracking on Student Achievement and Inequality: A Meta-Analysis, SAGE; Horn D. (2013), Diverging performances, Research in Social Stratification and Mobility]
- TEIP (Portugal's targeted school support programme) reduced student dropout and grade retention but did not close competency gaps relative to non-TEIP schools, and no rigorous longitudinal evaluation of its long-term student outcomes exists. [evidence: moderate — MDPI, Has the Portuguese Compensatory Education Program Been Successful; ScienceDirect Procedia 2014; CIIE Porto]
- Between 2016 and 2018, Portugal's grade-retention reduction initiative saved 112 million euros while reducing retained students by 26% across 663 schools. [evidence: strong — Público, Nov 12 2019; official Portuguese government reporting]
- Portugal's school selectivity index (PISA 2022) stands at 1.29 on the OECD scale, ranking 75th of 80 countries—among the lowest in the OECD. [evidence: strong — OECD Education GPS – Portugal, 2022]
- Cross-national evidence from multiple studies shows early tracking (age 10, as practiced in Austria, Germany, Hungary, and Slovakia) increases between-school achievement variance and social inequality without raising average achievement. [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Wößmann, foundational cross-national study; ScienceDirect 2026 synthesis]
- Hungarian municipalities that adopted 8-year grammar schools show that socio-economic status is the strongest predictor of mathematical test performance among tracked countries. [evidence: moderate — Gurzo, K., The long term effects of early tracking in schools, CEU eTD 2015]

## Interpretation
Portugal demonstrates that significant learning-outcome improvement is achievable in a comprehensive, non-selective system through targeted non-structural reforms—curriculum alignment, assessment transparency, retention reduction, and equitable school support—without lowering average achievement or restricting access. By contrast, Hungary's early-selection structure (age 10–12) creates inequality and performance stratification without a corresponding efficiency or achievement premium. The evidence suggests that early tracking solves no efficiency problem and creates substantial social-stratification problems; its persistence reflects structural inertia and political interest rather than pedagogical necessity. TEIP-style targeted support shows promise in reducing dropout but requires stronger design and rigorous evaluation to demonstrate competency-gap closure. Retention reduction, Portugal's clearest non-structural success, is both a fiscal and equity win when implemented systematically.

## Assumptions
- Portugal's comprehensive system is demographically and institutionally comparable to Hungary's in relevant dimensions (school resources, teacher training, family educational background distribution) such that its outcomes are transferable. [assumption]
- Curriculum clarity, school autonomy, and regular assessment drive outcomes independently of selection structure; their effects are not interactive with early tracking in ways that would reverse the ordering observed in Portugal. [assumption]
- The PISA index is a valid proxy for school system 'selectivity' and its observed ranking reflects genuine institutional differences rather than measurement artifact. [assumption]
- Reduction in grade retention can be achieved at scale through pedagogical and assessment reform without structural dismantling of the current school-type system. [assumption]

## Position
Hungary's early-selection system at ages 10–12 increases achievement inequality and socio-economic stratification without raising average performance, and similar learning gains to Portugal's are achievable through non-structural reforms (curriculum clarity, assessment transparency, retention reduction, targeted support) within a comprehensive framework.

## Uncertainties
- Whether targeted support (TEIP-type) can close achievement gaps rather than merely reducing dropout rates remains uncertain because no longitudinal study has tracked student competency trajectories beyond retention reduction. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: A 5–8 year longitudinal matched-cohort study comparing TEIP-enrolled and non-enrolled students, controlling for baseline socio-economic status and prior achievement, tracking assessed competency gain (not just exam pass rates).)
- The fiscal and pedagogical cost of phase-out or structural reform of Hungary's 8-year grammar school track (replacing it with comprehensive entry and specialist bands at 14+) has not been modeled in published Hungarian research. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: A scenario analysis prepared by Hungarian education ministry or major research institute, modeling capital reallocation, teacher redeployment, and transition costs over 10–15 years under different phase-in schedules.)
- Whether Hungary's school-choice system (szabad iskolaválasztás) amplifies or mitigates selection effects is empirically unknown; choice may create de facto academic sorting at comprehensive-system entry points. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A cross-municipal comparison of school-choice patterns and outcomes in comprehensive vs. tracked districts, with matching on initial school capacity and socio-economic intake, to isolate choice-driven stratification.)
- The long-term labor-market and civic outcomes (earnings, employment stability, political participation) of students who remained in a comprehensive system versus those tracked into lower academic grades in Hungary remain unquantified. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A linked administrative register study (tax authority, labor force survey, electoral records) following cohorts 20+ years post-secondary-entry, comparing tracked vs. comprehensive-entry outcomes within Hungary or using a quasi-experimental discontinuity at a reform boundary.)
- Whether a phased shift from 8-year to 6-year grammar school entry (age 12), preserving selection but delaying it, would reduce social stratification or merely postpone it is a structural question not addressed in the current evidence base. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Comparative research on countries that have experimentally implemented age-postponement of tracking (e.g., comparing 10 vs. 12 entry regimes in the same institutional context), ideally with randomized school-network pilots in Hungary.)
