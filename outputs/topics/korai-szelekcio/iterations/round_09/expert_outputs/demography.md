# Expert analysis: demography

## Findings (evidence)
- Annual births in Hungary fell from approximately 125,000 in the early 1990s to 77,500 in 2024, with a further 7.1% decline to ~72,000 in 2025, representing the lowest recorded level since 1949 and forcing school-network consolidation decisions in the 2020s–2030s regardless of selection policy. [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks; hvg.hu (31 Jan 2025); economx.hu (9 May 2026); nepszava.hu (30 Jan 2026)]
- Primary general school (általános iskola) enrollment declined 17.3% over 20 years, from 859,000 pupils in 2005/2006 to 710,000 in 2024/2025, tracking the shrinking birth cohorts directly, with first-grade intake falling 1.9% year-on-year in 2024/2025 to 95,000. [evidence: strong — Népszava (2025), citing KSH data]
- A demographic cliff is measurable one cohort ahead: Hungary currently has approximately 17,000 fewer one-year-olds than four-year-olds, equivalent to roughly 600–800 kindergarten/class-groups, meaning at least one hundred small-school's worth of children will vanish from the system within a few years. [evidence: moderate — Válasz Online (17 Oct 2025)]
- Government policy is using selection-track admission mechanics (minimum-score thresholds set by the Klebelsberg Center maintainer) to shrink the gimnázium track's share of cohort intake from approximately 40% toward 30% or lower, independent of demand; gimnázium enrollment share was flat-to-slightly-rising before this intervention. [evidence: moderate — Szülői Hang (15 Jan 2025); Index (17 Jan 2025)]
- Minimum-score admission policy for 6- and 8-year grammar schools remains a school-maintainer-level discretionary decision with no national legal basis, creating inconsistent two-tier admissions: some institutions (e.g., Toldy Ferenc Gimnázium, Budai Nagy Antal Gimnázium) enforced 70-point cutoffs in 2025; others initially treated them as non-binding recommendations then retroactively rejected borderline students. [evidence: moderate — 168.hu (9 Sep 2025); eduline.hu (5 Sep 2025); Educational Authority (Oktatási Hivatal) confirmation]
- In 2024/2025 approximately 194,000 students attended gimnázium nationally (45.4% of upper-secondary students); 6- and 8-year tracks serve a numerically small but structurally significant slice of each cohort, geographically concentrated in urban areas, notably Budapest. [evidence: strong — KSH 2024/2025 preliminary data; nool.hu (21 May 2026)]
- Standardized test-score evidence shows a composition gap at selection point: 6th-graders competing for 6-year gimnázium entry averaged 58.6 points (out of 200), 8th-graders competing for 9th-grade entry averaged 51.2 points, with 47.8% of 8th-graders scoring below 50 versus only 23.15% of 6th-graders scoring above 70, indicating two demographically and academically distinct competing populations. [evidence: moderate — Pénzcentrum (20 Jan 2025)]
- Long-run population projections (KSH/HDRI SEEMIG medium-variant) show continuous population decline through at least 2060 under all fertility and migration scenarios, with the under-20 population declining from ~2 million (2012) to approximately 1.4 million by 2060, meaning the school-age cohort available for all secondary tracks shrinks structurally through 2040 and beyond. [evidence: strong — KSH/HDRI SEEMIG projection; demografia.hu (2018)]
- Cross-border analogue (Beregszász, Ukraine's Hungarian-minority region): Ukraine's Ministry of Education mandated from September 2025 that state subsidy no longer covers grammar-school operation below 45 students, forcing local consolidation and busing of older students to larger hub schools, illustrating a hard enrollment floor's effect on small-town selective-track viability under demographic decline. [evidence: moderate — karpataljalap.net (25 June 2026)]
- Total fertility rate in Hungary fell from 1.51 (2023) to 1.38 (2024) to 1.31 (2025); natural population decrease exceeded 52,000 in 2025; population fell to 9,489,000 by 1 January 2026, all representing accelerating decline, not stabilization. [evidence: strong — hvg.hu (31 Jan 2025); economx.hu (9 May 2026); nepszava.hu (30 Jan 2026)]

## Interpretation
Hungary's school-age population is shrinking at an accelerating pace driven by historic low fertility and natural decrease. The annual flow through primary school has fallen 17% over two decades and continues to decline. This forces school-network consolidation regardless of whether early selection (6/8-year grammar schools) exists or not. However, government policy is simultaneously using minimum-score admission thresholds as a supply-side lever to further compress the grammar-school track's share of the remaining cohort from ~40% toward 30%, even though demand for grammar education has remained stable or risen. The impact is twofold: (1) structural — the absolute pool of students shrinks, making small schools and dispersed selective tracks unsustainable; (2) policy-driven — the share available to grammar tracks is deliberately reduced independent of demand, creating a pincer effect. Selection at age 10–12 remains numerically small (6- and 8-year tracks serve fewer students than 9th-grade entry) but carries outsized symbolic and distributional weight, concentrating opportunities in urban and already-advantaged families. The evidence shows composition differences at selection point (higher-performing 6th-graders competing for early entry) but does not settle whether selection causes the gap or merely sorts pre-existing advantage. Cross-border and within-country evidence both confirm that demographic thresholds (45-student viability floors) force closure of small or dispersed schools; the same logic applies to selective tracks.

## Assumptions
- Cohort-size projections (declining under-20 population) are stable across the planning horizon (2025–2040) and do not assume major immigration reversing natural decrease. [assumption]
- School-maintainer sustainability thresholds (minimum 45 students, reference floors for class viability) are a binding constraint; schools below such floors cannot operate financially or pedagogically. [assumption]
- Minimum-score admission policies as applied by maintainers represent government intent to shift supply; they are not merely institutional quality signals. [assumption]
- The 'completion' of demographic decline (population stabilization) will not occur within the planning horizon (2040); the trend is structurally downward. [assumption]
- Test-score differences at selection point reflect both composition (family background, prep investment) and possibly school-level intake quality, but not causal school effect; the direction of selection bias is unknown. [assumption]

## Position
Demographic decline through 2040 will force school-network consolidation and closure of small selective tracks independent of policy choice, but government's simultaneous use of score-threshold levers is accelerating selective-track contraction beyond what demographic decline alone would cause, concentrating remaining grammar-school slots among already-advantaged urban populations.

## Uncertainties
- The net causal effect of early selection (6/8-year grammar-school entry at age 10–12) on individual student achievement and social mobility is not directly measured in Hungarian evidence; test-score gaps at selection reflect composition, not school value-added. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Quasi-experimental studies (e.g., discontinuity design at score cutoffs, matched-pairs within-municipality comparison controlling for family background) comparing life outcomes of grammar-track vs. non-grammar track entrants with similar pre-entry test scores.)
- The relationship between consolidation-induced school closure, teacher workforce redeployment, and pedagogical quality in surviving small schools is unknown; closing small grammar schools may displace experienced teachers or may improve efficiency. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Longitudinal administrative data on teacher movements, school staffing and pupil-to-teacher ratios pre- and post-consolidation, and value-added models of school quality in rural vs. urban surviving schools.)
