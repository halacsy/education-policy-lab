# Expert analysis: international_comparison

## Findings (evidence)
- Difference-in-differences evidence from 75 countries/regions over 20 years shows early tracking increases test-score dispersion across PISA, PIRLS, and TIMSS cycles, though effect sizes vary substantially by study pair. [evidence: strong — Educational Assessment, Evaluation and Accountability (2021); https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11092-020-09346-4]
- Early-tracking countries show larger inequality increases between primary and secondary school than late-tracking countries; Germany (+0.71), Greece (+0.30) and Czech Republic (+0.25) contrast with Turkey (−0.63), New Zealand (−0.50), Canada (−0.32). [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann, NBER Working Paper 11124; https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w11124/w11124.pdf]
- Poland's 1999 delayed-tracking reform (replacing early streaming with comprehensive lower-secondary) yielded PISA math gains of 0.25 SD, reading 0.28 SD, science 0.16 SD by 2006, with approximately one-third attributed to instructional time and the remainder to compositional/resource changes. [evidence: strong — Jakubowski, Patrinos, Porta & Wiśniewski, Education Economics 24(6) 2016; https://ideas.repec.org/a/taf/edecon/v24y2016i6p557-572.html]
- Poland's labour-market follow-up (2022) found that vocational-track students who remained an extra year in comprehensive schooling benefited from better peers and teachers, with the reform reducing inequality; however, Poland reversed the reform in January 2017 after 18 years. [evidence: strong — Drucker, Horn & Jakubowski, Journal for Labour Market Research (2022); https://labourmarketresearch.springeropen.com/articles/10.1186/s12651-022-00317-4]
- Hungary has the OECD's highest socioeconomic achievement gaps: 53-point gap between children of graduate and non-graduate parents, compared to 33-point EU average; 37% of Hungarian students were low achievers in 2022. [evidence: strong — European Commission, Education and Training Monitor 2025 — Hungary; https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html]
- Hungary's selection architecture includes at least four formal selection points before higher education; in Central European systems (Czechia, Slovakia, Hungary) the formal primary/lower-secondary unity is a façade, because 6–8 grade secondary schools cream off high-performing, typically middle-class pupils as early as end of grade 5–7. [evidence: strong — CEU Center for Policy Studies (2020); https://cps.ceu.edu/sites/cps.ceu.edu/files/attachment/publication/3258/cps-working-paper-educ-social-selection-education-hungary-2020.pdf]
- Hungarian educational selectiveness shows 2–3 years of measured difference in intellectual development between class means within primary-school cohorts, and Hungary has selection at age 7 as a first point. [evidence: moderate — Csapó, Molnár & Kinyő, IEA; https://www.iea.nl/sites/default/files/2019-04/IRC2008_Csapo_Molnar_etal.pdf]
- 74% of 25–64 year-olds with at least one tertiary-educated parent also attained tertiary qualification in Hungary, indicating strong intergenerational transmission of educational advantage. [evidence: strong — OECD, Education at a Glance 2024 — Hungary; https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2024/09/education-and-a-glance-2024-country-notes_532eb29d/hungary_faab9f42/1b2a3510-en.pdf]
- Registry-backed: In PISA 2012–2022, the share of reading/maths score variance explained by socioeconomic status in Hungary is 19–25%, versus 12–14% OECD average. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2012-2022 country notes]
- Registry-backed: Cross-country difference-in-differences evidence shows earlier between-school tracking increases inequality of outcomes without raising mean performance. [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006, Economic Journal]
- As of 2024/25, Hungary has 880 secondary general-education (gimnázium) schools nationally, with eight-year programmes starting from grade 5 and six-year programmes from grade 7. [evidence: strong — Eurydice, Organisation of general secondary education — Hungary; KSH (2024/25)]
- A 2025 systematic review of five OECD PISA equity reports (2009–2022) plus academic studies found persistent, not uniformly improving, socioeconomic equity trends across PISA cycles 2000–2018/2022, suggesting cross-national equity gains from single structural levers are contested and cycle-dependent. [evidence: moderate — ScienceDirect systematic review (2024–2025); https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666374024000414]

## Interpretation
Hungary combines early selection (age 7, then age 10–12 into 6/8-year gimnázium), exceptionally high socioeconomic achievement gaps (53 vs 33 EU average), and strong intergenerational educational transmission. International evidence shows that early tracking systems increase inequality without raising mean achievement; Poland's natural experiment of delayed tracking produced gains of 0.25–0.28 SD and reduced inequality, though Poland later reversed this reform. Hungary's inequality is not due to mean underperformance but to the amplification of family background through its multi-point selection system. The transferability of Poland's or other late-tracking systems to Hungary faces political, resource, and institutional constraints not yet quantified; equity gains are robust in DiD literature but cycle-dependent in PISA trends. Whether early selection *solves* problems (e.g., teacher efficiency in heterogeneous classrooms) remains an unresolved trade-off between efficiency and equity.

## Assumptions
- The PISA cycles 2000–2022 are comparable across countries and years in terms of equity measurement methodology. [assumption]
- Selection effects (composition) and school value-added can be sufficiently disentangled in DiD and propensity-score-matching designs to attribute gains to delayed tracking rather than other concurrent reforms. [assumption]
- Poland's institutional and demographic context in 1999 is sufficiently similar to Hungary's current context that the Polish effect sizes could be transferable. [assumption]
- The 6/8-year gimnázium system is the primary driver of Hungarian socioeconomic inequality rather than other institutional factors (e.g., catchment-area segregation, resource allocation, teacher distribution). [assumption]
- The international late-tracking evidence applies to the 10–12 age selection point; earlier selection (age 7) may have different effects that are less studied. [assumption]
- Hungarian politicians and stakeholders would accept a phase-out of 6/8-year grammar schools if evidence of equity gains were sufficiently strong, though this is not certain. [assumption]
- Current data availability permits accurate measurement of selection effects independent of broader resource constraints or pedagogical factors. [assumption]
- The teacher shortage in Hungary does not fundamentally alter the expected benefits of delayed tracking, though it may slow implementation or reduce gains. [assumption]

## Position
International difference-in-differences evidence indicates that delaying selection from age 10–12 to age 14 would likely reduce Hungary's socioeconomic achievement gap by approximately 0.15–0.25 SD (based on Polish comparable gains), but this would require compensatory instructional resources and teacher supply that Hungary currently lacks in demonstrable quantity.

## Uncertainties
- Whether the Polish 1999 delayed-tracking reform's effect sizes (0.25–0.28 SD) would replicate in Hungary given different demographic composition (Roma representation, migration patterns) and school-system institutional differences. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Natural experiments or quasi-experiments within Hungary (e.g., regional pilot phase-outs of 8-year grammar schools with random assignment or propensity-score matching); administrative data on prior achievement, family SES, and post-selection outcomes for Poland and Hungary cohorts.)
- The causal attribution of Hungary's 53-point socioeconomic achievement gap to early selection versus other structural factors (catchment segregation, resource allocation within schools, teacher quality distribution, out-of-school learning inequality). (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Decomposition analyses isolating the contribution of selection (composition) from within-school and between-school resource differences using multi-level models and Hungarian census/administrative data linked to student outcomes.)
- Whether teacher supply and instructional-resource capacity in Hungary would permit full-scale implementation of delayed tracking without reducing quality for higher-achieving or vocational-track students. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Current teacher-shortage forecasts for Hungary (age-cohort, attrition, supply from initial teacher education, cross-border migration); cost estimates for expanding comprehensive-school capacity and teacher development programmes; simulations of class composition and teacher allocation under delayed tracking.)
