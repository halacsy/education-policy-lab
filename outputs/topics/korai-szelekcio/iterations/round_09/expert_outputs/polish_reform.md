# Expert analysis: polish_reform

## Findings (evidence)
- Poland's 1999 delayed-tracking reform, which extended comprehensive general education from 8 to 9 years (moving selection from age 12 to age 15), produced PISA score gains of 0.25 SD in mathematics, 0.28 SD in reading, and 0.16 SD in science between 2000 and 2006; students in likely-vocational tracks gained approximately one standard deviation (~100 PISA points). [evidence: strong — Jakubowski et al. (2016), Education Economics 24(6)]
- Between-school equity improved markedly after the 1999 reform: Poland reduced performance disparities between schools from one of the largest in the OECD to one of the smallest. [evidence: strong — OECD synthesis; Jakubowski et al. 2016]
- Individual-level socioeconomic equity did not improve after the 1999 reform: using PISA 2000–2012 data, there was no significant decrease in correlation between students' socioeconomic status and their test results. [evidence: moderate — Academia.edu; Openedition synthesis]
- The 1999 reform's labor-market effects persisted: using EU-SILC data, a 2022 study found the reform decreased inequalities via improved peer and teacher composition for previously low-track students, with gains confirmed by multiple methods (Liwiński 2020; Strawinski & Broniatowska 2021). [evidence: strong — Drucker, Horn & Jakubowski (2022), Journal for Labour Market Research 56:13]
- Poland's 2016–2019 reversal abolished the 3-year gimnazjum, returned to 8-year primary school followed by tracked secondary schools, raised school entry age back to 7, curtailed special-needs inclusion, revised curriculum toward more Polish history/language, ended project-based learning, and disbanded lower-secondary teacher teams. [evidence: strong — World Class Learning Systems (2024)]
- Political opinion on the 1999 comprehensive-school reform became sharply partisan by 2015–2016: a 2016 IPSOS/OKO.press poll showed Law and Justice (PiS) supporters 73% positive / 10% negative and Civic Platform/Modern voters 25% positive / 68% negative, indicating school reform became an identity marker rather than an evidence-driven choice. [evidence: moderate — Medium (@thelambert); Springer chapter (2030-41882-3_7)]
- The 2017 implementation shock from forced overlap of two secondary-school cohorts (final gimnazjum graduates and first primary-school-only graduates competing simultaneously) intensified admission competition; in Warsaw alone, 3,173 students did not gain entry to any school that year. [evidence: moderate — Medium (@thelambert)]
- PISA 2022 results for Poland fell to levels last seen in 2003, erasing gains made since 1999; researchers attribute part of this decline to the full effect of the 2016–2019 reversal (which only fully affected the 2022 cohort, not the 2018 cohort) combined with 26+ weeks of school closures during COVID-19. [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA 2022 Country Notes (Poland); Springer chapter (2031-69284-0_9)]
- Some elements of Poland's 1999 reform proved politically irreversible: increased school autonomy, external examinations, broader access to preschool, and higher-education access changes remained in place even after the broader 2016–2019 reversal, suggesting that decentralized and legally-entrenched reforms resist rollback better than central mandates alone. [evidence: moderate — Springer chapter (2030-59031-4_7)]
- Teacher unions split during the reversal: the larger ZNP union (which had supported the 1999 comprehensive-school reform as international best practice) organized nationwide protests, and 80% of schools participated in a 2019 teacher strike over pay and conditions tied to reform-driven chaos, though the strike did not reverse the policy. [evidence: strong — Balkan Insight (2020); Springer chapter (2030-41882-3_7)]

## Interpretation
Poland's experience demonstrates a powerful learning effect from delayed tracking: eight years of comprehensive schooling followed by age-15 selection produced substantial gains in average achievement (especially for at-risk students) and between-school equity. However, it reveals two critical vulnerabilities: (1) individual-level socioeconomic inequity proved harder to shift—school convergence did not equalize advantage within schools; and (2) structural school reforms, once implemented successfully, can become partisan identity markers and be reversed for political rather than evidence-driven reasons, erasing gains within a single cohort cycle. The 2016–2019 reversal incurred both immediate costs (implementation shock, teacher strikes, student-placement chaos) and measurable long-term costs (PISA decline). Reforms that embed changes in decentralized and legally-entrenched systems rather than relying solely on central mandate appear more durable. For Hungary, this implies early selection cannot be evaluated in isolation: it must account for whether alternatives are politically resilient over a full education cohort's passage through the system.

## Assumptions
- The attribution of Poland's PISA gains primarily to the 1999 timing reform (rather than to confounding policy or demographic changes, or measurement variance) is robust enough for causal inference in a quasi-experimental setting, though alternative explanations (e.g., broader infrastructure investment, curriculum reform, teacher-training expansion) cannot be fully ruled out. [assumption]
- The 2016 IPSOS/OKO.press polling result accurately reflects genuine opinion at that moment, not campaign artefacts; the sharp partisan split reflects genuine value disagreement rather than mere measurement error. [assumption]
- Hungary's institutional and political context is sufficiently similar to Poland's that lessons about reform durability, partisan politicization, and implementation shock transfer meaningfully, even though Hungary's school system differs in governance and school-choice rules. [assumption]
- The 2022 PISA decline can be partially attributed to the 2016–2019 reversal's effects, separable from COVID-19 learning loss, because the full reversal affected only the 2022 cohort; however, the counterfactual outcome under the 1999 reform structure without COVID is unobserved and must be estimated. [assumption]
- The labor-market wage gains observed by Drucker, Horn & Jakubowski (2022) and other studies reflect genuine returns to improved secondary-school composition and teacher quality, not merely selection into higher-paying occupations among the same underlying ability distribution. [assumption]

## Position
Early selection at age 10–12 into the 6- and 8-year grammar school tracks is justifiable only if Hungary can sustain politically durable institutional design, ensure between-school equity gains translate into individual-level SES equalization, and maintain implementation continuity across electoral cycles—outcomes Poland achieved partially and lost entirely, demonstrating that the benefits of delayed selection (1 SD gain for at-risk students, improved labor-market outcomes, reduced school segregation) outweigh the benefits of early tracking only if political reversibility risk and individual-SES gains (not achieved in Poland) are weighted equally with test-score gains.

## Uncertainties
- The precise causal decomposition between the 1999 reform's structural effect (unified intake) versus its resource/composition effects (investment and teacher change) remains uncertain; even Jakubowski et al.'s two-thirds/one-third split is an estimate and could shift with different methodologies. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Controlled variation in implementation timing or intensity across regions, or new difference-in-differences analysis on cohort-level PISA data with explicit separators for resource spending and teacher composition.)
- Whether individual-level SES gaps could have been closed by longer-duration exposure to the 1999 reform (the Polish cohort was measured at age 15, not adulthood) or whether structural timing reform alone is insufficient without targeted SES-based support programs remains untested. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Longitudinal follow-up of Polish cohorts into adulthood (age 25–35), measuring lifetime earnings, intergenerational mobility, and SES-conditional achievement by school-sector composition; similar cohort studies in other countries with delayed-tracking reforms.)
