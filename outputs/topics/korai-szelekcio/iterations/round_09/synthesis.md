# Synthesis

## Overview
The expert record converges on a well-evidenced empirical core — Hungary's early selection at age 10–12 into parallel 6- and 8-year grammar-school tracks is strongly associated with the EU's highest socio-economic school segregation, a 53-point parent-education achievement gap (versus 33 EU average), and a raw 116-point grammar/vocational gap that shrinks to 24 points once family background is controlled — while diverging sharply on what follows from this. International comparisons (Finland, Poland, Portugal) show delayed tracking can raise equity without harming average achievement, but Poland's reversal after 18 years and Finland's post-2000 erosion via school choice demonstrate that structural gains are neither self-sustaining nor immune to political reversal. Demographic collapse (fertility down to 1.31, births near historic lows) is forcing school-network consolidation regardless of selection policy, adding urgency but also complicating any reform's isolated evaluation. Legally, early selection is a narrow codified exception rather than the structural default, making it comparatively tractable to reform, but any change must respect felmenő-rendszer (grade-by-grade phase-in) protections. Politically, the 2026 Tisza government has moved selection reform from hypothetical to live agenda, but faces immediate Fidesz 

## Disagreement map
### Whether early selection itself (age 10–12) is the primary driver of Hungary's segregation, or one factor among several equally important structural mechanisms (school choice, catchment, maintainer growth)
- **hungarian_education_system, finnish_reform, portuguese_reform, education_finance**: Early selection at 10–12 is the primary structural cause and should be phased out; it is a codified, legally distinct exception whose removal is comparatively tractable. Why: Hungary's own 2000s comprehensivization reform raised the floor without lowering the ceiling; Portugal achieved comparable gains with zero early tracking; Finland's mobility gains came specifically from delaying tracking to 16.
- **school_network_planning, demography, legal_and_governance** (minority): Early selection is numerically small (8–12% of cohort) and geographically concentrated; school choice, catchment rules, and maintainer shifts (church-school growth) operate largely independently of the 10–12 selection point and would continue sorting students even if it were abolished. Why: ~40% of general schools (60% in villages) send zero pupils to 6/8-year tracks already; church-school enrollment share rose from 10.4% to 27% independent of formal selection-age law; demographic decline alone forces network consolidation regardless of selection policy.

### Whether the ~10% value-added observed in elite 6/8-year tracks justifies preserving some form of early selection
- **hungarian_education_system, education_finance**: The modest value-added is real but does not justify the system's equity cost; it does not offset the segregation and gap effects, and its causal robustness (peer effect vs. pedagogy) is itself uncertain. Why: The raw achievement gap collapses from 116 to 24 points once SES is controlled, showing most of the apparent advantage is composition, not school quality; Hungary's own de-tracking history shows gains without ceiling loss.
- **equity_and_social_mobility** (minority): Value-added is heterogeneous by track type — positive and consistent for the 8-grade gimnázium but negative at grade 8 (turning positive only by grade 10) for the 6-grade gimnázium — meaning the pedagogical case differs materially by track and cannot be treated uniformly. Why: HÉTFA Working Paper panel data (2008–2014) shows divergent value-added trajectories by track, meaning a single verdict on 'early selection's pedagogical value' oversimplifies the evidence.

### Whether non-state (church/foundation) maintainers can realistically be restricted from absorbing phased-out selective capacity
- **hungarian_education_system, implementation_planning**: Church and foundation maintainers can, in principle, be regulated or incentivized not to expand selective places to offset a state-sector reduction, treating maintainer behavior as policy-influenced and reversible. Why: Legislative and funding levers (building-transfer rules, normatíva changes) are cited as the mechanisms by which church expansion occurred, implying they could equally be used to constrain it.
- **political_feasibility, legal_and_governance, school_network_planning** (minority): This restriction assumption is directly contradicted by observed political and legal constraints: church-maintained gymnasium share rose from 10.4% to 27% independent of formal selection-age law, the Tisza government's 12-point programme explicitly commits that church maintainers keep their operating conditions, and church/foundation admissions operate under partially autonomous concordat-based frameworks that a national reform cannot simply override. Why: The coalition constraint (protecting religious institutions) and the demonstrated growth trend independent of law changes indicate maintainer behavior is not simply a policy dial that reform can turn; restricting it risks alienating a coalition partner or triggering legal challenge.

### Political risk profile of selection reform: what the dominant risks are and how tractable they are
- **hungarian_education_system, political_feasibility**: Elite-school resistance and middle-class flight (to church/private alternatives) are the dominant risks, but the demographic reframing (declining cohorts eroding the exam's rationing function) offers a genuine, untested political opening to lower this resistance. Why: Central admission data show 8-year track applications falling >14% year-on-year while enrolment totals stay flat, supporting a narrative that the exam's scarcity-rationing rationale is eroding on its own.
- **political_feasibility** (minority): The scenario's own assumption that church and private maintainers will eventually comply with phased contraction is itself contradicted by the coalition's explicit commitment to protect their operating conditions, meaning the named risks (elite resistance, middle-class flight) understate the binding constraint, which is maintainer non-compliance backed by coalition politics, not merely public opinion. Why: Tisza's 12-point programme explicitly commits to preserving church maintainers' prior operating conditions, a binding coalition-political fact that pre-empts any assumption of eventual compliance.

### Whether structural (selection-age) reform or non-structural reform (curriculum, assessment, retention policy) is the more promising lever for equity gains
- **portuguese_reform** (minority): Comparable or greater PISA gains (0.3 SD) are achievable through non-structural reform alone (curriculum clarity, school autonomy, assessment, retention reduction) without touching the selection age at all. Why: Portugal improved PISA scores by ~31 points between 2000 and 2015–2018 while remaining fully comprehensive, and its retention-reduction programme alone saved €112 million while cutting retained students by 26%.
- **polish_reform, finnish_reform, international_comparison, hungarian_education_system**: Structural (selection-age) reform is necessary because international difference-in-differences evidence directly links early tracking architecture to inequality, and non-structural improvements alone (as in Poland pre-1999 or Finland pre-1972) did not resolve tracking-driven sorting. Why: Poland's 1999 delayed-tracking reform produced 0.25–0.28 SD gains specifically attributable to the structural timing change; Finland's mobility gains were tied to the age-16 tracking delay itself, not merely parallel reforms.

### Whether political durability of any reform is achievable, given Poland's reversal precedent
- **political_feasibility, implementation_planning**: Cross-party durability is low-probability in the near term (2026–2028): Fidesz has already framed any reform as totalitarian, and the Tisza government's own pattern of rapid unilateral reversal of predecessor reforms (teacher evaluation) raises the base-rate expectation of future reversal. Why: Poland's delayed-tracking reform, despite documented achievement gains, was reversed after 18 years by a change of government, demonstrating that structural reforms without cross-party consensus or constitutional entrenchment are vulnerable regardless of evidence quality.
- **legal_and_governance** (minority): Early selection's narrow, separately-codified legal status (as an exception to the age-15 structural default) makes it comparatively more tractable and potentially more durable to reform than Hungary's broader maintainer or curriculum architecture, since legislative change here does not require restructuring the entire secondary system. Why: The Nkt. § 4 defines secondary education as beginning at grade 9 with 6/8-year gymnasium as the only statutory exception, meaning reform can proceed via a narrower legislative lever than a full system overhaul.

## What the experts agree on
- Hungary exhibits the EU's/OECD's highest socio-economic school segregation, with a 53-point parent-education achievement gap versus a 33-point EU average. [evidence: strong]
- The raw achievement gap between grammar and vocational-track students (116 points) shrinks substantially (to 24 points) once family background/SES is controlled for, indicating most of the gap is compositional rather than a school-quality effect. [evidence: strong]
- International difference-in-differences evidence shows early tracking increases inequality of outcomes without raising mean performance. [evidence: strong]
- Poland's 1999 delayed-tracking reform produced measurable PISA achievement gains (0.16–0.28 SD) and improved between-school equity, but individual-level socioeconomic inequity did not improve, and the reform was politically reversed after 18 years. [evidence: strong]
- Hungary's school-age population is shrinking rapidly (fertility rate down to 1.31 in 2025, lowest birth numbers since 1949), forcing school-network consolidation regardless of selection policy. [evidence: strong]
- Church-maintained schools' share of gymnasium provision has grown substantially (from roughly 10.4% in 2010 to about 27% in 2020/21), concentrated disproportionately in elite 6/8-year tracks. [evidence: strong]
- Any structural change to Hungary's selection age must proceed via felmenő-rendszer (grade-by-grade, cohort-by-cohort) phase-in with a minimum lead time of roughly one year, per constitutional-doctrine and legislative practice. [evidence: moderate]
- Teacher workforce constraints (aging workforce, acute shortages in rural and STEM areas) are a binding precondition that must be addressed before or alongside any structural selection-age reform. [evidence: strong]
- The magnitude of church-school funding advantage relative to state schools remains contested and unresolved by available sources. [evidence: contested]
