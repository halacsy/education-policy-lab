# Critique: cost_checker

## S1.cost_categories
Objection: Cost section labels 'low-cost option' and 'high-cost option' for SES-proxy infrastructure and notes 'magnitude unpublished' for exam redesign/enforcement, but provides no numeric ranges, no transitional vs steady-state split, and omits predictable legal/litigation and maintainer-compensation costs. This is a concrete omission: the scenario asserts options of varying fiscal footprint (low/high) and recurring revalidation/enforcement needs yet gives no per-school, per-pupil, or national-range estimates or any sensitivity to Hungary's 2026 fiscal-consolidation pressures, making the claimed affordability judgments (e.g., 'minimal fiscal footprint') unsupported by the evidence tags provided.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Provide a switchable costing annex: (a) unit-cost estimates (per-school and per-pupil) for the two SES-proxy options (self-declaration checklist vs verified administrative linkage) with low/medium/high ranges and assumptions (data-engineering, staffing, annual maintenance); (b) one-off and annualized psychometric redesign and validation costs with ranges (pilot, national rollout, recurring revalidation), and a sensitivity table showing total 5-year and 10-year fiscal envelopes under three fiscal scenarios (status-quo, constrained consolidation, expansion). Explicitly add line items and ranges for legal/litigation costs (maintainer lawsuits, Alkotmánybíróság cases) and for maintainer negotiation/compensation (agreements with church/foundation maintainers), and separate transition (pilot + rollout + upfront staffing) from steady-state monitoring/enforcement. Use Ministry of Finance budget classifications, Oktatási Hivatal staffing salary scales, and a percent-of-education-budget sensitivity to anchor ranges rather than leaving magnitudes 'unpublished'.

## S2.cost_categories
Objection: The cost entry flags 'compensatory funding or capacity controls' as 'medium-to-high, multi-year, currently unbudgeted' but gives no quantitative estimate, omits modelling the timing mismatch between state contraction and likely non-state absorption (church/foundation expansion) and fails to estimate the fiscal sensitivity of compensatory costs to deficit/consolidation pressure. It also omits explicit costs of monitoring/enforcement to detect offsetting non-state expansion and legal costs of applying funding/ licensing levers against partially autonomous maintainers — all material to the scenario's feasibility given the stated 6.2% deficit projection.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Add a quantified costing annex with (1) a per-school/per-region compensatory-investment estimate (classroom upgrades, staffing supplements, targeted programmes) and aggregated national low/medium/high scenarios tied to projected displaced pupil counts under the proposed capacity reductions; (2) a 3–7 year cashflow showing transition-year peak costs versus steady-state maintenance costs; (3) explicit monitoring/enforcement budget line (data systems, legal counsel, compliance audits) and an estimate of potential contingent liabilities from maintainer legal challenges; and (4) sensitivity analysis showing how compensatory funding needs change under fiscal-consolidation vs fiscal-flexibility scenarios (e.g., percent of 2027 education budget) so policymakers can see when costs become politically infeasible.

## S3.cost_categories
Objection: The scenario lists multiple high-cost categories (early diagnostic/remedial support, teacher retraining/reallocation, facility conversion, anti-sorting enforcement) but gives no aggregate fiscal range, no per-teacher retraining unit cost, no conversion-capital estimate per school, and omits the predictable interaction with ongoing teacher-salary reforms (2024–2031). It therefore conflates transition investment (large, front-loaded) with steady-state recurring costs and fails to show whether expected benefits (Poland-like gains) could plausibly be purchased within realistic Hungarian fiscal envelopes; this omission undermines the scenario's credibility given the repeated claim that total costs are 'unquantified'.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Disaggregate costs into: (A) transition (year 0–5) — teacher retraining (unit cost × number of teachers needing retraining, with an alternative low-intensity CPD cost estimate), facility conversion (sqm × regional construction-cost index × schools converted), and one-off IT/assessment system build; (B) steady-state (annual) — ongoing diagnostic/remedial staffing, enforcement/monitoring for anti-sorting rules, and additional teacher salary or hiring costs to cover shortfalls. Provide low/medium/high scenarios and a financing plan (phased budget requests, use of EU funds, reallocation of existing lines). Anchor unit estimates using Ministry of Education payroll data, national construction cost indices, and Poland/Finland post-reform budget experiences as priors. Explicitly model timing overlaps and constraints with the 2024–2031 salary reforms to show workforce-cost interactions and possible fiscal bottlenecks.

## S4.cost_categories
Objection: The scenario cites Portugal's EUR 112 million two-year saving from retention reduction as evidence that one element can be net-cost-saving, yet fails to translate this precedent into Hungary-specific net-cost estimates for TEIP-style targeted funding vs expected fiscal savings. It also labels the TEIP funding line 'no numeric fiscal range available' and treats statutory 'cardinal-law' funding-floor proposals as a political step without costing the implied budget rigidity. This leaves policymakers without a sense of the net budgetary trade-offs or the fiscal vulnerability of the package under projected consolidation.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Provide a net-cost model that: (1) quantifies gross TEIP-style funding (per-school and aggregated) under low/medium/high coverage; (2) estimates savings from grade-retention reduction scaled to Hungary using Portugal's EUR 112m two-year result adjusted by pupil population and per-student cost differentials (and show alternative, conservative scaling assumptions); (3) models the fiscal impact of making TEIP funding a cardinal-law floor (multi-year locked commitments) versus an annual budget line, including scenarios under fiscal consolidation (6.2% deficit) that identify when statutory protection is affordable or impossible; and (4) include contingency line items for teacher recruitment/training required to deliver the programme and a monitoring/evaluation budget to generate the longitudinal evidence the scenario itself notes is missing.
