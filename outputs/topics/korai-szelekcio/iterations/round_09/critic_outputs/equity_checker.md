# Critique: equity_checker

## S1.equity_impact
Objection: Equity_impact overstates likely distributional gains without modelling who is actually reached and who is excluded. The scenario asserts it will “likely reduce SES-skew within remaining selective places” while simultaneously noting quota displacement of middle‑SES/rural pupils and leaving ~40% of general schools (60% in villages) sending zero pupils to tracked programs unaddressed. This is a concrete internal contradiction: the claim of likely reduction rests on weak-to-moderate evidence for SES‑proxy quotas and a weak evidence base for coaching‑resistant exam design in Hungary. The scenario does not quantify (a) how many advantaged vs disadvantaged places the quota would reserve in urban vs rural settings, (b) the scale and profile of middle‑SES displacement, or (c) whether displaced middle‑SES pupils would shift to church/foundation schools or remain in local general schools — despite explicit uncertainty that maintainer plurality and autonomy may block uniform enforcement. Because the scenario treats quota/proxy effectiveness as central without evidence-backed distributional modelling, it risks producing perverse outcomes (increased geographic inequity and stigmatization of quota‑labelled entrants) not flagged in the equity_impact text. (Speculative: the displacement and maintainer-absorption pathways are plausible given the documented growth of church/foundation gimnáziums but not empirically modelled here.)
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Replace the qualitative ‘likely reduces SES‑skew’ claim with a distributional impact section based on counterfactual modelling and pilot rules: (1) run a pilot with linked admissions+address+SES administrative data and publish regression‑discontinuity estimates showing who gains/losses by SES decile, urban/rural status, and maintainer type; (2) include explicit safeguards in the scenario (e.g., minimum locality‑level quotas, reserved places for rural disadvantaged pupils, anti‑stigmatization admissions briefs, and compensatory general‑school investment scaled to displacement estimates); and (3) add an enforcement plan for church/foundation maintainers (legal mapping + conditional funding/contract clauses) so the equity claim is conditional on demonstrable cross‑maintainer compliance shown in pilot evaluation.

## S2.equity_impact
Objection: Equity_impact correctly flags intensified SES skew but fails to engage the strongest countervailing evidence and therefore omits the primary displacement pathway. The scenario asserts that shrinking state selective capacity will make remaining slots more SES‑skewed and that church/foundation expansion could absorb displaced demand, but it does not quantify the maintainers’ demonstrated propensity to expand (from 10.4% to 27% share) nor model the net effect on overall selective‑track access. Given the evidence status is explicitly ‘contested’ for capacity‑contraction effects, the equity_impact’s conclusion (that lower‑SES pupils lose access without compensating investment) is under‑specified: it omits (a) a scenario in which non‑state maintainers fully absorb displaced wealthy applicants, leaving state slots less SES‑diverse but overall selective access unchanged or more segregated; and (b) the likely fiscal shortfall in compensatory funding under projected consolidation. Without empirical projection of where displaced applicants actually enroll, the equity_impact is incomplete and risks misleading policymakers about net equity effects.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Rework equity_impact to present alternative distributional scenarios with data-driven probabilities: (1) include quantitative projections using historical admissions and maintainer‑growth trends to estimate share of displaced applicants absorbed by church/foundation/private schools vs general‑school retention; (2) model impacts on urban vs rural general schools’ peer composition and required compensatory funding per displaced pupil; (3) specify required policy levers to prevent offsetting non‑state expansion (e.g., time‑limited moratoriums on new selective licences tied to funding, or normatíva adjustments) and attach a feasibility/legal risk appendix; and (4) make the equity conclusion conditional on these levers being enacted and on a published financing plan for general‑school compensation.

## S3.equity_impact
Objection: Equity_impact correctly identifies that anti‑sorting rules are 'load‑bearing' but treats their enforceability as an open assumption rather than a central, operational requirement. The scenario states delaying selection will improve between‑school equity only if explicit anti‑sorting rules for choice and catchment are implemented and enforced; however, it does not specify which anti‑sorting measures are feasible against partially‑autonomous church/foundation maintainers, nor does it assess enforcement capacity (legal, administrative, political). It also omits the interaction with teacher‑shortage risks: if teacher reallocation fails, rural or disadvantaged areas could see quality declines, worsening inequality even as selection is delayed. Given the scenario relies on Poland/Finland analogies (moderate evidence) but Hungary has stronger maintainer plurality and coalition constraints, the equity_impact is under‑specified and overly optimistic absent a concrete anti‑sorting package and workforce plan.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Revise equity_impact to make the equity outcome explicitly contingent and operationalized: (1) enumerate a concrete anti‑sorting package (e.g., statutory catchment definitions, transparent common admissions with locality weighting, caps on selective intake growth by maintainer, conditional funding tied to desegregation metrics) and include a legal assessment of their enforceability against church/foundation autonomy; (2) add a teacher‑capacity mitigation plan (targeted recruitment, relocation incentives, retraining timeline with measurable milestones) and model regional quality risk if milestones miss; (3) commit to phased pilots where each anti‑sorting tool is tested with segregation index outcomes pre‑registered; and (4) downgrade any unconditional equity claim until pilot evaluations show enforceability and workforce adequacy.

## S4.equity_impact
Objection: Equity_impact flags a plausible equity‑neutral/negative outcome but leaves out a key distributional mechanism: how non‑structural reforms will interact with the existing gatekeeping to protect or harm low‑SES high‑performers. The scenario notes that leaving the 10–12 gate intact may legitimize early selection while compensatory measures help general schools, but it does not specify whether compensatory gains accrue to students who remain in general schools versus those who are subsequently excluded by gatekeeping. It also claims transferability from Portugal without reconciling Portugal’s low baseline segregation and different maintainer landscape. Consequently, the equity_impact is vague about which subgroups (multiply‑disadvantaged, rural, low‑SES high‑achievers) will benefit or be left behind and omits conditional policy protections for low‑SES high‑achievers who risk continued exclusion from elite tracks.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Tighten equity_impact by disaggregating beneficiaries and losers and adding protective mechanisms: (1) provide subgroup projections (multiply‑disadvantaged, low‑SES high‑achievers, rural pupils) using matched administrative data to show who would benefit from TEIP‑style funding and who remains excluded from selective tracks; (2) include targeted access measures for low‑SES high‑achievers (e.g., outreach, fee‑waived test‑prep, reserved boarding slots, or holistic admissions pathways) so compensatory investment does not only improve non‑elite outcomes while elite gates remain closed; (3) explicitly compare Hungarian baseline segregation metrics with Portugal’s and adjust transferability claims accordingly; and (4) make the scenario’s equity claim conditional on statutory funding‑floor protections for TEIP lines to avoid cut‑first fiscal risk.
