I'll research current evidence on Hungary's early selection system (6/8-year gimnázium tracks) — costs, equity effects, and alternatives.
# Research Notes (education_finance, Round 9)

**Finding 1 — Learning-gain estimate for 6/8-year tracks.** New Hungarian research (Horn Dániel, Corvinus University) finds students in 6- and 8-year gimnázium tracks show measurably larger competency development than similar-ability, similar-background peers who stayed in 4-year gimnázium, based on before/after national competency-test comparisons controlling for family background. Reported as "~10%" larger gains. Source: G7 Dirik podcast summary, Telex, July 4 2026, https://telex.hu/g7/podcast/2026/07/04/dirik-podcast-horn-daniel-hatosztalyos-nyolcosztalyos-gimnaziumok ; also https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/20260710_elitgimnazium-hat-es-nyolcosztalyos-szelekcio-iskolarendszer-kompetenciameres-transzparencia

**Finding 2 — Scale of the tracked population.** Only about 8% of a school cohort attends 6- or 8-year gimnázium programs, versus nearly two million children/students in the whole public+higher education system — meaning the debate concerns a small but politically salient slice of the system. Source: same Telex article above.

**Finding 3 — Segregation and SES gap, EU comparison.** The 2025 EU Education and Training Monitor for Hungary states Hungary has the highest level of segregation of students from less- and more-advantaged socio-economic groups into different schools in the EU (citing Gutiérrez et al. 2020 and PISA 2022), and that the performance gap between children of graduate vs non-graduate parents is 53 points in Hungary against a 33-point EU average. Source: https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html

**Finding 4 — Historical admissions selectivity by class/poverty.** An OFI (Oktatáskutató és Fejlesztő Intézet) study of 6/8-year gimnázium classes found strong socioeconomic sorting at intake: in classes where today's poor-family share is lowest (≤9%), historical rejection rates at admission were ~46%; in classes with the highest poor-family share (≥18%), rejection rates were only ~22%, implying much weaker gatekeeping for poorer intake classes/locations. Source: https://ofi.oh.gov.hu/iskolak-topon-6-es-8-evfolyamos-gimnaziumi-kepzes

**Finding 5 — Peer academic literature on Hungarian early tracking.** Multiple studies (Horn et al. 2016 chapter "Early tracking and competition – A recipe for major inequalities in Hungary"; Kertesi & Kézdi-based Hungarian Life Course Survey/NABC analyses) conclude that early tracking at ages 10-12 exacerbates social inequalities in educational outcomes, and that family background significantly impacts students' access to academic tracks, more than test scores. One synthesis states Hungary now operates the most selective education system in Europe per 2022 PISA results. Sources: https://www.academia.edu/109968054/Early_tracking_and_competition_A_recipe_for_major_inequalities_in_Hungary ; https://real.mtak.hu/42964/1/8_Hungary_final_5_February_2016_hd_u.pdf ; https://www.researchgate.net/publication/327244375_Chapter_8_Early_tracking_and_competition_-_A_recipe_for_major_inequalities_in_Hungary

**Finding 6 — International comparative evidence on delayed tracking.** A World of Labor/IZA review states that the effects of early tracking on average educational achievement are very modest, even slightly negative, while empirical evidence shows delaying tracking to a later age improves social mobility without significantly harming average achievement — i.e., no clear efficiency-equity trade-off in the cross-country literature. Source: https://wol.iza.org/articles/school-tracking-and-intergenerational-social-mobility/long

**Finding 7 — German state-level natural experiment on delaying tracking.** In Germany, most states track at age 10, which significantly increases educational inequality (Hanushek & Woessmann 2006; Entorf & Lauk 2008), while a few states (Berlin, Brandenburg) have introduced comprehensive primary schooling until grade 6 (age 12) specifically to reduce these negative equity effects. Source: https://docs.iza.org/pp140.pdf

**Finding 8 — Spain's 1990s comprehensive reform as a policy-alternative case.** Spain's 1990s reform delayed the start of vocational tracking from age 14 to age 16 and avoided differential treatment of students until 16, a change explicitly designed, per the OECD, for equity reasons — illustrating a costed, politically contested structural alternative to early selection. Source: https://www.educationnext.org/pisa-mission-failure-with-so-much-evidence-student-testing-why-do-education-systems-struggle/

**Finding 9 — Financing dispute around church-maintained gimnáziums (a key maintainer/political-economy angle).** Investigative reporting (Index, 2019, citing Költségvetési Felelősség Intézet analyst Romhányi Balázs) claimed church-maintained schools receive roughly four times more state support per student than state schools when normalized for actual spending categories, a claim the Ministry and Catholic Bishops' Conference explicitly disputed as methodologically flawed (comparing normatíva-based vs feladatfinanszírozás-based systems). This is a genuinely contested empirical claim relevant to the cost/political-economy side of selection, since church-run gimnáziums are disproportionately represented in the 6/8-year elite track. Source: https://index.hu/gazdasag/2019/05/08/negyszer_tobb_penz_forras_egyhazi_iskolak_allami_koltsegvetes_tanulok_diak_roma_cigany_szegregacio_elkulonites/ ; rebuttal explanation: https://folyoiratok.oh.gov.hu/uj-kozneveles/az-allami-es-egyhazi-fenntartasu-iskolak-finanszirozasarol

**Finding 10 — Proposed non-structural alternative: resourcing regular gimnáziums instead of restructuring.** Horn Dániel proposes, as one alternative to full structural reform (abolishing 6/8-year tracks), giving additional resources to ordinary ("sima") gimnáziums so that upper-middle-class parents have less incentive to seek out elite 6/8-year schools — an incentive-based rather than structural lever — and cites Portugal's decades-long, cross-government, data- and school-autonomy-based reform trajectory as an aspirational model for Hungary. Source: https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/20260710_elitgimnazium-hat-es-nyolcosztalyos-szelekcio-iskolarendszer-kompetenciameres-transzparencia

**Finding 11 — Elite public debate frames talent development vs. segregation as compatible, unresolved.** A July 2026 roundtable (sociologist Kende Ágnes, gimnázium directors Kampós András and Horváth Bálint) surfaced genuine practitioner disagreement: proponents argue the schools also perform "tehetséggondozás" (gifted education) and are not "only about selection," while critics emphasize the exclusionary/segregating effect and the difficulty of the central admissions exam for lower-SES families. No consensus reached; explicitly framed as requiring better data on the admissions process itself. Source: https://www.valaszonline.hu/2026/06/26/elit-gimnaziumi-igazgatok-szociologus-vita-szelekcio-oktatas/
