# Research Notes — Early Selection & the 6/8-Year Gimnázium (Finnish Comparative Evidence)

**1. Finland's reform delayed tracking from age 11 to age 16, not from age 10-12 to 18.** The Finnish comprehensive school reform (1972–1977) is the closest real-world natural experiment to "what if a country abolished early academic selection." 
The reform increased the age at which students are separated into vocational and general tracks from 11 to 16 as well as updated the curriculum to include more general content instead of vocational.
 Source: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0927537120301561

**2. Delaying tracking measurably raised intergenerational income mobility.** 
The results indicate that the reform reduced the intergenerational income elasticity by 23% from the pre-reform elasticity of 0.30 to post-reform elasticity of 0.23.
 This is one of the most-cited causal estimates in the tracking-and-equity literature (Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009, Journal of Public Economics). Source: https://ideas.repec.org/a/eee/pubeco/v93y2009i7-8p965-973.html

**3. The mobility gain was driven mainly by cognitive-skill gains among disadvantaged pupils.** 
In Finland, a move towards a more comprehensive system has been estimated to have reduced the intergenerational income elasticity from 0.30 to 0.23, with a large part of the effect driven by improved cognitive skills amongst pupils from more disadvantaged backgrounds (Pekkala Kerr et al., 2013; Pekkarinen et al., 2009).
 A comparable French delayed-tracking reform (age 11→13) produced convergent results: 
a delay in tracking from age 11 to age 13 led to improvements in completed education and a 6% increase in wages across affected cohorts, with most of the benefits concentrated amongst those from disadvantaged backgrounds (Canaan, 2020).
 Source: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/46497403_School_Tracking_and_Intergenerational_Income_Mobility_Evidence_From_the_Finnish_Comprehensive_School_Reform

**4. Effects were gendered and showed a lifecycle trade-off, not a uniform win.** 
The reform increased women's probability of enrolling in academic secondary and tertiary education and decreased the gender wage gap (Pekkarinen, 2008).
 But a lifecycle labor-market study found 
a negative effect on employment in early career and a positive effect later in the lifecycle, with results for labor earnings more nuanced... but a positive effect... in the late career.
 Source: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0927537120301561

**5. Equity gains from de-tracking are not permanent — they have eroded since ~2000 due to school choice and residential sorting, NOT because tracking returned.** 
SES achievement gaps declined after de-tracking reforms but have increased more recently,
 per an 18-assessment (1964–2015) analysis. 
Growing socio-economic and geographical disparities, as well as the development of enriched curricula for some classes ("weighted curricula") and greater school choice, are some of the factors behind increasing segregation and inequality in Finland's education system.
 Source: https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-981-13-9863-6_9 and https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2022/09/finland-s-right-to-learn-programme_58de2f6c/65eff23e-en.pdf

**6. This is a "sorting substitutes for tracking" finding directly relevant to Hungary's admissions/school-choice question.** 
In many countries, school choice policies have been viewed as a means of desegregating schools by removing the immediate link between home address and school allocation, however international research points to school choice increasing school segregation, and long-term research on the Helsinki metropolitan area demonstrates this in the Finnish context as well.
 Notably, 
performance of students in Finland differs more drastically within schools, and not as much across schools; the variation of students' outcomes is larger within schools (94% of total variation).
 Source: https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-981-19-8241-5_11 and https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2022/09/finland-s-right-to-learn-programme_58de2f6c/65eff23e-en.pdf

**7. What "carried" the Finnish reform: parallel investment in teacher education, not just the structural change.** 
In 1974, a reform took teacher education from teacher-training colleges to universities; in 1979, it was made even more rigorous, making a Master's degree a prerequisite for teaching.
 Analysts treat this as a co-equal pillar: 
one of the most important aspects of Finland's educational success seems to be university-based teacher education and teacher competence.
 Source: https://blogs.worldbank.org/en/education/finland-s-education-system-journey-success and https://dialnet.unirioja.es/descarga/articulo/8711495.pdf

**8. Implementation was gradual and centrally sequenced, then later decentralized — a political-economy detail relevant to transferability.** 
The Finnish comprehensive school reform was gradually implemented between 1972 and 1977, gradually implemented from Northern to Southern Finland in six implementation regions.
 
Opposition was most fierce in the densely populated Helsinki region... Helsinki was the last region to implement the reform.
 Tracking-within-school (streaming) persisted even after the structural reform and was abolished only later: 
after abolishing streaming in comprehensive school in the mid-1980s, therefore making learning expectations similar for all students, the achievement gap between low and high achievers began to decrease.
 Source: https://www.sole-jole.org/assets/docs/15569.pdf and https://library.apsce.net/index.php/ICCE/article/download/3849/3724/4812

**9. Hungary's current structure for comparison.** 
Since 1991, there have been six- and eight-year secondary schools in Hungary; pupils can enrol in eight-year secondary schools from the fifth grade and six-year secondary schools from the seventh grade, so education begins at lower secondary level (ISCED 2) and ends in the twelfth grade.
 
These selective schools are common in Hungary and allow academically able students to begin gymnasium-style education from age ten or twelve.
 Per-student spending context: 
Hungary spends approximately USD 7,072 per full-time equivalent student in public primary institutions... compared to OECD averages of USD 11,914.
 Sources: https://eurydice.eacea.ec.europa.eu/eurypedia/hungary/organisation-general-secondary-education and https://www.expatfocus.com/hungary/guide/hungary-education-and-schools

**10. Caveat on generalizability of the Finnish estimate.** The reform coincided with Finland's broader welfare-state build-out — 
during this time, social supports for children and families were also enacted, including health and dental care, special education services
 — meaning the causal estimate isolates tracking-age but not the full policy bundle. Source: https://edpolicy.stanford.edu/library/publications/543.html
