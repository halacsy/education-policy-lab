## Research Notes — Early Selection (Hungary) vs. Portugal's Non-Selective, Support-Based Path

**1. OECD system-design consensus against early tracking.** OECD's five overarching recommendations for equity include eliminating grade repetition, avoiding early tracking and deferring selection to upper secondary, managing school choice to avoid segregation, and designing equivalent upper-secondary pathways. Source: "The evolution of the OECD's position on equity in global education," ScienceDirect, 2025. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0738059325000392

**2. Cross-country evidence: tracking age affects equity, not mean performance.** A 40-country study confirms that 
the OECD showed that the age of first selection in an education system is correlated to inequity in performance but not to the mean performance level
, and newer multi-country studies 
conclude that early tracking increases achievement inequalities
. Poland's move toward a more integrated system produced 
a dramatic reduction in between-school achievement variance
 after its reform. Source: ScienceDirect, "Does between-school tracking really result in more achievement homogeneous schools?" 2026. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0276562426000120

**3. Hungary-specific evidence: three selection points, strong SES effect.** 
The Hungarian system is ideal to test the effect of early-selection on inequality of opportunity, since students are selected at three different ages; the early-selective academic tracks skim off the best students first at age 10, then at age 12
. Source: ResearchGate, "Early tracking and competition – A recipe for major inequalities in Hungary." https://www.researchgate.net/publication/327244375_Chapter_8_Early_tracking_and_competition_-_A_recipe_for_major_inequalities_in_Hungary

**4. OECD Hungary country profile (2015) confirms structural detail and equity risk.** 
Tracking can partially start at Grade 5 (age 10-11) or at Grade 7 (age 12-13), when students can apply to an 8-year or 6-year general secondary school; early school choice, selection may hamper equity
, while 
the impact of socio-economic background on mathematics performance of 15-year-olds was among the largest in OECD countries, with high variance between schools
. Source: OECD Education Policy Outlook: Hungary, 2015. https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/about/projects/edu/education-policy-outlook/379201-Hungary-Profile.pdf

**5. World Bank: SES gap equals years of schooling; Roma disproportionately in weaker tracks.** 
Performance in PISA varies significantly by students' socioeconomic backgrounds – differing by the equivalent of more than three years of schooling between students from the top and bottom quintile, significantly more than elsewhere in the EU
, and 
15-year-olds from socioeconomically disadvantaged background are disproportionately represented in vocational schools and vocational secondary schools, where quality is significantly below general secondary schools
. Source: World Bank, "Hungary: Skilling up – the next generation." https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/340561468196449667/pdf/101561-WP-P152132-Box393264B-PUBLIC-PISA-Hungary.pdf

**6. Portugal comparator: low tracking intensity, still high grade repetition legacy.** 
Portugal has a school system where school selectivity is less intense than in the other OECD and partner countries/economies
 (PISA Index rank 75/80, 2022), yet historically 
17% of students in Portugal reported that they had repeated a grade at least once (OECD average: 9%)
, and 
grade repetition tends to be less prevalent in high performing systems
. Source: OECD Education GPS, Portugal Country Profile, PISA 2022. https://gpseducation.oecd.org/CountryProfile?primaryCountry=PRT&treshold=10&topic=PI

**7. Portugal's non-structural reform delivered fast, large retention drops.** Via the Programa Nacional de Promoção do Sucesso Escolar (PNPSE, created by Resolução do Conselho de Ministros nº 23/2016), 
Planos de Ação Estratégica were created in 98% of eligible schools between 2016-2018, and by the end of that two-year period retention rates in basic education fell by 29%
. This was achieved without changing school structure or the selection system — pure school-level strategic planning plus support. Source: PNPSE official program page, Direção-Geral da Educação. https://pnpse.min-educ.pt/programa

**8. TEIP (Territórios Educativos de Intervenção Prioritária) — targeted, not structural, compensatory support.** Academic evaluation literature examines 
whether the Portuguese compensatory education programme has been successful in reducing disadvantaged schools' performance gaps, via a 15-year quantitative analysis of national exams
 (Ferraz, Neves & Nata, 2019, Education Sciences). TEIP channels extra resources/staff to schools in socioeconomically fragile territories without altering the comprehensive (non-tracked) structure. Source: "Sucessos e insucessos TEIP: uma avaliação com métodos mistos," Universidade do Porto repository. https://repositorio-aberto.up.pt/bitstream/10216/134167/2/474340.pdf

**9. Continued EU-funded targeted investment (2024-2025) confirms this remains the live policy lever.** The Algarve's PIPSE intermunicipal program received 
European Social Fund investment of €2.4M over a three-year school period
 explicitly to reduce retention/dropout rates within the TEIP framework, i.e., money is going to targeted support programs, not structural/track redesign. Source: Algarve 2030 / CCDR Algarve press note, July 2025. https://algarve.portugal2030.pt/2025/07/02/ccdr-algarve-e-algarve-2030-no-encontro-das-escolas-teip-4-na-escola-d-dinis-em-quarteira/

**10. Portugal is now debating going further: eliminating the 2nd cycle (5th-6th grade) and retention itself.** 
The president of the National Education Council argued for ending retentions and the 2nd cycle of basic education (5th and 6th grade)
, and pilot pedagogical-innovation schools (PPIP) tested 
whether it is possible to reach a zero retention rate
. Source: Observador, "O Governo vai mesmo acabar com os chumbos até ao 9.º ano?" https://observador.pt/explicadores/o-governo-vai-mesmo-acabar-com-os-chumbos-ate-ao-9-o-ano/

**11. PISA 2022 Portugal outcome context (post-reform baseline).** 
PISA 2022 results were lower than in earlier assessments in all three subjects; over 2018-2022, the gap between the highest- and lowest-scoring 10% narrowed in mathematics, while it did not change significantly in reading and science
. Also 
75% of students reported that in most mathematics lessons the teacher shows interest in every student's learning (OECD average: 63%), and 79% that the teacher gives extra help when needed (OECD average: 70%)
 — consistent with a support-intensive, non-selective culture. Source: OECD PISA 2022 Country Notes: Portugal. https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/pisa-2022-results-volume-i-and-ii-country-notes_ed6fbcc5-en/portugal_777942d5-en.html
