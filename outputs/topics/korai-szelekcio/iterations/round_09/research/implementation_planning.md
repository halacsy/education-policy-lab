## Research Notes — Implementation Planning: Early Selection & the 6/8-Year Gimnázium (Hungary)

**1. Teacher workforce is aging and structurally strained, which constrains any reform requiring new staffing patterns.** 
According to the latest national data (KSH, 2025), on full-time employed teachers in primary schools, the number of teachers in the younger age group (30–44 years) has declined, and as a result, half of all teachers in the 2024/2025 academic year were 50 or older, while only 7.2% of teachers were under 30.
 
Across OECD countries the teaching workforce is ageing, with particularly sharp increases between 2013 and 2023 in the share of teachers aged 50 and over in both primary and secondary education in Hungary, Latvia, Lithuania, Poland and Portugal.
 
In countries where the share was already high – such as Estonia and Hungary – it has remained elevated.
 Source: OECD Education at a Glance 2025, https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/education-at-a-glance-2025_1c0d9c79-en/full-report/how-severe-are-teacher-shortages-across-countries_781f4a97.html ; EU Education and Training Monitor 2025, https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html

**2. Absolute teacher numbers have shrunk over two decades, and attrition/entry pipeline is weak.** 
In the 2023/2024 school year, approximately 164,000 teachers were employed in institutions for initial and vocational education, which represents a decrease of over 21,000 compared to the school year of 2003/2004.
 
In 2022 the number of teachers was already below 140,000 [PDSZ estimate for public education], and according to the PDSZ, if the Status Law comes into effect, a further 5,000 teachers could leave the profession.
 
And in five years' time, a further 25,000 teachers could quit, the union has warned.
 
The shortage of teachers is most notable in the fields of math and natural sciences; among the less than 3,400 people who applied for pedagogical degrees at university this year, the most popular major was history-English combined.
 Source: Statista, https://www.statista.com/topics/9751/education-in-hungary/ ; RFE/RL, https://www.rferl.org/a/hungary-shortage-teachers/32475124.html

**3. Shortage severity is geographically uneven — rural/disadvantaged schools hit hardest, which is directly relevant to any plan to phase out early selection region-by-region.** 
Teacher shortages are greatest in rural areas, with the proportion of unqualified teachers in disadvantaged schools 2 to 3 times higher than in schools not disadvantaged.
 Source: EU Education and Training Monitor 2025, https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html

**4. A major salary reform is already underway (2024-2031), which is a load-bearing precondition for any staffing-dependent selection reform — its own timeline is long and EU-funded.** 
Hungary and the European Union agreed on a long-term programme of wage increases, backed by EUR 1.8 billion from EU funds between 2024 and 2031 – around HUF 735 billion at current exchange rates – representing an average increase of more than HUF 5 million (EUR 12,000) per teacher over the period.
 
The Government aims to ensure that the average salary of teachers reaches 80% of the average salary of higher education graduates during the period 2025-2031.
 
Employers are required to increase teachers' salaries and wages by an average of 10% at the institutional level, with effect from 1 January 2026.
 Source: European Social Fund Plus, https://european-social-fund-plus.ec.europa.eu/en/projects/hungary-raises-teachers-wages-eu-support ; Eurydice, https://eurydice.eacea.ec.europa.eu/eurypedia/hungary/national-reforms-school-education

**5. The school network is already dense and fragmented (small rural schools), which limits administrative room to consolidate or re-route cohorts without new transport/logistics investment.** 
According to 2024/25 data from the Central Statistical Office, secondary education is provided at 880 schools across Hungary; this dense network aims to ensure that every student can find a secondary general school close to their home, with the aim that the school be accessible by public transport in under an hour.
 
Between-school segregation is much stronger than within-school segregation partly because local self-governments mitigated declining pupil numbers by reducing parallel classes rather than closing/merging schools, leading to a large offer of small schools, especially in small rural settlements that operate only one class per grade.
 Source: Eurydice, https://eurydice.eacea.ec.europa.eu/eurypedia/hungary/organisation-general-secondary-education ; CEU working paper, https://cps.ceu.edu/sites/cps.ceu.edu/files/attachment/publication/3258/cps-working-paper-educ-social-selection-education-hungary-2020.pdf

**6. Comparative case — Poland 1999 reform delaying tracking is the most directly relevant sequencing precedent, and shows both a feasible timeline and a political-durability risk.** 
In May 1998 a draft of the comprehensive education reform was presented, in July the Sejm amended the law, in January 1999 the law was passed with an executive order on curriculum in February, while the first classes of new lower secondary schools began in September 1999 — the scarcity of time between announcement, legislation and implementation did not allow for proper consultations.
 This is a strong administrative-bandwidth warning: 
despite ministerial assurances of openness to critique, a true debate with such limited time was impossible.
 Source: Springer chapter "Reforming Education in Poland," https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-41882-3_7

**7. The Polish reform delivered measurable achievement gains for the group affected by delayed selection, giving an evidence base for expected effect size and timeframe of results.** 
Poland improved its score in mathematics by 0.25 of a standard deviation, in reading by 0.28 of a standard deviation, and in science by 0.16 of a standard deviation.
 
The age of first selection was postponed from age 15 to 16.
 Results became visible within roughly 3-7 years (PISA 2000/2003/2006 cycles). Source: Patrinos et al., World Bank/Taylor&Francis working paper, https://hpatrinos.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/poland-tracking1.pdf

**8. Poland's reform was politically reversed after ~18 years despite documented gains — a direct implementation-durability risk for any Hungarian reform of similar structural depth.** 
The PiS government introduced a major 2017 reform, phased over three school years starting 2017/2018, reverting the structure from 6+3+3/4 years back to 8+4/5 years.
 
Implementation began in the 2017/2018 school year for incoming first-graders, with the last gimnazjum class graduating in 2020/2021.
 This confirms a ~3-4 year phase-in/phase-out runway is administratively standard for structural K-12 reforms in the region, and that reforms lacking durable political buy-in can be undone even after empirical success. Source: Wikipedia/Grokipedia synthesis of Polish reform timeline, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Education_in_Poland ; https://grokipedia.com/page/Education_in_Poland

**9. Teacher retraining/CPD bandwidth is already fully committed to other current reforms (AI curriculum, National Core Curriculum updates), suggesting limited near-term capacity to also absorb a selection-structure reform's training needs.** 
Teachers have been required to attend an additional ten hours of distance learning between 1 September 2024 and 31 January 2025 to update their knowledge of the National Core Curriculum and the Érettségi requirements, and must answer 15 out of 20 questions correctly on a final exam.
 
The Minister and the Government Commissioner for AI were mandated to develop, by 31 March 2026, a roadmap for integrating AI education into public education, with implementation by 31 December 2026.
 
Teachers are required to complete 120 credits over five years, including at least 60 credits in content renewal courses and up to 60 credits in optional courses.
 Source: Eurydice, https://eurydice.eacea.ec.europa.eu/eurypedia/hungary/national-reforms-school-education ; https://eurydice.eacea.ec.europa.eu/eurypedia/hungary/national-reforms-early-childhood-education-and-care

**10. Segregation is administratively embedded in maintainer/school-choice structures, meaning any reform to selection must also touch admissions/catchment rules, not just track length — a multi-lever, not single-lever, implementation problem.** 
Free school choice codified 4 years before the regime change had effectively strengthened segregation trends, as more affluent and higher educated parents were more motivated to search and find schools with children of similar backgrounds.
 
Hungary has the highest level of segregation of students from less and more advantaged socio-economic groups into different schools, and Hungary's segregation of the most and least disadvantaged students was the highest in the EU in the 2022 PISA study.
 Source: EU Education and Training Monitor 2025, https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html ; Springer, Education of Roma and Educational Resilience in Hungary, https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-52588-0_6
