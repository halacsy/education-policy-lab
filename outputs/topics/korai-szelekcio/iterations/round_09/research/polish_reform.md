# Research Notes: Poland's 1999 Delayed-Tracking Reform and 2016/2017 Reversal — Evidence for the Hungarian Early-Selection Debate

## Finding 1: Effect size of delayed tracking on PISA scores (1999 reform)
The original quasi-experimental evaluation found Poland improved its PISA score in mathematics by 0.25 standard deviations, in reading by 0.28 SD, and in science by 0.16 SD following the 1999 reform that delayed tracking by extending comprehensive general education from 8 to 9 years. Using propensity-score matching and difference-in-differences, the authors estimate the gains for likely-vocational-track students at roughly one full standard deviation (~100 PISA points) between 2000 and 2006.
Source: Jakubowski, Patrinos, Porta & Wiśniewski (2016), "The effects of delaying tracking in secondary school: evidence from the 1999 education reform in Poland," Education Economics 24(6), https://hpatrinos.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/poland-tracking1.pdf ; also https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/09645292.2016.1149548

## Finding 2: Mechanism — who benefited, and by how much
Decomposition analysis attributes about two-thirds of the PISA 2000–2006 test-score gain to changes in resources/characteristics and one-third to the reform's structural effect itself; the paper concludes the reform improved likely-vocational students' scores "of about 100 points, or a whole standard deviation," while cautioning about the continued dangers of early tracking into vocational paths.
Source: same as above, https://hpatrinos.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/poland-tracking1.pdf

## Finding 3: Equity effects are contested at the school-disparity level but weaker at the individual-SES level
One synthesis states the reform "reduced performance differences between schools, from one of the largest disparities across countries within the OECD, to one of the smallest" — a between-school equity claim. However, a separate academic study directly testing individual-level socioeconomic equalization found that using PISA 2000–2012 data, "there was no significant decrease in correlations between socioeconomic status of students and their results" after the reform — i.e., school-level convergence did NOT translate into equalized individual-level SES gaps.
Sources: https://journals.openedition.org/ries/7801?lang=en ; https://www.academia.edu/33343970/Education_reform_and_inequality_fifteen_years_of_new_lower_secondary_schools_in_Poland

## Finding 4: Labor-market follow-through
A 2022 labour-economics study using EU-SILC data found the reform "has reached its initial goal of decreasing inequalities" via improved peer and teacher composition for previously low-track students, confirming positive labor-market effects also found by Liwiński (2020) and Strawinski & Broniatowska (2021), using different data/methods.
Source: Drucker, Horn & Jakubowski (2022), "The labour market effects of the polish educational reform of 1999," Journal for Labour Market Research 56:13, https://labourmarketresearch.springeropen.com/articles/10.1186/s12651-022-00317-4

## Finding 5: The 2016/2017 reversal — what changed structurally
The reversal abolished the 3-year gimnazjum and returned to 8 years of primary school followed by streamed general/technical/vocational secondary schools, cutting a year of general education; it also raised the school starting age back to 7 (removing near-universal early-childhood participation), curtailed inclusion of special-needs students in mainstream classes, revised curriculum toward more Polish history/language, ended mandatory project-based learning, and disbanded lower-secondary teacher teams.
Source: "The Power of System Reform: Evidence from Poland," World Class Learning Systems (2024), https://worldclasslearningsystems.com/the-power-of-system-reform-evidence-from-poland/

## Finding 6: Political economy of the reversal — durability lesson
Despite Poland's PISA success, the new lower-secondary schools "developed a poor public reputation," and in the 2015 campaign the opposition party "appealed to a general nostalgia for the 'good old times'" and won; once in power it "quickly reversed the earlier reforms, disregarding negative opinions of researchers and local authorities as well as the protests of parents and teachers." A 2016 IPSOS/OKO.press poll showed opinion split almost entirely along partisan lines: 73% of Law and Justice (PiS) supporters called the reform good and 10% bad, versus 68% of Civic Platform/Modern voters calling it bad and 25% good — evidence structural school reform in Poland became a partisan identity marker rather than an evidence-driven decision.
Sources: https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-41882-3_7 ; https://medium.com/@thelambert/education-in-poland-was-dismantling-of-gimnazjum-necessary-8adc9f2e48a8

## Finding 7: Implementation shock from rapid reversal
Because 2017 forced two cohorts (final gimnazjum graduates and first primary-school-only graduates) to compete simultaneously for the same secondary-school places, competition intensified sharply; in Warsaw alone 3,173 students reportedly did not get into any school that year — evidence of acute transition costs when a structural reform is unwound quickly rather than phased.
Source: https://medium.com/@thelambert/education-in-poland-was-dismantling-of-gimnazjum-necessary-8adc9f2e48a8

## Finding 8: Teacher-union and coalition dynamics around the reversal
Reporting describes the PiS government negotiating pay concessions with the PiS-friendly Solidarity teachers' union while the larger ZNP union (which had supported the original 1999 comprehensive-schooling reform as being "in line with international trends") organized nationwide protests; almost 80% of schools participated in a 2019 teacher strike over pay and conditions tied to reform-driven chaos, though it did not reverse the policy.
Sources: https://balkaninsight.com/2020/01/02/polish-parents-and-teachers-blast-political-education-reforms/ ; https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-41882-3_7

## Finding 9: PISA 2022 decline linked to the reversal (most recent evidence)
PISA 2022 results were "among the lowest ever measured by PISA in all three subjects, on par with results observed... in 2003" — erasing gains made since. Researchers attribute part of this to the reversal of the 1999 reform, since the reversal only fully affected the cohort tested in 2022 (not the 2018 cohort, which was still under the old gimnazjum structure) combined with COVID-19 school closures lasting at least 26 weeks in Poland.
Sources: https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/pisa-2022-results-volume-i-and-ii-country-notes_ed6fbcc5-en/poland_4a2c28c8-en.html ; https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-031-69284-0_9

## Finding 10: Which reform elements proved politically irreversible
Analysts note that "some positive changes are more difficult to reverse" — in Poland, increased school autonomy, external examinations, and broader access to preschool and higher education were changes "the new government could not alter" even amid the broader rollback — a distinction relevant to designing durable reforms (embed some elements in decentralized/legally-entrenched structures rather than central mandate alone).
Source: https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-59031-4_7
