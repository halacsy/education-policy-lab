## Research Notes — International Comparison: Early Selection, Tracking, and Equity

**1. Hanushek & Woessmann (NBER) cross-country DiD on tracking and inequality.** Using PISA/PIRLS/TIMSS across grade levels, the study finds that among early-tracking countries, Germany showed the largest increase in inequality between primary and secondary school (+0.71), followed by Greece (+0.30), Czech Republic (+0.25), and Italy (+0.22); the countries with the largest *decreases* in inequality were all late trackers (Turkey −0.63, New Zealand −0.50, Canada −0.32, United States −0.27). Source: "Does Educational Tracking Affect Performance and Inequality?" NBER Working Paper 11124. https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w11124/w11124.pdf

**2. Large-scale 75-country/region DiD synthesis (2021).** A meta-analytic difference-in-differences study spanning PISA, PIRLS, and TIMSS over 20 years across 75 countries/regions found that pooled estimates indicate early tracking increases the dispersion of test scores, though effect sizes vary substantially by study pair. Source: "Early tracking and different types of inequalities in achievement: difference-in-differences evidence from 20 years of large-scale assessments," Educational Assessment, Evaluation and Accountability (2021). https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11092-020-09346-4

**3. Poland's 1999 delayed-tracking reform — the closest natural experiment to a "raise the selection age" policy.** Poland replaced early streaming with a comprehensive lower-secondary "gimnazjum" that delayed vocational/general tracking by one year. Difference-in-differences and propensity-score-matching estimates found Poland's math score rose 0.25 SD, reading 0.28 SD, and science 0.16 SD between PISA 2000 and 2006, with roughly one-third of the gain attributed to increased instructional time and the remainder to compositional/resource changes. Source: Jakubowski, Patrinos, Porta & Wiśniewski, "The effects of delaying tracking in secondary school: evidence from the 1999 education reform in Poland," Education Economics 24(6), 2016. https://ideas.repec.org/a/taf/edecon/v24y2016i6p557-572.html ; full findings also at https://www.researchgate.net/publication/296973861_The_effects_of_delaying_tracking_in_secondary_school_evidence_from_the_1999_education_reform_in_Poland

**4. Labour-market follow-up on the Polish reform.** A 2022 follow-up study using DiD with propensity-score matching found the fast increase in Polish PISA scores after 1999 was concentrated among likely-vocational students who remained in comprehensive schooling an extra year, benefiting from better peers and teachers; the authors conclude the reform reached its goal of reducing inequality, but note the Polish government reversed the reform in January 2017, reinstating early tracking after 18 years. Source: Drucker, Horn & Jakubowski, "The labour market effects of the Polish educational reform of 1999," Journal for Labour Market Research (2022). https://labourmarketresearch.springeropen.com/articles/10.1186/s12651-022-00317-4

**5. Hungarian-authored comparative analysis of Hungary's own selectivity.** Csapó, Molnár & Kinyő's IEA-published analysis states Hungary belongs to the countries where the first selection point in the education system is at entrance, at age 7, with a second selection point after grade 8 that increases mean achievement differences between schools; they also report 2–3 years of measured difference in intellectual development between class means even within primary-school cohorts. Source: "Analysis of the Selectiveness of the Hungarian Educational System in International Context," IEA. https://www.iea.nl/sites/default/files/2019-04/IRC2008_Csapo_Molnar_etal.pdf

**6. CEU working paper on 6-8 grade "creaming" and Roma segregation context.** This paper notes that in the most selective Central European systems (Czechia, Slovakia, Hungary) the formal unity of primary/lower-secondary schooling is only a façade, because 6-8 grade secondary schools cream off the best-performing, typically middle-class pupils as early as the end of grades 5-7, and that Hungary has four formal selection points before higher education. Source: CEU Center for Policy Studies, "the Wider Context of the Segregation of Roma Pupils in..." (2020). https://cps.ceu.edu/sites/cps.ceu.edu/files/attachment/publication/3258/cps-working-paper-educ-social-selection-education-hungary-2020.pdf

**7. EU Education and Training Monitor 2025 — current equity gap figures for Hungary.** The 2025 country report states that on average in the EU there is a 33-point score gap between children of graduate and non-graduate parents, but this gap is 53 points in Hungary's case; it also notes 37% of Hungarian students were low achievers in 2022 (below the EU average of 43% but far above the EU target of 15%). Source: European Commission, "Education and Training Monitor 2025 — Hungary." https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html

**8. OECD Education at a Glance 2024 — intergenerational transmission.** OECD's Hungary country note reports that 74% of 25-64 year-olds with at least one tertiary-educated parent also attained a tertiary qualification, illustrating strong parental-education effects on attainment; NEET rate fell from 15.5% to 12.8% (OECD average 15.8%→13.8%, 2016-2023). Source: OECD, "Education at a Glance 2024: Hungary Country Note." https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2024/09/education-at-a-glance-2024-country-notes_532eb29d/hungary_faab9f42/1b2a3510-en.pdf

**9. Current institutional facts on the 6/8-year gimnázium network (2024/25 data).** Eurydice reports that since 1991 Hungary has had six- and eight-year secondary schools, with eight-year programmes starting from grade 5 and six-year from grade 7; as of 2024/25 KSH data, secondary general education (gimnázium) is provided at 880 schools nationally. Source: Eurydice, "Organisation of general secondary education — Hungary." https://eurydice.eacea.ec.europa.eu/eurypedia/hungary/organisation-general-secondary-education

**10. Recent literature-review evidence on PISA-based equity trend studies (2024-2025).** A 2025 systematic review synthesizing five OECD PISA equity reports (2009-2022) plus academic studies found persistent, not uniformly improving, socioeconomic equity trends across PISA cycles 2000-2018/2022, underscoring that cross-national equity gains from any single structural lever (like delaying tracking) are contested and cycle-dependent. Source: "Change in socioeconomic educational equity after 20 years of PISA: A systematic literature review," ScienceDirect (2024). https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666374024000414 ; companion piece: "A long road to educational equity: Tracking trends through PISA 2000–2018." https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666374025000111
