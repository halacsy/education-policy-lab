# Research Notes — Equity & Social Mobility: Early Selection (6/8-year gimnázium)

**Finding 1 — SES effect on achievement is among the largest in the OECD, tied to school selection.** OECD Hungary profile: "The impact of socio-economic background on mathematics performance of 15-year-olds was among the largest in OECD countries, with high variance between schools (school selection is made based on students' records)." Source: OECD Education Policy Outlook: Hungary. https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/about/projects/edu/education-policy-outlook/379201-Hungary-Profile.pdf

**Finding 2 — Hungary has the EU's highest socioeconomic school segregation.** EU Education and Training Monitor 2025: "Hungary has the highest level of segregation of students from less and more advantaged socio-economic groups into different schools (Gutiérrez et al., 2020)" and "Hungary's segregation of the most and least disadvantaged students was the highest in the EU in the 2022 PISA study." The parent-education achievement gap is 53 PISA points vs 33 EU average. Roma segregation: 62% of Roma children aged 6-15 attend schools where all/most pupils are Roma (FRA 2025). Source: https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html

**Finding 3 — PISA 2022: Hungary has one of the largest top-bottom SES achievement gaps and among the largest shares of disadvantaged low performers.** OECD GPS data: math variance between schools is "among the largest" vs OECD average; gap between top and bottom SES quarter is 56.8% (rank 7/80); "the share of disadvantaged low performers in maths is one of the highest among countries and economies participating in PISA," while advantaged low performers are among the lowest (rank 70/79). Source: https://gpseducation.oecd.org/CountryProfile?primaryCountry=HUN&treshold=10&topic=PI

**Finding 4 — Cross-national research links earlier tracking age to larger compositional/peer effects.** EERA-ECER blog on international research: "tracking age, and the proportion of students in private schools, predicted international differences in compositional effects" and "European countries with early tracking ages tended to have the largest compositional effects," while countries delaying tracking with high public-school shares had lower compositional effects. Source: https://blog.eera-ecer.de/socioeconomic-school-segregation/

**Finding 5 — Value-added evidence for 6- vs 8-grade gimnázium is mixed, not uniformly positive.** HÉTFA working paper (using 2008-2014 national competence-test panel, N≈1.1 million students): 8-grade gimnázium shows higher added value at grade 8 than general schools, and higher at grade 10 than 4-grade gimnázium; but 6-grade gimnázium shows *negative* added value at grade 8 (lower than general schools), becoming positive only by grade 10 vs 4-grade gimnázium. Source: https://hetfa.hu/wp-content/uploads/Hetfa_WP_2015_11_web.pdf_v%C3%A9gleges.pdf

**Finding 6 — Disadvantaged/Roma pupils are almost entirely absent from "kisgimnázium" (6/8-grade) intake.** Sociologist Berényi (interviewed by Qubit, citing his research): describes that in these schools statistically it is "mintha minden ötödik évben egy halmozottan hátrányos helyzetű gyerek jutna be" (roughly one multiply-disadvantaged child admitted every five years per school with three parallel classes), and traces the 8-grade gimnázium's revival to pre-1945 elitist/selective schooling reinstated after 1990 without foresight about system-wide effects. Source: https://qubit.hu/2022/06/15/olyan-gyerek-akinek-kozmunkasok-a-szulei-hat-olyan-itt-nincs

**Finding 7 — Large-scale administrative data now used to isolate high-poverty-school effects from selection.** Hermann & Kisfalusi (2026, International Journal of Comparative Sociology) use the Admin3 linked administrative panel (test scores at grades 6, 8, 10; secondary completion; HE entry) with matching methods to estimate causal effects of attending a high-poverty Grade-8 school on achievement and attainment; ~20-22% of students in their sample attend high- or extreme-poverty schools. Source: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/00207152231198434

**Finding 8 — Selectivity at the 6/8-grade gates has recently tightened via informal maintainer thresholds beyond central rules.** For the 2025/2026 admissions round, reporting shows individual school maintainers (tankerület) recommending or effectively requiring minimum central-exam scores (e.g., 70/100) for 6- and 8-grade gimnázium admission, on top of the (non-binding) national recommendation; some schools stated the threshold as "ajánlás" (recommendation) but treated it as a de facto cutoff, and admission lists were revised mid-process in March 2025, moving some students from admitted to rejected. Source: https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/20250905_kozepiskolai-felveteli-2025-minimum-ponthatar-gimnazium

**Finding 9 — Historical policy record: central authorities tried but failed to contain the spread of 6/8-grade gimnáziums in the 1990s.** OFI historical review: by the mid-1990s policy already treated these as "elit középiskolák" (elite secondary schools) and as segregation-generating institutions (Halász–Lannert 1998; Liskó–Fehérvári 1996); by decade's end government imposed central control of entrance exams and legally reaffirmed the 8+4 structure as the norm without banning the 6/8-grade alternative. Source: https://ofi.oh.gov.hu/iskolak-topon-6-es-8-evfolyamos-gimnaziumi-kepzes

**Finding 10 — Foundational Hungarian research base on segregation and selection.** Kertesi & Kézdi's primary-school segregation studies and Horn's kisgimnázium selection analysis (using value-added-type indicators comparing kisgimnázium pupils to all others) are the standard citations underlying most later Hungarian equity work on this topic. Sources: https://www.academia.edu/1378060/Segregation_in_the_Primary_School_System_in_Hungary_Causes_and_Consequences ; https://tarki.hu/tarsadalomkutatas/publikaciok/tpubl_a_927.pdf
