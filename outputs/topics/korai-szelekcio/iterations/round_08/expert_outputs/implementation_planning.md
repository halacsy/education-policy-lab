# Expert analysis: implementation_planning

## Findings (evidence)
- Hungary's teaching workforce is structurally aging: a large share of teachers are over 50 and relatively few under 30, constraining trained-staff supply to absorb any reallocation of pupils away from 6/8-year gimnazium tracks. [evidence: moderate — OECD Education at a Glance 2023]
- Maths and science teacher vacancies are concentrated in disadvantaged regions, making localized structural reform harder to staff equitably. [evidence: moderate — OECD Education at a Glance; PSZ/PDSZ surveys]
- Any cohort-by-cohort phase-out requires pre-announcement 2–3 years before admission to allow institutions to plan building use and staffing. [evidence: moderate — admissions-cycle convention; institutional planning norms]
- Steady-state per-pupil funding differences between track types are modest; dominant transition costs are teacher retraining, building reassignment, and administrative overhead. [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates — fresh figures not re-verified]
- Teacher-training capacity (4–6 year degree duration) cannot expand general-track staffing within a single electoral cycle without retention or reallocation of existing teachers. [evidence: moderate — Teacher degree duration; workforce-planning algebra]
- Central administrative structure (Klebelsberg centres) can enact statutory change rapidly but has historically uneven implementation-fidelity records at scale. [evidence: weak — governance structure observation; not quantitatively audited this round]

## Interpretation
Implementation feasibility is governed by teacher supply, admissions-cycle rigidity, and central-to-local capacity—not by political will. A realistic reform must announce cohort-specific carve-outs 2–3 years ahead, pair them with an explicit staffing and building-use plan, and accept ~8-year minimum timelines (one pupil-generation). Faster abolition without these front-loads disruption onto general-track schools and exacerbates regional equity gaps.

## Assumptions
- Reform targets genuine reduction or delay of selection age, not cosmetic track-renaming. [assumption]
- Central governance retains its concentrated structure during implementation; no major devolution to schools occurs. [assumption]
- Existing admissions calendars (notification, exam scheduling) remain broadly synchronized across regions. [assumption]

## Position
Any reform reducing or delaying early selection in Hungary must be implemented cohort-by-cohort over approximately one pupil-generation (~8 years), with admission decisions pre-announced 2–3 cycles ahead and explicitly paired with a teacher staffing and building-use plan, because teacher supply, admissions-cycle rigidity, and central-to-local coordination capacity—not political intent—are the binding constraints on feasible speed.

## Uncertainties
- Exact current proportion of pupils enrolled in 6/8-year gimnazium and exact count of affected secondary institutions is not re-verified via current administrative data. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Current Oktatási Hivatal / KSH enrollment tables disaggregated by program type, entry grade, and institution.)
- The magnitude, regional distribution, and subject-specific character of teacher vacancies determining realistic absorption capacity for track consolidation are asserted from older surveys but not freshly quantified. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Latest published OECD Education at a Glance Hungary chapter + latest PDSZ / PSZ member survey on vacancies and working conditions by county and subject area.)
- Whether central administrative capacity can deliver consistent, high-fidelity implementation (not merely statutory enactment) of a multi-year, multi-institution structural change at scale is asserted but not measured by audit or evaluation. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Fidelity evaluation of prior large-scale Hungarian educational structural reforms (NAT rollout 1995–2003, Klebelsberg centres launch 2013, building maintenance centralization 2016) measuring design-to-practice alignment.)
- Capacity of schools currently in disadvantaged regions to absorb newly-assigned pupils without quality degradation or staff burnout is not estimated; equity impact of concentration remains uncertain. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Costed scenario analysis including facility capacity surveys, teacher workload projections, and post-reform equity metrics (achievement gaps by SES and region) for each proposed timing.)
