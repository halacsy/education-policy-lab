# Expert analysis: equity_and_social_mobility

## Findings (evidence)
- In PISA, socio-economic status explains 19–25% of reading and mathematics score variance in Hungary, compared to 12–14% across the OECD average, indicating a steep SES gradient in achievement. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2012-2022 country notes]
- Approximately 8–12% of a Hungarian cohort enters 6- or 8-year grammar school (gimnazium) tracks at ages 10 or 12, admitted primarily through a central written examination, with entrants strongly concentrated in high-SES and urban families. [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics]
- Hungarian value-added studies find that the raw achievement advantage of early-selective grammar school tracks for admitted pupils is predominantly attributable to selection effects; the causal value-added component (school quality effect) is modest. [evidence: moderate — Horn (2013); Kertesi & Kezdi school-effect studies]
- Early selection in Hungary reproduces pre-existing socio-economic inequalities rather than sorting talent independent of family background, because entry is determined substantially by examination coaching, language-rich home literacy, and parental navigation capacity—all strongly correlated with SES. [evidence: moderate — domain knowledge; consistent with SES gradient findings and selection-effect literature]
- The concentration of high-SES pupils in early-selective tracks creates composition effects that may depress achievement and motivation among non-selected pupils in comprehensive schools, though the magnitude and mechanisms remain empirically under-studied in Hungary. [evidence: weak — international peer-effect literature; Hungarian-specific evidence not available]
- Hungarian early-selective grammar schools do not systematically integrate disadvantaged pupils, creating a two-tier system in which access to academic preparation is stratified by SES at age 10–12, before curricular differentiation in comprehensive schools would otherwise occur. [evidence: moderate — KSH admission statistics; international evidence on early tracking effects]
- Hungary's school choice framework and tuition-free state grammar schools create formal conditions for selection to rest on merit signaled by examination, but informal costs (coaching, travel, information burden) and family cultural capital remain significant barriers for disadvantaged pupils even when formally eligible. [evidence: moderate — school-choice and inequality literature; Hungarian-specific causal evidence sparse]
- No major Hungarian empirical study has quantified the long-run (post-secondary) returns to early-selective grammar school attendance for the population admitted, nor isolated the causal effect on non-admitted peers. [evidence: weak — domain knowledge; absence of published evidence acknowledged]
- International evidence from West European countries that have phased out early selection (e.g., Finland, Germany's federal reforms) shows modest or small labour-market gains and often improved equity outcomes, but institutional and cultural contexts differ from Hungary and transferability is not assured. [evidence: moderate — OECD education reviews; European comparative studies]
- The administrative and pedagogical capacity to implement a phase-out of 8-year grammar schools and consolidate academic preparation into comprehensive schools is uncertain; Hungary lacks recent pilots and detailed transition cost estimates. [evidence: weak — policy analysis; empirical implementation data not publicly available]
- Strong political resistance to phase-out of 6- and 8-year grammar schools exists among urban middle-class families, education professionals, and some school maintainers, partly because current selective tracks are perceived as refuges from mixed-ability comprehensive settings and as signaling devices in a status-conscious education market. [evidence: contested — Hungarian education policy debate; direct stakeholder evidence limited]

## Interpretation
Early academic selection in Hungary (at ages 10–12 via 8- and 6-year grammar schools) is a mechanism that largely replicates rather than disrupts pre-existing socio-economic inequality. Strong SES gradients in PISA achievement (19–25% vs. 12–14% OECD average) establish that Hungary has steep family-background effects; admissions statistics show that selection tracks concentrate high-SES, urban pupils; and value-added research indicates the tracks add modest causal value independent of intake composition. Together, these findings suggest that early selection amplifies initial inequality through a selection-effect mechanism—pupils with privileged backgrounds are identified early, concentrated, and receive differentiated instruction, while the residual comprehensive cohort becomes less heterogeneous in terms of mean ability and peer quality. The distributional consequence is widened inequality and reduced social mobility for non-admitted pupils. Non-selective peer effects on comprehensive-school pupils remain under-studied in the Hungarian context, as do long-run labour-market returns to admission. Policy design to phase out early selection faces implementation capacity and political-feasibility constraints.

## Assumptions
- PISA reading/maths variance attributions remain representative of the true contribution of family SES to Hungarian educational outcomes across different test instruments and ages. [assumption]
- Admission to 6- and 8-year grammar schools reflects the design intent of merit-based selection, even though coaching, information costs, and family cultural capital create de facto barriers for disadvantaged pupils. [assumption]
- The Hungarian school system has sufficient supply flexibility to absorb academic-track pupils into comprehensive schools without degrading instruction quality if early selection were phased out. [assumption]
- Socio-economic status effects on achievement persist even within the selective grammar school tracks, but are not substantially different in magnitude than in comprehensive schools (i.e., selection does not eliminate within-track SES gradients). [assumption]

## Position
Hungary should phase out 8-year grammar school admission at age 10 and consolidate academic preparation into comprehensive primary schools and differentiated but non-selective secondary pathways, because early selection amplifies socio-economic inequality without generating causal value-added gains sufficient to offset equity costs.

## Uncertainties
- Whether Hungary's 2023–2024 admission data and SES composition of grammar-school entrants have changed materially since the publication of the curated sources (2022 and earlier), and how the cost of living crisis has affected uptake of coaching and preparatory services among middle-class families. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Current admission statistics from Oktatási Hivatal for 2023–2024, disaggregated by SES (income quintile, parental education) of entrants to 6- and 8-year tracks.)
- The causal (value-added) gain for pupils admitted to 8-year grammar schools compared to similar-ability pupils admitted to comprehensive schools, isolating selection bias through instrumental-variable or regression-discontinuity methods. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Hungarian longitudinal study design with propensity-score matching or RD estimator, following admitted and near-threshold non-admitted cohorts through secondary and tertiary education, measuring labour-market outcomes at age 25+.)
- The magnitude of negative peer effects (composition spillovers) on comprehensive-school pupils when high-SES cohort is removed, and whether such effects vary by school type, urban/rural location, or teacher quality. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Quasi-experimental comparison of comprehensive-school cohorts before and after a phase-out of early selection (e.g., district-level policy discontinuity), measuring mean achievement, variance, and distributional effects on low-SES pupils.)
- The long-run (post-secondary, labour-market) causal returns to 8-year grammar school admission, whether these returns differ by decile of family income, and whether a policy phase-out would depress intergenerational income mobility for admitted cohorts. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Hungarian linked administrative data (school records + tax/income register) for grammar-school cohorts aged 10–12 in 2010–2015, with follow-up to age 30+ income and employment status, stratified by parental income quintile.)
- The political economy of phase-out: whether a gradual sunset of 8-year entry (e.g., no new cohorts after year N) with parallel upskilling of comprehensive-school staff and enrichment curricula would reduce political resistance, and what compensation/transition guarantees would be required. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Stakeholder consultation and cost-benefit analysis of feasible phase-out scenarios (transition timeline, retraining budgets, family compensation, comprehensive-school infrastructure investment), piloted in 2–3 voluntary districts.)
