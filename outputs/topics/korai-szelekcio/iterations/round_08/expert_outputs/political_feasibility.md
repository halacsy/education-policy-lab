# Expert analysis: political_feasibility

## Findings (evidence)
- Urban middle-class parents regard 6- and 8-year gimnazium places as a key mobility and insurance asset; prior attempts to curtail these structures met intense, well-organised resistance. [evidence: moderate — Domestic press and stakeholder analyses, 1990s-2010s]
- Poland's 1999 reform postponed tracking to age 15; PISA scores rose substantially by 2012, with analyses attributing part of the gain to delayed selection; the reform was reversed in 2016-2019 for political reasons. [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD]
- Hungary's government-dependent private school enrolment share (approximately 25.5%) is among the highest among PISA-participating countries, creating a substantial veto-player and absorption-risk actor for any gimnazium phase-down policy. [evidence: moderate — Eurydice, National reforms in school education – Hungary; Expat Focus, Hungary – Education and Schools (2025)]
- As of 2025, compulsory education in Hungary legally ends at age 16, unchanged despite OECD recommendations and demographic pressure; this low floor amplifies selection effects at age 10–12. [evidence: strong — Eurydice, National reforms in school education – Hungary (2025); How to Hungary, Compulsory Education in Hungary – 2025]
- Education policy changes in Hungary are delivered predominantly via ministerial decree (Government decrees 245/2024, 453/2024, and required distance-learning mandates) rather than statute, making them faster to reverse by a subsequent government. [evidence: strong — Eurydice, National reforms in school education – Hungary (2024–2025)]
- Teacher salary trajectory shows increases of 21.2–32.2% between 2024–2025, with a stated government commitment to reach 80% of average graduate earnings by 2025; this salary floor is a potential leverage point for reforms requiring teacher buy-in but is vulnerable to future fiscal consolidation. [evidence: strong — Eurydice, National reforms in school education – Hungary; Government decree 453/2024. (XII. 30.)]
- No visible legislative proposal or active policy signal to phase out 6- and 8-year gimnazium tracks appeared in 2024–2025 government announcements or Eurydice structural reviews; absence of contrary signal does not prove permanence but indicates no imminent statutory reform. ## Interpretation
Politically, a fase-down of Hungary's 6- and 8-year gimnazium system faces three overlapping barriers: (1) entrenched middle-class demand backed by decades of well-organized resistance, (2) structural veto power from government-dependent private and church maintainers (25.5% of the student base), and (3) absence of a binding political pact among stakeholder groups (teachers, early-childhood families, equity NGOs) on the preconditions for support — namely, funded teacher retraining, accelerated timeline (3–4 years not 8–10), and constitutional budget-floor guarantees. Teacher salary gains (21–32% annually) are a genuine buy-in asset, but rest on decrees not statutes, and fiscal consolidation pressures could reverse them. No visible legislative momentum exists, and compulsory-schooling floor at 16 (not 18) remains unchanged, limiting the pressure for reform. The Polish precedent shows that delaying tracking can raise equity and achievement, but Poland's reversal in 2016–2019 (for political reasons) warns against assuming electoral durability. Without multi-party consensus on implementation safeguards — binding timelines, funding floors, and piloted SES mechanisms — any reform will be vulnerable to electoral reversal and stakeholder defection.

## Assumptions
- Teacher supply is adequate (or can be made adequate within 3–4 years via accelerated retraining) to implement comprehensive-school intake with mixed-cohort mixed-ability instruction. [assumption]
- A socio-economic-status proxy for admission can be designed, piloted, and validated to be simultaneously accurate, gaming-resistant, and politically defensible. [assumption]
- Political reversal risk can be mitigated by statutory (not decree-based) commitments that survive changes of government. [assumption]
- Church and foundation maintainers will not systematically absorb high-achieving students into selective foundation schools if 6- and 8-year gimnazium places are phased down. [assumption]
- Delay in raising compulsory schooling floor from 16 to 18 will not fundamentally undermine the pressure or urgency for selecting-track reduction. [assumption]

## Position
Without a multi-party statutory pact on teacher retraining funding, accelerated phase-down (≤4 years), and constitutional budget-floor guarantees for disadvantaged schools, any decreto-based gimnazium reform will be politically reversible and stakeholder-fragile within a single electoral cycle.
