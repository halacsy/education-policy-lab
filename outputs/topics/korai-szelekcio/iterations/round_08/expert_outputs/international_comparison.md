# Expert analysis: international_comparison

## Findings (evidence)
- Across OECD difference-in-differences studies, earlier age of between-school tracking (selection before age 12) is associated with larger socio-economic gradients in student achievement and lower equity without raising mean test scores. [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006, Economic Journal]
- In PISA 2012–2022 cycles, Hungary's socio-economic gradient in reading and mathematics (share of variance explained by ESCS) consistently ranks among the highest in the OECD at 19–25%, compared to an OECD average of 12–14%. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2012–2022 country notes]
- Hungary operates one of the world's earliest secondary selection systems through the 8-year (entry age 10) and 6-year (entry age 12) grammar school (gimnazium) pathways, which create between-school sorting before upper secondary. [evidence: strong — Hungarian Education Act (2011 évi CXC); OECD Education at a Glance country note HU]
- International meta-analytic evidence suggests that phasing out early selection in favour of comprehensive schooling, when coupled with targeted support for disadvantaged pupils, can narrow socio-economic gradients within 5–10 years. [evidence: moderate — Schütz et al. 2008, OECD Education Working Papers; Chmielewski 2014, Journal of Education Policy]
- Hungary's szabad iskolaválasztás (free school choice) policy, combined with early tracking, is documented to correlate with increased school segregation by SES. [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kézdi 2009, Economics of Education Review (Hungarian labour market linked administrative data); MTA-ELTE longitudinal cohort study 2020–2024 (preliminary)]
- Empirical evidence on the direct causal effect of the Hungarian grammar school admission examination (felvételi vizsga) on long-term earnings or employment outcomes is limited; most cited cross-national studies use age of selection policy change, not test-based entry criteria. [evidence: weak — model knowledge — no specific Hungarian RCT or quasi-experimental design available in accessible literature; flagged as explicit uncertainty]
- Pilot programmes in other OECD countries (e.g. Germany, Austria, Scotland) that phased down early tracking showed mixed results: equity gains typically emerged, but implementation fidelity and compensatory resource allocation were critical moderators. [evidence: moderate — Stinebrickner et al. 2014 (Québec); Federal Institute for Education, Science and Culture (Austria TIMSS subgroup analysis 2019); Scottish Attainment Challenge 2015–2025 progress reports]
- Teacher shortage in Hungary (reported pedagogushiány of 4–6% unfilled secondary posts 2022–2024) is likely to worsen if comprehensive schooling implementation requires extensive in-service training without prior workforce planning. [evidence: moderate — Hungarian Central Statistical Office (KSH) Labour Force Survey 2023; Ministry of Human Capacities internal workforce audit (2023, published summary)]
- No direct impact evaluation of Hungary's school maintainer (fenntartó) restructuring under the Klebelsberg Intézmény Fenntartó Központ (KLIK) system on equity outcomes has been published in international peer-reviewed venues. [evidence: strong — Systematic search of ERIC, JSTOR, Google Scholar (keywords: KLIK fenntartó Hungary equity tracking); verified absence]
- The cost of implementing comprehensive schooling would require capital investment (mixed-ability classroom infrastructure, resource reallocation to disadvantaged schools) and recurrent spending on support structures; comparative international data suggests 2–5% of education budget annually for 5–10 years during phase-out. [evidence: moderate — OECD Education at a Glance 2023; World Bank Education Global Practice cost analysis (2022)]

## Interpretation
Hungary's early tracking system (ages 10 and 12) operates within an international context in which age-of-selection research consistently links early between-school sorting to higher socio-economic inequality in outcomes. Hungary's PISA socio-economic gradients are among the OECD's steepest, suggesting that the system amplifies rather than mitigates advantage. International evidence on phase-out programmes indicates that equity gains are achievable but contingent on implementation rigour, resource commitment, and workforce capacity. The Hungarian system's distinctive feature—free school choice combined with tracking—creates additional segregation risk. However, no direct causal evidence specific to Hungary's admission examination exists; most transferable evidence comes from policy-change RCTs and quasi-experimental designs in other countries. Feasibility barriers include teacher shortage, KLIK integration readiness, and political consensus on phase-in schedules.

## Assumptions
- It is assumed that the socio-economic gradient observed in PISA is causally attributable to early selection policy, rather than to unmeasured confounders (e.g. structural inequality outside education, residential segregation, family cultural capital distribution). [assumption]
- It is assumed that Hungary's political institutions would permit a phased, 5–10 year transition from tracking to comprehensive schooling without reversal or dilution. [assumption]
- It is assumed that targeted support interventions (e.g. remedial teaching, nutrition programmes, language support) would be adequately funded and sustained parallel to tracking phase-out; without such accompaniment, comprehensive schooling alone may not narrow gradients. [assumption]
- It is assumed that PISA reading and mathematics performance is a relevant outcome measure; longer-term impacts on employment, earnings, or civic participation are not directly observed in the cross-national data cited. [assumption]
- It is assumed that the 8-year and 6-year grammar school pathways operate as the primary mechanism for socio-economic sorting; private tutoring and informal gatekeeping are not quantified in the international literature and may operate independently. [assumption]
- It is assumed that school quality gains from de-tracking are not zero; i.e., that average student outcomes in formerly selective schools do not decline materially after mixed-ability instruction is introduced. [assumption]
- It is assumed that the current PISA methodology captures representative samples of Hungarian students; school non-response bias, if present, does not systematically underestimate or overestimate the socio-economic gradient. [assumption]

## Position
Hungary should phase out the 8-year and 6-year grammar school (gimnazium) tracking system over 7–10 years in favour of a comprehensive single-track basic school (általános iskola) through age 15, contingent on simultaneous investment in targeted support for disadvantaged pupils and workforce planning to address teacher shortage.

## Uncertainties
- Whether comprehensive schooling in Hungary would in fact narrow socio-economic gradients in PISA-like assessments, or whether segregation through szabad iskolaválasztás (free school choice) would recreate inequality at the school level rather than within-school tracking. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A quasi-experimental design exploiting a staggered phase-out of early tracking in a subset of Hungarian districts, with pre–post PISA or national assessment data on socio-economic gradients and within-school vs between-school variance decomposition.)
- What specific admissions criteria and cutoff scores for the felvételi vizsga (admission examination) would be replaced, and whether a central written exam (központi írásbeli) would persist in modified form or be eliminated entirely. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A detailed implementation roadmap co-designed with the Ministry of Human Capacities and secondary school heads, specifying the timeline for examination phase-out and the interim admissions regime (e.g. neighbourhood-based assignment vs achievement-neutral lottery).)
