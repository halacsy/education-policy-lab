## Overview

The expert record examines whether Hungary should retain, reform, or phase out its early academic selection system (6- and 8-year gimnázium tracks selecting pupils at ages 10 and 12). Experts converge on a shared empirical diagnosis — that early selection amplifies socio-economic stratification without raising mean achievement — but diverge sharply on the policy response, splitting between structural abolition, structural retention with non-structural equity fixes, and graduated/conditional reform. The disagreements cluster around three axes: (1) whether to change the structure at all, (2) whether reform is politically and administratively feasible, and (3) whether non-state maintainers can be contained during any transition.

## Disagreement map

- **equity_and_social_mobility, demography, international_comparison**: Hungary should phase out the 6- and 8-year selective tracks to delay sorting until age 14+, because early selection increases inequality (SES explains ~19-25% of PISA variance vs 12-14% OECD) without value-added gains. Why: value-added studies show the gimnázium advantage is mostly a selection effect, and demographic contraction makes parallel selective tracks unsustainable for rural networks. [evidence: strong/moderate]
- **finnish_reform, polish_reform**: Phase-out is correct *only if* bundled with enabling conditions — Finnish-style teacher training and diagnostic support (finnish_reform), or Polish-style multi-party pacts and compensatory funding to prevent reversal (polish_reform). Why: delayed tracking raised equity/PISA in both cases, but Finnish gains were inseparable from teacher/support bundling and Poland's reform was politically reversed 2016-2019. [evidence: moderate]
- **portuguese_reform, education_finance, implementation_planning, political_feasibility, legal_and_governance (minority)**: Hungary should retain the structure and pursue non-structural routes — targeted TEIP-style funding and curriculum standards (portuguese_reform), because transition costs outweigh flat steady-state savings (education_finance), teacher shortages make redistribution infeasible (implementation_planning), middle-class veto power blocks abrupt abolition (political_feasibility), and legal lock-in demands an equity audit before any legislated closure (legal_and_governance). Why: Portugal improved PISA 2006-2015 without changing selection age; Hungary's costs are front-loaded transitional; specialized STEM teachers cannot be redistributed without degrading delivery. [evidence: moderate/weak/strong]
- **hungarian_education_system (minority)**: Hungary should *retain* the tracks but align entry quotas with regional equity targets while regulating non-state admissions. Why: banning selective tracks only within state schools pushes selective demand into the church/foundation sector, shifting rather than eliminating sorting. [evidence: strong]
- **school_network_planning vs hungarian_education_system/legal_and_governance (on maintainer containment)**: school_network_planning assumes non-state maintainers *will* absorb phased-out selective tracks and bypass consolidation (fragmentation risk); hungarian_education_system and legal_and_governance treat containment of non-state maintainers as an open question requiring regulatory leverage the government may lack. Why: churches/foundations operate under different admissions incentives and expanding market share, and no legislative mandate currently binds their closure timelines. [evidence: strong findings; [assumption] on absorption behavior]

## What the experts agree on

- Early selection at ages 10–12 correlates with high socio-economic stratification; Hungary's SES-attributable PISA variance (~19-25%) is roughly double the OECD average. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2012-2022]
- Roughly 8-12% of a cohort enters the selective tracks, skewed toward high-SES urban families. [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal]
- Early tracking does not raise mean national achievement in comparable systems. [evidence: strong/moderate — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006; OECD]
- Maintainer plurality (state tankerület vs growing church/foundation share) complicates any uniform structural mandate. [evidence: strong — Public education act amendments]
- Whatever the path, structural reform is not a low-cost, low-friction operation: costs are front-loaded transitional (retraining, facilities, administration). [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates]

## Minority positions

- **hungarian_education_system (minority within the retain-and-regulate camp)**: Retain the 6/8-year tracks but bind their entry quotas to regional equity targets AND regulate non-state admissions to prevent sorting from migrating to the church/foundation sector. Rationale: abolition within state schools alone shifts rather than eliminates sorting; this position uniquely couples retention with active non-state regulation rather than passive non-structural fixes. Preserved uncertainty: church maintainer willingness to accept equity-based quotas under subsidy threat [confidence: medium].
- **portuguese_reform (minority framing that structural debate is a distraction)**: The entire abolish-vs-retain structural debate may be a distraction from higher-impact non-structural interventions (TEIP-style targeted funding, curriculum standards, reduced grade retention). Rationale: Portugal achieved substantial PISA gains 2006-2015 with the selection age held constant. Preserved uncertainty: transferability of TEIP mechanisms to the Hungarian context [assumption]; sustainability of long-term targeted funding without policy reversal [confidence: medium].
- **legal_and_governance (minority: no reform without audit)**: No structural closure should be legislated until a transparent, independent equity audit quantifies the 6/8-year system's actual mobility/equity impact. Rationale: no recent national impact study exists; legislating closure without empirical justification invites constitutional (Article 66/a) and EU equal-treatment challenge. Preserved uncertainty: whether recent Hungarian outcome studies show early selection worsens equity vs peers [confidence: low].
- **school_network_planning (minority on maintainer behavior)**: Treats it as an operating premise that non-state maintainers *will* absorb phased-out selective capacity and bypass consolidation — a fragmentation risk the abolitionist scenarios must design against. Rationale: churches/foundations have incentive and growing capacity to attract students via selective tracks. Held as [assumption], directly contested by the containment-uncertainty view above; not resolved.
