# Expert analysis: political_feasibility

## Findings (evidence)
- Urban middle-class parents regard 8- and 6-year gimnazium places as a key mobility/insurance asset, and prior attempts to curtail these structures have met intense, well-organized resistance. [evidence: moderate — Domestic press and stakeholder analyses, 1990s-2010s]
- Poland's 1999 reform postponed tracking to age 15 (gimnazjum), and PISA scores rose substantially by 2012, with analyses attributing part of the gain to delayed selection. [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD]
- Poland's 1999 tracking reform was reversed in 2016-2019 for political reasons. [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD]
- Selective 6- and 8-year gimnaziums in Hungary disproportionately serve socioeconomically advantaged families, concentrated in urban areas. [evidence: weak — Model knowledge]

## Interpretation
The political feasibility of reforming Hungary's early selection system is severely constrained by the mobilization capacity of its primary beneficiaries. The urban middle class has successfully vetoed past restructuring attempts because they view these schools as defensive hoarding mechanisms against a declining public education system. Furthermore, the Polish precedent demonstrates that even educationally successful structural reforms (such as delayed tracking) remain highly vulnerable to political reversal when a new regime seeks to mobilize populist or traditionalist sentiment. 

## Assumptions
- Middle-class resistance to reform will remain high unless they are provided with credible quality guarantees in the comprehensive primary school system. [assumption]
- The current Hungarian government prioritizes avoiding conflict with its core urban middle-class voters on education structural issues. [assumption]

## Position
Hungary should not attempt to abolish the 6- and 8-year gimnaziums abruptly, but should instead gradually phase them out by disincentivizing selective admissions while simultaneously upgrading standard primary school funding.

## Uncertainties
- The exact threshold of compensation (e.g., funding, extracurriculars) required to make comprehensive primary schools acceptable to urban middle-class parents. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: national representative survey data of urban parents on school choice trade-offs)
- The level of internal cohesion and resistance capacity of teachers' unions and school administrators within the 6- and 8-year gimnazium sector. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: institutional mapping and public statements from school leader associations)
