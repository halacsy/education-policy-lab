# Critique: evidence_checker
## S1.goal
Objection: The goal states "Retain the 6- and 8-year gimnázium tracks but rewrite entry rules and quotas so that access to selective places is less strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families." This is contradicted by the "Overview" section which states "Hungary should phase out the 6- and 8-year selective tracks to delay sorting until age 14+". The goal of S1 is to reform the existing system, not phase it out.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: The goal should accurately reflect the scenario's proposed mechanism, which is to reform rather than phase out the selective tracks.

## S1.mechanism
Objection: The mechanism states that replacing the central written admission exam with a less preparation-sensitive assessment "weakens the advantage that paid test-prep confers on high-SES applicants". However, the evidence cited is "moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013; Horn 2013 value-added studies show selective advantage is largely a selection effect". While value-added studies show the advantage is largely selection, they do not directly support the claim that a *less preparation-sensitive assessment* specifically weakens the advantage of *paid test-prep*. The evidence is too weak to support the claim about test-prep.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: The evidence should more directly support the claim that the proposed assessment change weakens the advantage of paid test-prep. Alternatively, the claim should be weakened to reflect what the evidence actually supports.

## S1.expected_benefits
Objection: The expected benefit states "Reduced test-preparation arms-race pressure on age-10 children insofar as the new assessment resists coaching". The evidence cited is "weak — model knowledge; note contradiction with political_risks, where scarcer/reformed channels may invite informal selection". This evidence is explicitly weak and notes a contradiction, making it insufficient to support the claimed benefit.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: The evidence for this benefit should be strengthened, or the benefit itself should be downgraded in its claim of certainty.

## S2.mechanism
Objection: The mechanism states "Fewer selective seats keep more high-achievers in the common track, which can improve peer composition in urban general-school upper grades". The evidence cited is "contested — the asymmetric peer-effect claim is weak in the source (equity_and_social_mobility tags it weak) and is the same contested link S3 relies on". This indicates that the evidence is contested and weak, which is insufficient to support the claimed benefit of improved peer composition.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: The evidence for this claim should be strengthened, or the claim should be presented as more uncertain.

## S3.mechanism
Objection: The mechanism claims "A weaker SES-outcome gradient can emerge within a decade via broader access to high-achieving peers". The evidence cited is "contested — the asymmetric peer-effect mechanism is tagged weak/contested in equity_and_social_mobility; the decade-scale outcome is not established for Hungary". This evidence is explicitly contested and not established for Hungary, making it insufficient to support the claimed benefit of a weaker SES-outcome gradient within a decade.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: The evidence for this claim should be strengthened, or the claim should be presented as more uncertain.

## S3.equity_impact
Objection: The equity impact states "Largest expected aggregate equity gain of the four scenarios, but also the largest risk: middle-class exit to non-state or private schools." This is followed by "That exit does not harm the exiting families; it concentrates the downside on the low-SES pupils left behind, so within-disadvantaged distributional harm is possible even if the aggregate gradient narrows." This presents a contradiction: if the aggregate gradient narrows, it implies an equity gain, but the scenario also states that within-disadvantaged harm is possible. The scenario does not adequately explain how these two seemingly opposing outcomes can coexist as the "largest expected aggregate equity gain" while also risking "within-disadvantaged distributional harm."
Severity: high
Suggested revision: The scenario needs to clarify how an aggregate equity gain can be the largest while simultaneously risking within-disadvantaged harm, or the framing of the equity impact needs to be adjusted to reflect this nuance more clearly.

## S4.mechanism
Objection: The mechanism states "Compensation is intended to offset, not merely mask, the effects of early sorting". The evidence cited is "weak — no country has demonstrated offsetting at Hungary's selection age; the Portuguese case did not run early selection at ages 10-12". This evidence directly contradicts the claim, as it states no country has demonstrated offsetting at Hungary's selection age, and the Portuguese case is not comparable due to different selection ages. Therefore, the evidence does not support the claim that compensation is intended to offset, but rather suggests it is an unproven intention.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: The claim that compensation is intended to offset should be weakened to reflect that this is an intention with no demonstrated precedent at Hungary's selection age, or the evidence should be strengthened to show such offsetting is possible.
