# Critique: coherence_checker
## S1.mechanism
Objection: The mechanism claims that replacing the central written admission exam with a less preparation-sensitive assessment weakens the advantage of paid test-prep, citing Kertesi & Kezdi 2013 and Horn 2013. However, the evidence tag for this claim is "moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013; Horn 2013 value-added studies show selective advantage is largely a selection effect". This suggests the cited evidence primarily indicates that the advantage is due to selection rather than test preparation itself, which weakens the argument that a less coachable assessment would significantly reduce the advantage conferred by paid test-prep.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Clarify how the cited evidence supports the claim that a less coachable assessment would weaken the advantage of paid test-prep, or revise the evidence tag to accurately reflect the nuance of the cited studies.

## S1.expected_benefits
Objection: The expected benefits state that reduced test-preparation pressure on children is expected "insofar as the new assessment resists coaching", citing "weak — model knowledge". This contradicts the "political_risks" section, which states that "Reformed/scarcer selection channels may invite informal selection (interviews, reputation) that reproduces SES sorting". If the reformed channels invite informal selection, it suggests that the pressure for preparation might not be reduced but rather shifted to different, potentially less transparent, forms of selection.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Reconcile the claim of reduced test-prep pressure with the risk of informal selection by either explaining how the new assessment mitigates informal selection or by acknowledging that the pressure might be redirected.

## S2.equity_impact
Objection: The equity impact states that "As caps tighten, residual selective places become even more SES-skewed absent an S1-type admission-rule change — this residual-skew effect is mechanical and near-certain (fewer seats, same demand, unchanged rules)." This directly contradicts the goal of the scenario, which is to "Shrink the share of each cohort entering selective tracks at ages 10 and 12". If the remaining selective places become *more* SES-skewed, it implies that the policy, without complementary admission rule changes, might exacerbate existing inequalities for those who still gain access to selective tracks, even as the overall number of such places decreases.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Acknowledge that without S1-type admission rule changes, the tightening caps in S2 could lead to a more pronounced SES skew among the *remaining* selective places, and explicitly state that the equity gains are contingent on or would be significantly enhanced by pairing with admission reform.

## S3.assumptions
Objection: The assumptions section states, "Sorting does not simply migrate to school choice between general schools and to non-state maintainers [assumption — this is asserted here yet the equity_impact field calls exactly this migration the largest risk; it is load-bearing and unevidenced]". This is a direct contradiction where an assumption is presented as a given, while the equity impact section explicitly identifies the same point as the "largest risk" and "un-evidenced". This undermines the foundational basis of the scenario's equity claims.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Remove the assertion that sorting does not migrate from the assumptions and instead frame it as a critical uncertainty or risk that needs to be addressed by the mechanism or further mitigation strategies. The equity impact section should then clearly articulate the consequences of this risk materializing.
