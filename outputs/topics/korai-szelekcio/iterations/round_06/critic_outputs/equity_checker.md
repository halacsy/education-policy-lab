# Critique: equity_checker
## S1.equity_impact
Objection: The scenario claims that "Gains accrue to high-ability low-SES pupils who already apply and are reweighted into selective seats," but this is contradicted by the assumption that "A workable SES indicator exists at application time" which is flagged as unresolved. If the SES indicator is not workable, then the intended beneficiaries of the gains cannot be reliably identified or targeted.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Clarify how the gains would be realized if the SES indicator is not workable, or explicitly state that the equity gains are contingent on resolving the SES indicator assumption.

## S2.equity_impact
Objection: The scenario states that "As caps tighten, residual selective places become even more SES-skewed absent an S1-type admission-rule change," which is a direct contradiction to the goal of improving equity. If the residual places become *more* skewed, then the policy is actively worsening the situation for disadvantaged groups, not improving it.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Reframe the equity impact to acknowledge that without complementary admission reforms (like S1), the tightening caps will exacerbate existing inequalities in access to the remaining selective places.

## S3.equity_impact
Objection: The scenario states that "The largest expected aggregate equity gain of the four scenarios, but also the largest risk: middle-class exit to non-state or private schools." It then claims that "That exit does not harm the exiting families; it concentrates the downside on the low-SES pupils left behind, so within-disadvantaged distributional harm is possible even if the aggregate gradient narrows." This is a significant equity concern that is not adequately addressed by the scenario's proposed benefits or mechanisms. The scenario acknowledges potential harm to low-SES pupils left behind but does not propose mitigation strategies.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Include specific mechanisms or policy interventions within S3 to mitigate the risk of middle-class flight to non-state/private schools and the subsequent concentration of disadvantage on remaining low-SES pupils.

## S4.equity_impact
Objection: The scenario claims that "Narrows the SES gradient at best partially, since it leaves the sorting mechanism itself intact and only compensates downstream." This implies that the scenario does not fully address the root cause of inequity, which is the early sorting mechanism. Furthermore, the scenario states that "if compensation only masks rather than offsets sorting it carries a dynamic equity cost flagged in political_risks: it risks legitimising early selection permanently, entrenching the very gradient it aims to reduce." This suggests a potential for long-term negative equity impact by reinforcing the existing inequitable system.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Explicitly state that this scenario is a partial solution and that its long-term equity benefits are uncertain and contingent on the compensation effectively offsetting, rather than masking, early selection. Acknowledge the risk of entrenching the existing gradient.
