# rejected_framings.md

## S1 — Admission reform within the current structure

- CHOSEN: Reweight access to selective places through a less-coachable assessment and regional equity quotas, without closing the selective tracks themselves; presumes a workable SES indicator can be built.

- REJECTED: "Holistic review + interview-based assessment replaces written exam" — reason: The political_risks section names informal selection (interviews, reputation) as a vector for reproducing SES sorting; this framing would invert the mechanism's core defense against coaching-based advantage and introduce a less-auditable, more-subjective channel through which high-SES social capital would reappear.

- REJECTED: "Blanket SES quota of 50% of selective seats, identical across all schools" — reason: No empirical basis in Hungarian administrative data for a specific percentage; the mechanism's credibility rests on quota design being defensible against both elite-school exemption claims and perceptions of arbitrary dilution. A fixed percentage forecloses the tiered, maintainer-specific negotiation that the implementation_steps outline.

- REJECTED: "Eliminate all non-state selective admission entirely; fold church/foundation selective schools into state system" — reason: This escalates from the stated goal (rewrite entry rules) to structural closure; it invites the church/private maintainer opposition named in political_risks at an order of magnitude beyond what subsidy-conditional rule harmonization carries, and it is not a tested route in the Hungarian policy context.

- REJECTED: "SES indicator is family-income tax return cross-linked with Oktatási Hivatal" — reason: Presupposes a unified administrative register that does not exist at application time; the assumptions acknowledge this gap, and the framing must stay within the realm of feasible proxies (address-based disadvantage, family benefit receipt) even though they are imperfect and gameable.

## S2 — Gradual phase-down of 6/8-year entry places

- CHOSEN: Cap and reduce 6/8-year entry places on a pre-announced decade-long trajectory with felmenő rendszer guarantees and an entrenchment mechanism to resist reversal.

- REJECTED: "Immediate 50% reduction in entry places in year 1, then hold steady" — reason: Violates the scenario's own mechanism (declining trajectory) and concentrates harm on a single cohort; the political_risks section explicitly flags transition-cohort vulnerability to higher-SES capture of shrinking seats, which a front-loaded reduction would exacerbate.

- REJECTED: "Cap applies only to state-maintained gymnasia; leave church and foundation schools uncapped" — reason: The assumptions explicitly flag non-state offset as a risk; the implementation_steps correctly name separate maintainer-agreement negotiation, but this framing abdicates that work, guaranteeing the outcome the mechanism is designed to prevent (selective demand migrating to unregulated maintainers).

- REJECTED: "Pair the cap with guaranteed reallocation of freed seats to vocational tracks" — reason: Shifts the problem rather than solving it; the freed seats do not exist in usable form (they are upper-grade selective places; vocational tracks operate separately). This framing also presupposes a curriculum bridge (general-school completion → vocational entry) that is not part of the mechanism and would require separate legislative design.

- REJECTED: "No legislative entrenchment; rely on the political difficulty of reversing a de facto decade-long commitment" — reason: The political_risks section cites Polish precedent for cheap reversal; the implementation_steps explicitly call for an entrenchment mechanism (multi-year notice, supermajority reversal requirement). A framing that omits this is incoherent with the scenario's own risk recognition and would leave the scenario vulnerable to the exact Polish-style reversal it names.

## S3 — Comprehensive school to age 14 (structural reform)

- CHOSEN: Postpone between-school selection to age 14 by phasing out 6/8-year entry, bundled with teacher retraining in differentiated instruction and diagnostic support; anchor against reversal via a cross-party pact with a reversible pilot fallback.

- REJECTED: "Eliminate selective tracks entirely; converge to a single comprehensive stream through age 18" — reason: Scope creep beyond the stated goal (delay selection to 14); it collapses the distinction between de-tracked primary and middle school versus the upper-secondary vocational/academic split that happens at 14 in any system. This framing also invites elite-school resistance that S3 already names as the "highest-intensity opposition of all scenarios"—extending it through age 18 would make entrenchment impossible.

- REJECTED: "Delay selection to 14, but leave general schools under-resourced; rely on teacher willingness to de-track" — reason: The mechanism explicitly tags the bundling with teacher-quality and diagnostic-support investment as necessary ("delay alone is insufficient"); this framing strips it away, converting the scenario into an unfunded structural change that the uncertainties flag as threatening delivery in disadvantaged regions.

- REJECTED: "Sorting does not migrate; assume families will accept general-school placement at 14 rather than flight to non-state schools" — reason: The equity_impact identifies middle-class exit as "the largest risk" and the assumptions concede it is "load-bearing and unevidenced." A framing that asserts no migration directly contradicts the scenario's own risk recognition; the mechanism must assume the risk exists and the diagnostic/support bundle is designed to mitigate it (not eliminate it).

- REJECTED: "Pilot in all districts simultaneously; national rollout in year 3" — reason: The implementation_steps correctly name a staged 2-3 district pilot (years 2-5) with evaluation before rollout; this framing omits the pilot discipline. Given the low confidence (low, per uncertainties) in Hungary-specific effect size and teacher supply viability, a full-scale simultaneous pilot risks locking in failure across multiple regions at once, destroying the reversibility that the cross-party pact is meant to preserve.

- REJECTED: "The cross-party pact is binding and cannot be renegotiated once the pilot begins" — reason: The implementation_steps outline the pact as a year 1-2 negotiation with a "defined fallback" if it fails; a framing that treats the pact as irreversible forecloses the adaptive governance the scenario's own political-risk section requires. Polish-style reversal happens because no fallback exists; S3's design virtue is that it does.

## S4 — Keep the structure, compensate general schools (Portuguese-style package)

- CHOSEN: Retain 6/8-year selective tracks unchanged; neutralize equity effects through TEIP-style targeted support for disadvantaged general schools, rigorous curriculum standards, and grade-retention reduction.

- REJECTED: "Compensation is 'politically inexpensive' and 'implementable under any government' [evidence: strong]" — reason: The coherence_checker and political_risks section both flag this as overclaimed. The cost_categories list compensation funding as "high" and "the first cut in fiscal consolidation"; the political_risks section explicitly states "gains are reversible across fiscal cycles." A framing that asserts strong evidence for implementability contradicts the scenario's own identification of serious budget vulnerability.

- REJECTED: "Compensation can fully offset (not merely mask) the sorting effect; Hungary can replicate the Portuguese outcome without the Portuguese lack of early selection" — reason: The evidence_status section concedes "whether compensation can offset (rather than mask or legitimise) selection at Hungary's selection age is unevidenced"; the uncertainties flag this as pivotal with low confidence. This framing imports the Portuguese effect size into a context with a structural difference (early selection at 10-12) that Portugal did not have; the transfer is uncertain and the scenario must preserve that uncertainty.

- REJECTED: "SES-based targeting formulas are self-executing; they reach the disadvantaged majority without stigma or mis-targeting" — reason: The assumptions section names both stigma risk and the "more consequential risk that SES-based targeting formulas mis-target or exclude portions of the disadvantaged population." A framing that asserts self-execution forecloses the implementation challenge the scenario itself identifies.

- REJECTED: "Teacher premia for disadvantaged schools are optional; the standards/support package works without them" — reason: The implementation_steps explicitly require teacher premia (year 2 onward); the cost_categories list "teacher premia for disadvantaged schools and standards/curriculum implementation (medium-high)." A framing that treats premia as optional is incoherent with the scenario's own design and falsely reduces the political-capital cost.

- REJECTED: "Compensation legitimises rather than entrench selection; it signals that selection is acceptable as long as the losers are compensated" — reason: The political_risks and equity_impact sections both name legitimisation as a risk, not a benefit. This framing inverts the scenario's risk-recognition into a feature, which would hide rather than surface the dynamic equity cost the scenario is designed to flag. S4's integrity depends on preserving the tension between the non-structural goal and the risk that compensation entrenches the very sorting it aims to offset.
