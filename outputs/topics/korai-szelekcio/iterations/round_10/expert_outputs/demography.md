# Expert analysis: demography

## Findings (evidence)
- Annual births in Hungary fell from ~125,000 in the early 1990s to 85–95,000 by the early 2020s, and fertility dropped below 1.3 by 2025; this 30–35% cohort collapse mechanically shrinks demand for all secondary tracks including 6/8-year grammar schools (gimnázium) regardless of selection policy design. [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks; Economx Oct 7 2025, Economx May 19 2026]
- System infrastructure is currently calibrated for ~110,000 annual cohorts, but will need to serve only ~72,000 within a few years—a 35% drop that forces school-network consolidation decisions independent of early-selection reform. [evidence: moderate — PSZ president Totyik Tamás, Economx May 19 2026]
- Regional demographic divergence is extreme: some districts record only 55–67 annual births, while Budapest districts and suburban areas show 40%+ divergence between current birth numbers and first-grade enrollment forecasts, making uniform national selection rules structurally unfit for local implementation. [evidence: moderate — Economx May 19 2026]
- Primary-school enrollment has fallen 17.3% in 20 years (from 859,000 in 2005/06 to ~749,000 today); the number of general-school sites (általános iskola) fell from 3,625 in 2021/22 to 3,537 in 2024/25, with accelerating closures. [evidence: strong — Népszava/KSH July 14 2025]
- Kindergarten enrollment fell from 341,000 in 2011/12 to 324,000 in 2024/25; the under-1 cohort is already ~17,000 smaller than the 4-year-old cohort, foreshadowing further primary and secondary contraction through 2040. [evidence: strong — Válasz Online Oct 17 2025; Népszava July 14 2025]
- Kertesi–Kézdi research established that Hungary's free-choice, early-tracking system allows schools discretion to hand-pick students, producing substantial socioeconomic segregation in primary and secondary schooling independent of demographic pressure. [evidence: strong — Kertesi–Kézdi; cited in 'Early tracking and competition – A recipe for major inequalities in Hungary']
- PISA 2022 data confirm that in Hungary socioeconomic status accounts for 25% of variation in mathematics performance versus an OECD average of 15%, indicating that early selection amplifies socioeconomic determinism of outcomes. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2022; MDPI 'How Rare and How Stable?']
- Union analysis shows small-school size is not the primary driver of underperformance; the correlation between test results and social background far exceeds that between results and school size, even though small rural schools disproportionately enroll disadvantaged pupils. [evidence: moderate — PSZ president Totyik Tamás, Economx Oct 7 2025]
- Union and government sources agree that school-network consolidation (mergers and closures) is 'nearly certain' within 5–10 years; currently 80 schools operate with no first grade and 200 operate with ~10 first-graders, making unified selection criteria operationally moot at many sites. [evidence: moderate — Economx July 15 2025; Válasz Online Oct 17 2025]
- In 2024/25, of students transitioning beyond general school, 44.8% (192,000) entered secondary grammar-school track (including 6/8-year routes), 41.9% entered technical/vocational secondary, and 13.3% entered non-matriculation vocational programs. [evidence: strong — KSH 'Oktatási adatok, 2024/2025']
- Hungary's population declined from 9.87 million (2011 census) to 9.6 million (2022 census), a 3.4% drop—the largest intercensal decline ever recorded; trend-based projections place population at 8.5 million by 2050, a further 13% decrease that directly shrinks the school-age population base. [evidence: strong — OSW Centre for Eastern Studies April 2025; Hungarian Central Statistical Office (KSH) 2022 census]
- Cost-function research (Hermann 2005b, OFI synthesis) shows per-pupil costs fall sharply as enrollment rises toward ~200 students, after which economies of scale flatten; consolidation below 200 pupils yields the largest unit-cost savings but aggregate national savings remain uncertain under different closure/merger scenarios. [evidence: moderate — Oktatáskutató és Fejlesztő Intézet (OFI) 'A kistelepülések kisiskolái – társulás?']
- Government framing resists the term 'closure,' instead emphasizing that consolidation ('összevonások') will protect small-settlement schools 'as long as there is demand' (amíg erre van igény), but this framing does not alter the mechanical force of cohort decline. [evidence: moderate — Válasz Online Oct 17 2025]
- Political leadership at all levels has not yet mounted a coordinated, public agenda for managing school-network consolidation despite union and government acknowledgment that it is unavoidable within 5–10 years. [evidence: moderate — Economx July 15 2025; Válasz Online Oct 17 2025; Economx Oct 7 2025]

## Interpretation
Cohort collapse—not selection design—is now the dominant structural force reshaping Hungary's school system through 2040. Births have fallen 30–35% from 1990s levels; the system is calibrated for 110,000 pupils annually but will serve ~72,000 within years. This mechanical shrinkage will force network consolidation (mergers, closures) in nearly every district, with extreme regional divergence meaning that uniform selection rules are becoming operationally moot: many schools already have no first grade. Simultaneously, early selection's documented equity cost (Kertesi–Kézdi, PISA 2022 data showing 25% vs. 15% SES-performance correlation) persists and will not be erased by demographic decline; if anything, concentration of intake in fewer, selective sites will amplify segregation unless deliberate integration safeguards are designed into consolidation. The political consensus—union, government, and researcher voices—is that consolidation is 'nearly certain,' yet no coordinated public agenda for managing it exists. This means the 6/8-year grammar-school decision cannot be isolated from the broader network-consolidation crisis. Reforming selection design alone (e.g., delaying it, widening access, moving to comprehensive lower-secondary) will not solve the system's core problem: too many underutilized buildings serving too few children. Yet consolidation design *without* attention to equity will likely sharpen already steep SES-driven sorting. The critical decision now is not 'early selection vs. comprehensive lower-secondary in the abstract,' but 'how to consolidate the network while protecting access and equity for disadvantaged pupils in shrinking rural regions.'

## Assumptions
- Cohort-decline projections through 2040 will not be reversed by a major unforeseen fertility rebound or policy-driven immigration surge. [assumption]
- School-network consolidation will proceed through some mix of closures, mergers, and distributed-model pilot programs rather than by a reversal to status-quo over-capacity. [assumption]
- The equity effects of early selection are not wholly subsumed by school size or rural–urban geography; they reflect hand-picking discretion by selective schools and remain addressable through policy design (e.g., random-draw admissions, admission ceilings on SES concentration). [assumption]
- Stakeholders (teachers, maintainers, families, local politicians) will need transparent information about consolidation timelines and network-redesign criteria to make informed choices about school transitions and planning. [assumption]

## Position
Cohort collapse through 2040 will force school-network consolidation regardless of whether Hungary retains or reforms early selection (6/8-year grammar schools), but the equity consequences of consolidation depend entirely on whether selection and network-redesign policies are coordinated to protect disadvantaged pupils' access to integrated, quality schooling in shrinking regions.

## Uncertainties
- Will Hungary's fertility rate stabilize at 1.2–1.3 or continue falling below 1.1? Projections are highly sensitive to this parameter and directly affect whether cohort size bottoms out at ~72,000 or falls further to ~60,000 annually by 2035. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Longitudinal fertility surveys (five-year updates to Eurostat projections), policy tracking (e.g., family support / immigration legislation changes), and direct cohort-component demographic modeling with scenario branching.)
- Will regional divergence permit a single national early-selection framework (e.g., 6- or 8-year grammar-school entry at age 10–12) to remain educationally meaningful in districts where first-grade enrollment falls below ~100 pupils, or will local variation in school structure become unavoidable? (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Detailed school-by-school enrollment forecasts and feasibility studies of mixed (single-track and multi-track) school models in low-density regions; pilot data from consolidation trials in rural areas.)
- How much of Hungary's documented socioeconomic-driven achievement gap (PISA: 25% vs. 15% OECD) is causally attributable to early selection versus to broader poverty, teacher shortages, and regional underinvestment? If consolidation occurs without explicit equity safeguards, will SES sorting worsen, improve, or remain unchanged? (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Causal-inference studies (e.g., quasi-experimental analysis of non-selective vs. selective cohorts matched on background); data on value-added (pedagógiai hozzáadott érték) by school type and SES intake; tracking of segregation indices before, during, and after consolidation pilots.)
