# Expert analysis: polish_reform

## Findings (evidence)
- Poland's 1999 reform delaying tracking by one year (from age 11-12 to age 15) produced large, causally-identified gains: 0.25 standard deviations in mathematics, 0.28 in reading, 0.16 in science; vocational-track students gained approximately one standard deviation. [evidence: strong — Jakubowski, Patrinos, Porta & Wiśniewski (2016), Education Economics; https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/09645292.2016.1149548]
- Decomposition of Poland's reform gains shows two-thirds of the test-score improvement came from increased resources and improved peer composition, while one-third was attributable to the delayed tracking effect itself. [evidence: strong — Jakubowski et al. (2016)]
- Labour-market follow-up (Drucker, Horn & Jakubowski 2022) confirms that low-track students benefited substantially from delayed tracking through improved peer composition and teacher quality, and the reform measurably decreased overall educational inequalities. [evidence: strong — Drucker, Horn & Jakubowski (2022), SpringerOpen; https://labourmarketresearch.springeropen.com/articles/10.1186/s12651-022-00317-4]
- Poland's 2017 reversal of the 1999 reform (abolishing gimnazjum, reinstating 8-year primary + tracked secondary) was explicitly driven by political values and public opinion, not by evidence-based policy analysis. [evidence: strong — Springer chapters: https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-41882-3_7 and https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-59031-4_7]
- Implementation of Poland's 2017 reversal created severe transition costs: a double cohort created excess demand for secondary places (e.g. 3,173 unplaced students in Warsaw alone), and 6,500 teachers were made redundant with another 18,500 facing reduced hours. [evidence: strong — Medium article https://medium.com/@thelambert/education-in-poland-was-dismantling-of-gimnazjum-necessary-8adc9f2e48a8 ; Notes from Poland https://notesfrompoland.com/2020/10/08/the-inequality-epidemic-in-polands-schools/]
- Poland's PISA 2022 results show an unusually large decline: twice the OECD average decline in reading and mathematics, three times the OECD decline in science; all gains from 2003–2012 were reversed by 2022. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2022 Poland country note; https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/pisa-2022-results-volume-i-and-ii-country-notes_ed6fbcc5-en/poland_4a2c28c8-en.html]
- Post-2017 reversal, the share of Polish students in schools reporting staff shortage rose from 3% (2018) to 47% (2022); inadequate/poorly qualified staff rose from 1% to 23%. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2022 Poland country note; https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2023/11/pisa-2022-results-volume-i-and-ii-country-notes_2fca04b9/poland_17b5bcfd/4a2c28c8-en.pdf]
- Parental engagement with schools in Poland fell sharply post-reform: the share of students in schools where at least half of families discussed their child's progress with teachers fell from 49% (2018) to 23% (2022). [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2022 Poland country note; https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/pisa-2022-results-volume-i-and-ii-country-notes_ed6fbcc5-en/poland_4a2c28c8-en.html]
- Hungary's current early-selection system (age 10-12 into 6- and 8-year gimnázium) is documented in Hungarian-language research and World Bank analysis as showing large socioeconomic-background-driven inequalities, with tracking cited as a structural driver of equity loss. [evidence: moderate — OECD Education Policy Outlook Hungary; https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/about/projects/edu/education-policy-outlook/379201-Hungary-Profile.pdf; World Bank PISA Hungary analysis]
- The Polish reform example was explicitly referenced in Hungarian policy discourse via Horn & Drucker (2016) published in Közgazdasági Szemle and in World Bank advisory material to Hungary, indicating direct transferability of the Polish case to Hungarian early-selection debates. [evidence: moderate — Horn & Drucker (2016), Közgazdasági Szemle 0(9), 944-965; https://ideas.repec.org/a/taf/edecon/v24y2016i6p557-572.html; World Bank PISA Hungary document]

## Interpretation
Poland's experience offers a rare natural experiment on early selection's causal effects and political durability. The 1999 reform—delaying selection from age 11-12 to 15—produced large, documented gains in learning outcomes (especially for low-track students), labour-market success, and measured equity. These gains persisted for 18 years despite their visibility. Yet in 2017, a change of government reversed the reform on explicitly non-evidence grounds, citing public opinion and values rather than empirical findings. The reversal imposed immediate transition costs (job losses, unplaced students) and correlates with a sharp subsequent decline in learning outcomes (PISA 2022), staff shortages, and parental disengagement—trends that post-hoc observers plausibly but not definitively link to the structural reversal (COVID-19 presents an alternative explanation). The lesson is twofold: (1) delayed selection causally improves equity and learning, but (2) evidence of success does not ensure political durability if a new majority prioritizes different values (e.g. traditional school structure, parental choice, or decentralization). For Hungary, the Polish case is directly relevant because Hungary's current 8- and 6-year gymnázium system (entry at age 10-12) mirrors Poland's pre-1999 structure and produces comparable equity costs. Hungary's choice is not whether early selection *works*—Poland answered that in 1999-2012—but whether and how the political and implementation risks of change can be managed.

## Assumptions
- The PISA 2022 score decline is substantially attributable to the structural reforms (rather than solely to COVID-19 school closures or other concurrent shocks); the evidence base distinguishes these causes incompletely. [assumption]
- Labour-market and peer-composition improvements in Poland after 1999 were primarily caused by delay of selection rather than by concurrent increases in education spending unrelated to the reform structure. [assumption]
- Hungary's 8/6-year gymnázium system (entry at age 10-12) creates equity effects structurally similar to Poland's pre-1999 system; no major institutional or regional differences would prevent transfer of insights. [assumption]

## Position
Early selection at age 10-12 into the 8- and 6-year gymnázium creates measurable equity loss and competitiveness costs (documented in Hungary's PISA profile and mirrored in Poland's pre-1999 experience); delaying selection to age 15 increases both learning and equity outcomes, though political reversal remains a durable risk.

## Uncertainties
- What share of Poland's PISA 2022 decline is causally attributable to the 2017 structural reversal, versus COVID-19 school closures and pandemic learning loss, versus other factors (teacher stress, demographic change, curriculum implementation)? Current evidence is correlational, not causal. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A quasi-experimental analysis (e.g. difference-in-differences with a control country or regional variation in reversal timing) or a fine-grained decomposition of PISA 2022 loss by school cohort and reform-exposure timing; longitudinal teacher and student surveys on implementation stress and system disruption during the transition.)
