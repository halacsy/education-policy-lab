# Expert analysis: implementation_planning

## Findings (evidence)
- Hungary's teaching workforce is aging rapidly, with half of all teachers aged 50 or older and only 7.2% under 30 as of 2024/2025, creating a structural constraint on any reform requiring teacher redeployment or retraining. [evidence: strong — KSH / Education and Training Monitor 2025 (European Commission)]
- A multi-year EU-funded teacher salary reform (EUR 1.8 billion, 2024–2031) is mid-implementation and consumes administrative bandwidth in parallel with any potential selection-system redesign, narrowing the realistic window for structural change. [evidence: strong — European Social Fund Plus / Hungary salary reform timeline 2025]
- A newly launched teacher performance-evaluation bureaucracy was rolled back within one year (June 2026) due to excessive administrative burden, signalling that education administration is already at or near capacity for new requirements. [evidence: strong — Telex, June 9, 2026; education minister Lannert Judit announcement]
- Capacity in the 6- and 8-year grammar school tracks has remained flat (25,962 students in 2024/2025) for over a decade despite strong demand, making both expansion and phase-out scenarios administratively and logistically rigid in the short term. [evidence: strong — KSH / Eduline, February 18, 2026]
- Demand signals shifted in 2026: six-grade grammar school applications rose 11% while eight-grade fell 14%, and demographic decline is reducing the structural urgency of the sorting mechanism itself independent of policy choice. [evidence: strong — Eduline, May 15, 2026; Portfolio.hu, June 11, 2026]
- The current education minister, initially critical of the elite-selection function of the 6/8-year system, retreated to a consultative 'social dialogue' stance within days of political pushback, indicating a low political ceiling for near-term structural abolition. [evidence: strong — Telex, June 6, 2026; WMN, June 8, 2026; Portfolio.hu, June 11, 2026]
- Admissions to 6- and 8-year grammar schools are decentralized to individual schools, which set their own criteria; any reform centralizing or redesigning admissions faces a baseline of thousands of independent school-level procedures. [evidence: strong — Oktatási Hivatal / Eduline, May 15, 2026; Pénzcentrum, November 21, 2025]
- Private preparation costs for 6/8-year admissions average 34,000 forints monthly and 3.5 hours per week, evidence that any redesigned admissions system risks simply relocating the socio-economic sorting mechanism unless explicitly counteracted. [evidence: moderate — Berényi Eszter (2019) cited in Telex, June 8, 2026]
- Compulsory instruction time in Hungary is below OECD average (666 hours/year primary, 796 lower secondary vs. OECD 804/909), constraining any reform extending the common phase without proportional teacher-cost increases. [evidence: strong — OECD Education at a Glance 2025]

## Interpretation
Implementation feasibility for any structural reform of early selection is severely constrained by four overlapping binding realities: (1) administrative bandwidth is saturated by a simultaneous salary reform and rollback of a failed evaluation system; (2) the teaching workforce is aging faster than it is being replaced, making capacity-intensive alternatives unrealistic in the medium term; (3) demand itself is already weakening due to demographic decline, reducing the political pressure for urgent action; and (4) political limits are evident — even a sympathetic education minister retreated immediately to 'consultation' rather than legislative change. The 2019 admission data show that current selection sorts primarily by family investment capacity. Any redesign that claims to address equity or meritocracy would need to explicitly counteract private preparation advantages, which requires new administration at a moment when schools are already struggling with excess bureaucracy. The realistic timeline for substantial reform is post-2031 (after salary reform completion), and the most feasible near-term path is incremental revision of admissions criteria or procedural transparency, not structural abolition or major alternatives.

## Assumptions
- Any structural redesign (e.g., comprehensive lower-secondary, teacher-recommendation admission) would require retraining or redeploying significant numbers of teachers; the current aged workforce makes this capability assumption fragile. [assumption]
- Political feasibility is determined by the stated positions of governing parties and church maintainers; a government changing composition or priority could alter the timeline, but no current legislative proposal for abolition exists. [assumption]
- The 3.5-hour-per-week private preparation figure applies to current admissions; a redesigned system might shift preparation intensity rather than eliminate it, and measurement of this outcome is not yet planned. [assumption]

## Position
Structural reform of early selection is politically and administratively feasible only after 2031, when teacher-salary reform concludes and demographic decline has reduced demand pressure; near-term alternatives are limited to incremental admissions-process transparency and criterion revision, not redesign of the track system itself.

## Uncertainties
- What proportion of the private preparation investment (3.5 hrs/week, 34k HUF/month) reflects parental anxiety about selection-system uncertainty vs. genuine preparation for content demands? (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Qualitative interviews with parents of applicants and non-applicants, plus item analysis of current admissions exams to measure genuine difficulty vs. preparability; comparison with selection systems in peer countries (e.g., Netherlands, Slovakia) where preparation intensity is known.)
- How much of the current demand for 6/8-year tracks is driven by elite-positioning behavior vs. perceived superior pedagogy or outcomes; would administrative redesign (e.g., lottery, teacher recommendation) alone reduce applications, or would demand simply shift to private schools? (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Quasi-experimental analysis of school-choice behavior following admissions-criterion changes in other regions (e.g., track elimination pilots, if any exist); household survey on motivations for 6/8-year choice vs. other secondary options.)
- What is the pedagogical and social-composition difference between 6/8-year gymnasium entry and entry at age 15 with the same pupil cohort; would a delayed-selection alternative deliver comparable academic outcomes with lower segregation? (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Longitudinal follow-up comparing matched cohorts entering grammar school at age 10/12 vs. age 15 in similar-quality institutions, measuring both achievement trajectories and peer-group composition stability over time.)
- Is the administrative decentralization of 6/8-year admissions (school-by-school criteria) a feature that reduces corruption/favoritism or a source of inequality; what is the cost in administrative bandwidth of centralizing admissions vs. the cost of standardizing criteria across 2000+ schools? (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Audit study measuring variance in actual admissions criteria and outcomes across high-demand vs. low-demand grammar schools; cost accounting of a hypothetical centralized admissions bureau (staffing, IT, training); international comparison of centralized vs. decentralized admissions costs and fraud rates.)
- What is the true long-run fiscal cost of a phase-out scenario (capital reallocation, possible teacher redundancy, transition funding) vs. the steady-state cost of maintaining dual tracks; has either been formally modelled? (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Detailed multipart fiscal impact modelling (capital, staffing, transition support, retraining) for three scenarios: status quo, gradual phase-out (5–10 years), accelerated abolition; sensitivity analysis on teacher retirement/attrition rates and building repurposing costs.)
