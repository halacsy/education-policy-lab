# Expert analysis: international_comparison

## Findings (evidence)
- Cross-country difference-in-differences studies consistently find that earlier tracking (before age 12–13) increases socio-economic achievement gaps without raising mean performance; meta-analysis of 21 PISA/TIMSS cycles across 75 countries confirms strong evidence of widened social gaps. [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006 (Economic Journal); van Elk et al. 2016 (RCIE); Educational Assessment, Evaluation and Accountability 2021 meta-analysis]
- Hungary's socio-economic achievement gap (19–25% of variance explained by ESCS) is among the OECD's highest, nearly double the average (12–14%); the gap in math between advantaged and disadvantaged students is cited as widest in the EU after Romania and Slovakia. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2022 Country Note Hungary; OECD PISA 2022 Vol. I (2023); ScienceDirect 2025 meta-comparison]
- Poland's 1999 reform delaying vocational tracking (from age 11 to 15) increased test scores by approximately one standard deviation for likely-vocational students, but OECD's 2011 re-evaluation found the underlying achievement gap persisted despite the reform, suggesting the gain was more from extended common instruction than from tracking-age delay alone. [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016 (Education Economics); OECD 2011 Poland reform evaluation]
- Poland reversed its 1999 tracking-delay reform in 2016, restoring earlier vocational selection despite research evidence of the reform's positive effects on test scores and labor-market outcomes—a direct example of research-supported reform being reversed for political reasons. [evidence: strong — PISA 2022 country reports; Liwiński 2020; Drucker et al. 2022 (cited in ResearchGate comparative)]
- Hungary's school segregation by socio-economic status is among the highest in the EU; free school choice (szabad iskolaválasztás), rather than residential segregation, is the primary driver of inter-school inequality in Hungarian towns. [evidence: strong — EU Education and Training Monitor 2025 Hungary; Kertesi & Kézdi 100-towns study; Hermann & Kisfalusi 2024 (forthcoming)]
- Horn's panel-data causal study finds that early selective tracks (kisgimnázium) provide higher value-added and boost tertiary-education continuation for higher-status students, but students remaining in general tracks lose relative to comprehensive alternatives; early selection increases inequality of opportunity. [evidence: moderate — Horn 2012 ('Early selection in Hungary: A possible cause of high educational inequality')]
- Access to 6/8-year grammar school admission examinations is unevenly distributed: elementary schools with disadvantaged-background pupils rarely offer exam-preparation support, and participation requires substantial family cultural, social, and financial capital investment. [evidence: moderate — Academia.edu 2018/2019 on_education-linked entry-process study]
- OECD explicitly identifies 'tracking students later' as a system feature associated with resilience and equity preservation in PISA 2022 analyses of high-performing, high-equity systems. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2022 Results Volume I (2023)]
- No Hungary-specific natural-experiment study (based on a policy change varying the legal age of entry into 6/8-year grammar school) has been found; the strongest Hungarian causal evidence is observational or matching-based, limiting direct within-country causal identification of tracking-age effects. [evidence: strong — Search audit (round 10, this memo)]
- Hungary's PISA results in mathematics and reading are among the lowest in the country's participation history (2022), and the top–bottom decile gap has not narrowed significantly from 2018–2022, indicating persistent within-cohort inequality. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2022 Country Note Hungary]
- The performance gap between children of graduates and non-graduates is 53 points in Hungary, compared to an EU average of 33 points, reflecting an intergenerational education-inequality marker beyond age-of-selection effects. [evidence: strong — EU Education and Training Monitor 2025, Hungary country report]
- Meta-analysis and natural-experiment evidence (Bavaria, Poland) show early tracking's negative effects are strongest for low-achieving students and students from disadvantaged backgrounds; high achievers show null or near-null effects, implying tracking preserves privilege rather than identifying talent. [evidence: strong — van Elk et al. 2016 (RCIE); Hanushek & Woessmann 2006; Piopiunik 2014 (Bavaria, cited in Springer 2021 meta-analysis)]

## Interpretation
Hungary combines the two strongest risk factors for education inequality: early selection at age 10–12 (via 8- and 6-year grammar schools) and a free school choice system that amplifies rather than mitigates socio-economic sorting. International evidence is unambiguous that early between-school tracking increases socio-economic gaps; Hungary's already-highest OECD inequality gradient (ESCS explains 19–25% of variance vs. 12–14% OECD average) and highest EU segregation suggest the current system is performing as the literature predicts. The mechanism is not mysterious: wealthier families (with higher cultural and financial capital) navigate admissions exams more effectively, and once selected into higher-status tracks, students face cumulative advantage (peer composition, teacher quality, school resources, tertiary access). The Poland reversal demonstrates that even policy-makers who observe positive evidence may retreat under political pressure. Hungary-specific causal studies (Horn; entry-process research) confirm the inequality-amplifying mechanism operates here. What is unknown is whether delaying selection from age 10–12 to age 14–15 would, in the Hungarian context, produce gains comparable to Poland's short-term test-score effects, or whether the underlying ESCS gradient would simply re-manifest at the later gatekeeping point—the OECD's 2011 Poland reassessment suggests the latter risk. The current system solves the problem of directing high-aptitude students into advanced instruction; it creates or amplifies the problem of frozen social mobility and high within-school peer-composition inequality; alternatives exist (later selection, comprehensive lower-secondary with ability-based curricula, targeted early intervention) but each carries fiscal, political, and implementation costs not yet fully quantified for Hungary.

## Assumptions
- That the international tracking-age evidence (Hanushek-Woessmann, van Elk, meta-analyses) is transferable to Hungary's institutional context; Hungary's specific grammar school admissions process, school-choice rules, and teacher-labor-market conditions may mediate or alter the typical tracking-inequality relationship. [assumption]
- That Hungary's high ESCS gradient (19–25% variance explained) persists across the entire age range, not just at the point of 10–12-year selection; if the gradient were concentrated only at entry to grammar school, delaying selection would have different effects than if it re-manifests at age 14–15. [assumption]
- That delaying selection without simultaneous measures to reduce residential/family-background segregation and to equalize teacher quality and resources across schools would not, by itself, eliminate the equality-of-opportunity problem; the Poland experience hints that extended common instruction may matter as much as the age shift. [assumption]
- That school-quality variation (value-added) within tracks is not so large as to offset selection effects; if low-track grammar schools deliver substantially higher value-added than general schools, the inequality harm from selection would be reduced. [assumption]
- That political feasibility constraints in Hungary are not so binding that evidence of inequality harm would motivate structural reform; Poland's reversal suggests that even positive evidence may not overcome political coalitions favoring early selection. [assumption]
- That the mechanisms of inequality (parental cultural/financial capital investment in admissions exam preparation, peer-composition effects, tracking-lock-in) are stable and not substantially mitigated by compensatory policies (gifted-and-talented programs, additional tutorial funding for disadvantaged students in grammar schools). [assumption]
- That no significant measurement error or attenuation bias exists in the observational/matching-based Hungarian causal studies (Horn, entry-process research); if unobserved confounders (e.g., unmeasured student motivation or family support) are large, the true selection effect may differ from reported estimates. [assumption]
- That Hungary's school-choice system and the legal framework for admissions exams will not change substantially during the implementation of any tracking-delay reform; if school choice is simultaneously restricted or exam rules are modified, the effects of age-of-selection change would be confounded. [assumption]
- That costs (fiscal, political, compliance) of alternative approaches (comprehensive lower-secondary plus ability-based in-school differentiation, later selection at 14–15, intensive early intervention to close parental-capital gaps) have been adequately researched in the Hungarian context; they may be substantially under- or over-estimated in the current policy debate. [assumption]
- That 'grammar school quality' (academic rigor, teacher credentials, resource endowment, tertiary placement) genuinely differs across the 6/8-year tracks and general schools at a scale that explains parental demand for selection; if quality differences are smaller than perceived, the selection mechanism would be partially decoupled from academic effectiveness. [assumption]

## Position
Early selection into 6/8-year grammar schools (at age 10–12) amplifies socio-economic achievement inequality in Hungary beyond the baseline set by family background, as indicated by international tracking-age evidence and Hungary-specific observational studies of segregation and selection mechanism, and delaying selection to age 14–15 (with intact comprehensive lower-secondary instruction and no weakening of school-choice amplification) would likely reduce this inequality measurably, though the size of the effect remains uncertain because no Hungarian natural experiment has tested the policy change.

## Uncertainties
- What is the causal effect of delaying selection from age 10–12 to age 14–15 specifically in Hungary's institutional context (school-choice system, school-maintainer structure, teacher labor market, tertiary admissions linkages)? (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A natural experiment (e.g., staggered implementation of a delayed-selection reform across regions or cohorts) combined with administrative data (matched student records, tertiary placement, labor-market earnings) to estimate within-Hungary difference-in-differences; absent this, credible cross-country comparison studies modeling the specific institutional features of the Hungarian system.)
- To what extent would the underlying socio-economic gradient in achievement re-manifest at the age 14–15 gatekeeping point (or later) if selection were delayed, such that delayed selection would not durably reduce inequality? (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Longitudinal data tracking student achievement, family characteristics, and school transitions across multiple age points (e.g., age 10, 14, 15, 16) to estimate whether ESCS-gradients are age-dependent or persistent; OECD's 2011 re-evaluation of Poland hints at this but does not resolve it for Hungary.)
