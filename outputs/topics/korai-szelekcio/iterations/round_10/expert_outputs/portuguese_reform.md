# Expert analysis: portuguese_reform

## Findings (evidence)
- Early tracking in international cross-country studies (26-country difference-in-differences) increases educational inequality measured by socioeconomic achievement gaps, with unclear or slightly negative effects on system-wide mean performance. [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Wößmann 2006 NBER/Economic Journal; replicated in 2021 meta-analysis (Lange et al., Springer) and 2024 review (de Gruyter Brill)]
- OECD cross-national analysis confirms that age of first selection correlates with inequity in performance but not with mean performance level across systems. [evidence: strong — OECD 2013, 2020a education indicators; confirmed in 2023 ScienceDirect policy review]
- Socioeconomic achievement gaps already exist at age 10 before formal academic selection, with approximately two-thirds of the age-15 gap and over half of the age-25–29 gap already visible at age 10 across 11 OECD countries. [evidence: strong — OECD Equity in Education 2013]
- Hungary has the highest level of socioeconomic segregation of students into different schools in the EU, with a 53-point parental-education achievement gap compared to the EU average of 33 points, as of 2022 PISA. [evidence: strong — EU Education and Training Monitor 2022 country report for Hungary]
- Hungarian administrative-data causal analysis (matching design) shows that attending a high-poverty school in grade 8 is significantly negatively associated with reading scores and later secondary-education attainment, suggesting school composition operates as a causal mechanism, not merely correlation. [evidence: strong — Hermann & Kisfalusi 2023, HUN-REN administrative-data study]
- Hungary's gimnázium track (general secondary school) achieves world-class performance equivalent to top PISA systems such as Switzerland, Japan, or Korea, coexisting with system-wide segregation and inequality. [evidence: moderate — World Bank PISA analysis of Hungary; publicly available but non-peer-reviewed comparative assessment]
- Portugal abolished academic selection at age 10–12 and adopted comprehensive basic education to grade 9, but substituted grade retention as its sorting mechanism, with 26.6–31.2% of 15-year-olds having been retained at least once by 2015–2018. [evidence: strong — Portugal PISA 2015, 2018; cited in EPAA meta-analysis]
- Meta-analyses of grade retention show null or slightly negative effects on academic competencies, indicating that retention-based sorting is not pedagogically superior to academic-track selection. [evidence: strong — EPAA systematic review, meta-analytic synthesis]
- Portugal's TEIP targeted-support program (15+ years, ~€100 million annually) shows mixed results: early school-leaving rates fell faster in TEIP schools than non-TEIP public schools 2007–2011, but achievement gaps in mathematics and Portuguese widened, and TEIP schools remain above national averages for retention and dropout. [evidence: moderate — MDPI 15-year evaluation 2020; Frontiers in Education follow-up 2026 using DGEEC administrative data]
- Portugal's socioeconomic equity in education worsened from 2015 to 2022 despite its non-selective comprehensive-school structure, with the share of low-SES students performing well falling from 21.0% (2015) to 16.0% (2022), and mathematics underachievement rising from 39.4% to 46.9%. [evidence: strong — EU Education and Training Monitor 2025 country report for Portugal]
- Hungary's free school-choice system (szabad iskolaválasztás) permits schools complete discretion to hand-pick their students in primary education, creating segregation independent of formal academic tracking. [evidence: moderate — Academia.edu historical analysis of Hungarian school-choice system; structural feature confirmed but older documentation]
- Portugal's 2006–2015 PISA improvement (within-country trend without lowering selection age) occurred via curriculum standards alignment, school clustering reform, TEIP targeted support, and retention-rate reduction. [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020 policy analysis]
- Early academic selection at age 10–12 and school-choice segregation are structurally separable policy levers: the former is a sorting mechanism by explicit track assignment, the latter operates via admission discretion and catchment-area choice independent of tracking's existence. [evidence: moderate — Synthesis from Hanushek & Wößmann 2006 and Hermann & Kisfalusi 2023; cross-referenced with Hungary school-choice literature]
- Removing early academic tracking without simultaneously addressing retention rates, school-choice discretion, or compensatory-resource distribution may relocate rather than resolve the sorting mechanism. [evidence: moderate — Inference from Portugal case (comprehensive model + high retention = shifted sorting) and Hungarian school-choice architecture]
- International evidence does not distinguish whether early-selection harm operates via earlier age of assignment, via the *existence* of permanent tracks, or via *segregation mechanisms* (admissions discretion, catchment), making optimal alternative designs empirically underdetermined. [evidence: contested — Synthesis of Hanushek & Wößmann; OECD-indicator studies; acknowledged in methodological critiques of tracking literature]

## Interpretation
The evidence converges that early selection (at age 10–12 into 6/8-year gimnázium tracks) increases inequality measured by socioeconomic achievement gaps without raising or raising mean performance. Hungary exemplifies the high-inequality, high-elite-performance tail of this distribution, with the EU's strongest segregation and largest parental-education gap. This is not primarily because selection *creates* the gaps—two-thirds of the age-15 achievement gap and over half of the later-life gap are already visible at age 10, suggesting that sorting at 10–12 locks in pre-existing disparities. However, Portugal's alternative (comprehensive basic school + no age-10 selection) is not a template for painless equity: it substitutes retention-based sorting (equally harmful or harmful-plus) and has *worsened* its own equity trends since 2015 despite the non-selective structure. This implies that the choice between early-academic-track selection and early retention/school-choice segregation is not a choice between equity and inequality, but between *different sorting mechanisms with different trade-offs*. The strongest independent lever identified is not the track's age or existence, but the admissions-discretion and school-choice architecture that allows hand-picking and creates de facto segregation parallel to formal tracking. Portugal's growth in PISA without changing selection age (2006–2015) via curriculum alignment, school clustering, TEIP support, and retention reduction suggests that *how schools are resourced and organized*, not *whether early tracking exists*, may be the primary controllable determinant of both equity and efficacy.

## Assumptions
- The research synthesized (primarily North American and Western European quasi-experimental designs) is generalizable to the Hungarian institutional context (Klebelsberg centre system, school-maintainer mix, selective admissions architecture). [assumption]
- "Inequality" measured by socioeconomic achievement gaps at standardized-test assessment (PISA, national exams) is a sufficient proxy for the broader concept of equity in educational outcomes and life chances. [assumption]
- The absence of documented causal evidence for a claim (e.g., whether abolishing early selection *alone* improves equity without structural changes elsewhere) does not imply the claim is false; it implies it is empirically undetermined and therefore subject to expert disagreement. [assumption]
- School-composition effects observed in administrative-data studies (Hermann & Kisfalusi) are not entirely mediated by selection; some represent true peer or resource-allocation causal effects that would persist even under a comprehensive-school regime. [assumption]
- The timing (2006–2015 for Portugal, 2022 for Hungary PISA) and geopolitical differences (post-crisis EU austerity pressures, COVID-19 impacts not fully captured) do not fundamentally alter the comparability of Portugal's and Hungary's structural choice set. [assumption]

## Position
Early selection at age 10–12 into grammar-school tracks increases socioeconomic inequality without raising system-wide mean performance in Hungary's context, but removing it without simultaneously reforming school-choice admissions discretion, retention rates, and within-sector resource allocation would likely relocate rather than eliminate the sorting mechanism.

## Uncertainties
- Whether the harm from early selection operates primarily via (a) the age at which assignment occurs, (b) the permanence of track assignment, or (c) the segregation mechanisms (admissions discretion, catchment-area choice) that coexist with or without formal selection, remains empirically undetermined and is not resolved by available international evidence. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Quasi-experimental designs or natural experiments isolating the effect of selection-age change (e.g., a regional or cohort-level phase-out of age-10 entry with selection-mechanism controls held constant) or randomized assignment of school-choice discretion vs. centralized-admissions regimes in matched samples.)
