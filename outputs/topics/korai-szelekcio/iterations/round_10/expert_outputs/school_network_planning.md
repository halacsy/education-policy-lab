# Expert analysis: school_network_planning

## Findings (evidence)
- The 6- and 8-year grammar school (gimnázium) network comprises approximately 25,962 enrolled students in 2024/25, with no expansion of places despite tenfold oversubscription at elite schools (e.g., 369 applicants for 38 places at ELTE Gyakorló's 8-year track). [evidence: strong — Eurydice/KSH 2024/25; eduline.hu Feb 2026]
- Cohort size is shrinking due to a 20% decline in births over the last four years, creating a structural surplus of 17,000 fewer one-year-olds than four-year-olds, equivalent to 600–800 class groups lost. [evidence: strong — Válasz Online Oct 2025; KSH demographic data]
- Feeder primary schools experience 20–30% class-size reduction by 7th grade as students depart for grammar school, leaving behind a bimodal residual population that typically does not attempt the entrance examination. [evidence: moderate — eduline.hu (citing 2019 research data); school-network planning documentation]
- Government policy has held capacity flat in 6/8-year tracks while steering students toward vocational training (technikum), a structural-fiscal choice away from selective grammar school expansion. [evidence: moderate — eduline.hu; Education Ministry policy direction documents]
- Urban and suburban zones face rising child-to-teacher ratios (up to 24:1 in some areas) while disadvantaged rural peripheries see concentration of disadvantaged students (up to 94% in certain settlements), creating geographic capacity asymmetry. [evidence: moderate — ResearchGate study (2025); regional education monitoring data]
- The performance gap between selected 8-year grammar students and the national average in 2024 competency assessment is approximately 200 points in mathematics, with Budapest 8-year students scoring ~100 points above the Budapest average, driving parental demand pressure. [evidence: strong — National competency assessment (központi írásbeli); eduline.hu]
- Admission thresholds to elite grammar schools are high and rising: successful oral-interview candidates scored 73–75 points, with government-recommended minimum of 70 points on the written examination for 6/8-year tracks. [evidence: strong — Central written examination statistics; penzcentrum.hu Jan 2025]
- Free school choice in Hungary amplifies socioeconomic sorting: higher-status parents move children to better-performing schools when local options are poor, while lower-status parents have limited capacity to do so, concentrating disadvantage. [evidence: strong — MTAK Kertesi–Kézdi (2004–2015) research archive; EPA repository]
- The January 2025 Hungarian-language entrance examination for 6-year grammar schools was the most difficult in 11 years according to participating schools' statistical analysis, taken by over 68,000 students nationwide. [evidence: moderate — Alternatív Közgazdasági Gimnázium statistics; Telex Jan 2025]
- Hungary's early school-leaving rate is 58.7% among Roma and 9.3% among non-Roma (2023), with higher rates in least-developed regions and villages, indicating severe regional and ethnic disparity independent of selection policy. [evidence: strong — EU Education and Training Monitor 2025; KSH early school-leaving statistics]
- School maintainers are increasingly fragmented: since 2013, most state schools are run by state school district centres (tankerületi központ), while churches and foundations maintain a growing share, complicating uniform structural mandates. [evidence: strong — Public Education Act amendments; maintainer statistics (tankerulet data)]
- School closures and consolidations are proceeding administratively through tankerület (state school district centre) decisions rather than as announced central policy, often without organized local resistance due to falling child numbers and municipal fiscal strain. [evidence: moderate — Válasz Online ministerial statements Oct 2025; municipal financial reporting]
- Current performance disparity within Hungary ranks among the starkest in the EU due to socioeconomic background, and disadvantaged schools face disproportionate teacher shortages, amplifying the sorting effect of early selection. [evidence: strong — EU Education and Training Monitor 2025; Hungarian teacher-shortage data]
- Approximately 7,000–8,000 children enter 6- and 8-year grammar schools annually in recent years, reflecting the track's rising popularity despite capacity constraints. [evidence: moderate — budapest.imami.hu; KSH enrolment data]

## Interpretation
Hungary's 6/8-year grammar school network operates as a high-demand, capacity-constrained system superimposed on a shrinking overall student population. The combination of birth-rate collapse (20% in four years), rigid capacity (no expansion despite tenfold oversubscription at elite schools), and administrative consolidation driven by fiscal stress creates a three-layer challenge: (1) structural – the network must shrink overall while early selection maintains bimodal class sizes in feeder primaries; (2) equity – free school choice amplifies socioeconomic sorting, and geography now stratifies access (urban capacity shortage, rural disadvantage concentration); (3) political – closures proceed silently via tankerület decisions rather than through debate about selection's rationale. The rising difficulty of entrance examinations and high performance gaps between selected and non-selected students indicate the system is operating at near-maximum selectivity for a frozen pool of places. Any alternative to early selection must be designed within a demographic-contraction context, not a growth scenario.

## Assumptions
- The 2024/25 network size and enrolment figures are stable proxies for current effective network capacity (i.e., no uncount unoficial private expansion and no shadow market in admissions). [assumption]
- The performance gap measured in the 2024 competency assessment reflects both selection (compositional effect) and school quality; without causal separation, the demand pressure driven by it conflates the two sources. [assumption]
- The administrative consolidation proceeding via tankerület decisions will continue at roughly current pace regardless of selection policy, driven by demographic collapse and municipal fiscal capacity. [assumption]
- The 'under one hour public transport' accessibility standard for grammar schools remains a design goal but is not systematically monitored or enforced as a binding constraint in network-planning decisions. [assumption]

## Position
Under demographic contraction, the 6/8-year grammar school network's capacity constraint is not primarily a selection-policy problem but a network-consolidation problem that selection policy amplifies by concentrating demand at elite schools and leaving shrinking feeder primaries economically unviable.

## Uncertainties
- No recent (2024–2026) quantitative data on actual commuting times and distances for 6/8-year grammar school entrants, versus the stated 'under one hour' accessibility policy aim, limits verification of how network consolidation would affect geographic equity. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Central tracking of commuting patterns via school transport cards (bérlet) or mobile-phone anonymized location data for the 6/8 cohort; comparison across settlement types (urban, suburban, rural) and socioeconomic quartiles.)
- Cost-per-pupil and lifecycle cost data comparing 6/8-year grammar tracks to 4-year grammar school and vocational school alternatives in the same network, adjusted for intake quality and regional context, are not publicly available in comparable form. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Transparent cost accounting per maintainer type (state, church, foundation) for each track over a 3-year rolling window, published by the Klebelsberg centres, enabling scenario-based fiscal impact modelling.)
- No recent (post-2020) quantitative study updates the causal selection-effect estimates from Kertesi–Kézdi (2004–2015) for current conditions, leaving unclear whether the performance gaps reflect mostly selection or early instruction quality differences. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: A quasi-experimental or matched-cohort study comparing value-added trajectories of students admitted vs. not admitted to 6/8 tracks, controlling for prior test scores and background, using 2018–2025 administrative data (if available under GDPR); sensitivity check on exam-difficulty calibration.)
- The political feasibility and implementation cost of any alternative to early selection (e.g., phased phase-out, comprehensive lower-secondary model, later-entry grammar school) depend on unstated negotiation positions of maintainers (churches, foundations, state sector unions), which are not documented in accessible form. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Structured stakeholder interviews (maintainers, teacher unions, parent organisations, municipal leadership) on explicit 'red lines' and cost-sharing model preferences in three selection-policy scenarios; publication of preliminary positions.)
- The long-term student demand for 6/8-year grammar places under continued demographic shrinkage (and whether 'prestige' demand will hold or collapse as cohort size halves) is unknowable without assumptions about parental value-weighting of academic prestige vs. proximity. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Cohort-tracing survey (2025–2030) of applicant and non-applicant families in shrinking and growing municipalities to measure stated preferences for grammar-school access vs. local proximity and community stability; sensitivity analysis of demand elasticity to seat reductions.)
