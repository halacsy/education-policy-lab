# Policy brief — Early academic selection and the 6/8-year gimnázium

This brief synthesises expert evidence and a structured stakeholder stress test on early academic selection (age 10-12) in Hungary's 6/8-year gimnázium system. It sets out what is well established, what remains contested among experts and among represented interests, and five policy alternatives — none of which is recommended as 'the' answer. Every claim below is tagged by kind ([fact]/[estimate]/[assumption]/[value]) and, for factual claims, by evidence strength. Disagreement and minority positions are preserved deliberately: they are the substance the eventual political decision must resolve, not noise to be smoothed away.

## Scenario key (full detail: scenarios.en.md)
- S1 — S1 — Admissions and intake-equity regulation: keep the 6/8-year structure but regulate the admission mechanism (SES-aware proxies, coaching-resistant exams, uniform thresholds). Full detail in scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md.
- S2 — S2 — Supply-side contraction: shrink the state-sector share of selective places via thresholds/capacity caps, leaving admission rules within remaining places unchanged. Full detail in scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md.
- S3 — S3 — Structural delay of the selection age (Finnish/Polish model): raise the tracking age toward 14+, converting 6/8-year entry into a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase, with concurrent anti-sorting rules. Full detail in scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md.
- S4 — S4 — Non-structural compensatory reform (Portugal-style): curriculum clarity, TEIP-style targeted funding, retention reduction and school-quality levelling, without changing the selection age. Full detail in scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md.
- S5 — S5 — Demographically-driven passive contraction: the no-new-policy baseline in which shrinking birth cohorts force network consolidation regardless of any active reform. Full detail in scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md.

## What we know
- Early selection into 6/8-year gimnázium tracks admits roughly 8-12% of each age cohort at age 10-12 via central written examinations, and entrants are strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Hungary exhibits among the highest socio-economic achievement-gap and school-segregation indicators in the OECD/EU (ESCS explains 19-25% of PISA variance vs. 12-14% OECD average; a 53-point parental-education achievement gap vs. a 33-point EU average). [fact] (evidence: strong)
- International cross-country meta-analyses (21-75 countries) consistently show that earlier academic tracking increases socio-economic achievement gaps without raising system-wide mean performance. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Poland's 1999 tracking-delay reform produced measurable equity and learning gains but was reversed in 2017 for explicitly political/values-based reasons rather than on evidentiary grounds. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Private admission-preparation costs (tutoring, prep courses) are substantial and unequally distributed, functioning as a de facto entry fee despite formally free tuition. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Hungary's annual birth cohort has declined by roughly 30-35% since the early 1990s, mechanically shrinking demand for all secondary tracks and forcing school-network consolidation independent of selection-policy design. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Church and foundation school maintainers, whose enrolment share has grown substantially since 2010 and who can freely select students unlike catchment-bound state schools, complicate any uniform structural reform of early selection. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- As of mid-2026, structural reform of early selection is a live but politically fragile issue: the education minister's critical remarks triggered strong opposition mobilization framing reform as coercive imposition, and the government has since retreated to a consultative rather than legislative stance. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- The measured value-added premium for selected students is predominantly attributable to selection/composition effects (cream-skimming, peer effects) rather than distinctive pedagogy, according to value-added panel studies and the current Education Minister's own competence-test decomposition. [fact] (evidence: moderate)

## What we consider likely
- SES-aware admission proxies and coaching-resistant exam design would likely reduce, but not eliminate, the correlation between preparation investment and admission success at the point of entry. [estimate]
- Shrinking state-sector selective capacity alone (S2) would likely be substantially offset by expanded church/foundation intake, given their roughly threefold enrolment-share growth since 2010, meaning net segregation reduction is unlikely without a parallel non-state constraint. [estimate]
- A structural delay of the selection age (S3) would likely reproduce Poland's or Finland's equity gains in Hungary only if anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment are enacted and enforced concurrently with the age change, not afterward. [estimate]
- Non-structural compensatory reform (S4) would likely leave low-SES high-performing pupils without improved access to selective tracks, since compensatory funding to their home schools does not remove the age-10-12 admission gate itself. [estimate]
- Passive demographic consolidation (S5), absent explicit equity safeguards, would likely concentrate rather than disperse selective-track segregation, since consolidation decisions are already proceeding through active administrative levers (tankerület decisions, score thresholds) that shape distributional outcomes. [estimate]

## Where experts disagree
### Whether early selection itself is the primary driver of inequality, or whether school choice/catchment/maintainer architecture is the deeper cause
- **international_comparison, finnish_reform, polish_reform, educational_psychology**: Early selection at age 10-12 is itself a structural driver of socio-economic segregation and achievement-gap widening, distinct from and additive to school-choice effects; international tracking-age evidence shows delaying selection reduces inequality. [value] Why: Grounded in cross-country difference-in-differences and natural-experiment causal designs (21-cycle PISA/TIMSS meta-analysis, Poland's causally-identified 0.25 SD gain) that isolate age-of-tracking as a treatment variable.
- **hungarian_education_system, portuguese_reform, education_finance** (minority): Free school choice and catchment-area sorting are structurally separable from formal tracking and would continue to drive segregation even if early selection were abolished; removing selection alone would relocate rather than resolve the sorting mechanism. [value] Why: Grounded in observational studies of Hungarian school-choice behavior (Kertesi-Kézdi) and the Portuguese counterfactual case, where abolishing academic tracking without addressing admissions discretion and retention practices left equity trends worsening.
### Whether the ~10% value-added premium for selected students reflects genuine pedagogical quality or composition/peer effects
- **hungarian_education_system, equity_and_social_mobility, educational_psychology, education_finance**: The measured value-added premium is predominantly attributable to selection/composition effects rather than distinctive pedagogy, and the Ministry's own competence-test analysis finds no measurable value-added once prior ability and family background are controlled. [value] Why: Grounded in value-added panel studies (Horn; HÉTFA 1.1M-student panel) and the Ministry's own competence-test decomposition holding initial ability and SES constant.
- **hungarian_education_system** (minority): The mechanism behind the 10% value-added gain remains genuinely unresolved — it could reflect peer effects, teacher assignment, or pedagogical structure, and treating it as settled composition-only would overstate certainty. [value] Why: Grounded directly in the primary researcher's own stated uncertainty (Horn, 2026 podcast) about decomposing the value-added figure, distinct from the Ministry's more definitive framing.
### Whether demographic collapse or selection-policy design is now the dominant force shaping the school network's future
- **demography, school_network_planning**: Cohort collapse (30-35% birth decline since the 1990s) is now the dominant structural force reshaping the school network through 2040, forcing consolidation regardless of selection-policy choice. [value] Why: Grounded in demographic-yearbook and administrative enrollment data showing mechanical, policy-independent cohort shrinkage.
- **equity_and_social_mobility, legal_and_governance, political_feasibility** (minority): The selection mechanism's equity and political-legal costs are independent problems requiring direct policy attention regardless of demographic trends, and consolidation without selection reform risks entrenching rather than resolving segregation. [value] Why: Grounded in legal-statutory analysis (maintainer veto structures, felmenő rendszer constraints) and equity-focused causal evidence (segregation indices, Roma school concentration).
### Whether structural reform of early selection is currently feasible, or whether legal, administrative, and political constraints make near-term abolition unrealistic
- **education_finance, hungarian_education_system**: Structural alternatives are financially and administratively feasible at moderate transition cost, and demographic decline has eroded the original efficiency rationale for early selection, making the system increasingly hard to justify. [value] Why: Grounded in cost-function analysis of the existing 880-school network suggesting steady-state costs differ modestly by track type and transition costs are the dominant financial variable.
- **legal_and_governance, implementation_planning, political_feasibility** (minority): Near-term structural abolition is legally and administratively unrealistic: the felmenő rendszer principle forces an 8-year phase-out, church-maintainer autonomy creates a political-legal veto point, the aging teaching workforce has limited redeployment capacity, and the minister has already retreated to a consultative stance under political pressure. [value] Why: Grounded in statutory text analysis combined with real-time political-event tracking (minister's June 2026 retreat, opposition mobilization, workforce demographic data).
### Whether the psychological costs of early selection constitute a distinct harm mechanism beyond economic/demographic and segregation arguments
- **educational_psychology** (minority): Beyond composition and segregation effects, early selection imposes a distinct psychological cost: the robustly-replicated big-fish-little-pond effect lowers academic self-concept among selected pupils, while competitive ranking pushes adolescents toward performance-avoidance rather than mastery orientation. [value] Why: Grounded in a distinct evidence base from educational psychology (Marsh & Hau's 41-country BFLPE replication, Deci & Ryan self-determination theory, Dweck/Elliot goal-orientation literature).

## What we don't know
- Whether SES-aware admission proxies would durably reduce family-capital-driven sorting or whether advantaged families would simply redirect investment toward new preparation or relocation strategies once the exam changes. [fact]
- Whether church and foundation maintainers can be brought into a uniform admission-equity or capacity regime without a specified legal-political mechanism, given their documented autonomy guarantees and growing enrolment share. [fact]
- Whether anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment can be made simultaneously enforceable with a structural delay of the tracking age, or whether enactment will lag and allow sorting to migrate before enforcement catches up. [fact]
- Whether Hungary's teacher workforce, given its current age structure and the concurrent 2024-2031 salary reform, can realistically absorb a comprehensive lower-secondary transition within a 2-9 year timeline. [fact]
- Whether TEIP-style targeted funding can be made durable against fiscal consolidation pressure in Hungary's specific budget environment, or whether it will be cut in the first consolidation round as historical patterns for targeted lines suggest. [fact]
- Whether demographically-driven consolidation, absent explicit equity safeguards, will concentrate or disperse selective-track segregation across regions, given documented extreme regional divergence in birth-rate decline. [fact]
- Whether the observed Polish 1999 test-score and equity gains would replicate in Hungary's specific institutional context (school-choice system, maintainer plurality, teacher labor market), since no Hungarian natural experiment has directly tested a tracking-age delay. [fact]
- Whether the exact magnitude and mechanism of the ~10% value-added premium (pedagogy vs. peer effects vs. teacher assignment) can be conclusively decomposed, given that even the primary researcher expresses unresolved uncertainty. [fact]

## What could be done
- **S1 — Regulate the admission mechanism, keep the track structure**: Introduce SES-aware admission proxies, coaching-resistant exam design, and uniform score-threshold rules within the existing 6/8-year gimnázium structure, aiming to reduce family-capital-driven sorting at the entry point without touching track structure or selection age.
- **S2 — Shrink the state-sector selective footprint**: Reduce the state-sector share of 6/8-year places via score thresholds, capacity caps, or demographically-driven consolidation, leaving admission criteria within remaining places largely unchanged.
- **S3 — Delay the tracking age structurally (Finnish/Polish model)**: Raise the statutory tracking age toward 14+, phasing out or converting 6/8-year entry points into a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase, paired with explicit, concurrently-enforced anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment.
- **S4 — Non-structural compensatory reform (Portugal-style)**: Pursue equity and achievement gains through curriculum clarity, TEIP-style targeted funding for disadvantaged schools, retention reduction, and school-quality levelling, without changing the 10-12 selection age or track structure.
- **S5 — Let demographic contraction proceed passively (status-quo baseline)**: Serve as the no-new-policy baseline in which shrinking birth cohorts force network consolidation and a natural decline in the selective track's absolute size and share, regardless of any active equity or structural intervention.

## What each option costs
- **S1**: Bounded equity gain: church/foundation maintainers (25-30% of enrolment) are not bound by decree-level rules and can absorb displaced advantaged demand, so the gain is confined to the state-sector admission-scoring mechanism, not a system-wide segregation reduction. [estimate]
- **S1**: New administrative and psychometric workload (exam-item development, SES-proxy verification, multi-maintainer compliance monitoring) falls on the system without a specified funded staffing allocation, risking teacher burden or degraded exam quality if uncompensated. [assumption]
- **S1**: Legal fragility: a decree-level reform can be reversed by a subsequent minister without primary legislation, making its durability contingent on political continuity, while elite gimnázium directors have demonstrated coordinated resistance capacity. [fact]
- **S2**: High risk of displacement, not reduction: church/foundation maintainers' roughly threefold enrolment growth since 2010 suggests they are structurally positioned to absorb displaced demand, meaning state-sector contraction alone likely relocates rather than reduces net selectivity, at the cost of state-school families' proximity/affordability. [estimate]
- **S2**: Unquantified teacher redundancy/redeployment risk from capacity caps, with no published staffing-transition plan, shifting adjustment cost onto the teaching workforce. [assumption]
- **S2**: Equity-optics backlash risk: state-sector-only contraction may be perceived as unilaterally disadvantaging state-school families relative to those with access to unconstrained church/foundation alternatives. [value]
- **S3**: Highest transition burden: an 8-year statutory phase-out (felmenő rendszer), multi-year teacher retraining and reallocation layered onto an aging workforce (half aged 50+, only 7.2% under 30) mid-way through an unrelated 2024-2031 salary reform, plus facility conversion costs, all currently unbudgeted. [fact]
- **S3**: Conditional equity payoff, not automatic: the scenario's central equity claim depends entirely on anti-sorting rules for choice and catchment being legislated and enforced concurrently — absent this, Poland's 2017-style erosion risk applies, and church-maintained schools may resist conversion without a compulsion mechanism. [estimate]
- **S3**: Highest political reversal risk of any scenario: Poland's 2017 reversal of its own evidence-based reform shows structural change is not durable against a change of government, and no cardinal-law entrenchment mechanism is specified here. [fact]
- **S4**: Lowest equity ceiling among structural-adjacent options: leaves the age-10-12 admission gate and the school-choice/catchment segregation channel fully intact, so low-SES high-performing pupils gain no structural protection against being screened out, even as their home schools receive compensatory resources. [estimate]
- **S4**: Fiscal durability risk: targeted funding lines are historically first-line cuts under fiscal consolidation pressure (Hungary's projected 6.2% deficit and rising debt), and cardinal-law protection, even if secured, remains subject to future renegotiation. [estimate]
- **S5**: Zero near-term political-implementation cost, but a governance-accountability cost: no coordinated national plan exists despite consolidation being 'nearly certain' within 5-10 years, and unmanaged consolidation is documented as likely to concentrate rather than disperse selective-track segregation absent equity safeguards. [estimate]
- **S5**: Extreme regional divergence in birth-decline rates (some districts down to 55-67 annual births) means a uniform national baseline produces very different local outcomes, risking politically salient regional inequity without any mechanism to address it. [fact]

## What research could resolve
- A discontinuity or matched-cohort analysis of admission outcomes and preparation-spending patterns before and after a pilot coaching-resistant exam redesign, tracked over multiple admission cycles, would clarify whether S1's equity gain is durable or displaced.
- A legal opinion on the scope for mandating admission-rule or capacity compliance among non-state maintainers, plus publication of the underlying church-state funding and autonomy accords, would resolve whether S1/S2's core displacement-prevention lever is legally available at all.
- A multi-year tracking study of enrolment shifts across all maintainer types following a state-sector capacity policy change, using disaggregated KSH data, would show whether S2 produces a net reduction in segregation or merely relocates it.
- A detailed teacher-workforce feasibility study modelling retirement/attrition rates, retraining capacity, and salary-reform bandwidth overlap, published before legislative enactment, would resolve whether S3's 2-9 year timeline is realistic.
- A staggered, regional natural experiment or pilot phase-in of delayed tracking in a subset of Hungarian districts, with matched administrative-data outcome tracking, would test whether Poland's gains would replicate in Hungary's specific institutional context.
- A longitudinal subgroup analysis tracking low-SES high-performing pupils' access to selective tracks and achievement trajectories before and after targeted-funding implementation, compared against a matched cohort under S1-style admission-equity reform, would resolve S4's key equity uncertainty.
- A published multi-year budget commitment with cardinal-law or equivalent statutory protection, tracked against actual disbursement through at least one fiscal-consolidation cycle, would test whether S4's targeted funding is durable.
- Detailed school-by-school enrollment forecasts and feasibility studies of mixed school models in low-density regions, plus segregation-index tracking before, during, and after consolidation, would clarify whether S5's passive baseline concentrates or disperses segregation.

## What people must decide
- Whether the priority is preserving parental school choice and maintainer autonomy (church/foundation/private pluralism), or achieving system-wide equity even where this constrains that autonomy — this is a value choice, not something evidence alone can settle. [value]
- Whether it is politically and socially acceptable to bind church and foundation maintainers into a uniform admission-equity or capacity regime through negotiation, statutory amendment, or compulsion — and if negotiation fails, whether the reform proceeds state-sector-only or not at all. [value]
- Whether the political system is willing to accept a multi-year, potentially cross-electoral-cycle structural transition (S3) with reversal risk (as in Poland 2017), or prefers faster, lower-risk but lower-ceiling interventions (S1, S4). [value]
- Whether disadvantaged pupils' access to the selective track itself is treated as a right requiring structural protection, or whether compensatory resourcing of their existing schools is judged an adequate substitute — a value choice about what equity requires. [value]
- Whether the teaching workforce's capacity constraints (aging, concurrent salary reform) should determine the sequencing and pace of any structural reform, even if this delays equity gains by years, or whether reform should proceed on its own timeline with workforce strain accepted as a cost. [value]
- Whether to treat passive demographic contraction (S5) as an acceptable default in the absence of political consensus on active reform, accepting its documented risk of concentrating rather than dispersing segregation, or whether inaction itself must be actively governed. [value]

## What to verify with real stakeholders
- A1 (Coaching-resistant exam design and SES-aware admission proxies can reduce family-capital advantage, but only if enforcement covers church and foundation maintainers too, otherwise displaced advantaged demand simply relocates and the equity gain becomes cosmetic.): irreducible_tradeoff — Extending equivalent binding rules to church/foundation maintainers is legally and politically constrained by autonomy guarantees (see A2); absent that extension, S1's equity gain is structurally bounded — this is a genuine trade-off between reform reach and maintainer autonomy, not a design flaw that a better decree could fix.
- A2 (Imposing uniform admission-equity thresholds on church and foundation maintainers without their negotiated consent would breach constitutionally and contractually protected maintainer autonomy and subsidiarity.): value_conflict — This is a genuine collision between equity as a system-wide goal and subsidiarity/institutional-autonomy as a constitutionally protected value; no technical redesign resolves which value takes precedence — only negotiation or a political-legal decision can.
- A3 (New coaching-resistant exam design, SES-proxy verification and multi-maintainer compliance monitoring create unfunded administrative and psychometric workload for teachers, with no cost category accounting for their time or training.): policy_design_fixable — This is addressable by explicit design choices — centralizing item-writing/verification in a dedicated exam board or compliance unit and budgeting funded, non-teaching hours — rather than an irreducible constraint; the record currently lacks this specification, which is a fixable gap.
- A4 (Shrinking only state-sector selective capacity while church and foundation maintainers can freely expand does not reduce selection pressure but merely relocates it, since their enrolment share has roughly tripled since 2010 with no legal mechanism to constrain it.): needs_more_info — Whether displacement occurs at a comparable scale to the state-sector contraction is empirically contested and not directly measured (as multiple reciprocity responses note); a pilot or legal-mechanism study is needed before S2's core equity claim can be assessed, not a policy redesign or value trade-off alone.
- A5 (Capacity caps in state-maintained selective schools carry unquantified teacher redundancy and redeployment risk, with no published staffing-transition plan to protect affected teachers.): policy_design_fixable — A published redeployment guarantee — no forced job loss, retraining support, seniority protection — is a concrete design addition that multiple stakeholders name as sufficient to shift their stance; this is a gap in the current design, not an inherent feature of capacity caps.
- A6 (Reducing state-sector selective capacity restricts families' predictable local schooling options and pushes them toward longer commutes or costlier private alternatives without actually reducing selection.): irreducible_tradeoff — If displacement toward church/foundation/private schools indeed occurs (per A4's unresolved empirics), families' access and equity goals pull in opposite directions under S2's design; resolving the empirical uncertainty (A4) would clarify but not eliminate this trade-off between contraction speed and family disruption.
- A7 (Delaying tracking to a later age is the only reform directly targeting the age-10-12 exclusion point, and strong cross-national evidence links delayed selection to reduced socio-economic achievement gaps without loss of average performance.): evidence_answerable — The cross-national association is strongly evidenced (evidence: strong, tracking_inequality registry); what remains open is Hungary-specific transferability, which a staggered regional pilot with matched administrative-data tracking (see research section) could directly test.
- A8 (A comprehensive-schooling transition risks eroding equity gains, as in Poland's 2017 reversal, unless anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment are legislated concurrently with the tracking-age delay, not left as an optional add-on.): policy_design_fixable — Multiple reciprocity exchanges converge on a specific, nameable fix: joint legislative packaging with named, operational anti-sorting enforcement mechanisms and a compliance-capacity audit; this is a sequencing/design requirement, not an irreducible conflict, though its feasibility remains uncertain (see A9).
- A9 (A comprehensive-lower-secondary conversion should not be layered onto a teaching workforce that is disproportionately aging and still absorbing the 2024-2031 salary reform, without a sequenced, funded retraining plan.): needs_more_info — The demographic facts are settled (evidence: strong), but whether a sequenced, funded retraining plan can be designed and delivered without workload increases is explicitly unresolved in the record — a workforce-feasibility study is the named missing input, not a value disagreement.
- A10 (Forcing church-maintained 6/8-year gimnázium tracks into a unified comprehensive structure without their consent would violate subsidiarity and religious-institutional autonomy, and imported comprehensive models are not a settled, durable solution given Poland's own reversal.): value_conflict — This combines a value conflict (autonomy vs. structural equity mandate) with a legal-design question (voluntary opt-out mechanism); the value core cannot be resolved by evidence, though a voluntary-adoption design could partially soften — not eliminate — the tension.
- A11 (Parents whose children are currently mid-transition through the existing 6/8-year system need an airtight, clearly published cohort-by-cohort transition calendar so no family is left guessing which rules apply to their child.): not_decision_relevant — Flagged as an attention sink: this is a genuine administrative-implementation concern but does not change which scenario (S1-S5) should be chosen or bear on the core equity/feasibility trade-offs; it is a downstream implementation detail to resolve once S3 (if chosen) proceeds, already effectively addressed by the felmenő rendszer principle inherent to any statutory phase-out.
- A12 (Targeted, TEIP-style funding and school-quality investment is the fastest, lowest-disruption way to improve outcomes in disadvantaged schools since it requires no teacher reallocation, retraining, or structural transition.): irreducible_tradeoff — Lower disruption and lower equity ceiling are two sides of the same design choice: S4's speed and low workforce risk are real, but so is its documented failure (per Portugal 2015-2022) to protect low-SES high performers from the selective gate — improving one dimension (implementation ease) does not resolve the other (structural equity).
- A13 (Compensatory funding to disadvantaged schools is not a substitute for structural admission reform: low-SES high-performing pupils still get screened out at the age-10-12 selective gate, and Portugal's 2015-2022 experience shows compensatory funding can coexist with worsening equity.): evidence_answerable — The core factual claim (early selective gates increase inequality) is strongly evidenced; the specific Portugal 2015-2022 causal mechanism is a single-case, contested inference, but a longitudinal subgroup-access study (named in research section) would directly resolve whether S4 alone protects low-SES high performers.
- A14 (Targeted funding floors for disadvantaged schools must receive durable statutory (cardinal-law) protection, since such lines are typically the first cut in fiscal-consolidation rounds, leaving the equity promise unfulfilled.): policy_design_fixable — A named design fix (cardinal-law protection with a published multi-year budget commitment tracked through a fiscal-consolidation cycle) is proposed and largely agreed upon across stakeholders; the residual uncertainty is whether such protection would hold against future qualified-majority amendment, which is a political-durability question rather than a design gap.
- A15 (Demographic-driven school consolidation is proceeding informally at the tankerület level with no coordinated national costing, staffing-transition plan, or equity safeguard, even though consolidation is documented as nearly certain within 5-10 years.): policy_design_fixable — A coordinated national staffing-transition and consolidation-equity framework is a concrete, nameable design addition to S5 that multiple stakeholders identify as sufficient to shift their stance — this converts S5 from a purely passive baseline into an actively governed one, which is a design choice available to policymakers now.
- A16 (Calling demographic contraction a 'passive' baseline is a framing error, since active administrative levers already shape its equity outcome, and doing nothing while acknowledging the risk is itself a policy choice with foreseeable disparate impact on Roma and poor families.): communication_fixable — The equity risk is already documented in S5's own equity-impact analysis (the scenario record itself concedes consolidation without safeguards is likely to worsen SES sorting); the issue raised here is that this acknowledgment is not made visible or legible as an active-stewardship framing to the public, which relabelling/transparent communication about existing levers would address.
- A17 (Families facing school merger or closure due to falling enrolment need a published, multi-year regional consolidation calendar with guaranteed minimum notice periods, since informal drift risks sudden disruption to children's routine and commuting.): policy_design_fixable — A published multi-year regional calendar with guaranteed notice periods is a concrete, implementable administrative design addition that directly addresses the stated concern, though the uncertainty over whether maintainers currently have the forecasting capacity to commit to such a calendar remains a residual feasibility question.
- A18 (Letting demographic contraction proceed through existing tankerület-level, local decision-making respects subsidiarity and avoids top-down state engineering justified by contested equity theory.): value_conflict — This is a direct value clash with A16's position (subsidiarity/local autonomy vs. active national equity stewardship); both sides concede the empirical question of whether local decisions concentrate advantage is genuinely contested, so the disagreement is fundamentally about which value should govern under uncertainty, not a fact that further evidence alone would settle.
- A19 (Church and foundation maintainers, now covering roughly 25-30% of enrolment, are exempt from decree-level admission-equity rules, risking a cosmetic equity gain unless independent monitoring confirms actual admission-rate changes for Roma and disadvantaged pupils.): needs_more_info — The exemption and enrolment-share facts are established, but whether displaced demand will meaningfully shift toward church/foundation schools and whether Roma/disadvantaged admission rates will actually change has not been measured; independent monitoring data collection is the named missing input, directly connected to A1's broader displacement question.

## Where the red herrings are
- A11: Whether an airtight, cohort-by-cohort published transition calendar exists for families mid-way through the current 6/8-year system is a legitimate administrative-planning concern for affected parents, but it does not change which scenario (S1-S5) should be adopted, nor the core equity/feasibility trade-offs among them — it is a downstream implementation detail to be resolved once a structural pathway (if any) is chosen, and is already substantially covered by the felmenő rendszer principle that governs any statutory phase-out by default.
