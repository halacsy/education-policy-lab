# Policy brief — Early academic selection and the 6/8-year gimnázium

This brief synthesises expert analysis and a structured stakeholder stress test on early selection (age 10-12 tracking into 6/8-year gimnázium) in Hungary. It tags every substantive claim by its evidentiary status — fact, estimate, assumption, or value — and preserves disagreement rather than resolving it artificially. Five policy scenarios are compared; none is presented as the answer. Stakeholder positions are drawn from a value-modeled stress test, not from real-world consultation, and must be verified with actual stakeholders before being treated as representative.

## Scenario key (full detail: scenarios.en.md)
- S1 — S1 — Admissions and intake-equity regulation: keep the 6/8-year gimnázium structure, but regulate the admission mechanism (SES-aware proxies, coaching-resistant exams, uniform thresholds). See scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md for full detail.
- S2 — S2 — Supply-side contraction: shrink the state-sector selective track's capacity via score thresholds or caps, leaving admission criteria within remaining places unchanged. See scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md for full detail.
- S3 — S3 — Structural delay of the selection age (Finnish/Polish model): raise the tracking age to 14+ via a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase, paired with enforceable anti-sorting rules for choice and catchment. See scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md for full detail.
- S4 — S4 — Non-structural compensatory reform (Portugal model): curriculum clarity, TEIP-style targeted funding, and retention reduction, without changing the 10-12 selection age. See scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md for full detail.
- S5 — S5 — Demographically-driven passive contraction: the no-new-policy baseline in which shrinking birth cohorts force network consolidation regardless of active reform. See scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md for full detail.

## What we know
- Hungary has among the EU/OECD's highest socio-economic segregation and SES–achievement association: socio-economic status explains roughly 19–25% of achievement variance, versus a 12–15% OECD average. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- International cross-country evidence (Hanushek & Woessmann; a meta-analysis of 21 PISA/TIMSS cycles across 75 countries) indicates early tracking widens socio-economic achievement gaps without raising mean system performance. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- The SES-skewed intake of 6/8-year gimnáziums is driven substantially by unequal access to admission-exam preparation (private tutoring costing HUF 34,000–140,000+), functioning as a de facto entry fee despite nominally free tuition. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Church and foundation gimnázium enrolment share has grown from roughly 10% (2010) to approximately 25–30% (2022) of secondary enrolment; these maintainers retain autonomous admission discretion and are constitutionally and contractually protected from uniform state-level admission mandates absent negotiated consent. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Full track-discontinuation is legally slow (up to 8 years via the felmenő rendszer / ascending-system principle and Nkt. amendment), while admissions-rule changes can proceed faster via ministerial decree. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Annual births fell from ~125,000 (early 1990s) to 85–95,000 (early 2020s), with 2024 around 77,500 and 2025 estimated near 72,000, representing a 30–35% multi-decade cohort collapse that will force school-network consolidation over the next 5–10 years regardless of selection policy. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- A significant share of the achievement gap between selected and non-selected pupils reflects a selection/peer-composition effect rather than distinctive school pedagogical value-added; a CEU study found null long-run labour-market effects of selective-track attendance. [fact] (evidence: moderate)
- Evidence-supported reform can be politically reversed independent of its evidentiary success: Poland reversed its successful 1999 delayed-selection reform in 2017 on value/opinion grounds, not evidence. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Hungary's teacher workforce is aging (over half aged 50+, only 7.2% under 30) and is concurrently mid-way through a multi-year salary reform (2024–2031) that absorbs administrative and political bandwidth. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- No Hungary-specific natural experiment on selection-age change exists; the strongest Hungarian causal evidence on selection effects remains observational or matching-based, so transferability of Polish/Finnish results to Hungary is not directly tested. [fact] (evidence: strong)

## What we consider likely
- Coaching-resistant exam design and SES-aware admission proxies would likely reduce, at least modestly, the correlation between family-capital investment and admission success within the state sector, though the magnitude for Hungary is not established. [estimate]
- Church and foundation maintainers are likely to absorb at least part of any advantaged demand displaced by state-sector admission tightening or capacity contraction, given their documented enrolment growth trend, though the exact magnitude of this displacement is not measured. [estimate]
- A structural delay of tracking age paired with weak or delayed anti-sorting enforcement would likely see socio-economic sorting re-manifest at the later gatekeeping point via school choice and catchment mechanisms, echoing the OECD's 2011 Poland re-evaluation. [estimate]
- Demographically-driven consolidation, absent explicit equity safeguards, is likely to concentrate rather than disperse selective capacity into fewer, more competitive and more advantaged-skewed sites. [estimate]
- Targeted (TEIP-style) compensatory funding, if durably protected, would likely improve some outcome measures (e.g. early school-leaving) in disadvantaged schools even without structural admission reform, but would likely leave the achievement gap for low-SES high performers screened out of selective tracks largely unaddressed. [estimate]

## Where experts disagree
### Does abolishing or delaying early selection by itself reduce inequality, or does sorting simply relocate?
- **polish_reform, finnish_reform, educational_psychology, international_comparison**: Delaying selection to age 14–15 causally improves both equity and learning, especially for lower-track pupils, as Poland's 1999 reform demonstrated with 0.25–0.28 SD gains. [value] Why: Strong cross-country meta-analysis plus Poland's causally-identified natural experiment show extended common instruction and improved peer composition raise outcomes without lowering the mean.
- **portuguese_reform** (minority): Removing early academic tracking without simultaneously reforming school-choice admissions discretion, grade retention and resource allocation would relocate rather than eliminate sorting — Portugal abolished age-10 selection but substituted retention (26–31% of 15-year-olds) and equity still worsened. [value] Why: Two-thirds of the age-15 gap is already visible at age 10, and Portugal's comprehensive model still worsened equity 2015–2022, showing the school-choice/resource architecture is the stronger lever than tracking age or existence.
### Is the ~10% value-added premium of selective-track pupils real pedagogy or composition/cream-skimming?
- **hungarian_education_system, equity_and_social_mobility, educational_psychology, education_finance**: The premium is predominantly a selection/peer-composition effect; once prior ability and background are controlled, genuine pedagogical value-added is modest or absent. [value] Why: Hungarian value-added studies (Horn, HÉTFA), the minister's competence-test analysis, and the CEU null long-run study point to composition, not distinctive teaching.
- **political_feasibility** (minority): The ~10% gain is a contested but real finding that complicates a purely equity-based abolition case and will be weaponized by pro-selection stakeholders. [value] Why: Whether a 10% gain justifies sorting costs is a value question, not settled empirics; the finding is tagged contested and cannot be dismissed as pure composition.
### When and how is structural reform feasible?
- **implementation_planning** (minority): Structural reform is feasible only after 2031, once teacher-salary reform concludes and administrative bandwidth frees up; near-term action is limited to incremental admissions-criterion revision. [value] Why: Saturated administrative bandwidth, an aging workforce (half over 50, 7.2% under 30), and a rolled-back evaluation bureaucracy make capacity-intensive reform unrealistic before 2031.
- **legal_and_governance, political_feasibility**: A faster route exists now: admissions rules can be changed by EMMI ministerial decree without the 8-year felmenő phase-out, making near-term selection-mechanism reform legally and politically live. [value] Why: Admissions are decree-level, not primary-legislation-level; the June 2026 debate and divided stakeholder coalitions open a real (if fragile) policy window for restriction short of abolition.
### Can non-state (church/foundation) maintainers be prevented from absorbing phased-out selective capacity?
- **finnish_reform, demography** (minority): With transparent allocation, anti-segregation safeguards and needs-based funding, migration to denominational/private selection can be contained as part of a coordinated reform design. [value] Why: Finland's positive-discrimination model and coordinated consolidation-with-equity design suggest safeguards can work — though both flag this as an [assumption].
- **legal_and_governance, hungarian_education_system, education_finance**: Church and foundation maintainers, now ~25–30% of enrolment and constitutionally shielded with autonomous funding agreements, can freely select and form a de facto political-legal veto, so restriction cannot be assumed to hold. [value] Why: Maintainer autonomy and church–state funding accords create a veto point requiring multi-party consent; abolishing state selective capacity likely shifts demand into freely-selecting non-state schools.
### Is early selection or demographic collapse the dominant force reshaping the system?
- **demography, school_network_planning**: Cohort collapse (30–35% decline, ~110k to ~72k pupils) — not selection design — is now the dominant structural force, forcing network consolidation regardless of selection policy. [value] Why: Many schools already lack a first grade; uniform national selection rules are becoming operationally moot, so the real question is how to consolidate while protecting equity.
- **equity_and_social_mobility, international_comparison, educational_psychology**: Selection remains the primary equity-relevant lever; demographic decline does not erase segregation and, if consolidation concentrates intake in fewer selective sites, it may amplify sorting. [value] Why: The SES–performance link and segregation persist independent of cohort size; the equity harm is a selection-mechanism property, not a demographic artefact.

## What we don't know
- Whether SES-aware admission proxies would durably reduce family-capital-driven sorting, or whether advantaged families would simply redirect investment toward new, harder-to-regulate preparation or relocation strategies once the exam changes. [fact]
- Whether church and foundation maintainers can be brought into a uniform admission-equity or threshold regime without a specified legal-political mechanism, given their documented autonomy guarantees and growing enrolment share. [fact]
- Whether state-sector capacity contraction produces a net reduction in selective-track segregation or is substantially offset by church/foundation/private expansion, given the documented threefold growth in non-state selective share since 2010. [fact]
- Whether the observed Polish 1999 test-score gains would replicate in Hungary's specific institutional context (school-choice system, maintainer plurality, teacher labor market), given that no Hungarian natural experiment has directly tested a tracking-age delay. [fact]
- Whether Hungary's teacher workforce, given its current age structure and the concurrent 2024–2031 salary reform, can realistically absorb a comprehensive lower-secondary transition within the proposed timeline. [fact]
- Whether TEIP-style targeted funding for disadvantaged schools can be made durable against fiscal consolidation pressure in Hungary's specific budget environment, given a projected 6.2% deficit and rising debt. [assumption]
- Whether demographically-driven consolidation, absent explicit equity safeguards, will concentrate or disperse selective-track segregation across regions, given documented extreme regional divergence in birth-rate decline. [fact]

## What could be done
- **S1 — Regulate the admission gate, keep the structure**: Introduce SES-aware admission proxies, coaching-resistant exam design and uniform score thresholds within the existing 6/8-year gimnázium structure, via ministerial decree, primarily affecting state-maintained schools.
- **S2 — Shrink the selective track's footprint**: Use score thresholds, capacity caps, or demographically-driven consolidation to reduce the state-sector share of 6/8-year places, leaving admission criteria within remaining places largely unchanged.
- **S3 — Delay the selection age structurally**: Raise the statutory first-tracking age (e.g. to 14+), converting 6/8-year entry points into a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase, paired with mandatory, concurrent anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment.
- **S4 — Compensate without changing selection**: Pursue curriculum clarity, TEIP-style targeted funding for disadvantaged schools, retention reduction, and school-quality levelling, without changing the 10–12 selection age or track structure.
- **S5 — Let demographic contraction proceed passively**: Adopt no new policy; let shrinking birth cohorts force network consolidation and a natural decline in the selective track's absolute size and share, via informal, tankerület-level decisions.

## What each option costs
- **S1**: Costs SES-proxy data infrastructure (unquantified, low-cost self-report versus high-cost verified administrative linkage), recurring exam-redesign and psychometric validation cost, and multi-year enforcement/monitoring capacity across ~2,000+ institutions; the equity gain it buys is bounded and possibly reversed by church/foundation exemption and preparation-investment relocation, so state-school families and equity advocates risk winning less than promised while elite-gimnázium directors bear the disruption of losing selectivity discretion. [estimate]
- **S2**: Costs medium-to-high, multi-year, currently unbudgeted compensatory funding or capacity-control measures for displaced demand, plus unquantified potential teacher-redundancy/redeployment costs; the primary losers are state-school families facing longer commutes or costlier alternatives and state-sector teachers facing job insecurity without a published redeployment plan, while church/foundation maintainers are structurally positioned to gain enrolment without matching constraint — meaning the equity payoff this option buys may not materialize even as its costs fall on identifiable groups. [estimate]
- **S3**: Costs unquantified early diagnostic/remedial support, teacher retraining and reallocation constrained by an aging, salary-reform-absorbed workforce, facility-conversion coordination, and a load-bearing, currently unbudgeted anti-sorting enforcement infrastructure without which the equity claim does not hold; teachers bear the largest workload and job-security risk, church-maintained schools bear a mission-autonomy threat absent voluntary opt-out, and families mid-transition risk being caught in an ambiguous cohort-by-cohort implementation window — the largest potential equity gain of all five options is paired with the largest implementation risk and political-reversal exposure (as in Poland's 2017 rollback). [estimate]
- **S4**: Costs roughly EUR 100 million annually (Portugal's TEIP scale, not yet Hungary-costed) for targeted funding plus unquantified school-quality-levelling resources, concentrated in the teacher-shortage-affected disadvantaged-school segment; the scenario's structural silence means low-SES high-performing pupils — who most need admission-equity protection or delayed gatekeeping — receive no structural protection against being screened out at the existing gate, so the primary risk-bearers are precisely the pupils this reform claims to help, while the funding itself risks being a first-line fiscal-consolidation casualty absent statutory protection. [estimate]
- **S5**: Costs no new political capital or legislation but incurs uncoordinated consolidation-related transition costs (building repurposing, staff redundancy) proceeding informally via tankerület decisions with no published costing, plus a foregone opportunity to design equity safeguards into consolidation; the losers are likely Roma and poor families in the hardest-hit regions where consolidation concentrates disadvantage, and teachers facing unplanned, chaotic restructuring, while the apparent 'winner' — political actors avoiding accountability — gains only a temporary reprieve since the consolidation is documented as 'nearly certain' regardless. [estimate]

## What research could resolve
- A discontinuity or matched-cohort analysis tracking admission outcomes and preparation-spending patterns before and after a pilot SES-aware/coaching-resistant exam redesign, across multiple admission cycles, to test whether advantaged families relocate investment rather than reduce it.
- Legal opinion or Constitutional Court guidance, plus publication of the underlying church-state funding and autonomy accords, clarifying the permissible scope for mandating admission-equity compliance among non-state maintainers.
- A multi-year tracking study of enrolment shifts across all maintainer types (state, church, foundation, private) following any state-sector capacity policy change, using disaggregated KSH data by programme length and maintainer, to test whether displacement occurs at comparable scale.
- A detailed teacher-workforce feasibility study modelling retirement/attrition rates, retraining capacity, and salary-reform bandwidth overlap, published before any legislative enactment of a comprehensive lower-secondary transition.
- A staggered, regional natural experiment or pilot phase-in of delayed tracking in a subset of Hungarian districts, with matched administrative-data outcome tracking against non-pilot regions, to test whether Polish/Finnish gains transfer to Hungary's institutional context.
- A published multi-year budget commitment for TEIP-style targeted funding, with cardinal-law or equivalent statutory protection, tracked against actual disbursement through at least one fiscal-consolidation cycle.
- A longitudinal subgroup analysis tracking low-SES high-performing pupils' access to selective tracks and achievement trajectories before and after targeted-funding implementation, compared against a matched cohort under S1-style admission-equity reform.
- Detailed school-by-school enrollment forecasts and feasibility studies of mixed school models in low-density regions, plus segregation-index tracking before, during, and after consolidation, to test whether passive contraction concentrates or disperses selective-track segregation.

## What people must decide
- Whether equity gains bounded to the admission mechanism alone (S1) are an acceptable interim step, or whether only a structural change to the selection age or track footprint (S2/S3) is a sufficient response to documented segregation levels — this is a value choice about how much structural change is proportionate to the harm. [value]
- Whether the state is willing to negotiate binding commitments with church and foundation maintainers as a precondition for any reform's credibility, or whether it accepts a reform confined to the state sector with a known, unaddressed displacement risk — a political choice about how far maintainer autonomy can be balanced against equity goals. [value]
- Whether structural reform (S3) should proceed on a timeline set by equity urgency, or should be sequenced to wait for teacher-workforce and salary-reform stabilization (potentially after 2031) — a choice between speed of equity gain and feasibility/workforce-welfare risk. [value]
- Whether compensatory, non-structural investment (S4) is treated as a standalone sufficient response or explicitly as a complement to structural reform — a value decision about whether resourcing alone discharges the state's equity obligation when the selective gate itself remains open. [value]
- Whether passive demographic contraction (S5) is an acceptable default absent active equity safeguards, or whether inaction itself constitutes a policy choice requiring explicit accountability for its foreseeable disparate regional impact. [value]

## What to verify with real stakeholders
- A1 (Coaching-resistant exams and SES-aware proxies can reduce family-capital advantage under S1, but only if enforcement covers church and foundation maintainers too — otherwise displaced advantaged demand relocates and the equity gain becomes cosmetic.): irreducible_tradeoff — The equity gain from admission-mechanism reform is structurally bounded by maintainer plurality and constitutionally protected autonomy: extending enforcement to church/foundation schools requires their negotiated consent, which cannot be guaranteed, so a partial, state-sector-only gain versus a fuller, harder-to-secure system-wide gain is a genuine trade-off, not a design flaw fixable by better drafting alone.
- A2 (Imposing uniform admission-equity thresholds on church and foundation maintainers without negotiated consent would breach constitutionally and contractually protected maintainer autonomy and subsidiarity.): value_conflict — This is a genuine collision between equity as a state-mandated uniform standard and subsidiarity/institutional autonomy as a constitutionally protected value; no technical fix reconciles compelled compliance with autonomy guarantees — only negotiated, consent-based agreement or acceptance of a bounded reform can proceed.
- A3 (New coaching-resistant exam design, SES-proxy verification and multi-maintainer compliance monitoring create unfunded administrative and psychometric workload for teachers, with no cost category accounting for their time or training.): policy_design_fixable — The workload risk can be mitigated by an explicit design choice — centralizing item-writing, verification and monitoring in a dedicated exam-board or compliance unit funded outside teacher contracts — rather than requiring a structural or value trade-off; this is precisely the kind of gap a funded implementation plan can close.
- A4 (Shrinking only state-sector selective capacity while church and foundation maintainers can freely expand does not reduce selection pressure but merely relocates it, since their enrolment share has roughly tripled since 2010 with no legal mechanism to constrain it.): needs_more_info — Multiple voices (including the reform-realism and research-institute clusters, in their own reciprocity responses) agree the displacement magnitude is genuinely unmeasured — the historical tripling trend is documented, but whether state-sector contraction specifically triggers comparable-scale relocation, versus partial absorption elsewhere or net reduction, has no published pilot or mechanism evidence either way.
- A5 (Capacity caps in state-maintained selective schools carry unquantified teacher redundancy and redeployment risk, with no published staffing-transition plan to protect affected teachers.): policy_design_fixable — This is a resourcing and sequencing gap, not a fundamental conflict: a published redeployment guarantee (no forced job loss, retraining support, seniority protection) as a precondition of capacity caps directly addresses the risk without abandoning the scenario's core mechanism.
- A6 (Reducing state-sector selective capacity restricts families' predictable local schooling options and pushes them toward longer commutes or costlier private alternatives without actually reducing selection.): irreducible_tradeoff — If capacity reduction proceeds without a matching non-state constraint (a live legal uncertainty), the family-access cost is a direct, foreseeable consequence of the scenario's own mechanism, not an incidental design flaw — improving the equity intent of contraction and preserving family access are in tension until the non-state displacement question is resolved.
- A7 (Delaying tracking to a later age is the only reform directly targeting the age-10-12 exclusion point, and strong cross-national evidence links delayed selection to reduced socio-economic achievement gaps without loss of average performance.): evidence_answerable — The cross-national evidence base itself is strong and not contested by any voice; what remains open is Hungary-specific transferability, which is an empirical question a pilot or natural experiment could substantially resolve rather than a values dispute.
- A8 (A comprehensive-schooling transition risks eroding equity gains, as in Poland's 2017 reversal, unless anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment are legislated concurrently with the tracking-age delay, not left as an optional add-on.): policy_design_fixable — Multiple reciprocity exchanges (eselyegyenlosegi_civil, fuggetlen_szakpolitikai_kutatomuhely, konzervativ_ertekvedo) converge on this being addressable through legislative design — coupling the tracking-age delay with named, operational, concurrently-enacted anti-sorting enforcement — rather than an unresolvable conflict; the open question is enforcement capacity, which is itself a design and resourcing matter.
- A9 (A comprehensive-lower-secondary conversion should not be layered onto a teaching workforce that is disproportionately aging and still absorbing the 2024-2031 salary reform, without a sequenced, funded retraining plan.): policy_design_fixable — The oktataspolitikai_reformmozgalom reciprocity response proposes a concrete mitigation — a phased, cohort-by-cohort felmenő-rendszer rollout with protected, funded retraining slots sequenced against the 2024-2031 salary timeline — which converts this from a blocking constraint into a sequencing and funding design problem, though feasibility of that specific sequencing itself remains a research question (see what_research_could_resolve).
- A10 (Forcing church-maintained 6/8-year gimnázium tracks into a unified comprehensive structure without their consent would violate subsidiarity and religious-institutional autonomy, and imported comprehensive models are not a settled, durable solution given Poland's own reversal.): value_conflict — This combines a genuine value conflict (institutional/religious autonomy versus state-mandated structural uniformity) with an empirical durability concern (Poland's reversal); the autonomy dimension has no technical fix without voluntary opt-out design, while the durability question is a distinct political-feasibility issue best read alongside it, not resolved by it.
- A11 (Parents whose children are currently mid-transition through the existing 6/8-year system need an airtight, clearly published cohort-by-cohort transition calendar so no family is left guessing which rules apply to their child.): not_decision_relevant — This is an implementation-detail request already addressed in principle by the felmenő rendszer (ascending-system) legal mechanism named across all structural scenarios; it does not change whether S3 should be adopted or how it compares to alternatives, and the ledger itself flags it as already_answered — it consumes attention without altering the core policy choice.
- A12 (Targeted, TEIP-style funding and school-quality investment is the fastest, lowest-disruption way to improve outcomes in disadvantaged schools since it requires no teacher reallocation, retraining, or structural transition.): irreducible_tradeoff — The speed/low-disruption advantage of S4 is real and evidenced, but it is directly traded off against a lower equity ceiling documented in the same record (Portugal's 2015-2022 worsening despite intact non-structural model) — this is not a design flaw to fix but a genuine choice between fast, safe, partial gains and slower, riskier, potentially larger gains.
- A13 (Compensatory funding to disadvantaged schools is not a substitute for structural admission reform: low-SES high-performing pupils still get screened out at the age-10-12 selective gate, and Portugal's 2015-2022 experience shows compensatory funding can coexist with worsening equity.): policy_design_fixable — The oktataspolitikai_reformmozgalom and fuggetlen_szakpolitikai_kutatomuhely reciprocity responses converge on a specific fix: pair S4 explicitly with a named, monitored low-SES high-performer track-access mechanism rather than treating S4 as a standalone substitute — this converts the objection from a fatal flaw into a condition for S4's credible design, though whether such a mechanism is effective remains an open research question.
- A14 (Targeted funding floors for disadvantaged schools must receive durable statutory (cardinal-law) protection, since such lines are typically the first cut in fiscal-consolidation rounds, leaving the equity promise unfulfilled.): policy_design_fixable — A concrete design remedy (cardinal-law protection) is already proposed and named by multiple voices; the residual uncertainty — whether such protection would survive a future qualified-majority amendment — is acknowledged by the kisgyerekes_szuloi reciprocity response as a legitimate open question rather than grounds to abandon the fix, so this is design-fixable with an honestly flagged durability caveat.
- A15 (Demographic-driven school consolidation is proceeding informally at the tankerület level with no coordinated national costing, staffing-transition plan, or equity safeguard, even though consolidation is documented as nearly certain within 5-10 years.): policy_design_fixable — The gap identified — absence of coordination, costing, and equity safeguards — is precisely what a national consolidation framework (named as a condition by multiple voices, including kormanyzati_reformrealizmus's own 'active stewardship' concession) could remedy without requiring abandonment of the demographic baseline itself.
- A16 (Calling demographic contraction a 'passive' baseline is a framing error, since active administrative levers already shape its equity outcome, and doing nothing while acknowledging the risk is itself a policy choice with foreseeable disparate impact on Roma and poor families.): communication_fixable — The underlying substantive point — that consolidation interacts with active levers and is not equity-neutral — is already documented and accepted in the S5 scenario's own equity-impact text; what remains is making this legible in how S5 is publicly framed and communicated, not a new policy design or an unresolved value conflict.
- A17 (Families facing school merger or closure due to falling enrolment need a published, multi-year regional consolidation calendar with guaranteed minimum notice periods, since informal drift risks sudden disruption to children's routine and commuting.): policy_design_fixable — This is a specific, implementable administrative-planning remedy (a published multi-year calendar with minimum notice periods) that maintainers could adopt without altering the demographic baseline itself; the scenario's own uncertainty about location/timing precision of triggers is a feasibility caveat, not a barrier to attempting the fix.
- A18 (Letting demographic contraction proceed through existing tankerület-level, local decision-making respects subsidiarity and avoids top-down state engineering justified by contested equity theory.): value_conflict — This is a genuine tension between subsidiarity/local self-determination and centrally-coordinated equity safeguarding; the voice itself concedes the empirical question of whether local decisions concentrate advantage is unsettled, but the underlying value choice — who should decide, local actors or the state — is not resolvable by more data alone.
- A19 (Church and foundation maintainers, now covering roughly 25-30% of enrolment, are exempt from decree-level admission-equity rules, risking a cosmetic equity gain unless independent monitoring confirms actual admission-rate changes for Roma and disadvantaged pupils.): needs_more_info — This restates A1/A2's substantive concern with a specific monitoring proposal attached; whether independent monitoring of actual admission-rate changes (not just score-composition shifts) is feasible and would detect the claimed cosmetic-gain risk is an empirical design question that a pilot with defined monitoring metrics could answer, distinct from the underlying autonomy conflict already addressed under A2.

## Where the red herrings are
- A11: The demand for an airtight, cohort-by-cohort transition calendar under S3 is legitimate as a family-planning concern, but it is already addressed in principle by the felmenő rendszer mechanism named in every structural scenario, and refining its exact publication format would not change whether S3 is adopted, how it compares to S1/S2/S4/S5, or any of the core trade-offs decision-makers must weigh — it consumes attention on an implementation detail rather than the decision itself.
