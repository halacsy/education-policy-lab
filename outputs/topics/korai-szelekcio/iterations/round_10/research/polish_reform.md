Research notes (prose, with full findings and URLs) — polish_reform domain, Round 10

**1. Poland's 1999 reform delayed tracking by one year and produced large, causally-identified test-score gains.** The Jakubowski, Patrinos, Porta & Wiśniewski (2016) study finds 
Poland improved its score in mathematics by 0.25 of a standard deviation, in reading, by 0.28 of a standard deviation, and in science, by 0.16 of a standard deviation
, and using propensity-score matching/difference-in-differences 
the reform is associated with an improvement in likely vocational students' scores of about 100 points, or a whole standard deviation
. Source: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/296973861_The_effects_of_delaying_tracking_in_secondary_school_evidence_from_the_1999_education_reform_in_Poland (also published in Education Economics 2016, https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/09645292.2016.1149548).

**2. Mechanism: about one-third of the gain is a genuine "delayed tracking" effect, two-thirds is resources.** 
We decompose the results and estimate that two-thirds of the observed test-score differential between PISA 2000 and 2006 is associated with the changes in characteristics or the level of resources, while one-third reflects
 the tracking delay itself. Source: same as above.

**3. Labour-market follow-up confirms equity gains, not just test scores.** Drucker, Horn & Jakubowski (2022) find 
after 1999 students in Poland were forced to sit an additional year in less selected classes than before and were taught by teachers who were less selected... this change was likely beneficial for the low-track children, as their composition of peers and teachers improved substantially... the reform has reached its initial goal of decreasing inequalities
. Crucially, this success did not protect the reform: 
despite being successful in improving the education outcomes... and the labour market outcomes of post-reform students... after 18 years, the Polish government reversed the reform in January 2017 and re-introduced the old system
. Source: https://labourmarketresearch.springeropen.com/articles/10.1186/s12651-022-00317-4

**4. The reversal was explicitly political/values-driven, not evidence-driven.** A Springer chapter states 
that reform (of 2017) was not rooted in evidence-based research and policy, but was a reaction to collective public op[inion]
[posure to the prior reform], and another source notes 
the evidence from international and national studies was not sufficient to stop the reversal of the reforms
. Source: https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-41882-3_7 and https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-59031-4_7

**5. The 2017 reversal (Anna Zalewska/PiS "Law on School Education") abolished gimnazjum and reinstated 8-year primary + tracked secondary.** 
the 2016 "Law on School Education" act was introduced by then-Minister of Education, Anna Zalewska. Gimnazjum was abolished, and replaced with 8 years of primary school... The changes began taking effect on 1 September 2017
. Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Education_in_Poland

**6. Implementation was chaotic and imposed real transition costs: double cohort, job losses, strikes.** In 2017 
6th graders stayed in their primary schools to continue their education for another two years. This year both, those graduating from gimnazjums and those graduating from primary schools, applied to high schools which resulted in competition being so high that in Warsaw alone, 3173 students did not get into any school
. On the labour side, 
in October 2017, after the introduction of the last educational reform, the Polish Teachers' Union (ZNP) informed that 6,500 teachers were being made redundant and a further 18,500 faced reduced hours or work in several schools or had taken retirement
. Sources: https://medium.com/@thelambert/education-in-poland-was-dismantling-of-gimnazjum-necessary-8adc9f2e48a8 ; https://notesfrompoland.com/2020/10/08/the-inequality-epidemic-in-polands-schools/

**7. PISA 2022 shows an unusually large Polish decline, plausibly linked to the structural reversal (contested causal attribution).** 
Did the subsequent reversal of key reforms contribute to Poland's unusually large decline in PISA 2022? ... the decline in Poland was twice the average decline in OECD countries in reading and mathematics, and 3 times the OECD decline in science
. The OECD country note adds nuance: 
2022 results are among the lowest ever measured by PISA in all three subjects, on par with results observed in reading and mathematics in 2003, and in science in 2006. All gains observed between these early assessments and 2012 were reversed by the drops observed between 2018 and 2022
. A Polish country-report source attributes the drop partly to competing explanations: 
after improving to above the OECD average in 2018, the 2022 survey showed a drop, and the share of students not meeting the minimum level increased to 23%. Some experts attribute this to long school closures during the COVID-19 pandemic and controversial educational reforms that eliminated a wide range of secondary school types
. Sources: https://worldclasslearningsystems.com/the-power-of-system-reform-evidence-from-poland/ ; https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/pisa-2022-results-volume-i-and-ii-country-notes_ed6fbcc5-en/poland_4a2c28c8-en.html ; https://bti-project.org/en/reports/country-report/POL

**8. Structural signal of eroded system quality: teacher-staffing crisis emerged post-reform.** 
In 2022, 47% of students in Poland were in schools whose principal reported that the school's capacity to provide instruction is hindered by a lack of teaching staff (and 23%, by inadequate or poorly qualified teaching staff). In 2018, the corresponding proportions were 3% and 1%
. Source: https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2023/11/pisa-2022-results-volume-i-and-ii-country-notes_2fca04b9/poland_17b5bcfd/4a2c28c8-en.pdf

**9. Parental engagement with schools fell sharply after the reversal — a possible equity-relevant channel.** 
In 2022, 23% of students in Poland were in schools whose principal reported that during the previous academic year at least half of all families discussed their child's progress with a teacher on their own initiative... In 2018, before the school structure reform, when most 15-year-old students attended gymnasium, the corresponding number was 49%
. Source: https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/pisa-2022-results-volume-i-and-ii-country-notes_ed6fbcc5-en/poland_4a2c28c8-en.html

**10. Direct Hungarian comparison: Hungary's own early-tracking system (age 10-12 into 6/8-year gimnázium) shows large, family-background-driven inequality, and a Hungarian-authored study of the Polish reform pre-dates and likely informed domestic debate.** A Hungarian-language paper by Horn & Drucker (2016), 
"Kisebb szelekció - nagyobb kereset. Az 1999-es lengyel oktatási reform hatásának vizsgálata [Narrower selection, wider demand. Examining the effects of the 1999 Polish education reform]," Közgazdasági Szemle
, vol 0(9), pages 944-965 — published in Hungary's leading economics journal, indicating direct policy-relevance transfer to the Hungarian debate. On Hungary itself, the World Bank notes 
the examples of other countries such as Poland show that improvements in equity and excellence
 [are jointly achievable], implying the Polish case was explicitly used as a comparator in World Bank advice to Hungary. Source: https://ideas.repec.org/a/taf/edecon/v24y2016i6p557-572.html ; https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/340561468196449667/pdf/101561-WP-P152132-Box393264B-PUBLIC-PISA-Hungary.pdf
