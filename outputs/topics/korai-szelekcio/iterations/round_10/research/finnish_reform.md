## Research Notes — Finnish Reform Domain (Round 10)

**1. Peruskoulu reform basics (1968–1970s): abolition of streaming at age 10-11**
Finland's pre-reform system was a dual-track structure — the kansakoulu (folk school) for most children and the academically selective lyseo (grammar school) mainly accessible to urban/higher-income families. The 1968 Basic Education Act (Peruskoululaki) replaced this with a unified 9-year comprehensive school, eliminating the early selection point.
Source: https://grokipedia.com/page/Education_in_Finland

**2. Motivation for abolishing selection was explicitly equity-driven, not just efficiency**
Grammar-school enrollment grew nearly tenfold (34,000 in 1955 to 324,000 in 1970) but "remained skewed toward privileged groups," which was a direct empirical driver of the reform.
Source: https://grokipedia.com/page/Education_in_Finland

**3. Reform outcome contested by critics domestically before PISA fame**
Academic and policy critics in Finland argued the comprehensive, non-selective model produced a "levelling effect, which gained more unsatisfactory results for all" prior to 2001 — i.e., domestic debate about the reform's costs was real and only reversed narrative-wise after PISA 2000 results.
Source: https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-59031-4_6

**4. Decentralization (1990s) reintroduced de facto school choice and selection mechanisms**
Legislative changes from the mid-1980s to 1998 (abolition of school district division in 1991, the 1998 Basic Education Act) legitimized parental school choice, even though formal streaming/tracking was not reintroduced.
Source: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/1474904118764938

**5. "Myth of non-selectivity" — Finnish comprehensive schools do select/track within schools**
Research on urban Finland finds that while there is no formal ability-tracking under age 15, "public comprehensive schools select and track their pupils through different admission criteria for different teaching classes within schools" — meaning covert/informal selection exists even in the nominally comprehensive system.
Source: https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-981-19-8241-5_12

**6. School choice is empirically linked to rising segregation in Finland's largest cities**
Long-term Helsinki-metropolitan research finds "international research points to school choice increasing school segregation, and our long-term research on the Helsinki metropolitan area demonstrates this in the Finnish context as well."
Source: https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-981-19-8241-5_11

**7. PISA 2022: Finland's between-school variance remains low despite overall decline**
Finland's National Agency for Education notes that "in the past, comprehensive school was more successful in levelling out the effect of students' backgrounds," but recent results show "differences within and even between schools have increased," though between-school variance is still only ~9% vs. OECD average ~30%.
Source: https://www.oph.fi/en/blog/pisa-results-reflect-broader-changes-finnish-society

**8. Steep, decade-long PISA decline predates the pandemic**
Official Finnish PISA 2022 reporting: "PISA 2022 results showed a decline in mathematical literacy among Finnish students. Finnish students scored 484 points on average, down by 64 points from the peak year of 2006 (548 points)," though Finland remains above the OECD average (472).
Source: https://okm.fi/en/pisa-2022-en

**9. OECD: decline steeper in Finland than OECD average, and one in four below Level 2 in math**
"However, the decline has been steeper in Finland than in the OECD on average" and "one in four students performed at the lowest level (below Level 2) in mathematics" (PISA 2022).
Source: https://okm.fi/en/pisa-2022-en

**10. Needs-based ("positive discrimination") funding as a non-structural equity lever — causal evidence**
A study by Mikko Silliman found Helsinki's positive-discrimination funding, targeted at schools by socioeconomic/immigrant composition, "increased the likelihood that pupils continue their studies in secondary education, particularly among immigrant pupils and Finnish-speaking boys," and cut the share of immigrant students not continuing after comprehensive school by one-fifth; the mechanism was schools hiring special needs assistants, not just raw funding.
Source: https://vatt.fi/en/-/students-in-helsinki-benefit-from-positive-discrimination-funding

**11. Teacher education as an infrastructural "carrier" of the reform's effects**
Since a 1979 reform, "the minimum qualification required for both comprehensive school and upper secondary school teachers was defined as a Master's degree requiring about five years of rigorous academic and practical work," combined with highly selective, research-based entry (roughly 10-15% acceptance rates) — a structural investment predating and co-occurring with the comprehensive reform, often cited as what let delayed selection work without sacrificing quality.
Sources: https://theeducationist.info/teacher-education-in-finland/ ; https://www.visitedufinn.com/what-qualifications-are-required-to-teach-in-finnish-schools/

**12. Hungary comparison — early tracking (age 10/12) and its equity association**
CEU research on Hungary's system: schools "cherry pick the high achiever students earlier, after the 4th grade at age 10 and the 6th grade at age 12," and Hungary is "one of those countries where the correlation between the student's socio-economic status and their mathematical test points is the strongest" internationally.
Source: https://www.etd.ceu.edu/2015/gurzo_klara.pdf

**13. OECD explicitly flags Hungary's early selection as an equity risk**
The OECD's Hungary Education Policy Outlook states plainly: "Early school choice, selection may hamper equity," and notes "the impact of socio-economic background on mathematics performance of 15-year-olds was among the largest in OECD countries, with high variance between schools."
Source: https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/about/projects/edu/education-policy-outlook/379201-Hungary-Profile.pdf

**14. Hungary's socioeconomic-performance link quantified**
One source cites PISA 2012 data showing "the impact of socio-economic status on student performance reaching 23.1% in mathematics ... well above the OECD average of 14.8%" for Hungary, with early tracking from age 10-13 cited as a contributing structural factor (note: this source, Grokipedia, is lower-tier/aggregator quality and should be treated as a weak/secondary citation pending verification against primary OECD PISA data).
Source: https://grokipedia.com/page/Hungarian_rettsgi
