# Research Notes: Early Selection and the 6/8-Year Gimnázium — Finance & Structural Cost Dimensions

## Finding 1: Scale and structure of the selective track
Hungary's system allows primary education to last 4, 6, or 8 years, with the 6- and 8-year variants layered onto the standard structure. 
Primary education can last for 4, 6 or 8 years, with 8-year education being the most widespread; the other two options were introduced in the early 1990s.
 Structurally, 
pupils can enrol in eight-year secondary schools from the fifth grade and six-year secondary schools from the seventh grade, meaning education begins at lower secondary level (ISCED 2) and ends in the twelfth grade.
 Currently roughly one in ten students uses this track: 
"Ma már minden tizedik nyolcadikos gimnáziumba jár általános helyett, és a kisgimik válogathatnak a legjobb képességű diákok között."

Source: Eurydice, "Organisation of general secondary education," https://eurydice.eacea.ec.europa.eu/eurypedia/hungary/organisation-general-secondary-education ; Index.hu (2019), https://index.hu/techtud/2019/12/05/hatosztalyos_nyolcosztalyos_gimnaziumi_felveteli_oktatas_szegregacio/

## Finding 2: Financing mechanism — normative per-pupil funding, not selection-specific
Hungarian public education (including the 6/8-year tracks) is funded through the same state normative (per-capita) grant system as other public schools; there is no separate "selection budget line," but normative funding rarely covers full costs. 
Since the combined normative budgetary contributions cover only about sixty-to-seventy percent of the costs institutions need to operate, on national average, the maintainer must provide the missing amount.
 This means the fiscal burden of running parallel selective and non-selective secondary tracks within the same normative-financing logic falls partly on maintainers (municipalities, churches, foundations) rather than being separately budgeted for selection itself.
Source: Lannert Judit, TÁRKI-TUDOK, "A közoktatás finanszírozásának lehetséges megoldásai," https://www.tarki-tudok.hu/file/finanszirozas/v_oktfin.pdf

## Finding 3: Church/foundation-maintained selective gimnáziums receive substantial state support
A large share of 6/8-year gimnáziums are church- or foundation-maintained, which draws on a supplementary "kiegészítő normatíva" on top of the base normative grant. 
"Érdemes megjegyezni, hogy az egyházi oktatás finanszírozása mintegy évi 50 milliárd Ft, ami az adóbevételek egy százalékát"
 teszi ki (approx. HUF 50 billion/year, historically ~1% of tax revenue) — though this figure is old (pre-2010s) and not selection-specific; it covers all church schooling, not only the 6/8-year track.
Source: same TÁRKI-TUDOK paper as above, https://www.tarki-tudok.hu/file/finanszirozas/v_oktfin.pdf

## Finding 4: Private/shadow-cost burden falls heavily on families, unevenly by income
The admissions test system generates a substantial, unequally distributed private cost. Prep-course prices for 2024/25 ran up to 
"A tanfolyamok szeptember közepén kezdődnek és egészen február közepéig tartanak... A díja 140 ezer forint, amit négy részletben (4x35 000 forint) kell befizetni."
 Individual/private tutoring for the entrance exam could run far higher: one source found 
"15 matematika felkészítő óráért 117 000 forintot, a magyarért pedig 78 000 forintot kérnek el."
 Sociological survey data show stark differences by grade cohort and family investment: 
among fourth-graders targeting the 8-year track, fewer than half of families paid for a course, while among sixth-graders targeting the 6-year track almost 70% used paid preparation, averaging about 3 hours 40 minutes of weekly prep time.
 This constitutes a real, quantifiable "shadow cost" of the selection system borne disproportionately by better-resourced families, functioning as a de facto entry fee even though tuition itself is free.
Source: Eduline (2024), https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/20240913_kozepiskolai-felveteli-elokeszito-2024-arak ; Eduline (2023), https://eduline.hu/erettsegi-felveteli/20230925_kozponti-felvetei-vizsga-felkeszito-altalanos-iskola ; Privátbankár/Berényi Eszter (2020), https://privatbankar.hu/cikkek/makro/kivaltsagosok-kerulnek-be-a-kozepiskolakba-.html

## Finding 5: Socioeconomic sorting effect is large and among the worst in the EU/OECD
This matters directly for the "who pays, who benefits" ledger. 
Hungary has the highest level of segregation of students from less and more advantaged socio-economic groups into different schools, and Hungary's segregation of the most and least disadvantaged students was the highest in the EU in the 2022 PISA study.
 On outcome gaps: 
socio-economically advantaged students (top 25%) outperformed disadvantaged students (bottom 25%) by 121 score points in mathematics in Hungary — larger than the average OECD gap of 93 points.
 Also 
on average in the EU there is a 33-point difference between the performance of a child of a graduate and a non-graduate parent, but this gap is 53 points in Hungary's case.

Source: European Commission, Education and Training Monitor 2025 — Hungary, https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html ; OECD PISA 2022 Country Note Hungary, https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/pisa-2022-results-volume-i-and-ii-country-notes_ed6fbcc5-en/hungary_3df2ae68-en.html

## Finding 6: No measurable "value added" from selection once starting level is controlled — new ministerial position (2026)
This is the most current and directly load-bearing development. Hungary's newly appointed Education and Child Affairs Minister, Lannert Judit (an education-finance researcher before taking office), stated in a June 2026 interview that competence-test data show no faster progress in 6/8-year gimnáziums once initial ability/background is accounted for: 
"a jelenlegi rendszer jelentős társadalmi szelekciót eredményez, miközben a kutatások nem igazolják, hogy ezek az intézmények nagyobb mértékben fejlesztenék a diákokat... A fejlődés üteme ugyanakkor nem gyorsabb, mint más iskolákban."
 Her summary framing: 
"A hat-nyolc évfolyamos gimnáziumban magasabb szintről indulnak, de ugyanolyan aztán tempóban haladnak. Tehát az egésznek tényleg csak annyi az értelme, hogy az elit kiszelektálja magát."
 She also linked this to demographic decline reducing the system's original rationale of scarce places for many applicants: 
"a folyamatosan csökkenő gyereklétszám mellett egyre kevésbé indokolható a jelenlegi szelekciós rendszer fenntartása... ma már nem az a helyzet, hogy túl sok gyerek verseng kevés férőhelyért."
 Politically, this has already triggered contestation: opposition (Fidesz) framed it as a threat to abolish the track entirely, which the minister denied, clarifying she proposed only a review/social dialogue, not abolition: 
"érdemes megvizsgálni a hat- és nyolcosztályos gimnáziumokhoz kapcsolódó középiskolai felvételi rendszert... Konkrétan úgy fogalmaztam, hogy ez a kérdés megéri a társadalmi egyeztetést."

## Finding 7: Network density / access-cost tradeoff of any structural alternative
Any reform toward a uniform (later-selection) structure interacts with Hungary's dense secondary-school network, which is a real infrastructure/transport cost consideration. 
According to 2024/25 KSH data, secondary education is provided at 880 schools across Hungary, a dense network that aims to ensure every student can find a secondary general school close to home, ideally accessible by public transport in under an hour.
 A structural reform that eliminated or restructured the 6/8-year track would need to reallocate this existing physical/staffing infrastructure rather than build new capacity — a moderate-cost, not a from-scratch-cost, transition, but with transition/re-accreditation costs for buildings and staff currently organized around the 5th/7th-grade entry points.
Source: Eurydice, https://eurydice.eacea.ec.europa.eu/eurypedia/hungary/organisation-general-secondary-education

## Finding 8: Historical precedent — targeted anti-segregation reform in the 2000s failed to reverse structural sorting
An earlier (2000s) ministry attempt to counter segregation via targeted funding/programs rather than structural change was reportedly insufficient: 
"Kezdetben bízott abban, hogy szakpedagógiai programokkal és célirányos finanszírozási konstrukciókkal javíthat a közoktatási esélyegyenlőtlenségen és fékezheti a szegregációt. Hamar világossá vált, hogy ezt a célt képtelenség elérni a közoktatási szisztéma egészének jelentős módosítása nélkül."
 This is directly relevant as historical evidence on the cost-effectiveness of non-structural (targeted funding/program) alternatives versus structural (track-abolition) reform: non-structural fixes were judged inadequate without broader systemic change.
Source: Sulinet/EOKIK report, "Cigánynak lenni Magyarországon," https://www.sulinet.hu/oroksegtar/data/magyarorszagi_nemzetisegek/romak/EOKIK/ciganynak_lenni_magyarorszagon_jelentes_2002_2006/pages/009_integracios_es.htm
