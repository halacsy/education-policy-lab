# Research Notes: International Comparison — Early Selection, Tracking Age, and Equity Effects

## 1. Core causal evidence: cross-country difference-in-differences (DiD) literature

**Hanushek & Woessmann (2006), the seminal DiD study** comparing PIRLS/TIMSS (primary) to PISA/TIMSS (secondary) across up to 26 countries: 
"The results suggest that early tracking increases educational inequality... there is also a tendency for early tracking to reduce mean performance."
 Method: 
"we employ an international differences-in-differences approach. We identify tracking effects by comparing differences in outcome between primary and secondary school across tracked and non-tracked systems."
 Source: https://hanushek.stanford.edu/sites/default/files/publications/Hanushek+Woessmann%202006%20EJ%20116(510).pdf (Economic Journal, 2006)

**A follow-up DiD study (van Elk/comparative, published Research in Comparative and International Education, 2016)** found 
"early tracking has a negative effect on mean performance of students, particularly in the domain of literacy"
 and, disaggregating by ability, 
"early tracking has a very strong negative effect on low achieving students, suggesting that disadvantageous peer- and environmental effects in the lower tracks may have detrimental consequences... By contrast, a null effect on the group of top achieving students was found, suggesting that comprehensive systems can equally challenge high performers."
 This result held after controlling for GDP/capita and Gini 
"which apparently did not change the main message."
 It also cites Horn (2009) on PISA 2003: 
"the larger effect of social origin on achievement in early tracking countries survived when other relevant differences in the education system (school autonomy, use of central exams) were controlled out."
 And Bol & Van de Werfhorst (2013) on PISA 2009: 
"early tracking was related to less equal opportunities after controlling out differences in wealth, educational expenditure and private schooling rates."
 Source: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/306527685_Educational_tracking_inequality_and_performance_New_evidence_from_a_differences-in-differences_technique

**A large meta-analytic replication (Educational Assessment, Evaluation and Accountability, 2021)** used 
"all accumulated data collected in the three largest international assessments... in the past 20 years in 75 countries and regions... combined data from a total of 21 cycles... We synthesized the effects using a meta-analytical approach and found strong evidence that tracking increased social achievement gaps."
 Source: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11092-020-09346-4 (Jan 2021)

**A specific natural experiment**: 
"Piopiunik (2014) combined German data from the PISA 2003 and 2006 cycles and found that lowering the age of tracking increased dispersion inequality significantly... This study focused on a policy change in the federal state of Bavaria, where the tracking age was lowered from sixth to fourth grade."
 Notably 
"The study provided no evidence that the effects differed for mathematics, reading, and science."
 Source: same Springer 2021 article, citing Piopiunik (2014).

**IZA working paper detail** (Hanushek/Woessmann underlying data): 
"inequality between primary and secondary school are all early trackers (Germany with an increase of 0.71, Greece 0.30, Czech Republic 0.25, and Italy 0.22)."
 Source: https://docs.iza.org/dp1901.pdf

## 2. Natural experiment: Poland's 1999 delayed-tracking reform (and its 2016 reversal)

Jakubowski, Patrinos, Porta & Wiśniewski (Education Economics, 2016) analyzed Poland's 1999 reform which delayed vocational streaming. Using propensity-score matching and DiD: 
"Estimates using propensity-score matching and difference-in-differences estimates show that delaying vocational education and increasing time on task have a positive and significant impact on student performance on the order of a standard deviation."
 More specifically for the affected (likely-vocational) students: 
"We conclude that the reform is associated with an improvement in likely vocational students' scores of about 100 points, or a whole standard deviation."
 The paper also warns: 
"once vocational school options are available again, when students are 16, test scores decline for those students who enter the vocational track."
 Sources: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/09645292.2016.1149548 ; full text at https://hpatrinos.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/poland-tracking1.pdf

**Important caveat from an earlier OECD evaluation (2011)** of the same reform tempers the causal story: 
"the reform helped to update the skills of the average student, but the negative effect of the tracking system was simply postponed by one year. The achievement gap noted in PISA 2000 is still evident and almost of the same magnitude."
 This suggests delaying tracking by itself did not eliminate the inequality associated with eventual tracking — it may have been more about added instructional time/resources. Source: https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2011/06/the-impact-of-the-1999-education-reform-in-poland_g17a1e9e/5kmbjgkm1m9x-en.pdf

**Crucially for transferability/policy-risk lessons: Poland reversed course.** 
"For Poland, PISA 2022 showed the impact of the pandemic and the reversal of the successful 1999 reform, which delayed vocational education. Research suggests that the 1999 reform substantially increased test scores (Jakubowski et al., 2016) and labor market outcomes (Drucker et al., 2022; Liwiński, 2020). Despite that, in 2016, the school structure was reversed to what Poland had before 1999."
 This is a live example of a research-supported reform being politically reversed — directly relevant to the "political risk" learning goal. Source: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/296973861 (chapter citing PISA 2022 country reports)

## 3. Hungary-specific PISA 2022 / EU Monitor equity statistics (fresh, 2023-2025)

- OECD average socioeconomic gap in math: 
"socio-economically advantaged students scored 93 points more in mathematics than their disadvantaged peers on average across OECD countries."
 Hungary is among the worst: 
"The performance gap related to students' socio-economic status is widest in Romania and the Slovak Republic, followed by Hungary, Israel and Chinese Taipei."
 Source (OECD PISA 2022 Vol I, 2023): https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2023/12/pisa-2022-results-volume-i_76772a36/full-report/the-state-of-learning-and-equity-in-education-in-2022_e65c570e.html

- Same OECD volume names structural features of "resilient" (equity-preserving) systems: 
"These include shorter periods of school closure; fewer obstacles to remote learning; keeping schools safe; ensuring greater discipline; keeping parents involved in students' learning; tracking students later; reducing grade repetition..."
 — i.e., OECD explicitly names later tracking as an equity-supporting system feature. Same URL as above.

- EU Education and Training Monitor 2025, Hungary country report (most recent available): 
"Hungary lags behind in terms of equity: on average in the EU, there is a 33-point difference between the performance of a child of a graduate and a non-graduate parent, but this gap is 53 points in Hungary's case."
 And on school-level segregation specifically: 
"Disadvantaged students are concentrated in certain schools. Hungary has the highest level of segregation of students from less and more advantaged socio-economic groups into different schools (Gutiérrez et al., 2020). Hungary's segregation of the most and least disadvantaged students was the highest in the EU in the 2022 PISA study."
 Source: https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html (2025)

- Hungary's own PISA trend context: 
"Mathematics and reading results in 2022 are, in contrast, among the lowest in the history of Hungary's participation in PISA, and close to those observed in 2015."
 and the top-bottom gap 
"Over the most recent period (2018 to 2022), the gap between the highest-scoring students (10% with the highest scores) and the weakest students (10% with the lowest scores) did not change significantly in mathematics, reading and science."
 Source: OECD PISA 2022 Country Note, Hungary: https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2023/11/pisa-2022-results-volume-i-and-ii-country-notes_2fca04b9/hungary_5f1ae58d.html

- A 2025 ScienceDirect meta-comparison confirms the pattern is longstanding, not new: 
"In the 2022 PISA report, children's economic, social, and cultural status (ESCS) explained 12%−16% of the variance in reading, science, and mathematics, on average, in the OECD countries. In some countries, these values were higher than 20% (Belgium, France, Hungary, Peru, and Argentina)."
 Source: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666374025000111 (Feb 2025)

## 4. Hungary-specific mechanism studies: how the 6/8-year gimnázium track drives selection and segregation

**Kertesi & Kézdi's 100-towns study** is the load-bearing Hungarian causal evidence on the *sorting mechanism* (not the tracking-age literature per se, but directly relevant to "which new problems it creates"): 
"The distribution of Roma and non-Roma students across schools has become considerably more unequal in Hungary since the 1980's... Our results indicate that in Hungarian towns, free school choice diminishes the role of residential distribution because many students commute to schools of their choice. Towns where such commuting is more pronounced are characterized by stronger inter-school inequalities."
 A secondary summary of this same research: 
"It is not residential segregation, but free school selection that plays the strongest influence on school segregation."
 Sources: https://www.academia.edu/70729339/School_segregation_school_choice_and_educational_policies_in_100_Hungarian_towns ; https://www.romaeducationfund.org/free-school-selection-creating-roma-only-schools-in-hungary/

**Horn (dissertation/mimeo, "Early Selection in Hungary")** — direct causal study of the kisgimnázium tracks specifically: 
"Using a unique panel dataset I show that early selective tracks are more beneficial for higher status families. These tracks have a higher value-added in reading and mathematical literacy and in continuing studies in tertiary education. Their student composition is of higher status and higher skilled students. Thus they lead to an increase in the inequality of opportunities. Weaker tests show that while students' of early selective tracks gain, those left in general tracks loose."
 This is arguably the single most directly on-point Hungarian causal finding for the brief's core question. Source: https://www.academia.edu/1378060/Segregation_in_the_Primary_School_System_in_Hungary_Causes_and_Consequences (also referenced as Horn 2012, "Early selection in Hungary: A possible cause of high educational inequality")

**Entry-process study on the 6/8-year gimnázium admission exams** (2018/2019, on_education-linked): 
"My paper scrutinizes the entry process to the early academic tracks of the selective academic grammar schools in Hungary. The paper emphasizes the gradual nature of the secondary school selection process due to this optional school choice for 4 and 6 grad-ers. The results show that elementary schools with pupils from disadvantaged social backgrounds do not offer any chance to their pupils to participate in this competition. Those who wish to take the exams need a very solid family background. Families need to invest all sorts of capital: financial, social and cultural alike, in order to stay in the competition."
 Source: https://www.academia.edu/40987820/A_SZEGREG%C3%81CI%C3%93S_J%C3%89GHEGY_CS%C3%9ACSA

**Ethnic segregation trend data (Kertesi/Kézdi/Hajdu, updated through 2021)**: 
"Socio-economic and ethnic segregation are highly correlated and have increased in the past decades (Hajdu et al., 2022; Kertesi and Kézdi, 2012)."
 and structurally: 
"Roma students attend lower-quality education and select school tracks with lower earning potential."
 Source: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/236062933_Ethnic_Segregation_Between_Hungarian_Schools_Long-run_Trends_and_Geographic_Distribution

**Recent (2024, forthcoming 2026) causal study on high-poverty schools in Hungary**, Hermann & Kisfalusi, situates the tracked system as a segregation amplifier: 
"due to residential segregation, free school choice at the primary level, and the highly stratified tracked system at the secondary level, school segregation by social status is among the highest in Hungary compared with other European countries."
 Source: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/00207152231198434

## 5. Notes on evidence quality / gaps identified during this search
- The DiD tracking-age literature (Hanushek-Woessmann tradition and its meta-analytic replications) is methodologically strong and consistent on the *inequality* effect direction (early tracking → more inequality) but weaker/mixed on the *mean performance* effect — several sources hedge with "tendency," "less clear."
- The Bavaria (Piopiunik) and Poland natural experiments are the closest thing to quasi-experimental causal identification specific to tracking-age changes; both point the same direction, but the OECD's own 2011 re-evaluation of Poland found the achievement gap persisted despite the reform, which complicates a simple "delay tracking = solved" narrative.
- I could not find, within this search budget, a Hungary-specific natural-experiment (e.g., a policy change in Hungary that varied the legal age of entry into 6/8-year gimnázium) that would allow a genuine within-country DiD estimate — the strongest Hungarian evidence I found is observational/matching-based (Horn; Hermann & Kisfalusi; Kertesi-Kézdi), not a reform-based natural experiment. This is a real gap for causal transferability claims about Hungary specifically.
- I did not find 2025/2026-dated new empirical studies specifically re-estimating Hungary's 6/8-year gimnázium effect post-2020; the most recent Hungary-specific causal/mechanism evidence found dates to 2023-2024 (Hermann & Kisfalusi; EU Monitor 2025 statistics).
