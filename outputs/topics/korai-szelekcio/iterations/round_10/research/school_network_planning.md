## RESEARCH NOTES — School Network Planning Domain (Round 10)

**1. Network size and stability of the 6/8-year gimnázium track.** In the 2024/25 school year, secondary general education (gimnázium) was provided at 880 schools nationwide, in a dense network designed for accessibility. 
According to 2024/25 data from the Central Statistical Office, secondary education is provided at 880 schools across Hungary, and this dense network aims to ensure that every student can find a secondary general school close to their home.
 
The aim is for the school to be accessible by public transport in under an hour.
 Source: Eurydice/KSH, https://eurydice.eacea.ec.europa.eu/eurypedia/hungary/organisation-general-secondary-education

**2. Capacity is frozen despite massive demand — the core network-planning fact.** 
25,962 students studied in six- or eight-grade gimnázium programs in 2024/2025, roughly matching prior years' figures — the number of places has not risen despite enormous demand for "small-gimnázium" places.
 
Last year's admission round saw some top gimnáziums with roughly tenfold oversubscription on a single track.
 Concrete examples: 
38 places drew 369 applicants at ELTE Gyakorló's eight-grade track (HVG's #1-ranked school)
, and 
32 places for the 6+1 track at Radnóti drew 300 applicants.
 Source: eduline.hu (Feb 2026), https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/20260216_hatosztalyos-nyolcosztalyos-gimnazium-tuljelentkezes-ferohely-elitkepzes-szegregacio

**3. Policy direction is actively away from expanding this track.** 
The places in 6- and 8-grade programs have been essentially unchanged for years — not surprising, since recent education-policy decisions have instead steered students toward vocational training after 8th grade.
 This is a structural/fiscal choice with network implications: capacity is being directed toward technikum, not selective gimnázium expansion. Same source as above.

**4. Feeder-school effect — "hollowing out" of primary schools.** 
Class sizes in feeder primary schools shrink by 20-30% by 7th grade as students leave for gimnázium, based on 2019 research data, leaving behind students who typically don't even attempt the entrance exam.
 This is a direct school-network-planning finding: selection creates a bimodal remaining population in the feeder schools, with implications for class viability/economics at the sending institutions. Source: eduline.hu, same URL as above.

**5. Demographic collapse is the dominant structural driver right now, independent of selection policy.** 
In the last four years the birth rate has fallen by nearly twenty percent, and kindergarten closures are already happening, with a wave of school closures looming.
 
There are now 17,000 fewer one-year-olds than four-year-olds in Hungary, equivalent to 600-800 kindergarten/class groups — at least a hundred small-school's worth of children fewer.
 This is the single most load-bearing macro fact for network planning: any selection-scenario costing must be run against a shrinking student population. Source: Válasz Online, Oct 2025, https://www.valaszonline.hu/2025/10/17/ovodak-iskolak-bezarasa-demografia-oktatas/

**6. Government's stated posture on rural school consolidation.** 
A ministerial commissioner stated the goal is not closure but protecting small settlements and small schools as long as there is demand for them, while acknowledging the government will do everything to keep education running "even through mergers and local solutions."
 
In most places there is little organized resistance because child numbers are already falling while costs rise, and financially drained municipalities cannot absorb the resulting deficit.
 This signals that closures/consolidations are proceeding administratively (via tankerület-level decisions) rather than as a announced central policy, which matters for political-risk assessment of any network-restructuring scenario. Source: Válasz Online, same URL.

**7. Growing urban/suburban vs. shrinking rural divide — capacity mismatch has geographic asymmetry.** 
Findings reveal a deepening divide between dynamic urban/suburban zones and disadvantaged rural peripheries: rapidly growing urban settlements face rising child-teacher ratios (up to 24:1 in some areas), indicating capacity constraints, while disadvantaged children have become increasingly concentrated in rural areas (up to 94% in certain settlements).
 This is directly relevant to school-network economics: it is not simply "shrinkage everywhere" but a redistribution that strains urban capacity while emptying out rural, disadvantaged areas — meaning any 6/8-gimnázium expansion or contraction plays out very differently across settlement types. Source: academic study (2025), https://www.researchgate.net/publication/396483025

**8. Performance gap between selected and non-selected students is large and visible in competency data, feeding the "network prestige" dynamics that drive oversubscription.** 
On the 2024 competency assessment, Budapest sixth-graders in eight-grade gimnázium programs scored at minimum about 200 points above the national average in math, and still around 100 competency points above the Budapest average.
 This gap (evidence: national census-based competency test, strong data source but doesn't establish causal effect of the track itself vs. selection-on-ability) is the engine of parental demand pressure on the network. Source: eduline.hu, same URL as #2.

**9. Admission threshold data show high and rising selectivity bar.** 
Oversubscription was also visible in oral-interview cutoff scores, with sixth-graders admitted to the best gimnáziums averaging 73-75 points out of the entrance test.
 
For fourth-graders (entering 5th grade / 8-year track), well over 40 points in both Hungarian and math out of the total were needed to be called for the oral round at an elite gimnázium.
 Government-recommended minimum thresholds were also introduced system-wide: 
a gimnázium track requires at least 50 points to enter, while 6- and 8-grade tankerület gimnáziums have a recommended threshold of 70 points on the central written exam.
 Sources: eduline.hu (as above) and penzcentrum.hu, https://www.penzcentrum.hu/oktatas/20250121/gimnaziumi-felveteli-2025-hiaba-a-minimumponthatar-ha-nehezebb-a-feladatsor-1173381

**10. Free school choice regime amplifies socioeconomic sorting around selection points (Kertesi-Kézdi framework, still the reference model though studies are from 2004-2015).** 
In Hungary's regime of free school choice, higher-status parents with better bargaining power can move their children into better institutions when the local zoned school is inadequate (since empty seats exist), but lower-status parents can only do this to a limited extent — sharpening differences between schools because segregated schools see declining education quality.
 
Selection is further reinforced by the multiplicity of selection points (structure-changing schools, "Híd" bridge programs), which amplify the sorting effect of performance gaps that already emerge early among students.
 Evidence grade: strong for the sorting mechanism (large administrative datasets), but somewhat dated (last major quantitative update ~2009-2015); no equally rigorous recent (2023-2026) replication found in this search. Sources: MTAK repository, https://real.mtak.hu/203927/1/706 ; EPA archive of Kertesi-Kézdi 2009, https://epa.oszk.hu/00000/00017/00164/pdf/1_kertesi-kezdi.pdf

**11. School-choice/admission-difficulty trend data (rising competitiveness of entrance exams).** 
The Hungarian-language entrance exam for six-grade gimnáziums had not been this difficult in 11 years, according to the Alternatív Közgazdasági Gimnázium's own statistics from the January 2025 central written exam, taken by more than 68,000 students at 555 designated schools nationwide.
 This indicates rising selectivity pressure/difficulty calibration over time, relevant to whether the exam is functioning as intended (spreading results) or simply raising stress. Source: Telex, https://telex.hu/belfold/2025/01/19/kozepiskolai-felveteli-javitas-akg-nehezseg-statisztika-irasbeli

**12. Broader system context — socioeconomic disparity remains extreme, teacher shortage compounds rural disadvantage.** 
Hungarian students perform in line with the EU average in basic skills, but the disparity among students due to their socio-economic background is among the starkest in the EU, and schools with a disadvantaged profile are also hit harder by teacher shortages.
 
The early-school-leaving rate is higher in the least developed regions, in villages, and among Roma (58.7%, versus 9.3% for non-Roma in 2023).
 This underlines that any selection-alternative scenario must be evaluated against a backdrop of severe regional/ethnic disparity already baked into the system. Sources: EU Education and Training Monitor 2025, https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html

**13. Track popularity/scale for context.** 
In recent years six- and eight-grade gimnáziums have become increasingly popular, with 7,000-8,000 children entering such schools annually.
 Source: budapest.imami.hu (parent-guidance site, moderate-strength secondary source, consistent with KSH figures above), https://budapest.imami.hu/orszagos-hirek-iskola-ovikezdes-kamasz/nyolc-hat-vagy-negy-osztalyosba-menjen-kisgimnaziumba
