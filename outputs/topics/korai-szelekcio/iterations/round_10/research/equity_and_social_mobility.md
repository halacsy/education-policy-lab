# Research Notes: Early Selection & the 6/8-Year Gimnázium — Equity & Social Mobility Domain

## Finding 1: Hungary has one of the most socially stratified school systems among PISA countries
World Bank analysis of PISA data states Hungary has "one of the most socially stratified secondary school systems in the EU, similar in stratification to countries in Latin America, characterized by a large socioeconomic segregation between schools," and notes this is "especially striking in a country with relatively low income inequality."
Source: World Bank, "Hungary: Skilling up – the next generation" (PISA analysis), https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/340561468196449667/txt/101561-WP-P152132-Box393264B-PUBLIC-PISA-Hungary.txt

## Finding 2: No efficiency gains found from early selection; delayed tracking may help all students
The same World Bank report concludes: 
"The findings suggest that there are no efficiency gains from introducing early selection of students and that, in fact, delayed tracking can promote better performance for all students."
 It also frames the core policy tension: 
"Hungary needs to adopt policies that promote quality and address socioeconomic disadvantage at the same time."

Source: same as above.

## Finding 3: 2022 PISA — Hungary shows very high school selectivity and strong SES-performance link
OECD's Education GPS country profile confirms: 
"Hungary has a school system where school selectivity is more intense than in the other OECD and partner countries/economies"
 (2.85 PISA Index, rank 9/80 in 2022). Grade repetition, by contrast, is below OECD average 
"Some 6% of students in Hungary reported that they had repeated a grade at least once (OECD average: 9%) after entering primary school."

Source: OECD Education GPS, Hungary Country Profile (PISA 2022), https://gpseducation.oecd.org/CountryProfile?primaryCountry=HUN&treshold=10&topic=PI

## Finding 4: Large SES-linked achievement gap in reading (PISA-derived commentary)
A Hungarian Conservative commentary on PISA data reports: 
"The gap in the reading comprehension scores of the 10 per cent most socio-economically advantaged students were 170 points higher than that of the 10 per cent most socio-economically disadvantaged pupils."
 It further notes 
"the attainment of students is largely determined by their socio-economic background, which hurts the chances of disadvantaged student to thrive"
 and that 
"Schools tend to have students from similar socio-economic family background – instead of mixing students from different social classes – creating a huge performance gap."

Source: Hungarian Conservative, "PISA: Inequality in the Hungarian School System" (Jan 2024), https://www.hungarianconservative.com/articles/culture_society/pisa-inequality-in-the-hungarian-school-system/
(Note: this is a secondary/opinion-outlet synthesis of PISA data, not the primary OECD report itself — flag as moderate-strength sourcing pending primary OECD table confirmation.)

## Finding 5: Domestic competency-test data — achievement gap widens sharply after selection age, and grows across grades
A domestic analysis of Országos Kompetenciamérés (National Competency Assessment) data states that 6th/8th-grade gimnázium students outperform regular-school peers by roughly 160-175 points in grade 6, and that the performance gap between the strongest and weakest micro-regions widens progressively: 
"a különbség 6. évfolyamról 8. évfolyamra növekszik, majd a 8. és a 10. évfolyam között még tovább emelkedik: matematikából 339, 448, illetve 684, szövegértésből 344, 385, illetve 567 képességpont a szóban forgó különbség az egyes évfolyamokon."
 (i.e., gaps in math/reading skill-points grow from ~339/344 at grade 6 to ~684/567 by grade 10).
Source: Országos Kompetenciamérés edition summary, https://df2513.com/edition.html

## Finding 6: Horn Dániel (Corvinus) — 6/8-year gimnázium students show ~10% larger value-added, but selection effect confirmed
In a July 2026 podcast and follow-up press coverage, economist Horn Dániel presents value-added modeling (comparing initial and later competency-test scores, controlling for family background) showing: 
"az itt tanulók átlagosan mintegy 10%-kal nagyobb fejlődést érnek el, mint a négyosztályos gimnáziumba járó, hasonló képességű és társadalmi hátterű társaik... olyan statisztikai modellek alapján, amelyek a diákok kezdeti és későbbi kompetenciaméréseit hasonlítják össze, figyelembe véve többek között a családi hátteret."
 However, he also concedes agreement with sociologist Lannert Judit that 
"a hat- és nyolcosztályos gimnáziumok erősítik a társadalmi szelekciót."
 He speculates the value-added gain may partly reflect positive ability-peer effects rather than pure pedagogy: 
"részben az állhat a különbség mögött, hogy a jó képességű gyerekek felfelé húzzák egymást a hat- és nyolcosztályos gimnáziumokban."
 Only a small share of a cohort is affected: 
"A hat- és nyolcosztályos gimnáziumokba egy évfolyam tanulóinak mindössze nyolc százaléka jár."

Sources: 
- Telex/G7 podcast summary, https://telex.hu/g7/podcast/2026/07/04/dirik-podcast-horn-daniel-hatosztalyos-nyolcosztalyos-gimnaziumok
- Eduline, https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/20260710_elitgimnazium-hat-es-nyolcosztalyos-szelekcio-iskolarendszer-kompetenciameres-transzparencia
- Blogászat republish, https://www.blogaszat.hu/2026/07/hogyan-lehet-felso-kozeposztaly.html

## Finding 7: Horn Dániel earlier academic paper — selection widens SES-competency correlation between grade 6 and grade 8
In his TARKI paper on the role of "kisgimnázium" (early-selective gimnázium) in selection, Horn finds regional comparisons show: 
"míg 6. évfolyamon esélyegyenlőtlenség tekintetében nem volt különbség a két kistérségi csoport között, 8. évfolyamra azokban a kistérségekben, ahol van elérhető nyolcosztályos kisgimnázium a SES szignifikánsan erősebben korrelál a kompetenciaeredményekkel."
 (i.e., before selection there's no SES-inequality difference between regions with/without an early-selective gimnázium; by grade 8, in regions where one exists, SES becomes a significantly stronger predictor of competency scores.)
Source: Horn Dániel, "A kisgimnáziumok szerepe a szelekcióban," TARKI, https://tarki.hu/tarsadalomkutatas/publikaciok/tpubl_a_927.pdf

## Finding 8: HÉTFA working paper — school-size/type value-added study confirms mixed pattern for 6-year gimnázium specifically
A HÉTFA Research Institute working paper using the 2008-2014 national competency-measurement panel (over 1.1 million students) finds: 
"Az iskolatípus hatása is ugyanaz, 10. évfolyamon, a hat és nyolc évfolyamos gimnáziumokba járó diákok esetén nagyobb a hozzáadott érték, mint a négy évfolyamos... A hatosztályos gimnáziumok hatása nem ennyire egyértelmû, 8. évfolyamon a hatás negatív (kisebb a hozzáadott értéke, mint az általános iskoláknak); 10. évfolyamon már egyértelmûen nagyobb a hozzáadott értéke, mint a négyosztályos gimnáziumoknak."
 This nuances the "10% gain" headline: the 6-year track's value-added is negative at grade 8 and only turns positive by grade 10.
Source: HÉTFA Research Institute Working Paper 2015/11, "Az iskola méretének hatása az iskola hozzáadott értékére," https://hetfa.hu/wp-content/uploads/Hetfa_WP_2015_11_web.pdf_v%C3%A9gleges.pdf

## Finding 9: Berényi Eszter (ELTE) research project on entry inequality and resilience in 6/8-year gimnáziums
A 2019-2024 ELTE research project titled "Belépés a 6 és 8 osztályos gimnáziumokba: esélyegyenlőtlenség és reziliencia" is quoted characterizing the system: 
"Magyarországon többek között a hat- és nyolcosztályos középiskolák révén előáll a világ egyik legszelektívebb oktatási rendszere, ami tovább növeli az egyenlőtlenséget és az egész oktatás eredményességét is erodálja."
 The researcher also notes a political-economy dynamic sustaining the system across social groups who each see it serving different aspirational goals (e.g., domestic elite universities vs. going abroad), which helps explain persistence despite equity critiques.
Source: Qubit interview with Berényi Eszter, https://qubit.hu/2022/06/15/olyan-gyerek-akinek-kozmunkasok-a-szulei-hat-olyan-itt-nincs

## Finding 10: Bavaria (Germany) — postponing/shifting tracking age causally lowers all-track performance via peer effects
A quasi-experimental German study of a school reform effectively changing tracking timing found: 
"the reform lowered students' performance both in the basic and middle track. The performance decreased by about 13 PISA points in both tracks which equals approximately the performance gain in about half a school year."
 Crucially, the basic-track decline was attributed to peer composition: 
"lower student performance in the basic track is most likely due to peer effects only."
 The reform also 
"increased the performance dispersion, suggesting greater inequality of opportunity since achievement is strongly correlated with family background."

Source: ScienceDirect, "The effects of early tracking on student performance: Evidence from a school reform in Bavaria," https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0272775714000594

## Finding 11: Northern Ireland de-tracking reform — non-elite schools losing top students hurts those left behind
Same review cites a Northern Ireland study: 
"Guyon, Maurin, and McNally (2012) investigate an educational reform in Northern Ireland that led to a large increase in the share of students admitted to the elite track at age 11. They find a strong positive overall effect of this de-tracking reform on the number of students passing national examinations at later stages and a negative effect on student performance in non-elite schools who lost their most able students."
 This is directly relevant to the "peer effects on non-selected pupils" question: expanding the selective track helps entrants but appears to harm those remaining in general schools by removing high-achieving peers.
Source: ResearchGate summary of "The Economics of Tracking in Education" (Betts) citing Guyon, Maurin & McNally 2012, https://www.researchgate.net/publication/361503747_The_Effect_of_School_Tracking_on_Student_Achievement_and_Inequality_A_Meta-Analysis

## Finding 12: Romania — delaying tracking by two years had no aggregate labor-market effect, but helped disadvantaged students reach academic track (without raising university completion)
Malamud and Pop-Eleches's evaluation is cited: 
"evaluate a reform in Romania which postponed tracking of students into vocational and academic schools by two years, finding overall no effect on university completion, labor-market participation, or earnings. For disadvantaged students, they find an increased probability to finish the academic track which does, however, not translate into a higher probability to complete university."
 This is a useful counter-example showing delayed tracking is not automatically transformative for social mobility outcomes, tempering over-optimistic "delay solves everything" framing.
Source: same ResearchGate summary as Finding 11.

## Finding 13: Systematic literature review — mixed/contested evidence on ability grouping's harm to disadvantaged students
A recent (2025) European Sociological Review study frames the state of evidence: prior meta-analyses (Education Endowment Foundation 2021) find little average difference between grouped vs. mixed-ability classes, but 
"students in different ability groups will have lower-achieving...class peers, potentially disrupting their work and negatively impacting their achievement... The opposite holds true for those allocated to higher ability groups, who will benefit from positive peer effects. Much of the concern and empirical evidence regarding the negative impacts of between-class ability grouping focuses on those placed in the lowest groups... This tends to be students from disadvantaged backgrounds."
 This supports treating ability-grouping/tracking effects as genuinely contested in the general literature, not settled either way.
Source: Oxford Academic, European Sociological Review, "Does within-school between-class ability grouping harm the educational outcomes of socio-economically disadvantaged children?", https://academic.oup.com/esr/article/41/5/722/7925690

## Finding 14: Meta-analysis confirms tracking widens social achievement gaps (systemic evidence, large-N)
A meta-analytic difference-in-differences study combining 21 cycles of assessments states: 
"Following the seminal paper by Hanushek and Wößmann (2006), we combined data from a total of 21 cycles of primary and secondary school assessments to estimate difference-in-differences models for different outcome measures. We synthesized the effects using a meta-analytical approach and found strong evidence that tracking increased social achievement gaps."

Source: Springer, Educational Assessment, Evaluation and Accountability, "Early tracking and different types of inequalities in achievement," https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11092-020-09346-4

## Finding 15: Arany János Tehetséggondozó Program (AJTP) — a compensatory alternative aimed at disadvantaged, talented rural students
The AJTP is a 15+ year national program that channels disadvantaged, talented students from small settlements into good-quality gimnáziums with a preparatory/compensation year; official description: 
"Az Arany János Tehetséggondozó Program hátrányos helyzetű, ám tehetséges, 5000 lakosnál kisebb településekről származó fiatalok jó minőségű oktatást biztosító gimnáziumokban történő középiskolai tanulmányait támogatja. A 15 éve működő Arany János Tehetséggondozó Program (AJTP) keretei között és támogatásával mára több mint 5000 hátrányos helyzetű tehetséges tanuló szerzett érettségit."
 This represents a targeted-support alternative/complement to structural detracking — worth noting as a lower-political-risk, higher-cost-per-capita compensatory mechanism, though its scale (a few thousand students nationally) is far smaller than the population affected by 6/8-year gimnázium selection.
Source: Oktatáskutató és Fejlesztő Intézet cost-benefit analysis of AJTP, https://ofi.oh.gov.hu/publikacio/hatranyos-helyzetu-tanulok-tovabbtanulasat-segito-arany-janos-tehetseggondozo-program

## Finding 16: Government-dependent private/church schools amplify stratification alongside gimnázium tracking
OECD/PISA-based commentary notes: 
"The percentage of students attending government-dependent private schools in Hungary is one of the highest among PISA-participating countries and economies, at around 25.5%."
 This is structurally intertwined with the gimnázium selection question because maintainer choice (church, foundation, municipal/state) interacts with the 6/8-year admissions filter to further sort students by SES.
Source: Expat Focus / OECD PISA 2022 data summary, https://www.expatfocus.com/hungary/guide/hungary-education-and-schools ; corroborated by OECD Education GPS, https://gpseducation.oecd.org/CountryProfile?primaryCountry=HUN&treshold=10&topic=PI
