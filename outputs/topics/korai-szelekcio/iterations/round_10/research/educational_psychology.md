# Research Notes — Educational Psychology Domain: Early Selection & the 6/8-Year Gimnázium

**1. Hungary tracks unusually early, and selection is near-universal in coverage.** Compared to the OECD average (age 15), Hungary begins partial tracking at Grade 5 (age 10-11) via the 8-year gimnázium and again at Grade 7 (age 12-13) via the 6-year gimnázium; around 85% of Hungarian students attend schools that can select students based on records. Source: OECD Education Policy Outlook: Hungary, https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/about/projects/edu/education-policy-outlook/379201-Hungary-Profile.pdf

**2. Hungary already had unusually large socioeconomic performance gaps in PISA, tied to school selection.** The OECD profile notes the impact of socio-economic background on math performance was among the largest in OECD countries, with high variance between schools attributable to selection based on student records. Source: same OECD PDF as above.

**3. Current (2025) EU comparative data show Hungary now has the single worst segregation profile in the EU.** The 2025 Education and Training Monitor states Hungary has the highest level of segregation of students from less and more advantaged socio-economic groups into different schools, and that this was true again in the 2022 PISA study; the socioeconomic performance gap is 53 points vs. an EU average of 33. Additionally, 62% of Roma children aged 6-15 attend schools where all or most pupils are Roma. Source: European Commission, Education and Training Monitor 2025, Hungary country report, https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html

**4. Hungarian domestic research (Csapó et al., IEA/national assessment data) confirms selection sharply widens between-school variance after grade 8.** The paper on the selectiveness of the Hungarian educational system found the second selection point (after primary school, grade 8) increases mean achievement differences between schools in every domain, and that Hungarian between-school differences are relatively stable but the F-values (between-school variance ratio) rise from around 5 at the start of primary school to 25 by the end of secondary school. Source: Csapó, Molnár et al., IEA 2008, https://www.iea.nl/sites/default/files/2019-04/IRC2008_Csapo_Molnar_etal.pdf

**5. Domestic sociological work on the 8-year gimnázium ("kisgimnázium") shows it selects on both status and prior ability, and confers a large achievement premium.** Horn Dániel's TÁRKI paper found that students in 8-year gimnáziums score significantly higher (a 0.10-0.25 SD advantage) than comparable students in other school types even controlling for socioeconomic status, and that this select group represents only 2-3% of students yet the SES effect on this selection is very large. Source: Horn Dániel, "A kisgimnáziumok szerepe a szelekcióban," TÁRKI, https://tarki.hu/tarsadalomkutatas/publikaciok/tpubl_a_927.pdf

**6. Very recent (July 2026) reporting on Horn Dániel's newest research quantifies a ~10% larger learning-gain effect for 6/8-year gimnázium students vs. matched peers, but flags that peer/cream-skimming effects, not necessarily pedagogy, likely drive this.** According to this reporting, 
Horn Dániel kutatásai szerint a hat- és nyolcosztályos gimnáziumok tanulói átlagosan mintegy 10 százalékkal nagyobb fejlődést érnek el, mint a négyosztályos gimnáziumba járó, hasonló képességű és társadalmi hátterű társaik.
 Critically, 
a társak hatása különösen fontosnak tűnik: a válogatott tanulók pozitívan hatnak egymás teljesítményére, miközben azokban az osztályokban, ahonnan a legjobb diákok kisgimnáziumba távoznak, a bent maradók átlagos teljesítménye visszaesik.
 Only about 8% of a given cohort attends these 6/8-year gimnáziums, per this reporting. Sources: Telex/G7 Dirik podcast summary, https://telex.hu/g7/podcast/2026/07/04/dirik-podcast-horn-daniel-hatosztalyos-nyolcosztalyos-gimnaziumok ; Eduline, https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/20260710_elitgimnazium-hat-es-nyolcosztalyos-szelekcio-iskolarendszer-kompetenciameres-transzparencia

**7. Horn (per the same July 2026 reporting) proposes two policy alternatives: full restructuring to a 6+6 system, or demand-side incentives keeping the current structure.** As reported, 
egyfelől teljes struktúrát át lehetne alakítani, például úgy, hogy mostantól a 6+6 osztályos rendszer lenne az alapvetés, másfelől pedig különböző ösztönzőket kellene kitalálni... hogy a sima gimnáziumok több forrást kaphatnának, ami arra ösztönözné a jó képességű gyerekek szüleit, hogy ne vigyék el őket hatosztályos iskolákba.
 Portugal is cited as a comparison case for data-driven, cross-government-term education reform. Source: same Eduline article.

**8. International meta-analytic/cross-country evidence (Hanushek & Wößmann 2006, and replications) is the most load-bearing causal evidence on tracking generally.** Using difference-in-differences across PISA/TIMSS/PIRLS, they found: 
early tracking increases educational inequality; while less clear, there is also a tendency for early tracking to reduce mean performance; therefore, there does not appear to be any equity-efficiency trade-off.
 A 2021 replication using 20 years of data across 75 countries similarly 
found strong evidence that tracking increased social achievement gaps, that it had smaller but [effects on mean performance].
 However, one Polish reanalysis (Jakubowski 2010) using a different TIMSS/PISA pairing found no significant effect, illustrating that 
research to date on the effects of between-school tracking on inequalities in achievement and on performance has been inconclusive... different studies used different data, focused on different domains, and employed different measures of inequality.
 Sources: Hanushek & Wößmann, NBER Working Paper 11124, https://www.nber.org/papers/w11124 ; Springer replication, https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11092-020-09346-4

**9. Delayed-tracking natural experiment: Poland's 1999 reform (raising the comprehensive-schooling age by one year) produced large test-score gains, especially for lower-ability/vocational-track students, though later partially reversed.** The study found 
that, on average, the reform was associated with significant improvements: Poland improved its score in mathematics by 0.25 of a standard deviation, in reading, by 0.28 of a standard deviation, and in science, by 0.16 of a standard deviation.
 A companion Hungarian-language paper by Horn & Drucker directly analyzed this reform's relevance, titled "Kisebb szelekció - nagyobb kereset" (Narrower selection, wider return), suggesting Hungarian economists have already applied this natural experiment to domestic policy debate. However, 
despite the international recognition the Polish education system has been recently changed back alike to the old system functioning before 1999,
 showing delayed tracking is not politically permanent even where evidence favors it. Sources: Jakubowski, Patrinos, Porta & Wiśniewski, Education Economics 2016, https://hpatrinos.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/poland-tracking1.pdf ; EERA conference paper, https://eera-ecer.de/ecer-programmes/conference/24/contribution/48951

**10. The Big-Fish-Little-Pond Effect (BFLPE) is one of the most replicated findings in educational psychology and applies directly to selective-school self-concept, including cross-culturally and long after graduation.** The original 26-country test found 
the big-fish-little-pond effect (BFLPE)... posits that a student will have a lower academic self-concept in an academically selective school than in a nonselective school,
 and this was later shown to generalize across 41 culturally and economically diverse countries (Seaton, Marsh & Craven 2009, cited in Wikipedia bibliography). A German longitudinal study using the country's tracked system found 
the predictive effects of individual achievement test scores and school grades on math self-concept are very positive, whereas the predictive effects of school-average achievement are negative (the BFLPE)... substantial at the end of high school and was still substantial 2 years... or 4 years... later.
 A 2018 meta-analysis (Fang et al.) further confirms this is a robust, cross-study pattern. Sources: Marsh & Hau, PubMed abstract https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12971085/ ; Marsh, Trautwein, Lüdtke, Baumert & Köller, 2007, https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.3102/0002831207306728 ; Frontiers meta-analysis, https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2018.01569/full

**11. Qualitative/historical account: Hungary's 6/8-year gimnázium system emerged from a specific early-1990s political moment without deliberation on systemic effects, and researchers flagged selectivity risk as early as the late 1990s but the issue was politically minimized due to small enrollment share.** A 2022 Qubit interview with sociologist Berényi states 
a 8 osztályos gimnázium... az 1945 előtti sokkal elitistább és szelektívebb oktatás feltámasztása volt... A rendszerváltó hangulatban, a pluralizáció feletti örömben senki sem gondolkodott azon, hogy miként hatnak majd a kisgimnáziumok az egész rendszerre.
 Source: Qubit, https://qubit.hu/2022/06/15/olyan-gyerek-akinek-kozmunkasok-a-szulei-hat-olyan-itt-nincs

**12. Structural/historical framing: pre-1990 Hungary had a comprehensive 8+4 system with catchment areas; the shift to free school choice and early selection was a deliberate 1985-1993 deregulation.** Source: "Early tracking and competition – A recipe for major inequalities in Hungary," https://www.academia.edu/126047095/Early_tracking_and_competition_A_recipe_for_major_inequalities_in_Hungary — this source states 
the communist era forced a rather comprehensive system on Hungary (8-year-long primary and 4-year-long secondary education, catchment areas, centralized curriculum and financing). Stemming from as early as 1985, but completed mainly in 1993, Hungary liberated and decentralized its system... free school choice, free school establishment and early selection of students became possible.
