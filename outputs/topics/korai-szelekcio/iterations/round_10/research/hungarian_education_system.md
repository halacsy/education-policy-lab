# Research Notes: Early Selection and the 6/8-year Gimnázium (Round 10)

**1. Enrollment scale and capacity stagnation (2024/2025).** According to KSH data reported by Eduline, 25,962 students were enrolled in 6- or 8-year gimnázium programs in the 2024/2025 school year, a figure roughly unchanged from previous years despite enormous oversubscription — e.g., 369 applicants for 38 places at ELTE's practice gimnázium, and 300 applicants for 32 places in the Radnóti's 6+1 track. Source: https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/20260216_hatosztalyos-nyolcosztalyos-gimnazium-tuljelentkezes-ferohely-elitkepzes-szegregacio (Feb 2026)

**2. Achievement gap and social composition at admission.** The same Eduline piece cites a 2019 study finding that admission to 6/8-year tracks is concentrated among children whose parents have the time, skill, motivation, and money to prepare them (tutoring, open days), and that 2024 competency-measurement data show Budapest 6th-graders in 8-year gimnázium math scoring roughly 200 points above the national average and ~100 points above the Budapest average. Source: https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/20260216_hatosztalyos-nyolcosztalyos-gimnazium-tuljelentkezes-ferohely-elitkepzes-szegregacio

**3. New 2026 causal-style evidence on value-added.** Economist Dániel Horn (Corvinus University) reports in a July 2026 G7 "Dirik" podcast that students in 6/8-year gimnáziums show roughly 10% greater development than comparable-ability, comparable-background peers in 4-year gimnáziums, based on statistical models comparing initial and later competency-test results controlling for family background. Horn stresses this doesn't resolve whether the gain is from peer effects, better teachers being assigned to these classes, or an intrinsically better 6+6/8+4 pedagogical structure. Sources: https://telex.hu/g7/podcast/2026/07/04/dirik-podcast-horn-daniel-hatosztalyos-nyolcosztalyos-gimnaziumok and https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/20260710_elitgimnazium-hat-es-nyolcosztalyos-szelekcio-iskolarendszer-kompetenciameres-transzparencia

**4. Scale of the selective sector vs. total system.** Horn notes only about 8% of a given age cohort attends 6/8-year gimnáziums, versus nearly 2 million children/students in public and higher education overall — underlining that this is a small but highly salient elite segment. Source: https://telex.hu/g7/podcast/2026/07/04/dirik-podcast-horn-daniel-hatosztalyos-nyolcosztalyos-gimnaziumok

**5. Church-school selectivity growing.** A 2024 Dialectical Anthropology article states that church-run educational institutions in Hungary have doubled in number since 2010, and that unlike state-run primary schools bound to catchment-area intake, church-run schools can freely select their students, giving them greater autonomy and often stronger financial support. Source: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10624-024-09743-x (Nov 2024)

**6. Long-run academic and Roma segregation persists.** European Roma Rights Centre reporting states that 2024 research demonstrates Hungary's education system continues to fail many Romani children, referencing the unresolved 2013 ECtHR judgment and the still-open 2016 EU infringement proceedings on Romani school segregation. Source: https://www.errc.org/news/hungary-new-research-reveals-the-stubborn-persistence-of-segregation-of-romani-pupils

**7. Peer-reviewed causal study on poverty concentration (2023/2026).** Hermann and Kisfalusi, using large-scale Hungarian administrative data, find that attending a high-poverty school in Grade 8 is negatively associated with reading scores and secondary-education attainment, with a large direct negative effect on attainment beyond the indirect test-score channel. Source: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/00207152231198434 (Journal version 2026; original Sept 2023 at https://krtk.hun-ren.hu/en/2023/10/school-segregation-student-achievement-and-educational-attainment-in-hungary-by-zoltan-hermann/)

**8. PISA 2022 equity indicators.** OECD's Hungary country profile shows the ESCS (economic/social/cultural status)–math performance link explains 25.1% of variance (rank 3 of 79 systems), among the strongest socioeconomic-performance associations in PISA 2022. Source: https://gpseducation.oecd.org/CountryProfile?primaryCountry=HUN&treshold=10&topic=PI. The OECD's global PISA 2022 volume also notes the socioeconomic performance gap is widest in Romania and Slovakia, followed by Hungary, Israel, and Chinese Taipei. Source: https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/2023/12/pisa-2022-results-volume-i_76772a36/full-report/the-state-of-learning-and-equity-in-education-in-2022_e65c570e.html

**9. Historical baseline on early-selective track advantage (Horn 2013, cited in multiple sources).** Academic thesis work summarizes that early selective tracks show higher value-added in reading/math literacy and tertiary continuation, but their student composition skews toward higher-status/higher-skill students, increasing inequality of opportunity; weaker tests suggest students remaining in general tracks lose out. Source: https://www.academia.edu/1378060/Segregation_in_the_Primary_School_System_in_Hungary_Causes_and_Consequences

**10. Long-term labor-market null effect (CEU thesis using 1990s tracking-school rollout).** A CEU dissertation using event-study/fixed-effects methods on the expansion of tracking schools finds zero effects on tertiary education rates and labor-market outcomes (employment, unemployment, occupational choice), contradicting expectations that early selection would better prepare selected children for higher education. Source: https://www.etd.ceu.edu/2015/gurzo_klara.pdf

**11. Structural alternative floated by researchers (2026).** Horn suggests two broad alternative levers: (a) restructuring the whole system so 6+6 becomes the general model, or (b) creating incentives so upper-middle-class families keep children in less selective schools without banning the elite track outright. Source: https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/20260710_elitgimnazium-hat-es-nyolcosztalyos-szelekcio-iskolarendszer-kompetenciameres-transzparencia

**12. Non-structural policy currently pulling in the opposite direction.** Eduline reports that recent education-policy decisions have instead pushed students toward vocational training after Grade 8 (technikum), including higher stipends for vocational students than for some university students (e.g., general-medicine students), which is not a lever aimed at reducing gimnázium selectivity. Source: https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/20260216_hatosztalyos-nyolcosztalyos-gimnazium-tuljelentkezes-ferohely-elitkepzes-szegregacio
