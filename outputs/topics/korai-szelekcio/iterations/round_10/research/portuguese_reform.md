## Research Notes — Early Selection (Hungary 6/8-year gimnázium) viewed through the Portuguese comprehensive-school lens

**1. Cross-country causal evidence: early tracking raises inequality without raising average performance.**
The seminal Hanushek & Wößmann difference-in-differences study across up to 26 country comparisons found that 
the results suggest that early tracking increases educational inequality, and while less clear, there is also a tendency for early tracking to reduce mean performance, so there does not appear to be any equity-efficiency trade-off.
 Source: https://www.nber.org/papers/w11124 (also https://hanushek.stanford.edu/sites/default/files/publications/Hanushek+Woessmann%202006%20EJ%20116(510).pdf)

**2. Newer meta-analytic replications confirm the direction, sharpen the inequality channel.**
A 2021 meta-analysis combining 20 years of large-scale assessments 
found strong evidence that tracking increased social achievement gaps, that it had smaller but real effects on overall achievement inequality
, and a 2024 review states 
overall, tracking appears to increase social inequalities without fostering educational achievement
, though it notes 
there is no definite consensus
 among all scholars. Sources: https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11092-020-09346-4 ; https://www.degruyterbrill.com/document/doi/10.1515/zfsoz-2024-2014/html?lang=en

**3. OECD's own cross-national indicator work reaches the same conclusion on age-of-selection.** 
The OECD (2013, 2020a) showed that the age of first selection in an education system is correlated to inequity in performance but not to the mean performance level.
 Source: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0276562426000120

**4. Socioeconomic gaps are already large at age 10, before formal selection even occurs** — meaning tracking at 10-12 locks in, rather than creates, existing disparities. 
Disparities in performance related to socio-economic status develop early – even among pupils as young as 10 – and widen throughout students' lives; on average across 11 OECD countries, about two-thirds of the achievement gap observed at age 15 and more than half of the gap among 25-29 year-olds was already seen among 10-year-olds.
 Source: https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/equity-in-education_9789264073234-en.html

**5. Hungary is now the most segregated system in the EU by socioeconomic status, and the parental-education achievement gap is far above the EU average.** 
On average in the EU there is a 33-point difference between the performance of a child of a graduate and a non-graduate parent, but this gap is 53 points in Hungary's case; Hungary has the highest level of segregation of students from less and more advantaged socio-economic groups into different schools, and this was the highest in the EU in the 2022 PISA study.
 Source: https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html

**6. Hungarian administrative-data research confirms the school-composition channel operates causally, not just descriptively.** Hermann & Kisfalusi (2023), using matching on large-scale administrative data, 
examine the effects of attending a high-poverty school in Grade 8 on academic achievement and later educational attainment, finding that attending a high-poverty school is negatively associated with reading scores and secondary education attainment, while there is no significant association with math scores.
 Source: https://krtk.hun-ren.hu/en/2023/10/school-segregation-student-achievement-and-educational-attainment-in-hungary-by-zoltan-hermann/

**7. The elite gimnázium track itself performs at world-class level — the "efficacy" argument's strongest evidentiary basis — but this coexists with, and may be enabled by, the segregation described above.** A World Bank analysis notes 
the performance of students in the general secondary schools (Gimnázium), which follow a purely academic track, is equivalent to the average of top PISA-performers like Switzerland, Japan, or Korea.
 Source: https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/340561468196449667/pdf/101561-WP-P152132-Box393264B-PUBLIC-PISA-Hungary.pdf — This is the empirical crux of the trade-off debate: high performance for the selected minority vs. system-wide inequity.

**8. Portugal's own alternative model — no early academic tracking (comprehensive basic education to grade 9, differentiation only at 15) — is NOT a clean success story either; it substitutes grade retention as its sorting mechanism, and retention is only slowly declining.** 
In Portugal, according to PISA 2015 and 2018 data, 31.2% and 26.6% of 15-year-olds had been retained at least once during their school career.
 
Meta-analyses indicate that the benefits of grade retention for students' academic competencies can be null or even negative.
 Source: https://epaa.asu.edu/index.php/epaa/article/download/7586/3076 — This is directly relevant to Hungary: abolishing early academic tracking without also curbing retention/streaming-by-other-means may simply relocate the sorting mechanism rather than remove it.

**9. Portugal's targeted compensatory-school program (TEIP), the closest structural analogue to "support instead of selection," shows mixed, contested results after ~15+ years.** An MDPI 15-year evaluation found 
there seems to have been a larger decrease in early school leaving rates (retention/non-completion) in TEIP schools than in non-TEIP public schools from 2007/2008 to 2010/2011
, but simultaneously 
there is an increase in the gap between TEIP and non-TEIP public schools in the results of national exams/assessment tests in Mathematics and Portuguese Language.
 A 2026 follow-up using DGEEC administrative data confirms persistence of the gap: 
TEIP schools consistently present higher retention and dropout rates than national averages, together with considerable variation across border municipalities, pointing to the continuing influence of territorial conditions in educational outcomes.
 Sources: https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7102/9/4/270 ; https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/education/articles/10.3389/feduc.2026.1812912/full

**10. Portugal's recent PISA trajectory shows equity actually worsening despite the non-selective structure and TEIP, warning against assuming "no early tracking" is sufficient on its own.** The 2025 EU Education and Training Monitor for Portugal reports 
socio-economic background has a strong influence on educational outcomes, with 46.9% underachieving in maths in 2022, compared with 39.4% in 2018; inequalities have widened in line with EU trends with only 16.0% of low socio-economic status students performing well, declining from 21.0% in 2015.
 Source: https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/portugal.html

**11. School choice / hand-picking, not only formal tracking, is an independent segregation driver relevant to both countries.** Regarding Hungary specifically, older but structurally relevant research states 
Hungary adopted such a free school choice system for its elementary schools, with a complete discretion allowed for schools to hand-pick their students.
 Source: https://www.academia.edu/1378060/Segregation_in_the_Primary_School_System_in_Hungary_Causes_and_Consequences — This suggests the admissions/school-choice architecture around the 6/8-year gimnázium (not the tracking age per se) may be an independently manipulable lever, separate from the track's existence.

**Synthesis note for the structured-analysis phase:** The strongest, most convergent evidence (multiple independent methodologies, cross-country DiD, meta-analyses, OECD indicators, Hungarian administrative-data causal study) supports: early tracking → higher inequality, unclear-to-negative effect on system-wide mean performance, with Hungary as close to a worst-case realization (highest EU segregation + above-average parental-background gap) alongside a genuinely high-performing elite track. Portugal's comprehensive counter-model is not a template for painless equity: it shows that removing early *academic* tracking does not by itself fix equity if retention, school-choice segregation, and under-resourced compensatory programs remain — Portugal's own equity trend has *worsened* recently despite no age-10 selection. This argues for treating "abolish early tracking" and "fix admissions/choice/retention practices" as two distinct policy levers, not one.
