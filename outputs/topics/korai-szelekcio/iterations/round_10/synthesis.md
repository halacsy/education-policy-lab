# Synthesis

## Overview
Twelve expert analyses converge on a well-evidenced empirical core — Hungary's 6/8-year gimnázium tracks select roughly 8–12% of each cohort at age 10–12 through central examinations that strongly favor high-SES, urban families; Hungary shows the EU/OECD's highest socio-economic segregation and achievement-gap indicators; the measured ~10% value-added premium for selected students is contested as to mechanism (pedagogy vs. peer composition/cream-skimming); and international comparative evidence (Finland, Poland, Portugal, Bavaria, Romania, Northern Ireland) shows early tracking reliably widens inequality without raising mean performance, though reform durability and mechanism transfer to Hungary remain open questions. Beyond this shared empirical base, the record diverges sharply on what follows: whether the core problem is the selection mechanism itself, the school-choice/maintainer architecture, demographic collapse, legal-institutional rigidity, political fragility, financial incentives, or psychological costs to non-selected pupils — each expert grounding a partially distinct causal story and thus a distinct reform lever. No expert claims the status quo is costless, but there is no consensus on which lever would work, how fast, or whether removing early selection would relocate rather than resolve the sorting problem.

## Disagreement map
### Whether early selection itself is the primary driver of inequality, or whether school choice/catchment/maintainer architecture is the deeper cause
- **international_comparison, finnish_reform, polish_reform, educational_psychology**: Early selection at age 10-12 is itself a structural driver of socio-economic segregation and achievement-gap widening, distinct from and additive to school-choice effects; international tracking-age evidence (Hanushek-Woessmann meta-analyses, Poland's 1999 reform, Finland's peruskoulu) shows delaying selection reduces inequality. Why: Grounded in cross-country difference-in-differences and natural-experiment causal designs (21-cycle PISA/TIMSS meta-analysis, Poland's causally-identified 0.25 SD gain) that isolate age-of-tracking as a treatment variable, holding other institutional features constant across comparison cases.
- **hungarian_education_system, portuguese_reform, education_finance** (minority): Free school choice and catchment-area sorting are structurally separable from formal tracking and would continue to drive segregation even if early selection were abolished; removing selection alone would relocate rather than resolve the sorting mechanism (as seen in Portugal's retention-based substitute sorting). Why: Grounded in observational studies of Hungarian school-choice behavior (Kertesi-Kézdi) and the Portuguese counterfactual case, where abolishing academic tracking without addressing admissions discretion and retention practices left equity trends worsening — showing the causal locus is admissions/choice architecture, not selection age per se.

### Whether the ~10% value-added premium for selected students reflects genuine pedagogical quality or composition/peer effects
- **hungarian_education_system, equity_and_social_mobility, educational_psychology, education_finance**: The measured value-added premium is predominantly attributable to selection/composition effects (cream-skimming, peer effects) rather than distinctive pedagogy, and the current Education Minister's competence-test analysis finds no measurable value-added once prior ability and family background are controlled. Why: Grounded in value-added panel studies (Horn; HÉTFA 1.1M-student panel) and the Ministry's own competence-test decomposition holding initial ability and SES constant, which attribute most of the raw gap to composition rather than instructional quality.
- **hungarian_education_system** (minority): The mechanism behind the 10% value-added gain remains genuinely unresolved — Horn himself acknowledges it could reflect peer effects, teacher assignment, or pedagogical structure, and treating it as settled composition-only would overstate certainty. Why: Grounded directly in the primary researcher's own stated uncertainty (Horn, 2026 podcast) about decomposing the value-added figure, distinct from the Ministry's more definitive framing.

### Whether demographic collapse or selection-policy design is now the dominant force shaping the school network's future
- **demography, school_network_planning**: Cohort collapse (30-35% birth decline since the 1990s) is now the dominant structural force reshaping the school network through 2040, forcing consolidation regardless of selection-policy choice; selection-policy reform cannot be considered in isolation from this mechanical demographic shrinkage. Why: Grounded in demographic-yearbook and administrative enrollment data showing mechanical, policy-independent cohort shrinkage (KSH figures, network capacity mismatches) that will force consolidation decisions irrespective of any selection reform outcome.
- **equity_and_social_mobility, legal_and_governance, political_feasibility** (minority): The selection mechanism's equity and political-legal costs (segregation, examination-based exclusion, maintainer veto points) are independent problems requiring direct policy attention regardless of demographic trends, and consolidation without selection reform risks entrenching rather than resolving segregation. Why: Grounded in legal-statutory analysis (maintainer veto structures, felmenő rendszer constraints) and equity-focused causal evidence (segregation indices, Roma school concentration) that treat the selection mechanism as an independently actionable policy lever, separate from demographic capacity planning.

### Whether structural reform of early selection is currently feasible, or whether legal, administrative, and political constraints make near-term abolition unrealistic
- **education_finance, hungarian_education_system**: Structural alternatives are financially and administratively feasible at moderate transition cost (tens to low hundreds of millions of forints), and demographic decline has eroded the original efficiency rationale for early selection, making the system increasingly hard to justify. Why: Grounded in cost-function analysis of the existing 880-school network (per-pupil cost curves, historical 2000s reorganization precedent) suggesting steady-state costs differ modestly by track type and transition costs are the dominant financial variable, not systemic unaffordability.
- **legal_and_governance, implementation_planning, political_feasibility** (minority): Near-term structural abolition is legally and administratively unrealistic: the felmenő rendszer (ascending-system) principle forces an 8-year phase-out for any Nkt. amendment, church-maintainer autonomy creates a political-legal veto point, the teaching workforce is aging with limited capacity for redeployment, and the current minister has already retreated to a consultative stance under political pressure. Why: Grounded in statutory text analysis (Nkt. felmenő rendszer clause, maintainer funding-agreement structure) combined with real-time political-event tracking (minister's June 2026 retreat, opposition mobilization, workforce demographic data) that together identify concrete institutional and political blocking mechanisms, not merely cost estimates.

### Whether the psychological costs of early selection (self-concept, motivation) constitute a distinct harm mechanism beyond the economic/demographic and segregation arguments
- **educational_psychology** (minority): Beyond composition and segregation effects, early selection imposes a distinct psychological cost: the robustly-replicated big-fish-little-pond effect lowers academic self-concept among selected pupils, while competitive ranking structures push adolescents toward performance-avoidance goal orientation rather than mastery orientation, and stigmatized/low-SES pupils face amplified (though contested-magnitude) teacher-expectancy and stereotype-threat effects. Why: Grounded in a distinct evidence base from educational psychology (Marsh & Hau's 41-country BFLPE replication, Deci & Ryan self-determination theory, Dweck/Elliot goal-orientation literature) identifying a within-individual psychological mechanism — self-concept and motivational orientation shifts — that is causally and conceptually separate from the compositional/economic sorting mechanisms cited by other experts.

## What the experts agree on
- Early selection into 6/8-year gimnázium tracks admits roughly 8-12% of each age cohort at age 10-12 via central written examinations, and entrants are strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families. [evidence: strong] (hungarian_education_system, equity_and_social_mobility, school_network_planning, education_finance, legal_and_governance, educational_psychology)
- Hungary exhibits among the highest socio-economic achievement-gap and school-segregation indicators in the OECD/EU (ESCS explaining 19-25% of PISA variance vs. 12-14% OECD average; 53-point parental-education gap vs. 33-point EU average). [evidence: strong] (hungarian_education_system, international_comparison, equity_and_social_mobility, portuguese_reform, education_finance, demography, school_network_planning, educational_psychology)
- International cross-country evidence (meta-analyses across 21-75 countries) consistently shows that earlier academic tracking increases socio-economic achievement gaps without raising system-wide mean performance. [evidence: strong] (international_comparison, portuguese_reform, equity_and_social_mobility, educational_psychology, finnish_reform)
- Poland's 1999 tracking-delay reform produced measurable equity and learning gains but was reversed in 2017 for explicitly political/values-based reasons rather than on evidentiary grounds, demonstrating that research-supported reform is not politically durable. [evidence: strong] (polish_reform, international_comparison, educational_psychology)
- Private admission-preparation costs (tutoring, prep courses) are substantial and unequally distributed, functioning as a de facto entry fee despite formally free tuition, and are largely accessible only to higher-income families. [evidence: strong] (education_finance, legal_and_governance, implementation_planning, hungarian_education_system)
- Hungary's annual birth cohort has declined by roughly 30-35% since the early 1990s, mechanically shrinking demand for all secondary tracks and forcing school-network consolidation independent of selection-policy design. [evidence: strong] (demography, school_network_planning, implementation_planning)
- Church and foundation school maintainers, whose share has grown substantially since 2010 and who can freely select students unlike catchment-bound state schools, complicate any uniform structural reform of early selection. [evidence: strong] (hungarian_education_system, legal_and_governance, political_feasibility, school_network_planning)
- As of mid-2026, structural reform of early selection is a live but politically fragile issue: the education minister's critical remarks triggered strong opposition mobilization framing reform as coercive imposition, and the government has since retreated to a consultative rather than legislative stance. [evidence: strong] (political_feasibility, implementation_planning, legal_and_governance)
