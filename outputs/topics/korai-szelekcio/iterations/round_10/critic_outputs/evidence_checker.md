# Critique: evidence_checker

## S1.mechanism
Objection: The scenario tags as “moderate” evidence the claim that a “coaching‑resistant exam design and SES‑aware admission proxies could reduce the correlation between private tutoring investment and admission success.” The curated registry does not support a moderate causal claim here: prep‑cost and parental‑attachment studies (Berényi/Eduline references invoked in the text) document the existence and distribution of private preparation, but do not provide causal evidence that specific test redesigns reliably break the link between private tutoring and admission outcomes. The closest relevant registry items (parent_attachment [moderate]; school_choice [moderate]) document parental demand and sorting channels, not tested interventions on exam design. Labeling the effect as “moderate” overstates what the sources support — at present the evidence for effective, scalable, coaching‑resistant exam designs that reduce SES‑bias is weak and context‑dependent.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Downgrade the evidence tag to weak and reframe the mechanism to reflect uncertainty: (a) state that empirical support for exam redesign reducing tutoring advantage in Hungary is limited; (b) add a concrete evidence‑building step (a multi‑cohort pilot RCT or regression‑discontinuity evaluation of redesigned items with psychometric validation and pre/post measures of prep‑spending and admitted cohorts) before scaling; and (c) cite international psychometric trial literature (or note its absence) rather than implying proven causal impact.

## S1.assumptions
Objection: The scenario assumes ministerial decree (EMMI rendelet) is sufficient to implement SES‑aware admission proxies and uniform thresholds because admissions “currently sit at decree level rather than in the Nkt.” This misreads the legal/maintainer landscape as represented in the curated registry: legal [strong] says 6/8‑year forms and maintainer arrangements are anchored in the public education act and maintainer funding/ autonomy agreements create veto points. In practice, church and foundation maintainers have legally protected autonomy and may not be bound by a ministry decree in the same way as state schools. The text therefore understates the legal barriers and overstates the reach of a decree without primary‑law change or negotiated maintainer consent.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Replace the assumption with a legally qualified pathway: (a) commission and cite a formal legal opinion mapping which admission rules can be changed by decree versus which require Nkt. amendment or maintainer agreement; (b) add an explicit implementation branch that either (i) secures maintainer buy‑in via negotiated amendments to funding/maintenance contracts or (ii) pursues a statutory amendment (with an expected felmenő timeline) if enforceability across non‑state maintainers is required; and (c) budget time for parliamentary or contractual negotiation in the timeline and uncertainty sections.

## S3.assumptions
Objection: S3 treats as an implementable assumption that Hungary’s existing teacher workforce can absorb an extended comprehensive lower‑secondary phase without a Finland‑scale, decade‑long professionalization, even while noting the workforce is ageing. The curated registry includes teacher_shortage [strong] (sustained shortage and an ageing workforce concentrated in disadvantaged regions). That evidence contradicts the scenario’s implicit confidence that large‑scale transitions can proceed on the proposed 2–9 year timeline without an explicit, modelled workforce plan. The scenario therefore underestimates the magnitude and timing constraints imposed by retirements, recruitment pipelines, and retraining capacity.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add a binding teacher‑workforce feasibility requirement before committing to timelines: (a) commission a detailed workforce feasibility study modelling retirements, attrition, recruitment rates, and retraining throughput under multiple scenarios; (b) condition timelines and teacher‑professionalization commitments on the study’s outputs (e.g., phased, regionally staggered pilots where capacity exists); and (c) include contingency plans (temporary staffing, targeted incentives for shortage regions, and explicit budget lines for multi‑year retraining) if workforce modelling shows the proposed schedule is infeasible.
