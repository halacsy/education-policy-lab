# Critique: devil_advocate

## S1.assumptions
Objection: The scenario's pivotal assumption—that decree-level admission-equity rules (SES-aware quotas, uniform thresholds) can be designed and enforced “with reasonable consistency” across state, church, and foundation maintainers—rests on no concrete legal or political mechanism and contradicts the scenario's own acknowledgement of constitutionally-protected maintainer autonomy. The cited evidence level (moderate/weak) does not support the claim that a ministerial decree can bind church/foundation schools: existing funding agreements and autonomy protections create a clear veto point so enforcement is implausible absent primary-law change or renegotiated funding accords. If this assumption fails, the reform will be limited to state schools and displaced advantaged demand will simply relocate to non-state selective places, collapsing the scenario's load-bearing equity claim.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Replace the blanket enforceability assumption with an explicit legal-political pathway: (a) commission and publish a legal opinion mapping which aspects of admissions can be lawfully conditioned for church/foundation maintainers under existing funding agreements; (b) specify whether primary legislation (Nkt. amendment) or renegotiated funding contracts are required and include a timeline; (c) design concrete incentive-compatible instruments (e.g. conditional supplementary normative funding, voluntary maintainer compacts with phased compliance bonuses, or targeted procurement of places) and model their likely uptake; or else recast S1 as state-only admissions reform and explicitly model displacement effects to non-state providers.

## S2.assumptions
Objection: S2 assumes state-sector capacity caps or minimum-score thresholds can materially shrink the selective footprint without being offset by church/foundation or private expansion, yet the scenario itself reports strong evidence of a threefold rise in non-state selective enrolment (10%→25–30%). No legal, fiscal, or negotiated mechanism is specified to prevent non-state actors from absorbing displaced demand. Absent such mechanisms, contraction in the state sector will almost certainly be neutralized by expansion elsewhere, turning the policy into mere reallocation rather than net desegregation.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: A workable S2 must include explicit, evidence-grounded countermeasures to absorption risk: (1) model displacement scenarios using maintainer- and programme-level enrolment data to quantify likely non-state uptake under different contraction sizes; (2) propose concrete levers to limit absorption—e.g. temporary moratoria on new selective-class approvals tied to funding, conditionality clauses in state funding transfers to non-state maintainers, negotiated capacity-purchase agreements, or fiscal disincentives for opening new selective places—and obtain a legal review of their viability; (3) if legal constraints preclude these levers, reframe S2 as a partial, state-only contraction and append an explicit evaluation plan that measures net system-level segregation (state + non-state) before claiming equity gains.

## S3.assumptions
Objection: The scenario hinges on the assumption that Hungary's existing teacher workforce can absorb a shift to a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase without completing a Finland-scale, decade-long teacher-professionalization programme. This contradicts the scenario's own strong-evidence citation that Finland's gains were bundled with 5-year master-level teacher education and long-term professionalization; it also conflicts with documented Hungarian constraints (half of teachers aged 50+, only 7.2% under 30, ongoing 2024–2031 salary reform). If the workforce cannot deliver higher-quality comprehensive instruction, delaying tracking risks lowering instructional quality system-wide and nullifying the expected equity and attainment benefits.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Make workforce capacity an explicit precondition and operationalize it: (a) commission a detailed teacher-workforce feasibility study (retirement/attrition projections, recruitment capacity, retraining throughput and costs) and publish thresholds that must be met before national roll-out; (b) build a phased pilot plan where delayed tracking is trialled regionally only after targeted investments in recruitment, 1–3 year in-service master-equivalent upskilling, and mentoring for reallocated teachers; (c) budget and timetable the requisite teacher-education expansion (student places, scholarships, accelerated CPD) and tie national scaling to independently reviewed teacher-quality benchmarks rather than assuming competence can be produced in parallel with legislative change.

## S4.assumptions
Objection: S4 assumes TEIP-style targeted funding commitments will be durable despite the scenario's own acknowledgement of a projected 6.2% deficit and historical patterns where targeted lines are first to be cut. The evidence cited (moderate) for TEIP impact does not address the fiscal-political durability question: without statutory ring-fencing, targeted funding is vulnerable to consolidation, which would negate intended equity benefits and leave admission-discretion and school-choice segregation untouched. This is a concrete failure mode: funding that disappears during consolidation yields no lasting school-level change for disadvantaged pupils.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Strengthen fiscal durability and reduce dependence on politically-fragile targeted lines: (a) produce a multi-year costed budget for the TEIP-style programme and specify funding sources (earmarked tax, social contributions, EU funds, or reallocated recurrent budgets); (b) propose legal protections (cardinal-law earmark, multi-year budget commitments, or constitutionally-backed safeguards) and model scenarios showing programme resilience under a 3–5% consolidation shock; (c) redesign the intervention to contain a universal baseline (smaller per-school guaranteed floor) plus scalable targeted top-ups so that core benefits persist even if targeted budgets are partially reduced.
