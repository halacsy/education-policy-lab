# Critique: equity_checker

## S1.equity_impact
Objection: The equity_impact claim — that admission-mechanism reform will 'likely produce a modest reduction' in family-capital-driven sorting — is concrete in direction but vague and incomplete about who is actually reached and who is left out. It fails to (a) identify which subgroups of disadvantaged children gain access (e.g. low-SES high-performing Roma pupils, rural high-performers, urban low-SES pockets), (b) model the absorptive response of non-state maintainers (church/foundation) under plausible non-compliance rates, and (c) specify enforcement/incentive mechanisms that would prevent advantaged families from redirecting private preparation into new channels. This is not just a wording gap: the scenario leans on an evidence tag of 'moderate' for admission-mechanism effects but cites no Hungarian causal estimate that isolates decree-level admission changes from maintainer substitution or private-market adaptation; therefore the stated equity outcome is unsupported at the level of subgroup distribution and durability. (Speculative: the claim about modest reduction assumes effective cross-maintainer enforcement and little market adaptation, neither evidenced.)
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Revise the equity_impact to (1) disaggregate expected effects by concrete subgroups (low-SES high-performers, rural students, Roma students, urban poor) using available Oktatási Hivatal/KSH admissions and enrollment microdata; (2) present scenario variants under explicit non-state compliance rates (e.g. 0%, 50%, 100%) showing projected changes in selective-track SES composition and net displacement; and (3) add a named enforcement/incentive package (legal instrument or fiscal lever) describing how church/foundation maintainers would be bound or incentivized, with a short legal-evidence footnote that explains what existing documents do/ do not support about ministerial reach. If empirical microdata are unavailable, add a transparent microsimulation (assumptions listed) rather than the present undifferentiated 'likely modest' phrasing.

## S2.equity_impact
Objection: The equity_impact correctly flags displacement risk but then asserts that contraction 'is likely to relocate rather than reduce net selectivity' without quantifying which communities or cohorts are harmed and on what timescale. Concretely: it does not identify whether contraction would disproportionately exclude (i) low-SES high-performing children who cannot follow selective places into non-state schools, (ii) rural students for whom travel/time costs make accessing consolidated selective sites infeasible, or (iii) marginal middle-SES families. The scenario's own evidence tag is 'weak' for the mechanism, yet it moves quickly to a strong normative claim about likely displacement — a mismatch between evidence strength and the certainty of the projected equity outcome. The absence of region-by-region capacity and maintainer-response modelling means the scenario cannot reliably state whether net segregation rises or simply moves location. (Speculative.)
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Amend the equity_impact to report explicit distributional projections: (1) run/commission a regionally disaggregated model showing how capacity caps would reallocate selective places across state vs non-state maintainers and estimate which SES/ethnic subgroups would gain/lose under plausible maintainer-expansion scenarios; (2) include mobility/accessibility effects (travel time, boarding choices) to show rural vs urban differential impacts; and (3) align language to evidence strength — replace categorical words like 'likely' with outcome ranges conditional on empirically-specified counterfactuals (e.g. 'if non-state expansion is unconstrained, model X shows net segregation rises by Y index points'). Add monitoring metrics to detect displacement within the first two admission cycles.

## S3.equity_impact
Objection: While S3 correctly conditions equity gains on concurrent anti-sorting rules, its equity_impact still treats the Poland/Finland comparative evidence as broadly transferable without sufficiently anchoring who wins and who loses during the long felmenő (phase-in) transition. It omits a cohort-level risk analysis: the scenario does not say which enrolled cohorts (those mid-transition) will lose selective-track access, nor does it quantify short- to medium-term harms to disadvantaged students if anti-sorting enforcement lags. It also downplays the teacher-workforce distribution problem — the scenario assumes retraining and reallocation can be sequenced around an aging teacher corps without showing how disadvantaged areas will be prioritized; that omission understates an important equity channel (teacher shortages hitting disadvantaged schools harder). The scenario's use of strong evidence from Finland is not wrong, but it lacks careful contextualization given the large institutional differences, so the equity-claim is overstated relative to the available Hungary-specific evidence. (Speculative about transition cohort impacts and enforcement speed.)
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Revise equity_impact to include (1) a cohort-by-cohort impact table showing which birth cohorts are affected during the felmenő rollout and how their probability of selective-track access changes; (2) a sequencing and enforcement timeline with named early-warning indicators (admissions composition by SES, maintainer compliance rates) and contingency measures if anti-sorting rules are delayed; (3) an explicit teacher-deployment priority plan that models retirements and reallocations and guarantees staffing minimums for disadvantaged schools during the transition; and (4) a caveat reconciling Finland evidence with Hungarian maintainer plurality and workforce constraints, reducing claims of automatic transfer and instead presenting conditional effect sizes or ranges.

## S4.equity_impact
Objection: The equity_impact labels non-structural reform as 'plausibly equity-neutral-to-negative' but fails to specify which disadvantaged subgroups are most likely to be left behind (notably low-SES high-performers who are screened out at age 10-12), and it does not propose concrete design elements that would mitigate that specific failure mode. It also treats TEIP durability and funding protection as an assumption despite listing strong fiscal-consolidation risk — yet the scenario's policy success relies on long-run targeted funding. The evidence tag for Portugal is 'moderate' and the scenario correctly notes mixed outcomes, but it does not quantify how Portugal's mixed results map onto Hungary's distinct maintainer and school-choice architecture, leaving policymakers without clear diagnostics on whether compensatory funds would substitute for admission access. (Not strictly unsupported but insufficiently specific.)
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Strengthen equity_impact by (1) explicitly naming the subgroup 'low-SES high-performing 10–12-year-olds' as the primary at-risk population and explaining why compensatory funding to home schools does not substitute for selective-track access; (2) propose concrete program additions to TEIP-style funding aimed at mitigating this gap (e.g., guaranteed admission preference/quotas for disadvantaged high-performers, transport/boarding subsidies, targeted outreach and diagnostics, pre-admission bridging curricula); (3) include a financing durability plan (cardinal-law funding floor or escrowed multi-year commitment) and sensitivity analysis showing outcomes under funding cuts; and (4) add monitoring indicators that disaggregate outcomes for low-SES high-performers separately from school-average metrics.
