# Critique: coherence_checker

## S1.assumptions
Objection: The assumption that ‘Legal reform via ministerial decree (EMMI rendelet) is sufficient to implement these changes’ is contradicted elsewhere in the scenario: the Political risks and Equity impact explicitly note that church and foundation maintainers are constitutionally autonomous and likely not bound by decree-level admissions rules. The scenario provides no legal evidence that a ministerial decree can override maintainer autonomy, so the assumption overstates the legal reach of the chosen instrument and understates the need for explicit mechanisms to bring non-state maintainers into compliance.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Replace the blanket assumption with a conditional one: state that decree-level reform can change admissions for state-maintained schools but not automatically for church/foundation maintainers. Require a concrete legal risk assessment and a specified compliance strategy: (a) commission a short legal opinion mapping when funding-conditionality or quality-conditioning (normative grants) can lawfully be used to induce non-state compliance; (b) add an implementation sub-step to negotiate Memoranda of Understanding with major church/foundation maintainers and pilot the decree in state schools first; and (c) if MOUs fail, specify fallback options (targeted primary-legislation, funding conditionality, or limited statutory amendments) with timelines and decision-points.

## S2.assumptions
Objection: The scenario assumes that state-sector score thresholds or capacity caps ‘can shrink selective provision without being fully offset by church/foundation or private-sector expansion.’ This directly contradicts evidence and the scenario's own text noting a documented threefold growth in non-state selective enrolment and that non-state maintainers are legally free to expand. No evidence in the record supports the claim that state-only contraction will not be absorbed by non-state expansion.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Make the assumption explicit and conditional: acknowledge that state-sector contraction will likely be offset absent concurrent constraints on non-state expansion. Add concrete policy pairings to the scenario: (a) design simultaneous fiscal disincentives (e.g. withdraw supplementary normative grants for any newly created non-state selective places) or moratoria on new selective-place approvals; (b) require new selective places (state or non-state) to pass a national equity-impact review and obtain central approval; or (c) couple contraction with S1-style admissions reforms and funding-conditional compliance agreements with non-state maintainers. Specify legal workstreams to test the viability of each lever before implementing caps.

## S3.implementation_steps
Objection: The implementation timeline (Parliament amend Nkt. year 1; Ministry legislate anti-sorting rules year 1-2; teacher-professionalization year 2-10; conversion year 3-9) is internally inconsistent with the scenario's own assumptions and uncertainty statements about the teacher workforce: half the workforce is aged 50+, only 7.2% under 30, and a major salary reform occupies 2024–2031. The plan presumes sizeable, near-term retraining/redeployment capacity and political-administrative bandwidth that the evidence and assumptions identify as constrained, making the proposed sequencing infeasible as written.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Recast implementation steps into a staged timeline tied to workforce feasibility milestones. Concretely: (a) add a required teacher-workforce feasibility study (model retirements, recruitment needs, retraining capacity, and salary-reform bandwidth) to be completed prior to any Nkt. amendment and published within 12–18 months; (b) convert the one-step national amendment into a phased pilot (select 3–5 regions/districts for delayed tracking and enforceable anti-sorting rules) with 4–6 year evaluation windows; (c) sequence large-scale teacher-professionalization to scale up only after pilots demonstrate workforce absorptive capacity, and tie national roll-out triggers to measurable recruitment/training milestones (e.g. X new certified lower-secondary teacher graduates per year, Y% reduction in vacancy rate). This aligns legal change with demonstrated operational capacity.

## S4.expected_benefits
Objection: The scenario claims S4 'is the fastest-to-implement scenario within existing legal instruments' while the Implementation steps explicitly propose seeking statutory (cardinal-law) protection for targeted funding—a move that is politically and procedurally slow and contradicts the 'fastest-to-implement' claim. The evidence tag for 'avoids multi-year felmenő rendszer' is weak and does not justify simultaneous reliance on a slow cardinal-law strategy if speed is the objective.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Unbundle the speed vs durability trade-off and present two explicit implementation tracks: (a) a rapid-track operational package for immediate impact (pilot TEIP-style targeted funding using existing budgetary authority, short-term ministerial guidance on curriculum clarity and retention reduction, and monitoring over 12–24 months); and (b) a durability-track that pursues statutory/cardinal-law protection in parallel but accepts a longer timeline and political negotiation process. For each track, add concrete timelines, interim safeguards (e.g. multi-year budget commitments, escrowed contingency funds, or international/donor bridging finance), and metrics for when to escalate from rapid pilots to statutory entrenchment.
