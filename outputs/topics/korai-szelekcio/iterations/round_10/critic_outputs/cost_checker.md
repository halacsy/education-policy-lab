# Critique: cost_checker

## S1.cost_categories
Objection: The cost_categories list omits several concrete, potentially large fiscal items required for the scenario to reach the claimed coverage. Specifically: (a) the fiscal and legal costs of bringing church and foundation maintainers into compliance (negotiation costs, conditional-funding redesign, compensation or transitional grants, and litigation/contingency reserves) are not included; (b) enforcement staffing and recurring inspection-budget estimates for cross-maintainer compliance (county-level kormányhivatal capacity, central monitoring unit, compliance IT) are absent and unbounded; (c) SES-proxy verification options lack numeric one-off and operating-cost ranges (privacy-compliant administrative linkage vs. self-declaration), and (d) ongoing psychometric item-bank maintenance and secure exam-administration recurring costs are unquantified. Because church/foundation non-compliance is explicitly flagged as a major offset risk, leaving these costs unspecified materially understates the fiscal exposure and the implementation feasibility of decree-level reform.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add a dedicated fiscal-sensitivity annex that (1) itemises enforcement/legal/incentive costs targeted at non-state maintainers (negotiation teams, transitional grants, conditional funding redesign, legal contingency fund); (2) models low/medium/high scenarios for enforcement intensity with staffing (FTEs by county + central unit) and recurring budgets; (3) provides numeric ranges for SES-proxy options (e.g. one-off administrative-linkage setup vs. annual operating costs for verification; and low-cost self-report plus audit-sample design) and for psychometric item-bank development/maintenance (one-off item-development pilots + annual validation budget); and (4) maps which of these costs are one-off transition versus recurring steady-state expenditures. If precise national data are unavailable, require commissioning a short costing study (3–6 month) using per-school and per-FTE unit-costs to produce defensible ranges.

## S2.assumptions
Objection: The scenario rests critically on the unsupported assumption that shrinking state-sector selective capacity will not be fully or largely offset by church/foundation or private expansion. The text notes non-state expansion historically but does not model the displacement dynamics nor the fiscal/regulatory instruments required to prevent absorption of displaced demand. Concretely, cost_categories omit the expense and legal complexity of any credible countermeasures (adjusted normative grants, conditional approvals, regulatory ceilings, or incentive packages), and they exclude transition costs tied to teacher redeployment/severance and local school consolidation sequencing. Without modeling offset risk, the scenario wrongly treats state-capacity reduction as a controllable lever rather than one likely to trigger compensatory supply responses by non-state actors.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Replace the blanket assumption with an explicit displacement model: construct three counterfactuals (full offset by non-state expansion; partial offset; no offset) and estimate their probability and fiscal implications. For each, add cost lines for (a) regulatory/fiscal levers to deter non-state expansion (legal review cost + projected revenue/loss from conditional funding formulas or adjusted normative grants), (b) teacher-transition costs (per-teacher severance, retraining, redeployment budgets), and (c) targeted compensatory grants to disadvantaged areas if selective places concentrate. Commission an enrolment-shift simulation using recent maintainer-growth rates to bound likely absorption, and use that to produce concrete budget ranges and legal steps required to make contraction effective rather than merely symbolic.

## S3.cost_categories
Objection: Cost_categories fail to separate and quantify the very large, time-phased costs that will determine feasibility: (a) teacher-professionalization at the scale implied by invoking the Finnish model (multi-year, Master-level training) is absent—no per-teacher retraining vs. new-entry cost ranges, capacity constraints, or sequencing relative to the ongoing 2024–2031 salary reform are estimated; (b) facility-conversion costs (merging 6/8-year buildings into unified lower-secondary provision) are unquantified and not disaggregated by region despite documented regional demographic heterogeneity; and (c) anti-sorting enforcement infrastructure (data systems, appeals bodies, legal monitoring) lacks one-off and recurring cost ranges. This conflation of transition (high, front-loaded) versus steady-state (potentially lower) costs obscures fiscal affordability and the interaction with teacher labour-market constraints noted elsewhere in the synthesis.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Revise cost_categories into a phased costing schedule (short/medium/long-run) that separates transition versus steady-state expenditures. Specifically: (1) produce per-teacher cost ranges for alternative professionalization pathways (short CPD course vs. full Master route vs. blended incentives), estimate total cohort numbers needing retraining or replacement, and model budget-year impacts given the salary-reform envelope; (2) produce facility-conversion cost estimates per school (minor retrofit vs. major rebuild) with regional multipliers tied to demographic forecasts; (3) cost the anti-sorting enforcement stack (data systems, monitoring agency FTEs, legal appeals body, compliance audits) with one-off implementation and annual operating budgets; and (4) recommend a pilot phased roll-out in selected districts with a detailed costed pilot to validate assumptions before national legislation.
