# Critique: cost_checker

## S1.cost_categories
Objection: Cost section omits actionable fiscal ranges and conflates one-off vs ongoing costs: it lists 'SES-proxy data infrastructure', 'exam redesign', and 'enforcement' but provides no numeric ranges, no FTE or capital estimates for monitoring, and omits likely high-cost items (legal/fiscal incentives or compensation to secure church/foundation maintainer buy-in; litigation/legal-opinion costs; communications/consultation budget). It also fails to model fiscal sensitivity to maintainer non-compliance (e.g. 0%, 50%, 100% adoption), which is central to whether decree-level reform is effective.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add a disaggregated costing annex and sensitivity model. Concretely: (a) separate one-off vs recurring costs and provide indicative ranges (illustrative example to be refined by a commissioned costing study): exam redesign one-off 200–800 million HUF + annual psychometrics/maintenance 50–200 million HUF; low-cost SES-proxy implementation (self-report) 10–50 million HUF one-off vs full administrative linkage 300–900 million HUF one-off + 50–150 million HUF/year IT/ops; enforcement build-out: 10–40 regional inspector FTEs (annual wage+overheads 200–800 million HUF/year) plus case-management/legal team 100–300 million HUF/year. (b) add explicit line for legal/incentive costs to bring non-state maintainers into compliance (e.g. conditional funding swaps, compensation or negotiated grant incentives) with a low/medium/high package (0 / 500M–1.5B HUF / 1.5B–5B HUF). (c) run scenario sensitivity across maintainer uptake rates (0%,50%,100%) and report fiscal outcomes and per-student cost under each. (d) assign responsibility and timeline: Ministry commissions a 3–6 month costing study with input from Oktatási Hivatal and Kormányhivatal, publishing a workbook of assumptions and ranges to be used in regulatory impact assessment.

## S2.cost_categories
Objection: The cost section claims potential fiscal savings from shrinking selective capacity but fails to model transition (short-term redundancy/repurposing) versus steady-state savings, and it omits the likely high fiscal cost of countermeasures needed to prevent displacement to church/foundation/private providers (e.g. adjusted normative grants, targeted conditional funding, legal advisory costs). No sensitivity analysis quantifies net fiscal impact under plausible displacement rates.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Revise to split transition vs steady-state costs and include displacement-sensitivity modelling. Specifically: (a) itemize transition costs — building repurposing/closure (estimate per school: refurbishment 20–150M HUF depending on scope), teacher redeployment/severance (per-teacher one-off 1.5–6.0M HUF or retraining costs 0.5–3.0M HUF), and student reallocation transport costs — and aggregate by plausible closure scales (10/25/50 schools). (b) quantify steady-state savings per reduced selective-place (normative grant reduction per place) and model payback period against transition costs. (c) add costs/options to deter offsetting expansion by non-state maintainers: fiscal levers (normative grant tapering or supplemental conditional funding) with legal-opinion and compensation budget lines (illustrative ranges: 500M–2B HUF contingency). (d) run three displacement scenarios (no displacement, partial (50%), full displacement) and report net fiscal position and equity outcomes. (e) recommend commissioning a 6–8 week fiscal-impact modelling exercise by Ministry finance + Klebelsberg Center with published assumptions.

## S3.cost_categories
Objection: Cost items list needed categories (teacher retraining, facility conversion, anti-sorting enforcement) but provides no timeline-linked cost phasing or clear distinction between scale-up (startup) and steady-state budget implications. Crucially, it ignores fiscal interaction with the ongoing 2024–2031 salary reform (bandwidth conflict) and omits modelling of teacher workforce replacement costs driven by an aging workforce (recruitment, higher entry-salary offers, expanded teacher-education intake). Without these, feasibility and affordability are not evaluable.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Rework cost_categories to (a) phase costs over the felmenő rendszer timeline (years 1–10) distinguishing start-up (legislative design, curriculum rewrite, initial CPD, initial facility works) from recurring steady-state (higher teacher headcount, ongoing CPD, additional pupil support); (b) include workforce-replacement modelling: estimate cost of expanding teacher-education intake (per-cohort subsidy and mentoring, e.g. per-trainee 300–1,200k HUF), accelerated recruitment wage-premium for early-career hires, and pension/retirement overlap costs; (c) model interaction with the 2024–2031 salary reform by creating two scenarios — sequential (delay large hiring until after 2031) vs parallel (implement with higher short-term wage-pressure) — and provide fiscal ranges under each; (d) itemize anti-sorting enforcement costs: central monitoring unit (10–20 FTEs) + regional compliance officers + IT system (one-off 200–600M HUF, annual ops 50–150M HUF); (e) recommend producing a teacher-workforce feasibility and cost study (12 months) before committing to statutory age change, with published sensitivity to retirement and recruitment rates.
