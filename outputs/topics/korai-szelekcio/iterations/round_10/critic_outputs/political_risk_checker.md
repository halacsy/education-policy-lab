# Critique: political_risk_checker

## S1.assumptions
Objection: The scenario asserts that a ministerial decree (EMMI rendelet) is sufficient to implement SES-aware quotas/uniform thresholds across state, church, and foundation maintainers. This is a concrete legal-and-political error: the record repeatedly documents church/foundation maintainer autonomy and funding-agreement protections (constitutional/contractual) that exempt them from unilateral executive decree—so the assumption that a decree can bind all maintainers is unsupported by the evidence and misstates the reform's enforceability.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Replace the decree-sufficiency assumption with a legally-grounded implementation pathway. Concretely: (a) commission and cite a formal legal opinion mapping which admission rules can be set by decree vs. which require an Nkt. amendment or renegotiated funding agreements; (b) add an alternative route in the scenario (e.g., pursue an Nkt. amendment with cross-party buy-in or introduce conditional fiscal incentives tied to maintainer compliance); (c) where immediate decree-only action is attempted, limit it explicitly to state-maintained schools, add a mandatory pilot and evaluative sunset clause, and model how non-state opt-outs would affect net impact.

## S2.assumptions
Objection: S2 assumes state-sector capacity caps or thresholds can shrink the selective footprint without being offset by expansion from church/foundation or private providers, yet the scenario itself cites strong evidence that non-state selective enrolment grew roughly threefold since 2010. There is no modelling of supply-side response nor any specified legal/fiscal instrument to prevent offsetting expansion—this is a concrete planning omission that undermines the core mechanism.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Explicitly remove the unsupported neutral-offset assumption and insert concrete countermeasures and modelling. Concretely: (a) add a demand-supply elasticity model using historical maintainer expansion rates to project likely offset magnitude under several policy responses; (b) specify legally-viable levers to limit non-state expansion (e.g., statutory moratorium on new selective places for maintainers receiving public top-ups, conditionality in funding agreements, or negotiated caps with church/foundation representatives); (c) require a legal opinion on constitutionality and a fiscal-impact estimate of incentive/cap options before piloting caps at the state level.

## S3.assumptions
Objection: The scenario assumes Hungary's existing teacher workforce can absorb a Finland-style comprehensive lower-secondary transition without undertaking a Finland-scale, decade-long teacher-professionalization programme. This conflicts with documented evidence in the synthesis (half of teachers aged 50+, only 7.2% under 30; existing 2024–2031 salary reform) and with strong evidence that Finland's gains depended on years of teacher-education investment. The scenario therefore lacks a concrete workforce-feasibility basis and understates timeline/risk.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Replace the implicit workforce-capacity assumption with an explicit workforce feasibility package. Concretely: (a) require a published teacher-workforce feasibility study (modelling retirements, recruitment pipeline, retraining capacity, and interaction with the 2024–2031 salary reform) as a precondition for national roll-out; (b) adopt a phased/pilot implementation tied to workforce metrics (e.g., pilot delayed tracking in selected regions only after meeting recruitment/training thresholds), including temporary contingencies (short-term hiring incentives, targeted class-size adjustments, or international recruitment) if metrics lag; (c) quantify and budget a multi-year teacher-education expansion (e.g., target percentage of entrants to 5-year MA programmes, scholarships, and retraining slots) aligned to the reform timeline.
