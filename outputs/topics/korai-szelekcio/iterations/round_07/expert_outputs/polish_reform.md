# Expert analysis: polish_reform

## Findings (evidence)
- Poland's 1999 education reform postponed academic tracking from age 15 (entry to the new *gimnazjum*) [evidence: strong — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD].
- Following this reform, Polish PISA scores rose substantially by 2012, with causal analyses attributing a significant portion of these gains directly to the delayed selection [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD].
- Despite these measurable educational gains, the 1999 reform was fully reversed between 2016 and 2019 due to political reasons and shifting public support [evidence: moderate — OECD].
- Hungary currently maintains early academic selection through its 6- and 8-year secondary grammar school (*gimnázium*) tracks, which select students at ages 10 and 12 [evidence: strong — model knowledge].

## Interpretation
The Polish experience demonstrates a clear trade-off between educational efficiency and political durability. While delaying tracking to age 15 systematically raises average student performance and improves equity, the structural elimination of elite tracks creates concentrated middle-class and institutional resentment. In Hungary, reforming or abolishing the 6- and 8-year *gimnázium* system would likely yield similar long-term equity and aggregate performance gains, but it will face severe political backlash from vocal, resourceful urban parents and prestigious schools if implemented as a top-down mandate without broad consensus.

## Assumptions
- Educational outcomes and equity dynamics in Poland are structurally comparable to those in Hungary due to shared post-communist institutional legacies [assumption].
- Hungarian PISA performance and social mobility metrics are currently depressed by the early selection processes of the 6- and 8-year grammar schools [assumption].

## Position
Hungary should phase out early tracking at ages 10 and 12 in favor of a delayed-tracking system at age 15, but must anchor this transition in long-term multi-party pacts and compensatory funding for local schools to prevent the political reversal experienced in Poland.

## Uncertainties
- The precise degree of resistance from Hungarian middle-class electorates compared to the Polish public in 2016 [confidence: medium; would be reduced by: comparative opinion polling on school choice and educational equity in both countries].
- The fiscal cost of upgrading primary school infrastructures to accommodate delayed tracking across all Hungarian municipalities [confidence: low; would be reduced by: a comprehensive school-by-school infrastructural audit by the Hungarian Ministry of Education].
