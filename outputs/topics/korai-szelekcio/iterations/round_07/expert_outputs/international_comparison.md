# Expert analysis: international_comparison

## Findings (evidence)

- Earlier between-school tracking (academic selection before age 12) increases socio-economic gradient in outcomes without raising mean performance [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006, Economic Journal]. Hungary's 6- and 8-year gimnazium entry at ages 10–12 places it in the early-tracking category.

- Hungary's PISA socio-economic gradient (SES explains 19–25% of reading/maths variance) substantially exceeds the OECD average of 12–14% [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2012–2022 country notes]. This indicates equity disadvantage relative to peers.

- Cross-national evidence shows countries with delayed tracking (e.g., Finland, Japan entry at 15) and comprehensive systems show lower SES gradients and comparable or higher mean achievement [evidence: moderate — PISA structural comparisons, Brunello & Checchi 2007]. However, direct causal attribution is contested because selection systems co-vary with other institutional features (curriculum, teacher quality, funding equity).

- The timing and selectivity of Hungarian entry examinations (competitive, cognitively demanding) is likely to amplify advantage for SES-advantaged families with access to tutoring and cultural capital [evidence: moderate — structural logic + international tutoring literature; Hungarian-specific quantification not available in curated sources].

## Interpretation

The evidence suggests that Hungary's early selection architecture is associated with *higher* inequality without demonstrated mean-performance benefit. The international record is consistent: earlier tracking → steeper SES gradient. Hungary's current gradient already exceeds international norms, implying selection may be reinforcing rather than mitigating inequality.

Delayed selection (moving entry to age 14–15) or reducing selectivity (broadening admission criteria beyond exam performance) would align with evidence-based practice. However, this does not guarantee Hungarian outcomes will converge to OECD averages unless accompanied by curriculum, assessment, and equity-targeted investment reforms.

## Assumptions

- The causal direction runs from tracking architecture to inequality, not reverse [assumption]. (Confounders: systemic funding, teacher sorting, family investment behaviour.)
- PISA gradients are a valid proxy for equity concerns in Hungary's context [assumption].
- Evidence from other high-income OECD economies transfers to Hungary's institutional and demographic context [assumption — transferability is partially contested in comparative education literature].

## Position

Hungary should pilot delayed selection (moving 6- and 8-year gimnazium entry to age 13–14 or later) and broaden admission criteria to reduce early-stage stratification, because international difference-in-differences evidence indicates early selection increases inequality without raising mean performance, and Hungary's current SES gradient already exceeds the OECD average.

## Uncertainties

- **Does Hungary's exam-based selection mechanism disproportionately advantage high-SES families?** [confidence: medium]. *Would be reduced by:* longitudinal study of exam preparation disparities by family income/education; Hungarian household survey on tutoring uptake by SES quintile.

- **What is the causal effect of delaying selection specifically in Hungary?** [confidence: low]. *Would be reduced by:* natural experiment (reform cohort comparison) or RCT-style quasi-experimental design if selection rules vary by region/year.

- **Are there unobserved equilibrium effects (e.g., teacher effort, parental involvement) that offset the inequality reduction from later selection?** [confidence: low]. *Would be reduced by:* mechanism studies in countries that implemented similar reforms (e.g., Germany's Länder, England's comprehensive transition).

- **Do high-achieving students in comprehensive systems experience ceiling effects or reduced acceleration?** [confidence: medium]. *Would be reduced by:* comparative analysis of top-quartile achievement trajectories in early- vs. late-tracking OECD systems.
