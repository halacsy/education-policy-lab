# Critique: equity_checker
## S2.equity_impact
Objection: The field correctly identifies residual selective places becoming more SES-skewed, but it underplays who is left out: lower-SES pupils in general schools who lose access to selective seats without receiving any compensatory improvement unless the contested peer-effect mechanism materializes. The cited admission-statistics evidence supports current SES skew, not a guaranteed system-wide equity gain from shrinking seats. As written, the equity impact treats reduced exposure to early sorting as an equity benefit while acknowledging that the remaining pathway may become more exclusive.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: State explicitly that S2 may be equity-negative for low-SES high achievers if seat reductions remove their already-limited access to selective tracks without producing measurable improvements in general-school quality or peer composition.

## S3.equity_impact
Objection: The claim that this scenario has the "largest expected aggregate equity gain" is too strong for the evidence cited. The field itself says the largest risk is relocation of sorting into non-state, private, catchment, and choice channels, and the synthesis identifies those channels as already-operating stratification mechanisms. The international delayed-tracking evidence supports a plausible equity rationale, but it does not show that Hungary would reduce aggregate sorting unless those substitute channels are controlled.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Downgrade the aggregate equity-gain claim to conditional: S3 has the largest potential equity gain only if maintainer-based and school-choice sorting are regulated alongside abolition.

## S4.equity_impact
Objection: The field says equity gains are bounded by the depth and permanence of funding commitments, but that is incomplete: the scenario may be equity-negative by legitimizing and stabilizing the early selection mechanism while directing compensatory resources downstream. The cited Portuguese evidence supports improved outcomes under a broad reform bundle, not that compensation can offset Hungary’s age-10/12 selection or prevent privileged families from continuing to capture selective-track advantages.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add that S4 could entrench inequality if targeted funding improves disadvantaged schools only marginally while preserving high-SES access advantages in selective tracks.

## S1.equity_impact
Objection: The field names non-applicants as left out but does not identify the likely distributional losers among applicants: middle-SES or rural pupils without qualifying SES proxy status may face reduced access if quotas are implemented, while the highest-SES families can adapt through tutoring, relocation, or non-state options. Because the SES proxy is unresolved and no Hungarian pilot exists, the equity impact cannot assume that reweighting reaches the intended low-SES pupils rather than better-informed families able to document eligibility.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Specify which groups gain and lose under SES-aware admissions, and state that equity impact remains unsupported until the SES proxy is shown to reach disadvantaged applicants without being gamed.
