# Critique: political_risk_checker

## S1.political_risks
Objection: The risk that non-state maintainers resist uniform equity mandates is understated as merely "weak" and framed as inferred from maintainer plurality. The synthesis says maintainer fragmentation is treated as a major complicating constraint by every governance expert and that school_network_planning/legal_and_governance flag church/foundation maintainers as able to absorb or bypass uniform reform. The source supports more than a generic autonomy concern: it supports a concrete reversal/bypass pathway through maintainer fragmentation.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Upgrade the evidence tag to moderate and specify the risk as "church/foundation maintainers may legally or politically bypass uniform admission rules, shifting selective demand outside the state sector unless the reform includes enforceable cross-maintainer sanctions and monitoring."

## S2.implementation_steps
Objection: The implementation plan says the Ministry will "monitor non-state maintainer intake for offsetting expansion and adjust regulatory levers if detected," but it never names the regulatory levers. This leaves the scenario's central political-risk mitigation empty: the assumptions already admit that non-state expansion could offset state caps, and the political_risks field identifies the same bypass channel. Monitoring without precommitted authority is not an entrenchment design.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add explicit contingency powers before rollout: cross-maintainer quota rules, admission-reporting duties, funding/licensing conditions, and automatic cap adjustments if church/foundation expansion offsets state-sector reductions.

## S3.political_risks
Objection: The reversal risk is named but not mitigated. The field correctly cites the Polish 1999-2016/19 reversal as moderate evidence, but the scenario's only entrenchment device is a vague "multi-party political pact" in implementation_steps. The Polish source supports exactly the opposite of complacency: measurable gains did not prevent repeal. Without legal, fiscal, and constituency-lock-in mechanisms, the scenario's core abolition claim remains politically unsupported.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add an entrenchment package: phased statutory sunset dates, protected transition funding, binding grandfathering rules, teacher-time guarantees, public reporting on high-achiever outcomes, and negotiated commitments from major parties and non-state maintainers.

## S4.political_risks
Objection: The budget-vulnerability risk is under-evidenced but still central enough that the scenario should not treat "politically less disruptive" as a plausible expected benefit. The cited source only supports an inference that targeted funds are cuttable in fiscal consolidation; it does not show Hungarian fiscal history for TEIP-like programmes. If this weakly evidenced risk materializes, the compensation mechanism collapses while early selection remains entrenched.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Either supply Hungary-specific fiscal-continuity evidence for targeted education programmes or downgrade the expected_benefits claim about lower political disruption and add a hard mitigation: multi-year statutory funding floors or formula-based protected grants.
