# Critique: devil_advocate

## S3.goal
Objection: The claim that abolition would “align Hungary with the international evidence base on delayed tracking and equity” is overstated. The cited evidence supports an association between later tracking and lower SES gradients across systems, but the scenario itself concedes that Hungary’s sorting may migrate to school choice, catchment, and non-state maintainers. If the reform cannot control those channels, the core alignment claim collapses from “delayed tracking improves equity” to “one formal tracking channel is removed while other sorting channels remain.”
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Reframe the goal as eliminating formal 6/8-year entry only, and make equity gains conditional on enforceable regulation of school-choice, catchment, and non-state-maintainer substitution.

## S2.expected_benefits
Objection: The benefit “stronger peer composition in urban general-school upper grades” rests on the weakest causal link in the scenario. The evidence tags admit the peer-effect mechanism is contested, and the scenario’s own equity_impact field says residual selective places may become more SES-skewed while non-state maintainers may absorb displaced demand. The sources support that caps reduce state-sector selective seats; they do not support the stronger claim that high-achievers remain in common-track classrooms in equity-improving numbers.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Downgrade this benefit to an uncertainty or remove it as a primary expected benefit unless paired with evidence that displaced pupils actually remain in general schools rather than moving to non-state selective options.

## S4.goal
Objection: The phrase “neutralize its socio-economic inequities” is unsupported. The Portuguese evidence supports system-level improvement under a broad reform bundle, not neutralization of inequities caused by selection at ages 10-12. The scenario’s own evidence_status and equity_impact fields concede that compensation may only partially narrow downstream gaps while leaving the sorting mechanism intact. This is not neutralization; it is mitigation under optimistic funding and implementation assumptions.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Replace “neutralize” with “partially mitigate downstream inequities,” and specify that the scenario does not claim to offset the SES effects of early selection unless long-run Hungarian pilot evidence demonstrates that result.

## S1.mechanism
Objection: The mechanism depends on a “less preparation-sensitive assessment,” but the cited evidence does not show that aptitude, portfolio, or lottery-among-qualified designs would resist SES advantage in Hungary. Kertesi & Kezdi and Horn support the point that observed selective-track advantage is largely selection rather than value-added; they do not validate a replacement assessment as coaching-resistant. Portfolio systems in particular may plausibly increase high-SES advantage through parental support unless independently evidenced.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Separate the replacement options and tag each with its own evidence level; treat lottery-among-qualified as the only clearly prep-reducing option unless Hungarian pilots show aptitude or portfolio assessment reduces SES-skewed preparation effects.
