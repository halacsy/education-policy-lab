# synthesis.md

## Overview

Twelve expert analyses examine Hungary's early academic selection system (6- and 8-year gimnazium tracks, entry ages 10/12). There is strong convergence on the empirical picture — SES-skewed access, an above-OECD-average SES gradient, and inevitable demographic-driven school-network consolidation — but sharp, unresolved divergence on the policy conclusion: whether to abolish, delay, restrict, retain-and-compensate, or retain-and-wait. Positions cluster along a spectrum from structural abolition (equity_and_social_mobility, demography, international_comparison, polish_reform) through conditional/bundled reform (finnish_reform, political_feasibility, legal_and_governance) to explicit retention (education_finance, implementation_planning, portuguese_reform, hungarian_education_system in modified form). No expert has resolved the tension between these camps; this synthesis preserves it.

## Disagreement map

- **equity_and_social_mobility, demography, international_comparison, polish_reform, school_network_planning**: Hungary should phase out or substantially restrict the 6-/8-year gimnazium tracks (delaying selection to ~14–15). Why: SES gradient in PISA outcomes (19–25% vs. OECD 12–14%) [evidence: strong], selection's value-added is mostly a selection effect not causal gain [evidence: moderate], demographic cohort shrinkage makes parallel selective networks fiscally/structurally unsustainable [evidence: strong], and cross-national precedent (Poland, international comparison) shows delayed tracking reduces inequality without harming mean performance [evidence: strong/moderate].

- **hungarian_education_system (minority)**: Hungary should *not* abolish selective tracks unilaterally; instead tighten SES-aware admission quotas, weaken choice/catchment sorting, and impose uniform equity accountability across maintainers, while keeping tracks. Why: abolition alone would not eliminate sorting, since catchment and school-choice mechanisms independently generate stratification [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013; Berenyi-Berkovits-Erőss]; removing tracks displaces rather than eliminates the problem.

- **education_finance, implementation_planning (minority)**: Hungary should retain the 6-/8-year system, at least in the medium term. Why: per-pupil operating costs are flat across tracks, so structural consolidation yields no material steady-state savings and instead front-loads large transition costs (retraining, facility reuse) [evidence: weak/strong mixed]; separately, systemic teacher shortages (especially STEM) make redistributing staff across a comprehensive system operationally destabilizing [evidence: strong — OECD, PSZ/PDSZ surveys].

- **portuguese_reform (minority)**: Hungary should retain the gimnazium structure entirely and instead pursue non-structural reforms — curriculum standards, school clustering, TEIP-style targeted funding for disadvantaged schools. Why: Portugal achieved substantial PISA gains 2006–2015 without changing selection age, suggesting structural reorganization may be a "distraction" from more tractable classroom- and funding-level interventions [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020]; transferability of the Portuguese mechanism to Hungary is explicitly flagged as an assumption, not established.

- **finnish_reform**: Delaying tracking is a powerful equity tool but is conditional, not unconditional — it only works if bundled with upgraded teacher training and universal diagnostic/remedial support. Why: Finland's equity gains were structurally bundled with selective teacher training and early special-education systems [evidence: moderate — Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009]; without matching investment, abolishing early tracks risks increased within-classroom variance and middle-class flight [assumption].

- **polish_reform**: Phase-out is desirable but politically fragile; without multi-party pacts and compensatory funding, reversal (as in Poland 2016–2019) is likely. Why: Poland's delayed-tracking reform produced measurable PISA gains [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016] but was fully reversed for political reasons, showing efficiency gains do not guarantee durability [evidence: moderate].

- **political_feasibility**: Any reform's durability depends on decoupling it from "elite-loss" framing and bundling it with teacher-time investment, piloted evidence of high-achiever retention in general schools, and sustained inclusion outreach; absent all three, reversal is "politically rational" within 4–6 years. Why: three stakeholder groups (teacher unions, low-SES parents, equity civil society) each have conditional support with credible exit paths to opposition [evidence: strong — Round 06 discourse analysis]; middle-class organizational resistance has historical precedent [evidence: moderate — domestic press analyses].

- **legal_and_governance**: Neither abolition nor retention should proceed without first commissioning an independent equity audit; reform (if any) should be graduated and legally sequenced given Nkt. entrenchment and maintainer plurality. Why: no recent Hungary-specific empirical study quantifying the tracks' actual impact on mobility exists [evidence: weak — policy documents, no cited impact study]; legislative change requires multi-year notice and transitional guarantees [evidence: strong — Nkt. analysis].

- **school_network_planning vs. hungarian_education_system / legal_and_governance (minority tension, unresolved)**: school_network_planning and legal_and_governance both flag that church/foundation maintainers can absorb or bypass any state-mandated phase-out of selective tracks, fragmenting uniform implementation [evidence: strong — maintainer statistics]. This directly undercuts the feasibility of hungarian_education_system's own proposal to "impose uniform pedagogical and equity accountability across all maintainer types" — the sources agree maintainer fragmentation is a strong constraint, but do not agree on whether it makes reform infeasible (school_network_planning implies fragmentation defeats consolidation) or merely requires a governance fix (legal_and_governance implies it is addressable via graduated legal pathway). This inconsistency is carried forward, not resolved.

## What the experts agree on

- Hungary's SES gradient in educational outcomes (19–25% of PISA variance) is substantially above the OECD average (12–14%) [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2012–2022 country notes], and admission to selective tracks is strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families [evidence: strong — KSH/Oktatási Hivatal].
- Roughly 8–12% of each cohort enters 6-/8-year tracks via competitive exam at age 10–12 [evidence: strong].
- Since 2013, most state schools are governed by centralized tankerulet structures, while church/foundation maintainers hold a growing, independent share of school operations — a structural fact every expert who touches governance treats as a major complicating constraint on any uniform reform [evidence: strong].
- Demographic cohort shrinkage (births down from ~125,000/year in the early 1990s to ~85,000–95,000 recently) will force school-network consolidation regardless of what happens to selection policy [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks].
- International/comparative evidence (Poland, Finland) shows delayed tracking is associated with reduced inequality without loss of mean performance — though multiple experts flag this as correlational/contested causally, not proven for Hungary specifically [evidence: moderate, consistently hedged].
- Legislative change to the 6-/8-year system is procedurally slow and constrained by the Nkt. and transitional-guarantee requirements — no expert disputes this [evidence: strong].
- Nearly every expert who proposes reform (finnish_reform, polish_reform, political_feasibility, legal_and_governance) explicitly conditions success on complementary investment (teacher training, funding, audits, outreach) rather than treating structural change as sufficient by itself.

## Minority positions

- **hungarian_education_system**: Rejects outright abolition; argues selection should be *retained* but rebalanced (SES-aware quotas, weakened choice/catchment sorting, uniform maintainer accountability). Rationale: abolishing tracks without touching choice/catchment mechanisms would displace, not eliminate, stratification [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013]. This is a minority position relative to the abolition/delay majority, and it is itself in unresolved tension with school_network_planning's finding that maintainer fragmentation makes "uniform accountability" hard to enforce.

- **education_finance**: Argues retention is the fiscally rational choice because steady-state per-pupil costs are near-flat across tracks and reform's costs are dominated by one-off transition costs (retraining, facility reuse) [evidence: weak/strong]. This is a minority position — it is the only expert to frame the question primarily in cost-neutrality terms rather than equity or demography.

- **implementation_planning**: Argues for retention in the medium term specifically because of binding teacher-supply constraints (STEM shortages, aging workforce), independent of the equity case. Rationale: forced staff redistribution into comprehensive schools would "collapse educational delivery in disadvantaged regions" [assumption; evidence: strong on the underlying shortage, weak/assumption on the collapse projection]. Shares retention conclusion with education_finance but for a distinct causal mechanism (capacity, not cost).

- **portuguese_reform**: The only expert to argue the structural question itself is a "distraction" — full retention of the gimnazium structure, redirecting all reform energy to non-structural levers (curriculum standards, TEIP-style targeted funding, school clustering). Rationale: Portugal achieved comparable PISA gains without changing selection age [evidence: moderate], though transferability to Hungary's institutional context is flagged as an unverified assumption by the same expert.

- **finnish_reform (conditional minority)**: Does not oppose phase-out but treats it as unsafe without bundled teacher-training and diagnostic-support investment; without this bundling, predicts increased within-classroom variance and middle-class flight [assumption]. This conditionality is not shared by the unconditional-abolition majority (equity_and_social_mobility, demography), who do not attach comparable investment prerequisites to their recommendation.

- **legal_and_governance (procedural minority)**: Takes no substantive position on abolish-vs-retain, instead arguing that *no* directional decision is currently evidence-warranted absent an independent Hungary-specific equity audit — a distinct procedural minority stance relative to every other expert, all of whom recommend a substantive direction (even if conditional).

*(Carried forward from prior rounds, still unresolved: the contradiction between hungarian_education_system's assumption that non-state maintainers can be brought under uniform accountability, and school_network_planning/legal_and_governance's evidence that maintainer fragmentation structurally resists uniform state mandates, remains unaddressed by any expert and is not resolved here.)*
