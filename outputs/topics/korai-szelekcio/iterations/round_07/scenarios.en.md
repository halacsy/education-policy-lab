# Policy scenarios

## S1 — Admission reform within the current structure
**Goal**
Retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium tracks but rewrite entry rules and quotas so that access to selective places is less strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families, while acknowledging this does not touch the wider choice/catchment sorting landscape [evidence: strong — KSH/Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics on current skew].

**Mechanism**
- Replacing the central written admission exam with a less preparation-sensitive assessment (e.g. aptitude-based, portfolio, or lottery-among-qualified) weakens the advantage that paid test-prep confers on high-SES applicants [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013; Horn 2013 show most raw advantage is selection effect, not direct evidence the new assessment itself resists coaching].
- Introducing SES-aware quotas or weighted selection reweights entry probabilities toward lower-SES applicants at a given ability level [evidence: weak — no Hungarian pilot exists; mechanism is model knowledge].
- Uniform equity accountability rules across state, church and foundation maintainers close the loophole whereby non-state maintainers escape reform via their own admission policies [evidence: weak — legal feasibility only, no implementation precedent cited].

**Evidence status**
contested — the SES-skew problem is strongly evidenced, but every remedy mechanism (coaching-resistant assessment, SES quota, cross-maintainer accountability) is weak or unverified in the Hungarian context.

**Assumptions**
- A workable SES indicator or proxy can be constructed and administered at application time [assumption — contradicted by feasibility_checker: Hungary has no administrative student-level SES register usable at application, and any proxy risks gaming per political_feasibility's note that gatekeeping must be 'demonstrably independent of school management'].
- Church and foundation maintainers can be brought under a uniform admission-equity mandate without a separate legislative fight [assumption — legal_and_governance flags maintainer plurality as a structural obstacle].
- A less-coachable assessment is psychometrically achievable within a few years [assumption — no validated Hungarian instrument currently exists].

**Expected benefits**
- Gains accrue to high-ability low-SES pupils who already apply and are reweighted into selective seats [evidence: weak — contingent on the unresolved SES-indicator assumption above; benefit does not extend to low-SES pupils who do not apply at all].
- Reduced test-preparation arms-race pressure on age-10 children insofar as the new assessment resists coaching [evidence: weak — model knowledge; directly in tension with the political_risks item below that reformed channels may invite informal selection instead].
- Preserves existing school identities, buildings, and staff deployment, avoiding the transition costs associated with structural abolition [evidence: moderate — education_finance notes per-pupil cost differences across tracks are modest, so this specific benefit is plausible].

**Equity impact**
Potentially narrows access skew among applicants, but leaves untouched the prior-stage inequities in who applies at all (information, tutoring access, geographic proximity) and does not address the broader choice/catchment sorting that the hungarian_education_system expert identifies as operating independently of track entry. Net equity gain is bounded and contested pending a workable SES proxy.

**Cost categories**
- Assessment redesign, psychometric validation and piloting — cost: medium-to-high, since the description requires multi-year psychometric development beyond typical one-off exam-redesign costs [evidence: weak — no unit-cost figure available; cost_checker flagged prior 'medium' label as likely understated].
- SES-proxy data infrastructure (register or verified self-report system) — cost: medium, no existing register to build from.
- Cross-maintainer compliance monitoring — cost: low-to-medium, recurring.

**Implementation steps**
- Oktatási Hivatal — design and pilot a new, less-coachable assessment instrument, including psychometric validation (timeline: year 1-3).
- Ministry of Education / KK — develop and legally test an SES-weighting or quota mechanism compliant with constitutional equality provisions (timeline: year 1-2).
- Legislature — amend Nkt. to mandate uniform admission-equity rules across state, church and foundation maintainers (timeline: year 2-3).
- Oktatási Hivatal — run parallel pilot admissions in a subset of districts before national rollout (timeline: year 3-4).
- Ministry of Education — national rollout with monitoring of SES composition of entrants (timeline: year 4-5).

**Political risks**
- Reformed/scarcer selection channels may invite informal selection (interviews, reputation) that reproduces SES sorting [evidence: weak — plausible mechanism, no Hungarian precedent cited by political_risk_checker].
- Urban middle-class parents, who treat selective places as a mobility/insurance asset, may organise against perceived dilution of merit-based entry [evidence: moderate — political_feasibility documents 1990s–2010s precedent of such mobilisation].
- Non-state maintainers may resist uniform equity mandates as an infringement on institutional autonomy [evidence: weak — inferred from legal_and_governance's maintainer-plurality finding].

**Uncertainties**
- Whether a workable, gaming-resistant SES proxy can be built at all given the absence of a student-level SES register [confidence: low — would be reduced by: a feasibility study of administrative data linkage options run by KSH/Oktatási Hivatal].
- Whether a genuinely less-coachable assessment format exists that maintains predictive validity [confidence: low — would be reduced by: piloting alternative assessment formats against coaching-intervention groups].
- Whether informal selection substitutes for formal selection once formal channels are reformed [confidence: low — would be reduced by: monitoring interview/reputation-based admission practices post-reform in comparable systems].

## S2 — Gradual phase-down of 6/8-year entry places
**Goal**
Reduce over time the number and share of 6- and 8-year gimnazium entry places, shrinking the selective sector gradually rather than abolishing it outright, to lower the share of the cohort exposed to early sorting while managing legal, maintainer, and demographic constraints.

**Mechanism**
- Year-on-year caps on state-maintained-school selective intake reduce the absolute number of pupils sorted at age 10/12 [evidence: strong — quota mechanics are a direct administrative lever, per legal_and_governance's Nkt.-based framing].
- Fewer selective seats keep more high-achievers in the common track, which can improve peer composition in urban general-school upper grades [evidence: contested — the asymmetric peer-effect mechanism is tagged weak/contested by equity_and_social_mobility, and devil_advocate/evidence_checker flag this as a weak causal link].
- Demographic cohort shrinkage (births down from ~125,000 to ~85,000-95,000/year) provides a natural occasion to consolidate selective seats without net school closures, easing the political framing [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks].

**Evidence status**
mixed — the demographic and quota-mechanics claims are strong, but the equity-improving peer-effect mechanism that is meant to justify the reform is contested/weak.

**Assumptions**
- Caps on state-maintained schools are not simply offset by non-state maintainers (church, foundation) expanding their own selective intake [assumption — directly contradicted by school_network_planning's finding that non-state maintainers can 'absorb selective tracks and bypass national consolidation plans', and by political_risks/uncertainties below].
- Grandfathering of enrolled cohorts is legally and administratively manageable over a multi-year phase-down [assumption — consistent with legal_and_governance's finding on transitional guarantees, but cost implications are separately flagged].
- Demographic contraction provides political cover rather than triggering compensatory local resistance to seat closures [assumption — untested].

**Expected benefits**
- Stronger peer composition in urban general-school upper grades from retained high-achievers [evidence: contested — mechanism itself tagged weak/contested per equity_and_social_mobility and devil_advocate].
- Aligns school-network rationalization with demographic necessity, reducing the political cost of consolidation relative to a standalone reform [evidence: moderate — school_network_planning and demography findings support the underlying demographic logic, though the political-cost inference is [assumption]].
- Avoids abrupt legal challenge by proceeding through incremental, legally defensible quota adjustments rather than abolition [evidence: weak — inferred from legal_and_governance's note on multi-year notice periods, not directly evidenced as risk-reducing].

**Equity impact**
As caps tighten, residual selective places become even more SES-skewed absent an S1-type admission-rule change — this residual-skew effect is mechanical and near-certain (fewer seats, same or increased competition, same access advantages for high-SES families) [evidence: moderate — inferred directly from the admission-statistics finding of strong SES skew]. Net system-wide equity gain therefore depends heavily on whether non-state maintainers absorb displaced demand, which would relocate rather than reduce SES sorting.

**Cost categories**
- Parallel-regime administration while grandfathered cohorts run under old rules alongside capped intake — cost: medium-to-high, and increasing over the likely decade-long span rather than a short 'transitional' window [evidence: weak — no per-cohort cost data; cost_checker flagged the original 'medium, transitional' label as likely underestimating duration-driven costs].
- Teacher redeployment from shrinking selective-track positions to general-school posts — cost: medium, given national wage-grid rigidity noted by education_finance.
- Legal/administrative cost of phased quota-setting and dispute resolution — cost: low-to-medium, recurring annually.

**Implementation steps**
- Legislature — amend Nkt. to authorise a multi-year, scheduled reduction in state-maintained selective intake quotas (timeline: year 1).
- KK/Ministry of Education — set annual seat-reduction targets calibrated to demographic decline curves by region (timeline: year 1-2, recurring annually through year 8-10).
- Oktatási Hivatal — administer grandfathering rules for currently enrolled cohorts (timeline: year 1-10, continuous).
- Ministry of Education — monitor non-state maintainer intake for offsetting expansion and adjust regulatory levers if detected (timeline: year 2-5).
- KK — redeploy or retrain teaching staff as selective-track positions shrink (timeline: year 3-8).

**Political risks**
- Middle-class parents perceive shrinking seats as a threat to a mobility/insurance asset and mobilize [evidence: moderate — political_feasibility documents this pattern from the 1990s–2010s, though the specific magnitude for this scenario is not separately evidenced].
- Church and foundation maintainers expand selective offerings to capture displaced demand, undermining the policy's equity intent [evidence: weak — plausible per school_network_planning's structural argument, not separately quantified].
- Reversal pressure across electoral cycles as the Polish precedent shows durability of even successful reforms is fragile [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD document the Polish reversal].

**Uncertainties**
- Whether non-state maintainers will in fact expand selective intake to offset state-sector caps [confidence: low — would be reduced by: registry data on maintainer-level admission policy responses during a pilot phase].
- Whether the contested peer-effect mechanism materializes at a magnitude sufficient to justify the reform's equity rationale [confidence: low — would be reduced by: longitudinal peer-effect studies in Hungarian primary schools following selection events].
- Whether political durability survives multiple electoral cycles given demonstrated Polish reversal risk [confidence: low — would be reduced by: comparative opinion polling and tracking of coalition commitments across cycles].

## S3 — Comprehensive school to age 14 (structural reform)
**Goal**
Abolish 6- and 8-year gimnazium entry points and move to a comprehensive lower-secondary structure until age 14, aligning Hungary with the international evidence base on delayed tracking and equity.

**Mechanism**
- Removing early-selection entry points eliminates the formal sorting event at age 10/12, delaying academic differentiation to age 14+ [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006 establish the general cross-national relationship between early tracking and SES gradients].
- A weaker SES-outcome gradient can emerge within a decade via broader access to high-achieving peers [evidence: contested — the asymmetric peer-effect mechanism underlying this claim is tagged weak/contested by equity_and_social_mobility and by devil_advocate/evidence_checker].
- Bundling the reform with upgraded teacher training and early diagnostic/remedial support is necessary for equity gains to materialize, per the Finnish precedent [evidence: moderate — Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009, though the causal weight of tracking-delay versus teacher-reform bundling is explicitly not separable].

**Evidence status**
moderate overall on the cross-national tracking-inequality relationship, but contested on whether Hungary-specific peer-effect and displacement mechanisms will deliver the promised gains without the Finnish-style bundled investment.

**Assumptions**
- Sorting does not simply migrate to school choice between general schools and to non-state maintainers [assumption — directly contradicted by this scenario's own equity_impact field below, which identifies exactly this migration as the largest single risk, and by feasibility_checker/coherence_checker flagging the same internal contradiction].
- Political ownership survives two electoral cycles, given multi-year implementation and legal transition timelines [assumption — contradicted by political_feasibility's explicit finding that no such durability window is assured; Polish reversal within ~17 years is the closest precedent].
- Teacher supply and quality in comprehensive schools can be upgraded fast enough to prevent the increased-heterogeneity effects the Finnish case warns against [assumption — implementation_planning flags Hungary's teacher shortage as 'binding' and likely to worsen under this scenario].

**Expected benefits**
- A weaker SES-outcome gradient within a decade, following international precedent [evidence: moderate — cross-national comparative evidence (PISA structural comparisons; Brunello & Checchi 2007), though the specific mechanism claimed (peer effects) is itself contested — see mechanism tag above].
- Alignment of Hungary's structural framework with OECD systems whose SES gradients are lower despite comparable or higher mean achievement [evidence: moderate — PISA structural comparisons, though causal attribution is contested because tracking co-varies with other institutional features].
- Removes the demographic vulnerability concentrated on rural general schools that current early selection creates by draining top performers to urban selective tracks [evidence: moderate — demography and school_network_planning findings on rural cohort shrinkage and school viability].

**Equity impact**
Largest expected aggregate equity gain of the four scenarios, but also the largest risk: middle-class exit to non-state or private schools, or to general-school choice/catchment sorting, which the hungarian_education_system expert identifies as already-operating parallel stratification mechanisms. This exit risk is not shown to leave the exiting or remaining pupils unharmed; equity_checker and coherence_checker both flag that the scenario's own text does not resolve whether sorting is reduced in aggregate or merely relocated to choice-based and maintainer-based channels.

**Cost categories**
- Nationwide teacher retraining in differentiated instruction — cost: high, though no precise per-cohort/per-district unit cost is available, and cost_checker notes this absence itself understates likely total exposure given the scale of the workforce affected [evidence: weak — no numeric estimate cited].
- Building/facility repurposing for gimnazium campuses shifting to different age cohorts — cost: high, per education_finance's finding that transition costs (not steady-state costs) dominate structural reform.
- Early diagnostic and remedial support system build-out (Finnish-style bundling requirement) — cost: high, recurring.
- Legal/legislative transition process, including grandfathering of enrolled cohorts — cost: medium, per legal_and_governance's multi-year notice period requirement.

**Implementation steps**
- Legislature — amend Nkt. to set a fixed sunset date for 6/8-year entry, with grandfathering for already-enrolled cohorts (timeline: year 1-2).
- Ministry of Education — commission and fund a national teacher retraining programme in differentiated/heterogeneous-classroom instruction (timeline: year 1-4).
- KK/Ministry of Education — design and pilot universal diagnostic and early remedial support systems in a subset of districts (timeline: year 1-3, national rollout year 4-6).
- Ministry of Education — negotiate multi-party political pact to lock in the reform beyond a single electoral cycle, learning from the Polish reversal precedent (timeline: year 1-2).
- Oktatási Hivatal — monitor middle-class exit to non-state maintainers and school-choice sorting patterns, with contingency regulatory levers (timeline: year 2-10, continuous).

**Political risks**
- Middle-class and church/foundation flight relocates sorting rather than removing it [evidence: weak — plausible given non-state maintainer expansion capacity documented by school_network_planning, but political_risk_checker notes no direct evidence is offered for this specific behavioral outcome].
- Reversal within one to two electoral cycles, mirroring the Polish 1999-2016/19 trajectory, especially if framed as elite loss [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD document the Polish precedent directly].
- Teacher union non-cooperation if retraining is not resourced with dedicated planning time rather than lump-sum funding [evidence: strong — political_feasibility's Round 06 discourse analysis on teacher union conditionality].

**Uncertainties**
- Whether sorting migrates to school choice/catchment and non-state maintainers rather than being reduced in aggregate [confidence: low — would be reduced by: matched-pair school comparisons tracking SES composition across all maintainer types pre/post reform].
- Whether political ownership can survive two electoral cycles given the explicit political_feasibility finding of no assured durability window [confidence: low — would be reduced by: multi-party pact tracking and polling data across the implementation period].
- Whether teacher supply/quality upgrades can occur fast enough to avoid the Finnish-reform's warned-of heterogeneity/flight dynamics [confidence: low — would be reduced by: workforce exit tracking and comprehensive teacher union survey data].

## S4 — Keep the structure, compensate general schools (Portuguese-style package)
**Goal**
Retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system unchanged but neutralize its socio-economic inequities through non-structural interventions: targeted funding for disadvantaged schools (TEIP-style), rigorous national curriculum standards, and reduced grade retention.

**Mechanism**
- Targeted funding packages (TEIP-style) for disadvantaged general schools raise instructional quality and resources for the majority of pupils who remain outside selective tracks [evidence: moderate — Portugal's PISA gains 2006-2015 are documented, though attribution to TEIP specifically versus the whole reform bundle is not separately isolated].
- Rigorous, standardized national curriculum requirements reduce quality variance across schools regardless of maintainer or selectivity status [evidence: moderate — Portuguese case; Crato 2020, transferability to Hungary is [assumption]].
- Compensation is intended to offset, not merely mask, the effects of early sorting [evidence: weak — no country has demonstrated offsetting at Hungary's selection age (10-12); the Portuguese case did not alter its selection age, so this is an extrapolation beyond the cited evidence].

**Evidence status**
moderate on the Portuguese outcome trend itself, but weak/extrapolated on whether the same package would offset (rather than mask) the specific SES gradient produced by selection as early as age 10, which has no direct international precedent.

**Assumptions**
- Compensation can offset, not just mask, the effects of early sorting [assumption — flagged by assumption_checker as unverifiable ex ante since no country evidence exists at Hungary's selection age; contradicted by this scenario's own equity_impact statement that it addresses effects only 'downstream' while leaving the sorting mechanism intact].
- Non-structural interventions are politically easier to implement and sustain than structural changes [assumption — portuguese_reform's own framing; contradicted by this scenario's political_risks section noting budget vulnerability as a first-cut fiscal-consolidation target].
- Portugal's TEIP and curriculum-standards mechanisms transfer to Hungary's institutional and socio-economic context despite differing teacher-supply conditions [assumption — implementation_planning documents a binding Hungarian teacher shortage not present in the Portuguese case].

**Expected benefits**
- Avoids the transition costs and legal/legislative disruption of structural reform, since the 6/8-year framework and maintainer arrangements remain untouched [evidence: moderate — legal_and_governance and education_finance both support that structural change carries high procedural and transition cost, which this scenario avoids].
- Raises instructional quality in the general-school majority sector regardless of selection status, potentially narrowing outcome gaps even without altering entry rules [evidence: moderate — Portuguese PISA trend 2006-2015, contingent on the transferability assumption above].
- Politically less disruptive than S2/S3 in the near term, since no group loses an existing selective place [evidence: weak — this framing is directly contradicted by the political_risks section below, which flags budget vulnerability and the risk of permanently legitimizing early selection as its own distinct political liabilities].

**Equity impact**
Narrows the SES gradient at best partially, since it leaves the sorting mechanism itself intact and only compensates downstream — it does not address the root selection event that the international_comparison and equity_and_social_mobility experts identify as the primary structural driver of Hungary's above-average SES gradient. This scenario is the only one of the four that does not attempt to change who is sorted or when, so equity gains are bounded by the depth and permanence of funding commitments rather than by structural change.

**Cost categories**
- TEIP-style targeted funding for disadvantaged schools — cost: high, and cost_checker flags this as the first line likely to be cut in fiscal consolidation, making the nominal 'high' cost figure also a high-risk/low-durability commitment [evidence: weak — no numeric range available].
- National curriculum standards development and rollout — cost: medium, one-off plus periodic revision.
- Grade-retention-reduction support programmes (remedial instruction, diagnostic tools) — cost: medium, recurring.
- Monitoring and evaluation infrastructure to verify compensation is offsetting rather than masking sorting effects — cost: low-to-medium, recurring.

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education — design TEIP-equivalent targeted funding formula for disadvantaged general schools (timeline: year 1).
- Ministry of Education / Oktatási Hivatal — develop and publish rigorous national curriculum standards applicable across all maintainer types (timeline: year 1-2).
- KK — roll out grade-retention-reduction and diagnostic support programmes nationally (timeline: year 2-4).
- Ministry of Finance — secure multi-year budgetary commitment (ideally cross-party) to prevent first-round fiscal-consolidation cuts to targeted funding (timeline: year 1, renewed at year 4-5).
- Oktatási Hivatal — establish independent monitoring to track whether SES gradients actually narrow or merely stabilize (timeline: year 3-10, continuous).

**Political risks**
- Budget vulnerability: targeted funding for disadvantaged schools is a plausible first-cut target in fiscal consolidation, undermining the durability of the compensation mechanism [evidence: weak — cost_checker's inference, no direct fiscal-history citation for Hungary specifically].
- Legitimising early selection permanently: compensation may entrench rather than dismantle the sorting engine, foreclosing future structural reform options [evidence: weak — plausible institutional-lock-in argument, not separately evidenced by political_risk_checker].
- Teacher shortage undermines credible delivery of the compensation package, given implementation_planning's finding that Hungary's teacher shortage is 'binding' even absent any structural reform [evidence: strong — OECD Education at a Glance; PSZ/PDSZ surveys].

**Uncertainties**
- Whether compensation can genuinely offset rather than mask the effects of early sorting at Hungary's selection age, given no country precedent [confidence: low — would be reduced by: a long-run Hungarian pilot with matched comparison districts tracking SES-gradient trajectories over a decade].
- Whether sustained, multi-year funding for targeted programmes can survive Hungarian budgetary and political cycles without reversal [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: analysis of Hungarian budgetary commitments and legislative continuity in education policy].
- Whether Hungary's teacher shortage (more acute than Portugal's baseline) will blunt the effectiveness of Portuguese-style interventions [confidence: high — would be reduced by: comparative studies on teacher supply and qualifications in Hungarian disadvantaged schools versus historical Portuguese levels].
