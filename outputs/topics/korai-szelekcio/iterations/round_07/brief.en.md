## What we know

- Hungary's SES gradient in educational outcomes (19–25% of PISA variance) is substantially above the OECD average (12–14%) [fact — evidence: strong, OECD PISA 2012–2022 country notes].
- Admission to selective 6-/8-year gimnazium tracks is strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families [fact — evidence: strong, KSH/Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics].
- Roughly 8–12% of each cohort enters 6-/8-year tracks via competitive exam at age 10–12 [fact — evidence: strong].
- Since 2013, most state schools are governed by centralized tankerület structures, while church/foundation maintainers hold a growing, independent share of school operations [fact — evidence: strong].
- Demographic cohort shrinkage (births down from ~125,000/year in the early 1990s to ~85,000–95,000 recently) will force school-network consolidation regardless of selection policy [fact — evidence: strong, KSH demographic yearbooks].
- Legislative change to the 6-/8-year system is procedurally slow and constrained by the Nkt. and transitional-guarantee requirements [fact — evidence: strong].
- Hungary's teacher shortage (especially STEM) is a binding constraint independent of any structural reform [fact — evidence: strong, OECD Education at a Glance; PSZ/PDSZ surveys].
- Hungary has no administrative student-level SES register usable at application time [fact — evidence: strong, feasibility_checker].

## What we consider likely

- International/comparative evidence associates delayed tracking with reduced inequality without loss of mean performance [estimate — evidence: moderate, consistently hedged as correlational; Hanushek & Woessmann 2006, Brunello & Checchi 2007].
- Most of the selective tracks' raw value-added is a selection effect rather than causal gain [estimate — evidence: moderate, Kertesi & Kezdi 2013; Horn 2013].
- Poland's delayed-tracking reform produced measurable PISA gains but was politically reversed within ~17 years, showing efficiency gains do not guarantee durability [fact — evidence: moderate, Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD].
- Portugal achieved substantial PISA gains 2006–2015 without changing its selection age, via TEIP-style targeted funding and curriculum standards [fact — evidence: moderate; attribution to TEIP specifically not isolated from the whole reform bundle].
- Under any state-sector cap or abolition, non-state maintainers can absorb or bypass consolidation, relocating rather than reducing sorting [estimate — evidence: strong on maintainer capacity, weak/unquantified on the behavioral relocation prediction].
- Preserving school identities and buildings avoids transition costs, since per-pupil steady-state cost differences across tracks are modest [estimate — evidence: moderate, education_finance].

## Where experts disagree

- **Abolish/restrict vs. retain**: equity_and_social_mobility, demography, international_comparison, polish_reform, school_network_planning argue for phasing out or substantially restricting early tracking [value + estimate], because early sorting drives the SES gradient and demographic shrinkage makes parallel networks unsustainable. Against this, education_finance and implementation_planning argue for retention [value + fact], because steady-state savings are near-zero while transition costs and binding teacher shortages make redistribution operationally destabilizing.
- **Conditional vs. unconditional delay**: finnish_reform holds that delayed tracking only works if bundled with teacher training and diagnostic/remedial support [estimate — Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009]; the unconditional-abolition camp attaches no comparable investment prerequisite [value].
- **Structural vs. compensatory**: portuguese_reform argues the structural question is a "distraction" and reform energy should go to non-structural levers [value + estimate], while the abolition camp treats the sorting event itself as the root cause [value].
- **Whether reform is decidable now**: legal_and_governance argues no directional decision is evidence-warranted absent an independent Hungary-specific equity audit [value], since no recent Hungary-specific impact study exists [fact — evidence: weak].
- **Maintainer fragmentation — infeasibility vs. governance fix**: school_network_planning implies fragmentation defeats consolidation; legal_and_governance implies it is addressable via a graduated legal pathway [assumption vs. assumption, unresolved]. This directly undercuts hungarian_education_system's proposal for uniform cross-maintainer accountability.

## What we don't know

- Whether a workable, gaming-resistant SES proxy can be built without a student-level SES register [known-unknown — confidence: low; resolvable by a KSH/Oktatási Hivatal administrative-data-linkage feasibility study].
- Whether a genuinely less-coachable assessment format exists that maintains predictive validity in Hungary [known-unknown — confidence: low; no validated instrument exists].
- Whether informal selection (interviews, reputation) substitutes for formal selection once formal channels are reformed [known-unknown — confidence: low; untested in Hungary].
- Whether non-state maintainers would in fact expand selective intake to offset state-sector caps, and at what magnitude [known-unknown — the capacity is documented, the behavioral magnitude is not].
- Whether the contested peer-effect mechanism materializes at a magnitude sufficient to justify S2/S3's equity rationale [known-unknown — confidence: low; the mechanism is tagged weak/contested].
- Whether compensation can offset (not merely mask) early-sorting effects at Hungary's selection age (10–12) [known-unknown — no country precedent; Portugal did not alter its selection age].
- Whether political ownership can survive two electoral cycles [known-unknown — confidence: low; no assured durability window].
- **Assumption (not known-unknown)**: that teacher supply/quality can be upgraded fast enough to prevent Finnish-warned heterogeneity/flight dynamics [assumption — contradicted by the binding-shortage finding].
- **Assumption (not known-unknown)**: that demographic contraction provides political cover rather than triggering compensatory local resistance [assumption — untested].

## What could be done

- **S0 — Do nothing / audit first (baseline)**: retain the current structure and commission an independent Hungary-specific equity audit before any directional decision [value — legal_and_governance's position].
- **S1 — Admission reform within the current structure**: replace the coaching-sensitive exam with a less preparation-sensitive assessment, add SES-aware weighting, and impose uniform cross-maintainer equity rules [estimate — problem strongly evidenced, every remedy mechanism weak/unverified].
- **S2 — Gradual phase-down of 6/8-year entry places**: year-on-year caps on state-maintained selective intake, calibrated to demographic decline [fact on quota mechanics; contested on peer-effect equity rationale].
- **S3 — Comprehensive school to age 14**: abolish 6/8-year entry points, bundled with teacher retraining and diagnostic/remedial support [estimate — moderate on cross-national tracking relationship, contested on Hungary-specific delivery].
- **S4 — Retain structure, compensate general schools (Portuguese-style)**: TEIP-style targeted funding, national curriculum standards, reduced grade retention [estimate — moderate on Portuguese trend, weak/extrapolated on offsetting early sorting].

No single scenario is crowned; each addresses a different part of the problem and carries distinct unresolved risks.

## What each option costs

- **S0**: Cost low; harm is opportunity cost — the strongly-evidenced SES skew persists while the audit runs [value]. Winners: those favouring the status quo and institutional autonomy. Losers: low-SES/Roma applicants who continue to face coaching-driven exclusion [value].
- **S1**: Assessment redesign/psychometric validation medium-to-high [estimate — no unit-cost figure; prior 'medium' label flagged as understated]; SES-proxy infrastructure medium; cross-maintainer monitoring low-to-medium recurring. Trade-off: gains accrue only to high-ability low-SES pupils who already apply — prior-stage inequity (who applies at all) is untouched [fact — evidence: moderate]. Risk: reform relocates to informal selection; middle-class mobilization against perceived merit dilution [estimate]. Winners: reweighted low-SES applicants. Losers: high-SES families losing test-prep advantage; teachers if verification burden or unvalidated instruments are pushed onto them [value].
- **S2**: Parallel-regime administration medium-to-high and duration-driven [estimate — flagged as understating decade-long span]; teacher redeployment medium under wage-grid rigidity; annual quota administration low-to-medium. Trade-off: residual selective places become mechanically more SES-skewed absent an S1-type change [estimate — evidence: moderate, near-certain]; a decade-long "zombie" parallel regime harms teacher security and may worsen exodus [value + estimate]. Winners: none clearly; demographic alignment eases consolidation. Losers: 10 cohorts under a system deemed inequitable; selective-track teachers facing deployment uncertainty [value].
- **S3**: Teacher retraining high [estimate — no unit cost, flagged as understating exposure]; facility repurposing high; diagnostic/remedial build-out high recurring; legal transition medium. Trade-off: largest expected aggregate equity gain but largest risk — middle-class exit relocating sorting, and teachers becoming the shock absorber if bundling lags the structural change [value + estimate]. Winners: low-SES pupils if the reform survives and is resourced. Losers: capable-family choice, non-state maintainer niches, teachers if under-resourced [value].
- **S4**: TEIP-style funding high and first-cut vulnerable in fiscal consolidation [estimate]; curriculum standards medium; retention-reduction support medium recurring. Trade-off: leaves the sorting mechanism intact and compensates only downstream, risking permanent legitimation of early selection (institutional lock-in) [value + estimate]. Winners: general-school majority if funding is durable; non-state maintainers whose autonomy is untouched. Losers: sorted-out pupils whose selection-event harm cannot be compensated downstream; the case for future structural reform [value].

## What research could resolve

- A KSH/Oktatási Hivatal administrative-data-linkage feasibility study on whether a gaming-resistant SES proxy can be built — most decision-critical for S1 and for S2's residual-skew fix.
- Piloting alternative assessment formats against coaching-intervention groups to test coaching-resistance and predictive validity — decision-critical for S1.
- Longitudinal Hungarian peer-effect studies following selection events — decision-critical for S2 and S3's equity rationale.
- Matched-pair school comparisons tracking SES composition across all maintainer types pre/post reform — resolves the sorting-migration risk central to S2 and S3.
- Registry data on maintainer-level admission-policy responses during a pilot — resolves the non-state-absorption magnitude.
- A long-run pilot with matched comparison districts tracking SES-gradient trajectories — the only way to test whether S4 compensation offsets rather than masks.
- An independent Hungary-specific equity audit of the tracks' actual mobility impact — the currently-missing baseline (legal_and_governance).

## What people must decide

- **Whether early sorting is itself the wrong, or only its unequal access** [value] — this is the fork between S3 (abolish the event) and S1/S4 (fix access or compensate). No evidence resolves it.
- **How much maintainer autonomy yields to equity accountability** [value] — the church/foundation autonomy vs. uniform-mandate conflict has no technical fix.
- **Whether to accept bounded, near-certain gains now (S1) or pursue larger but riskier structural change (S3)** [value].
- **How much weight to give durability risk** [value] — whether a reform reversible within an electoral cycle (Polish precedent) is worth committing to without a multi-party pact.
- **Whether teachers may be asked to absorb transition risk before bundled investment arrives** [value] — a sequencing and distributive-justice choice, not a technical one.
- **Whether compensation that leaves sorting intact is acceptable, or is institutional lock-in a disqualifying harm** [value].

These are the honest answer, not failures to resolve.

## Minority positions

- **hungarian_education_system**: Rejects outright abolition; selection should be *retained* but rebalanced (SES-aware quotas, weakened choice/catchment sorting, uniform maintainer accountability) [value + estimate — evidence: moderate, Kertesi & Kezdi 2013]. Rationale: abolishing tracks without touching choice/catchment mechanisms would displace, not eliminate, stratification. Remains in unresolved tension with school_network_planning's finding that maintainer fragmentation resists uniform accountability.
- **education_finance**: Retention is the fiscally rational choice because steady-state per-pupil costs are near-flat across tracks and reform costs are dominated by one-off transition costs [fact/estimate — evidence: weak/strong mixed]. The only expert framing the question primarily in cost-neutrality terms.
- **implementation_planning**: Retention in the medium term specifically because of binding teacher-supply constraints; forced staff redistribution would "collapse educational delivery in disadvantaged regions" [assumption on collapse; fact on the underlying shortage — evidence: strong]. Shares the retention conclusion with education_finance but for a distinct capacity mechanism.
- **portuguese_reform**: The structural question is itself a "distraction"; redirect all reform energy to non-structural levers [value + estimate — evidence: moderate], though transferability to Hungary is flagged by the same expert as an unverified assumption.
- **finnish_reform (conditional minority)**: Does not oppose phase-out but treats it as unsafe without bundled teacher-training and diagnostic support; without bundling, predicts increased within-classroom variance and middle-class flight [assumption]. This conditionality is not shared by the unconditional-abolition majority.
