## Overview

The expert record presents a comprehensive analysis of the potential reform or phase-out of Hungary's early academic tracking system, specifically the 6- and 8-year grammar schools (*gimnázium*), which select students at ages 10 and 12. While experts broadly recognize that early tracking exacerbates socio-economic segregation and inequality of outcomes without raising overall academic performance, they strongly disagree on the optimal policy response, political feasibility, financial impact, and implementation readiness.

## Disagreement map

- **international_comparison, finnish_reform, polish_reform, equity_and_social_mobility, demography**: Hungary should phase out or abolish early selection into 6- and 8-year grammar schools to improve equity and reduce socio-economic disparities.
  Why: Cross-country evidence indicates early tracking increases inequality of outcomes without raising mean performance. In Hungary, these tracks act as escape routes for high-SES families, accelerating sorting and accelerating the collapse of rural school networks under demographic cohort shrinkage. [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006; OECD PISA; KSH demographic yearbooks]
- **hungarian_education_system, school_network_planning** (minority): Hungary should retain early-selective grammar school tracks but restrict, regulate, or align their entry requirements and quotas with regional equity targets.
  Why: Simply banning selective tracks in state schools will likely push selective demand into the growing non-state sector (churches and foundations), shifting rather than eliminating sorting. Additionally, 6-year programs should be restricted to regional urban hubs to manage demographic school-network consolidation. [evidence: strong — Public education act amendments; maintainer statistics]
- **portuguese_reform** (minority): Hungary should retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system but deploy non-structural reforms, such as targeted funding for disadvantaged schools (TEIP-style) and rigorous national curriculum standards.
  Why: Portugal's trajectory demonstrates that substantial improvements in academic outcomes and equity can be achieved without politically disruptive structural reorganizations. Non-structural interventions allow educational authorities to focus resources directly on classroom-level quality. [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020]
- **education_finance** (minority): Hungary should retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system because the front-loaded transition costs of structural consolidation outweigh any marginal steady-state operational savings.
  Why: Per-pupil operational funding differences across tracks are flat, meaning transition costs (retraining specialized secondary teachers to teach lower secondary, and modifying physical infrastructure) would dominate initial budget cycles. [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates]
- **implementation_planning** (minority): Hungary should retain the 6- and 8-year gimnázium system in the medium term, delaying structural integration until teacher supply stabilizes.
  Why: The transition process would overwhelm Hungary's fragile, understaffed school administration. The acute shortage of science and mathematics teachers makes it functionally impossible to redistribute staff across comprehensive schools without degrading the quality of existing academic tracks. [evidence: strong — OECD Education at a Glance; PSZ/PDSZ surveys]
- **legal_and_governance, political_feasibility**: Hungary should not abruptly abolish the system, but rather pursue a phased transition or gradual disincentivization of selective admissions.
  Why: A gradual phase-out ("felmenő rendszer") is legally required to protect acquired rights and respect complex bilateral agreements with church maintainers. Furthermore, abrupt top-down abolition faces severe resistance from vocal, resourceful urban middle-class parents who view these schools as mobility assets. [evidence: strong — Nkt. (public education act) analysis; moderate — Domestic press and stakeholder analyses]

## What the experts agree on

- **Early tracking impact**: Early academic selection at ages 10 and 12 segregates students by socio-economic status rather than cognitive ability, contributing to Hungary's exceptionally high PISA socio-economic gradient. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2012-2022 country notes; moderate — model knowledge]
- **Demographic contraction**: Hungary is experiencing a significant demographic decline (births falling from 125,000 in the 1990s to 85,000–95,000 recently), which will force school-network consolidations in the 2020s–2030s regardless of tracking policy. [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks]
- **Maintainer fragmentation**: The growing share of schools run by churches and foundations since 2013 complicates uniform structural mandates, as their admissions and structures are governed by separate agreements or legal frameworks. [evidence: strong — Public education act amendments; maintainer statistics]
- **Flat steady-state operational costs**: Per-pupil operational funding differences between standard primary and selective secondary tracks are modest; structural reform costs are primarily transition-driven. [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates]
