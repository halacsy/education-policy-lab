## Evidence

* Delaying academic tracking until age 14 or 15 reduces the impact of parental background on achievement [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006; Pekkarinen et al. 2009; Jakubowski et al. 2016].
* Early academic selection at ages 10 and 12 segregates students by socio-economic status rather than cognitive ability, contributing to Hungary's exceptionally high PISA socio-economic gradient [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2012-2022 country notes; moderate — model knowledge].
* Hungary is experiencing a significant demographic decline (births falling from 125,000 in the 1990s to 85,000–95,000 recently), which will force school-network consolidations in the 2020s–2030s regardless of tracking policy [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks].
* Phasing out early selection prevents demographic cohort shrinkage from accelerating the collapse and forced consolidation of rural primary school networks [evidence: moderate — model knowledge; demography findings].
* The growing share of schools run by churches and foundations since 2013 complicates uniform structural mandates, as their admissions and structures are governed by separate agreements or legal frameworks [evidence: strong — Public education act amendments; maintainer statistics].
* A gradual phase-out ("felmenő rendszer") is legally required to protect acquired rights and respect complex bilateral agreements with church maintainers [evidence: strong — Nkt. (public education act) analysis].
* Per-pupil operational funding differences across tracks are flat, meaning transition costs (retraining specialized secondary teachers to teach lower secondary, and modifying physical infrastructure) would dominate initial budget cycles [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates].
* Aligning entry requirements and quotas with regional equity targets and regulating admissions in non-state schools restricts socio-demographic sorting [evidence: moderate — hungarian_education_system findings].
* Enforcing admissions rules through tankerület audits and ministerial decrees restricts selective enrollment [evidence: weak — model knowledge].
* Moderately reduces socio-economic sorting in urban centers by regulating selective tracks [evidence: moderate — hungarian_education_system findings].
* Intense, well-organized resistance to structural tracking reforms exists among urban middle-class parents who view early tracks as a key mobility asset [evidence: moderate — domestic press and stakeholder analyses, 1990s-2010s].
* Gradually reducing enrollment caps and restricting tracks to regional urban hubs reduces between-school sorting [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA country notes; school_network_planning findings].
* Mitigates geographic sorting by limiting early selection to regional hubs [evidence: moderate — school_network_planning findings].
* Reduces the socio-economic gradient in student outcomes over the long term through gradual track reduction [evidence: moderate — international_comparison findings].
* Postponing academic selection substantially reduces the socio-economic gradient in reading and mathematics scores [evidence: strong — OECD PISA country notes; Pekkarinen et al. 2009] and improves average achievement levels over the long term [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016].
* Postponing tracking carries a high risk of a Polish-style political reversal due to middle-class resentment and lack of consensus [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016].
* Deploying targeted funding for disadvantaged schools (TEIP-style) and rigorous national curriculum standards improves outcomes without structural reorganization [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020].
* Avoiding the transition and friction costs of structural reorganization maintains administrative stability [evidence: weak — implementation_planning findings].
* A Portuguese-style package averts massive administrative friction and transition costs [evidence: weak — education_finance findings] and improves student performance in targeted disadvantaged schools without altering the tracking age [evidence: moderate — Crato 2020].
* The acute shortage of science and mathematics teachers makes it functionally impossible to redistribute staff across comprehensive schools without degrading the quality of existing academic tracks [evidence: strong — OECD Education at a Glance; PSZ/PDSZ surveys].

## Interpretation

* The early selection tracks (*gimnázium*) act as escape routes for high-socioeconomic status (SES) families, accelerating demographic and geographic school segregation [interpretation].
* Retaining the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system under structural constraints (such as urban hub restrictions or equity quotas) attempts to manage demographic school-network consolidation while avoiding full structural disruption [interpretation].
* Relying on non-structural reforms, such as targeted school intervention budgets (TEIP-style) and rigorous standards, assumes classroom-level quality improvements can compensate for the segregating effects of early tracking [interpretation].
* The transition process of comprehensive school integration would likely overwhelm Hungary's understaffed school administration, and teacher redistribution is constrained by acute math and science teacher shortages [interpretation].
* Any abrupt abolition of early tracking is legally and politically high-risk, necessitating a phased transition ("felmenő rendszer") to respect acquired rights and prevent middle-class flight [interpretation].

## Assumptions

* Parent behavior is primarily driven by the desire to maximize educational advantage for their children [assumption].
* The government lacks the immediate political or legal leverage to fully homogenize admissions and funding policies across state, church, and foundation schools [assumption].
* Elite gimnaziums will not bypass reformed admissions rules via oral exams or alternative diagnostic pathways [assumption].
* Middle-class parents will accept admissions quotas rather than seeking to bypass them through non-state alternatives or shadow education providers [assumption].
* Non-state school maintainers can be restricted from absorbing the phased-out selective capacity [assumption].
* Teachers currently assigned to 6- and 8-year academic tracks can be successfully redeployed into standard 4-year programs [assumption].
* Church and private maintainers will eventually comply with the phased contraction under structural rules [assumption].
* Postponing academic selection requires a school infrastructure capable of handling high student heterogeneity [assumption].
* The Hungarian comprehensive track (general school) currently lacks the equivalent teacher quality and support systems that supported successful international transitions [assumption].
* Hungarian PISA performance and social mobility metrics are currently depressed by the early selection processes [assumption].
* Non-structural interventions (standards, targeted funding) are politically easier to implement and sustain than structural changes like altering school tracking ages [assumption].
* The mechanisms of Portugal's TEIP and curriculum standards are transferable to the Hungarian socio-economic and institutional context [assumption].
* The teacher shortage cannot be mitigated in the short term through wage increases or recruitment alone [assumption].

## Recommendations

* **Option A: Regulate Admissions and Implement Quotas (Within Current Structure)**
  Keep the 6- and 8-year gimnazium tracks but regulate entry requirements and implement regional equity quotas. Under centralized supervision (Tankerületek), enforce these rules through administrative audits and ministerial decrees to restrict selective socio-demographic sorting.
* **Option B: Gradual Capacity Phase-down**
  Establish a gradual contraction of selective tracks. Reduce enrollment caps, restrict tracking eligibility to regional urban hubs, and phase out excess capacity over time ("felmenő rendszer") to align with legal protections of acquired rights and avoid immediate structural collapse.
* **Option C: Comprehensive School Reform (Postponing Tracking to Age 14)**
  Pass legislative amendments to abolish all academic selection before grade 8, transitioning to a comprehensive lower-secondary school system. Accompany this transition with nationwide teacher retraining programs in differentiated instruction and remedial support to manage classroom heterogeneity.
* **Option D: Non-Structural Compensation (Portuguese-style Targeted Package)**
  Retain the current early selective structure but focus resources on closing the achievement gaps. Implement targeted funding packages for low-performing primary schools (TEIP-style), establish rigorous national curriculum standards, and create teacher retention incentives for marginalized regions.

## Open questions

* **Jurisdictional Reach and Non-State Compliance**: Can the government legally and politically enforce admissions quotas or capacity reductions on church- and foundation-maintained schools without triggering systemic litigation or driving high-SES flight to unregulated private providers?
* **Resource and Teacher Readiness**: Does the public school system possess the administrative capacity and teacher supply (particularly in science and mathematics) to handle student heterogeneity if tracking is postponed, or will comprehensive reforms degrade overall academic quality in the short term?
* **Policy and Financial Continuity**: Can Hungary secure the long-term budgetary and political consensus required to sustain targeted funding (TEIP-style) or multi-year teacher retraining programs across electoral cycles to prevent reform dilution or reversal?
