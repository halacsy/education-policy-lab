# Critique: evidence_checker

## S3.mechanism
Objection: The claim that "postponing academic tracking until age 14 or 15 significantly reduces the impact of parental background on achievement" is tagged a single "strong" evidence grade backed by three citations, but two of those three (Pekkarinen et al. 2009 for finland_comprehensive, Jakubowski et al. 2016 for poland_reform) are graded "moderate" in the curated registry, not "strong." Only tracking_inequality (Hanushek & Woessmann 2006) is registry-graded "strong." Bundling a strong and two moderate sources into one "strong" tag overstates the aggregate evidentiary weight for this specific causal claim about Hungary.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Split the tag into "strong (cross-country DiD: Hanushek & Woessmann) + moderate (single-country reform evaluations: Pekkarinen et al.; Jakubowski et al.)" or downgrade the combined claim to "moderate" and note that only the cross-country comparison is strong evidence.

## S1.mechanism
Objection: The first mechanism bullet claims aligning entry requirements/quotas with equity targets and regulating non-state admissions "restricts socio-demographic sorting," tagged "moderate — hungarian_education_system findings." "hungarian_education_system" is not a source in the curated registry; the closest registry entries (school_choice [moderate], legal [strong]) actually document that free choice plus early selective tracks *produces* sorting and that phase-out (not admissions regulation) requires legislative action — neither directly supports the claim that quota-based admissions regulation *restricts* sorting in a system with parallel non-state maintainers outside Ministry jurisdiction. This is an unsupported causal claim dressed as moderate registry evidence.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Either cite a registry source that actually tests admissions-quota effects, or downgrade the tag to "weak — model knowledge" and state explicitly that no registry source evaluates quota efficacy against non-state displacement.
