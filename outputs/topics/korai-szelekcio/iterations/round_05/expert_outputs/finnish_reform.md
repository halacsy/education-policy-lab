# Expert analysis: finnish_reform

## Findings (evidence)
- The introduction of Finland's comprehensive school reform (*peruskoulu*), which postponed academic tracking until age 16, significantly reduced the impact of parental background on student achievement. [evidence: moderate — Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009]
- The equity benefits of the Finnish reform cannot be isolated from its structural bundling with highly selective teacher training and robust, early special education support systems. [evidence: moderate — Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009]
- Hungary's early tracking system (6- and 8-year gimnaziums starting at ages 10 and 12) selects students into academic tracks significantly earlier than the post-reform Finnish system. [evidence: strong — model knowledge]

## Interpretation
Postponing academic tracking is a powerful tool for equity, but it does not work in isolation. The Finnish success demonstrates that delaying selection to age 16 only yields high equity because it was coupled with high-quality, standardized teacher preparation and intensive, early remedial support for struggling students. If Hungary abolishes its 6- and 8-year gimnaziums without simultaneously upgrading teacher quality and support structures in comprehensive schools, student variance will likely increase within classrooms, potentially leading to middle-class flight from public education.

## Assumptions
- Postponing academic selection requires a school infrastructure capable of handling high student heterogeneity. [assumption]
- The Hungarian comprehensive track (general school) currently lacks the equivalent teacher quality and support systems that supported the Finnish transition. [assumption]

## Position
Hungary should phase out early selection into 6- and 8-year gimnaziums to improve equity, but only if this structural change is bundled with systemic investments in standardized teacher training and universal diagnostic support systems.

## Uncertainties
- The precise causal weight of the postponed tracking itself versus the accompanying teacher reforms in achieving Finland's equity outcomes. [confidence: low; would be reduced by: comparative natural experiments isolating tracking from teacher reform policy]
- The willingness of the Hungarian middle class to support comprehensive schools rather than seeking private or alternative selective schooling if early tracking is abolished. [confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Hungarian parent survey data and school choice simulation models]
