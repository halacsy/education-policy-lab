# Critique: political_risk_checker

## S2.political_risks
Objection: The scenario names reversal risk explicitly ("A successor government can freeze or reverse the trajectory cheaply (Polish precedent)") but the mechanism and implementation_steps contain no entrenchment design to counter it — the only countermeasure is relegated to uncertainties as an untested "cross-party pact or statutory entrenchment attempt," not a designed feature of the policy. A decade-long cap trajectory with no legal lock-in (e.g., cardinal-law status, multi-year budget statute, EU-funding conditionality, or independent-body oversight with automatic continuation) is not a mitigated risk, it is an acknowledged and unaddressed one. Citing the Polish case as the risk driver without specifying why Hungary's design would resist the same freeze mechanism (which in Poland was a simple ministerial/legislative reversal) is an evidence gap, not just a caveat.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add an implementation step that legally entrenches the cap trajectory (e.g., requires supermajority or cardinal-law amendment to alter, or ties disbursement of the advanced-programme fund to an independent multi-year formula insulated from annual budget votes), and move "cross-party pact" from an uncertainty into a precondition for launch.

## S3.political_risks
Objection: The scenario lists "Polish-style reversal after government change destroys sunk transition costs" as a risk and simultaneously stakes the entire mechanism's legitimacy on a cross-party pact attempted as implementation step 1 — but nowhere addresses what happens if the pact fails, which given the scenario's own admission that this is the "highest-intensity opposition of all scenarios," is the more likely outcome than success. There is no fallback entrenchment path (referendum lock, constitutional-level protection for enrolled-cohort guarantees extended to the phase-out schedule itself, staged legal irreversibility per cohort) described anywhere in mechanism or implementation_steps. A structural reform this large, admitted to require "political ownership survives at least two electoral cycles" as an assumption, cannot rely on a pact attempt with no Plan B.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Specify a fallback entrenchment mechanism independent of cross-party agreement (e.g., irrevocable felmenő rendszer extended cohort-by-cohort so each year's phase-out becomes a fait accompli that a successor government cannot cheaply reverse without disrupting pupils already inside the new system), and state explicitly what happens to the transition if the pact fails in year 1.

## S1.political_risks
Objection: The political_risks section names only elite-school/middle-class parents and church/private maintainer exemption-seeking as opposition, but the scenario's own uncertainties section flags a second, unaddressed opposition channel — the test-preparation industry, whose adaptation is cited as a driver of the "weak" evidence rating on reduced coachability. A commercial sector with revenue at stake from the admission exam's format has direct incentive to lobby against the assessment redesign (e.g., via media campaigns framing it as "opaque" or "unfair"), yet this is entirely absent from political_risks. Understating the opposition set to two actors when a third, better-resourced actor is implicit in the evidence discussion is a gap that affects feasibility, not phrasing.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Add the test-preparation industry as a named opposition actor with its likely tactic (reframing the new assessment as unfair/opaque to parents) and a corresponding mitigation (e.g., public transparency of item design, independent audit of the new assessment's coachability).
