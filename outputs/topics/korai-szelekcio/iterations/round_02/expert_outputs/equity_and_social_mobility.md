# Expert analysis: equity_and_social_mobility

## Findings (evidence)
- In PISA, the share of reading and mathematics score variance explained by socio-economic status (SES) is among the highest in the OECD for Hungary, standing at approximately 19-25% across cycles compared to the OECD average of 12-14% [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2012-2022 country notes].
- Roughly 8-12% of a cohort enters the 6- or 8-year gimnazium tracks, selected at age 10 or 12 via the central written admission examination, with entrants strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics].
- Hungarian value-added studies find that the raw advantage of early-selective gimnazium tracks is mostly a selection effect, meaning the causal (value-added) gain for admitted pupils is modest [evidence: moderate — Horn (2013); Kertesi & Kezdi school-effect studies].
- The early sorting of pupils at age 10 or 12 concentrates lower-SES students in standard primary schools, depriving them of high-achieving peer groups [evidence: weak — model knowledge].

## Interpretation
The Hungarian education system functions as an engine of stratification rather than mobility. The early selection process into 6- and 8-year gimnaziums does not produce substantial academic value-added for the selected pupils, but it successfully screens out low-SES and rural students. This segregation deprives non-selected students of positive peer effects, exacerbating the OECD-leading link between family background and student performance.

## Assumptions
- Positive peer effects are critical for the academic and social development of non-selected pupils [assumption].
- Reducing early selection will not harm the absolute academic performance of high-ability students [assumption].

## Position
Hungary should phase out the 6- and 8-year selective gimnazium tracks to delay student sorting until age 14, thereby reducing socio-economic segregation and improving equity without sacrificing student progress.

## Uncertainties
- The precise magnitude of negative peer effects on the students remaining in standard primary schools after the top 8-12% are selected [confidence: medium; would be reduced by: longitudinal peer-effect studies in Hungarian primary schools following selection events].
- The degree of political and public resistance from urban, high-SES parents to phasing out these elite tracks [confidence: low; would be reduced by: national representative polling on educational reforms].
