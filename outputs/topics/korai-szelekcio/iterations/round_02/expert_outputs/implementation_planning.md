# Expert analysis: implementation_planning

## Findings (evidence)
- Hungary suffers from a persistent, systemic shortage and aging of teachers, particularly in mathematics and sciences and within marginalized regions, meaning any structural restructuring must compete for this limited human capital [evidence: strong — OECD Education at a Glance; PSZ/PDSZ surveys].
- Per-pupil funding discrepancies between educational tracks remain modest, indicating that structural reform costs are primarily driven by transitional friction (administration, retraining, facility realignment) rather than long-term operational costs [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates].
- Secondary grammar schools (gimnázium) offering 6- and 8-year programs disproportionately attract higher-performing students early, concentrating administrative and pedagogical resources on select cohorts [evidence: moderate — Model knowledge].

## Interpretation
Eliminating or heavily modifying 6- and 8-year gimnaziums represents a high-friction administrative operation. While removing early tracking could theoretically desegregate student cohorts, the transition process (reallocating staff, redesigning curricula, and managing regional school consolidation) would overwhelm Hungary's fragile, understaffed school administration. The acute shortage of specialized teachers in science and mathematics makes it functionally impossible to redistribute teaching staff across comprehensive school systems without severely degrading the quality of existing academic tracks.

## Assumptions
- The administrative capacity of the Hungarian public education sector is insufficient to run major structural reorganizations and curriculum redesigns simultaneously [assumption].
- The teacher shortage cannot be mitigated in the short term through wage increases or recruitment alone [assumption].

## Position
Hungary should retain the 6- and 8-year gimnázium system in the medium term, delaying structural integration until teacher supply stabilizes, because the transitional administrative burden of dismantling it would collapse educational delivery in disadvantaged regions.

## Uncertainties
- The precise cost of retraining programs and building reallocations required to phase out early selection [confidence: medium; would be reduced by: detailed Ministry of Finance transition budget forecasts and regional audit data].
- The exact rate at which teachers currently working in elite 6- and 8-year schools would exit the public system entirely if forced to teach in standard comprehensive schools [confidence: low; would be reduced by: comprehensive teacher union survey data and workforce exit tracking].
