# Expert analysis: hungarian_education_system

## Findings (evidence)
- Roughly 8-12% of a cohort enters 6- or 8-year gimnázium tracks, selected at age 10 or 12 largely via the central written admission examination; entrants are strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families. [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics]
- Free school choice plus early selective tracks produces strong between-school sorting already at the primary stage in cities; abolition of tracks alone would not eliminate sorting through catchment and choice. [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013; Berényi-Berkovits-Erőss school choice studies]
- Since 2013 most state schools are run by state school district centres (tankerület), while churches and foundations maintain a growing share of schools, complicating any uniform structural mandate. [evidence: strong — Public education act amendments; maintainer statistics]
- Early tracking systems generally exacerbate social inequalities without improving overall national academic achievement levels compared to comprehensive education systems. [evidence: moderate — Model knowledge / OECD PISA studies]

## Interpretation
The Hungarian school system is characterized by early and severe structural segregation. The 6- and 8-year gimnázium tracks act as escape routes for high-SES families from the public primary sector, accelerating sorting. Because non-state maintainers (churches/foundations) operate under different regulatory incentives and admissions rules, simply banning selective tracks within state schools will likely push selective demand into the non-state sector, shifting rather than eliminating sorting.

## Assumptions
- Parent behavior is primarily driven by the desire to maximize educational advantage for their children. [assumption]
- The government lacks the immediate political or legal leverage to fully homogenize admissions and funding policies across state, church, and foundation schools. [assumption]

## Position
Hungary should retain the 6- and 8-year gimnázium tracks but align their entry requirements and quotas with regional equity targets, while concurrently regulating non-state school admissions to limit socio-demographic sorting.

## Uncertainties
- The exact proportion of students from disadvantaged backgrounds who successfully access non-state selective tracks. [confidence: low | would be reduced by: Oktatási Hivatal student registry data cross-referenced with family tax/benefit records]
- The willingness of church maintainers to accept uniform equity-based quotas under threat of subsidy withdrawal. [confidence: medium | would be reduced by: surveys and qualitative interviews with church maintainer representatives and Ministry officials]
