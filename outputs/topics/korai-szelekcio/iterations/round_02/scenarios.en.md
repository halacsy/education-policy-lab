# Policy scenarios

## S1 — Admissions Reform within the Current Structure
**Goal**
Mitigate socio-demographic sorting at ages 10 and 12 without changing school structures by reforming admission mechanisms and introducing equity-based quotas.

**Mechanism**
- Aligning entry requirements and quotas with regional equity targets, and regulating non-state school admissions, limits socio-demographic sorting [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013; Berényi-Berkovits-Erőss school choice studies]
- Amending written admission exams or reweighting scores to prioritize SES diversity limits the concentration of high-SES, urban pupils in selective tracks [evidence: moderate — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics]

**Evidence status**
moderate — local school sorting and selective track demographic imbalances are well-documented, but the specific efficacy of quota-based admissions within a highly segregated, multi-maintainer system is only moderately supported

**Assumptions**
- Parent behavior is primarily driven by the desire to maximize educational advantage for their children [assumption]
- The government lacks the immediate political or legal leverage to fully homogenize admissions and funding policies across state, church, and foundation schools [assumption]
- Elite gimnaziums will not bypass reformed admissions rules via oral exams or alternative diagnostic pathways [assumption]
- Middle-class parents will accept admissions quotas rather than seeking to bypass them through church- or foundation-run school entry [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Reduced concentration of privilege within the selective tracks without incurring high transition costs [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates]
- Preservation of elite academic tracks while opening pathways for high-ability, lower-SES pupils [evidence: weak — Horn (2013); Kertesi & Kezdi school-effect studies]

**Equity impact**
Moderately improves equity within selective tracks, but does not address baseline sorting in primary schools, and runs a high risk of middle-class bypass through non-state schools unless non-state admissions are strictly regulated.

**Cost categories**
- Admissions administration and test design adjustments
- Monitoring and enforcement compliance costs for state school district centres (tankerületek)
- Litigation and legal defense costs arising from challenges by non-state maintainers or parent groups

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education — Amend the ministerial decree governing secondary admissions to mandate equity quotas and score reweighting
- Oktatási Hivatal — Redesign the central written admission examinations to reduce socio-economic bias
- Tankerületek — Audit local admission practices and enforce quota compliance across state schools

**Political risks**
- Intense, well-organized resistance from urban middle-class parents who view early admission as a defensive mobility asset
- Legal disputes and opposition from church and foundation school maintainers over state encroachment on admissions autonomy
- Administrative circumvention by schools utilizing alternative selection criteria (e.g., oral exams or school-level assessments)

**Uncertainties**
- The exact proportion of students from disadvantaged backgrounds who successfully access non-state selective tracks (confidence: low | would be reduced by: Oktatási Hivatal student registry data cross-referenced with family tax/benefit records)
- The willingness of church maintainers to accept uniform equity-based quotas under threat of subsidy withdrawal (confidence: medium | would be reduced by: surveys and qualitative interviews with church maintainer representatives and Ministry officials)

## S2 — Gradual Phase-Down of 6/8-Year Entry Places
**Goal**
Gradually reduce enrollment caps in 6- and 8-year gimnaziums to disincentivize early selection, transitioning them eventually to standard 4-year programs.

**Mechanism**
- Gradually reducing enrollment caps in early selective tracks limits academic selection and reduces between-school sorting [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA country notes; Kertesi & Kezdi 2013]
- Applying a gradual phase-out (felmenő rendszer) protects the acquired rights of currently enrolled students and ensures constitutional compliance [evidence: strong — Nkt. (public education act) analysis]

**Evidence status**
moderate — supported by evidence on the sorting effects of early selection and legal requirements for transitional periods, though long-term mitigation of sorting is moderated by potential middle-class flight to alternative sectors

**Assumptions**
- A gradual phase-out (felmenő rendszer) satisfies the constitutional principles of legal certainty and acquired rights [assumption]
- Middle-class resistance to reform will remain high unless they are provided with credible quality guarantees in the comprehensive primary school system [assumption]
- Non-state school maintainers can be restricted from absorbing the phased-out selective capacity [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Long-term reduction in socio-demographic school segregation as students remain in primary tracks longer [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA 2012-2022 country notes]
- Smooth administrative transition minimizing immediate facility adjustments [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates]

**Equity impact**
Significantly improves equity in the long run by reducing between-school sorting, though the extent of this improvement depends heavily on whether middle-class parents bypass the reform by moving children to private or church schools.

**Cost categories**
- Bilateral contract renegotiations with non-state school maintainers
- Curriculum adjustments and staff reorganization within phasing-out schools
- Potential legal liability and compensation claims if transition timelines violate acquired rights

**Implementation steps**
- National Assembly — Amend the Public Education Act (Nkt.) to legally define a declining cap on 6- and 8-year class sizes and entry quotas
- Ministry of Education — Renegotiate agreements with church maintainers to restrict replacement classes
- Klebelsberg Centre — Oversee the transitional staffing plans as secondary teaching hours shift toward 4-year tracks

**Political risks**
- Sustained political lobbying and backlash from elite secondary school administrations and alumni networks
- Inter-regime reversal risks during the multi-year phase-in period, where a future government might overturn the phased caps to capture middle-class votes
- Loss of middle-class voters in urban centres who view the phase-out as an attack on meritocratic institutions

**Uncertainties**
- The exact timeframe required to pass constitutional muster for altering foundation school charters (megbízhatóság: alacsony; csökkentené: az Alkotmánybíróság korábbi, szerzett jogokkal kapcsolatos határozatainak jogi elemzése)
- The precise willingness of major church maintainers to voluntarily renegotiate their structural agreements in exchange for compensatory funding (megbízhatóság: közepes; csökkentené: a Magyar Katolikus Püspöki Konferencia és a Református Egyház képviselőivel folytatott strukturált interjúk)

## S3 — Comprehensive School to Age 14 (Structural Reform)
**Goal**
Abolish all 6- and 8-year gimnaziums and transition to a comprehensive lower-secondary school structure for all students until grade 8 (age 14).

**Mechanism**
- Postponing academic tracking until age 14 or 15 reduces the impact of parental socio-economic status on student achievement [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006; moderate — Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009; Jakubowski et al. 2016]
- Postponing academic tracking improves aggregate national performance by elevating the achievement floor [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD]
- Phasing out selective tracks prevents demographic shrinkage from accelerating the collapse of rural primary school networks [evidence: moderate — KSH demographic yearbooks; model knowledge]

**Evidence status**
strong — robust international and comparative empirical evidence supports the equity benefits of delaying tracking, though the domestic implementation capacity is constrained

**Assumptions**
- PISA socio-economic status gradients are valid proxies for long-term equity of opportunity in the labor market [assumption]
- The general cross-country findings on tracking apply to the specific institutional and cultural context of Hungary [assumption]
- Postponing academic selection requires a school infrastructure capable of handling high student heterogeneity [assumption]
- The Hungarian comprehensive track (general school) currently lacks the equivalent teacher quality and support systems that supported the Finnish transition [assumption]
- Educational outcomes and equity dynamics in Poland are structurally comparable to those in Hungary due to shared post-communist institutional legacies [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Substantial reduction in the variance of outcomes explained by parental background [evidence: strong — OECD PISA country notes]
- Stabilization of regional school networks and mitigation of rural primary school depopulation [evidence: moderate — KSH demographic yearbooks]
- Improvement in average national achievement on international assessments [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016]

**Equity impact**
Maximizes educational equity by eliminating formal early sorting pathways, preventing the concentration of low-SES students in segregated primary tracks, and enhancing peer effects, though it runs a high risk of driving affluent families into private schooling.

**Cost categories**
- Teacher retraining and pedagogical professional development for handling heterogeneous classrooms
- Physical building modification and classroom reconfiguration costs across municipalities
- Administrative and legal costs associated with rewriting structural agreements and local transport adjustments

**Implementation steps**
- National Assembly — Enact a comprehensive ban on selective tracks before grade 9, aligning the Public Education Act
- Ministry of Education — Launch a nationwide teacher retraining program focused on differentiated instruction
- Klebelsberg Centre — Conduct regional school audits and reallocate facilities to accommodate shifted cohorts

**Political risks**
- Massive political backlash and protests from urban middle-class families and prestigious gimnazium boards
- High risk of a Polish-style political reversal if a future administration campaigns on restoring elite tracks
- Immediate migration of high-SES students to private or shadow education providers, creating informal segregation

**Uncertainties**
- The precise causal weight of the postponed tracking itself versus the accompanying teacher reforms in achieving Finland's equity outcomes (confidence: low; would be reduced by: comparative natural experiments isolating tracking from teacher reform policy)
- The willingness of the Hungarian middle class to support comprehensive schools rather than seeking private or alternative selective schooling if early tracking is abolished (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Hungarian parent survey data and school choice simulation models)
- The precise proportion of the Hungarian PISA variance explained by the 6- and 8-year gimnazium systems versus standard tracking at grade 8 (megbízhatóság: közepes; csökkentené: Hungarian administrative student-level panel data matching track choices with primary school test scores)
- The potential flight of high-SES students to private or shadow education providers if early public tracking is abolished (megbízhatóság: alacsony; csökkentené: evaluation of private school enrollment trends in countries that recently postponed tracking, such as Poland)

## S4 — Keep the Structure, Compensate General Schools (Portuguese-Style Package)
**Goal**
Retain early tracking structures but deploy targeted non-structural interventions to raise performance and equity in lagging general primary schools.

**Mechanism**
- Implementing rigorous curriculum standards, school clustering, and targeted support (TEIP-style) improves performance without altering tracking structures [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020]
- Targeted funding routing to schools with low-SES concentrations improves outcomes and reduces grade retention [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020]

**Evidence status**
moderate — supported by the Portuguese reform experience, though the transferability of this success is highly conditioned by Hungary's severe regional teacher shortages

**Assumptions**
- Non-structural interventions (standards, targeted funding) are politically easier to implement and sustain than structural changes like altering school tracking ages [assumption]
- The mechanisms of Portugal's TEIP and curriculum standards are transferable to the Hungarian socio-economic and institutional context [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Improves student performance in disadvantaged areas without triggering middle-class resistance or political backlash [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020]
- Avoids the transition and friction costs of structural reorganization [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates]

**Equity impact**
Improves school performance in disadvantaged areas and mitigates extreme performance deficits, but leaves primary-level sorting and demographic tracking untouched, excluding disadvantaged pupils in non-targeted schools.

**Cost categories**
- Targeted funding programs for designated disadvantaged schools (TEIP-style recurring budgets)
- Development and administration of standardized national assessment systems
- Teacher retention and relocation incentive schemes for marginalized regions

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education — Design and fund a targeted intervention program (TEIP-style) for disadvantaged schools
- Oktatási Hivatal — Develop rigorous national curriculum standards and diagnostic evaluation frameworks
- Klebelsberg Centre — Implement school clustering and coordinate targeted resource allocation to lagging units

**Political risks**
- Very low risk of middle-class backlash as elite tracks remain intact
- Risk of budget cuts or diversion of targeted funds to other fiscal priorities in the absence of structural anchors
- Frustration from teachers' unions if accountability and standards are raised without matching base salary increases

**Uncertainties**
- The precise impact of Hungarian teacher shortages on the viability of implementing Portuguese-style curriculum standards and targeted school support plans (confidence: high; would be reduced by: comparative studies on teacher supply and qualifications in Hungarian disadvantaged schools versus historical Portuguese levels)
- Whether Hungarian political consensus can support sustained, long-term funding for targeted school intervention programs without policy reversals (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: analysis of Hungarian budgetary commitments and legislative continuity in education policy)
