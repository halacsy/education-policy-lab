# Rejected Framings: Policy Scenarios for Secondary Admissions Reform

This document details the alternative framings, policy options, and conceptual boundaries considered for each scenario before arriving at the chosen formulations.

## S1 — Admissions Reform within the Current Structure
- CHOSEN: Aligning entry requirements and quotas with regional equity targets, and regulating non-state school admissions, limits socio-demographic sorting while adjusting exam weightings.
- REJECTED: Mandatory Centralized Allocation System — reason: Excluded because a fully centralized, algorithm-based school matching system that ignores parent preferences would trigger extreme middle-class backlash and exceed the current legal authority of the tankerületek over non-state maintainers.
- REJECTED: Voluntary Diversity Charters for Elite Tracks — reason: Rejected as an ineffective mechanism [evidence: weak] because elite schools lack the institutional incentives to voluntarily dilute their high-SES student concentrations without mandatory quotas or financial penalties.

## S2 — Gradual Phase-Down of 6/8-Year Entry Places
- CHOSEN: Gradually reducing enrollment caps in 6- and 8-year gimnaziums to disincentivize early selection, transitioning them eventually to standard 4-year programs.
- REJECTED: Immediate Moratorium on New 6/8-Year Classes — reason: Rejected due to severe constitutional risks regarding legal certainty and acquired rights; an immediate freeze would violate transitional period requirements and trigger instant litigation from church maintainers.
- REJECTED: Tuition Fees for Early Selective Tracks — reason: Rejected because introducing fees to offset demand would legally conflict with the constitutional right to free public education and worsen, rather than mitigate, socio-demographic sorting.

## S3 — Comprehensive School to Age 14 (Structural Reform)
- CHOSEN: Abolish all 6- and 8-year gimnaziums and transition to a comprehensive lower-secondary school structure for all students until grade 8 (age 14).
- REJECTED: Extension of Primary Schooling to Age 16 (10-Grade Schooling) — reason: Feasibility constraints make a 10-year comprehensive school model impossible to implement, as the current educational infrastructure and teacher supply are severely strained and cannot support a nationwide physical consolidation of this scale.
- REJECTED: Parallel Selective-Vocational Tracking with Postponed Selection — reason: Rejected because keeping vocational tracking alongside academic tracks while merely changing the age threshold fails to eliminate the sorting mechanics identified in Jakubowski et al. 2016, and duplicates administrative overhead.

## S4 — Keep the Structure, Compensate General Schools (Portuguese-Style Package)
- CHOSEN: Retain early tracking structures but deploy targeted non-structural interventions to raise performance and equity in lagging general primary schools.
- REJECTED: Voucher-Based Funding for Disadvantaged Students — reason: A voucher system enabling low-SES students to attend private selective tracks was rejected because it accelerates the demographic and financial drain on rural primary school networks, exacerbating regional segregation.
- REJECTED: Comprehensive Merger of Segregated Rural Schools — reason: Discarded due to high transition costs and local political resistance; forced physical mergers of rural schools do not address the acute teacher shortage and would trigger severe logistical hurdles for student transport.
