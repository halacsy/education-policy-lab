# Critique: cost_checker

## S1.cost_categories
Objection: The cost list contains no fiscal magnitude or evidence tag at all — every item ("admissions administration," "monitoring and enforcement," "litigation") is a bare category with no order-of-magnitude estimate, while the scenario's own "expected_benefits" claims "without incurring high transition costs" [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates]. A claim about *low* transition cost cannot rest on a cost_categories list that itself supplies zero quantification; the weak-tagged budget-chapter source is not shown to actually support "low," so the benefit claim is unsupported, not merely weakly supported. The list also omits a major likely cost driver flagged by the synthesis's "Non-State Maintainers" consensus point: compensatory payments or subsidy adjustments to church/foundation schools needed to induce compliance with equity quotas, which is the most probable route by which the reform's actual fiscal exposure would materialize.

## S2.cost_categories
Objection: "Curriculum adjustments and staff reorganization within phasing-out schools" collapses what should be two distinct and large cost items — (a) one-time transition costs (retraining, contract renegotiation) and (b) a structural steady-state cost: surplus or redundant subject-specialist teachers (particularly in the humanities/sciences taught only in the upper grades of 6/8-year tracks) who cannot simply be "reorganized" without severance, relocation, or early-retirement payouts. The synthesis's own "Financial Profile of Reform" consensus states transition costs (not steady-state) dominate [evidence: weak], yet this scenario's cost list has no severance/redundancy line at all and no split flagging which items are one-off versus recurring — a transition-vs-steady-state confusion that could lead planners to underbudget the single largest line item in a felmenő-rendszer phase-down.

## S3.cost_categories
Objection: "Local transport adjustments" is buried as a minor sub-item under "Administrative and legal costs," but the synthesis's own agreed fact on demography ("Hungary's annual births have declined... forcing network consolidation decisions" [evidence: strong]) implies that abolishing 6/8-year tracks nationally, combined with ongoing rural depopulation, will require materially larger student transport networks to reach consolidated comprehensive schools in areas losing population — this is a recurring steady-state operating cost, not an administrative one-off, and it is not represented as such anywhere in the list. Given that S3's own expected_benefit claims "stabilization of regional school networks" [evidence: moderate — KSH demographic yearbooks], the cost side must show the transport/logistics cost of achieving that stabilization; currently it does not.

## S4.cost_categories
Objection: The evidence_status paragraph explicitly identifies "Hungary's severe regional teacher shortages" as the binding constraint on transferability of the Portuguese model, yet the cost_categories list only offers a vague "teacher retention and relocation incentive schemes for marginalized regions" with no salary-premium or recruitment-cost estimate.
