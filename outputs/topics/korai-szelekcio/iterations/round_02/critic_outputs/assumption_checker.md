# Critique: assumption_checker

## S1.assumptions
Objection: The assumption "Elite gimnaziums will not bypass reformed admissions rules via oral exams or alternative diagnostic pathways" is directly contradicted by this same scenario's own Political risks field, which lists "Administrative circumvention by schools utilizing alternative selection criteria (e.g., oral exams or school-level assessments)" as an active risk. The scenario cannot simultaneously assume non-bypass and flag bypass as a named risk; the mechanism's efficacy claim (moderate, Kertesi & Kezdi 2013; Berényi-Berkovits-Erőss) rests on quota/exam reweighting holding, but the cited school-choice literature documents exactly this kind of adaptive circumvention historically, so the assumption is not merely unverified — it is contradicted by evidence already invoked elsewhere in the same record.

## S2.assumptions
Objection: The assumption "Non-state school maintainers can be restricted from absorbing the phased-out selective capacity" is unsupported and conflicts with the scenario's own Equity impact statement ("depends heavily on whether middle-class parents bypass the reform... to private or church schools") and with the synthesis's agreed fact that "churches and foundations maintain a growing share of schools" precisely because maintainer fragmentation "complicates uniform structural mandates." No evidence tag is offered for the restrictability of non-state maintainers at all — the only cited evidence (Nkt. legal analysis, strong) supports the felmenő rendszer's constitutionality, not the state's capacity to bind church/foundation maintainers to capacity limits.

## S3.evidence_status
Objection: The overall "strong" evidence_status rating is inconsistent with the scenario's own assumptions list, which flags that (a) Hungary "lacks the equivalent teacher quality and support systems that supported the Finnish transition," (b) Poland's comparability rests on unverified "shared post-communist institutional legacies," and (c) PISA SES gradients are only assumed, not shown, to proxy long-term labor-market equity. Hanushek & Woessmann (2006) and Pekkarinen et al. (2009) support tracking-age effects on achievement variance in their own national contexts, not transferability to Hungary's specific teacher-supply and institutional conditions — the transferability gap is exactly what the assumptions admit is unresolved. A record with five explicit non-transferability assumptions describing the core causal chain cannot be labeled "strong" without qualification distinguishing the cross-country base rate (strong) from the Hungary-specific applicability (weak/unsupported).

## S4.expected_benefits
Objection: The claim "Avoids the transition and friction costs of structural reorganization [evidence: weak]" mischaracterizes the scenario's own cost profile: the Cost categories field lists "recurring" TEIP-style budgets, national assessment system development, and "teacher retention and relocation incentive schemes for marginalized regions" — the last of which is described elsewhere (Evidence status field) as constrained by "severe regional teacher shortages," a structural problem this package does not resolve. Framing the benefit as cost avoidance ignores that the uncertainty section separately rates confidence "high" that teacher shortages threaten the viability of the whole package, meaning the expected benefit and the flagged uncertainty are in tension: the same shortage that is asserted (high confidence) to threaten implementation is not counted against the "avoids costs" framing.
