# Critique: political_risk_checker

## S1.political_risks
Objection: The risk list catalogs opposition (middle-class mobilization, church-maintainer litigation, oral-exam circumvention) but omits the single most consequential reversal vector: the mechanism itself is implemented via ministerial decree ("Amend the ministerial decree governing secondary admissions"), not primary legislation. A decree-based quota system can be undone by a future minister with no legislative debate, no supermajority, and no judicial review comparable to what would apply to a statute. The scenario proposes no entrenchment device (e.g., elevating the quota rule to the Nkt. itself, or tying it to EU funding conditionality) despite this being the cheapest and fastest reversal path of any scenario in the set. Listing "administrative circumvention by schools" as a risk while ignoring the far larger risk of "administrative reversal by government" is a substantive omission, not a phrasing issue.

## S2.political_risks
Objection: The assumptions section explicitly flags as uncertain whether "non-state school maintainers can be restricted from absorbing the phased-out selective capacity," yet the political_risks section never carries this into a risk statement. If church maintainers expand their own 6/8-year tracks to absorb displaced demand (a live possibility given the maintainer-fragmentation fact the synthesis itself flags as agreed-upon), the felmenő rendszer produces sorting migration rather than sorting reduction — a political risk (loss of political credit for a reform that visibly fails) as much as a mechanism risk. Its absence from political_risks means the scenario understates a specific, named opposition channel (church-sector capacity expansion) rather than just "backlash from elite school administrations."

## S3.political_risks
Objection: The scenario names "High risk of a Polish-style political reversal" and the synthesis independently confirms Poland's reform was "fully reversed between 2016 and 2019" [evidence: moderate], meaning this is not a hypothetical but a documented near-analogue outcome. Despite acknowledging this, S3's implementation_steps rely entirely on ordinary National Assembly legislation ("Enact a comprehensive ban... aligning the Public Education Act") with no entrenchment mechanism proposed (no cardinal-law status, no constitutional anchoring, no multi-cycle sunset-proofing) even though Hungary's own constitutional framework contains a category of cardinal laws requiring two-thirds majorities that could have been discussed as a deliberate design choice. Naming the risk without proposing or even evaluating an entrenchment option, given a real precedent of full reversal, understates what "high risk" should imply for mechanism design.
