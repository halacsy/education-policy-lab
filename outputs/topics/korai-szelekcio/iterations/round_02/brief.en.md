## Evidence

* The 6- and 8-year grammar schools select students at ages 10 and 12 [evidence: strong].
* This early tracking disproportionately serves socio-economically advantaged, urban families [evidence: strong/weak].
* Hungary exhibits one of the highest shares of PISA score variance explained by socio-economic status within the OECD, hovering around 19–25% [evidence: strong].
* Since 2013, public education has been centralized under state school district centres (tankerület), but churches and foundations maintain a growing share of schools [evidence: strong].
* Hungary's annual births have declined from ~125,000 in the early 1990s to ~85,000–95,000 in recent years, forcing network consolidation decisions in the 2020s–2030s regardless of selection policy [evidence: strong].
* Per-pupil funding differences between tracking paths are modest; the dominant costs of any structural reform are transition costs (retraining, facilities, administration), not steady-state operational costs [evidence: weak].
* Poland's 1999 reform delayed tracking to age 15, resulting in PISA gains [evidence: moderate], but was fully reversed between 2016 and 2019 due to political reasons [evidence: moderate].
* Aligning entry requirements and quotas with regional equity targets, and regulating non-state school admissions, limits socio-demographic sorting [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013; Berényi-Berkovits-Erőss school choice studies].
* Amending written admission exams or reweighting scores to prioritize SES diversity limits the concentration of high-SES, urban pupils in selective tracks [evidence: moderate — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics].
* Reduced concentration of privilege within the selective tracks without incurring high transition costs is expected under S1 [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates].
* Preservation of elite academic tracks while opening pathways for high-ability, lower-SES pupils is expected under S1 [evidence: weak — Horn (2013); Kertesi & Kezdi school-effect studies].
* Gradually reducing enrollment caps in early selective tracks limits academic selection and reduces between-school sorting [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA country notes; Kertesi & Kezdi 2013].
* Applying a gradual phase-out (felmenő rendszer) protects the acquired rights of currently enrolled students and ensures constitutional compliance [evidence: strong — Nkt. (public education act) analysis].
* Long-term reduction in socio-demographic school segregation as students remain in primary tracks longer is expected under S2 [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA 2012-2022 country notes].
* Smooth administrative transition minimizing immediate facility adjustments is expected under S2 [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates].
* Postponing academic tracking until age 14 or 15 reduces the impact of parental socio-economic status on student achievement [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006; moderate — Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009; Jakubowski et al. 2016].
* Postponing academic tracking improves aggregate national performance by elevating the achievement floor [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD].
* Phasing out selective tracks prevents demographic shrinkage from accelerating the collapse of rural primary school networks [evidence: moderate — KSH demographic yearbooks; model knowledge].
* Substantial reduction in the variance of outcomes explained by parental background is expected under S3 [evidence: strong — OECD PISA country notes].
* Stabilization of regional school networks and mitigation of rural primary school depopulation is expected under S3 [evidence: moderate — KSH demographic yearbooks].
* Improvement in average national achievement on international assessments is expected under S3 [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016].
* Implementing rigorous curriculum standards, school clustering, and targeted support (TEIP-style) improves performance without altering tracking structures [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020].
* Targeted funding routing to schools with low-SES concentrations improves outcomes and reduces grade retention [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020].
* Improving student performance in disadvantaged areas without triggering middle-class resistance or political backlash is expected under S4 [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020].
* Avoiding the transition and friction costs of structural reorganization is expected under S4 [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates].

## Interpretation

* Early selection mechanisms in Hungary function as a structural driver of socio-demographic sorting, segregating students based on parental background rather than cognitive merit [interpretation].
* The fragmentation of school maintainers (state, church, and foundation) undermines the state's capacity to implement uniform educational reforms, as non-state tracks can absorb selective student demand and bypass public restrictions [interpretation].
* Demographic decline makes school network consolidation inevitable; however, maintaining early selection tracks in this context risks accelerating the depopulation and collapse of rural primary schools [interpretation].
* While structural reforms delaying tracking offer the largest potential gains in equity, they are highly vulnerable to middle-class resistance, legal challenges regarding acquired rights, and political reversals similar to the Polish experience [interpretation].
* Non-structural interventions (such as Portuguese-style targeted funding and standards) can mitigate performance deficits in disadvantaged areas with minimal political friction, but they do not resolve baseline structural segregation or tracking-induced sorting [interpretation].

## Assumptions

* Parent behavior is primarily driven by the desire to maximize educational advantage for their children [assumption].
* The government lacks the immediate political or legal leverage to fully homogenize admissions and funding policies across state, church, and foundation schools [assumption].
* Elite gimnaziums will not bypass reformed admissions rules via oral exams or alternative diagnostic pathways [assumption].
* Middle-class parents will accept admissions quotas rather than seeking to bypass them through church- or foundation-run school entry [assumption].
* A gradual phase-out (felmenő rendszer) satisfies the constitutional principles of legal certainty and acquired rights [assumption].
* Middle-class resistance to reform will remain high unless they are provided with credible quality guarantees in the comprehensive primary school system [assumption].
* Non-state school maintainers can be restricted from absorbing the phased-out selective capacity [assumption].
* PISA socio-economic status gradients are valid proxies for long-term equity of opportunity in the labor market [assumption].
* The general cross-country findings on tracking apply to the specific institutional and cultural context of Hungary [assumption].
* Postponing academic selection requires a school infrastructure capable of handling high student heterogeneity [assumption].
* The Hungarian comprehensive track (general school) currently lacks the equivalent teacher quality and support systems that supported the Finnish transition [assumption].
* Educational outcomes and equity dynamics in Poland are structurally comparable to those in Hungary due to shared post-communist institutional legacies [assumption].
* Non-structural interventions (standards, targeted funding) are politically easier to implement and sustain than structural changes like altering school tracking ages [assumption].
* The mechanisms of Portugal's TEIP and curriculum standards are transferable to the Hungarian socio-economic and institutional context [assumption].

## Recommendations

Instead of selecting a single scenario as a definitive solution, educational policy should be evaluated across multiple viable pathways depending on the prioritized objectives, legal constraints, and implementation capacities:

* **Pathway A: Admissions and Equity Quotas (S1)** Focuses on reforming secondary admissions rules and reweighting scores to mandate equity quotas. This targets socioeconomic sorting within the existing track structure, minimizing upfront transition costs, but requires robust regulatory oversight to prevent schools from using alternative entry mechanisms and to stop middle-class flight to non-state sectors.
* **Pathway B: Gradual Phase-Down of Early Entry (S2)** Focuses on a progressive reduction of enrollment caps in 6- and 8-year gimnaziums. This strategy respects acquired rights and constitutional principles through a transitional phase-out (*felmenő rendszer*), though its long-term success depends on restricting capacity expansion in non-state schools and improving primary school quality.
* **Pathway C: Structural Consolidation to Age 14 (S3)** Focuses on transitioning to a comprehensive lower-secondary system by abolishing early tracking tracks altogether. This maximizes equity outcomes and helps stabilize rural school networks, but demands significant investments in teacher retraining for heterogeneous classrooms, upgrades to comprehensive school infrastructure, and mitigation strategies against middle-class flight to private schooling.
* **Pathway D: Targeted School Compensation (S4)** Focuses on deploying targeted funding, rigorous standards, and school clustering (TEIP-style) to raise performance in lagging schools while keeping the tracking structure intact. This avoids transition costs and political backlash, but relies heavily on resolving teacher shortages in marginalized regions to ensure viability.

## Open questions

* What is the exact timeframe required to pass constitutional muster for altering foundation school charters, and how can agreements with church maintainers be legally renegotiated to prevent them from absorbing selective capacity?
* What is the willingness of the Hungarian middle class to support comprehensive schools rather than seeking private or alternative selective schooling if early tracking is abolished or capped?
* How severe is the impact of current teacher shortages (especially in math and science) on the feasibility of implementing both differentiated instruction in comprehensive schools and Portuguese-style support packages in disadvantaged regions?
* Can long-term funding and policy commitments for non-structural school support programs be politically sustained without structural anchors to prevent future budget diversion or policy reversals?
* What is the precise proportion of the Hungarian PISA variance explained by the 6- and 8-year gimnazium systems versus standard tracking at grade 8?
