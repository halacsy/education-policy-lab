## Overview
The reform of Hungary's early academic tracking system—consisting of the 6- and 8-year grammar school (*gimnázium*) tracks selecting students at ages 10 and 12—presents a complex trade-off between social equity and institutional feasibility. While international comparisons, demographic shifts, and equity analyses suggest that early tracking exacerbates socioeconomic segregation without raising overall performance, experts are deeply divided on the path forward. Proponents of structural reform advocate for phasing out these early tracks to improve equity, whereas other experts warn of high transition costs, severe teacher shortages, legal and maintainer complications, and the threat of political reversal.

## Disagreement map
- **international_comparison**, **finnish_reform**, **polish_reform**, **equity_and_social_mobility**, **demography**: Hungary should phase out early tracking into 6- and 8-year grammar schools to improve equity and prevent demographic decline from accelerating the collapse of rural school networks. Why: Cross-country evidence shows early selection segregates students by socio-economic status rather than cognitive ability, and delaying selection systematically reduces the impact of parental background on achievement.
- **hungarian_education_system** (minority): Hungary should retain the 6- and 8-year tracks but align their entry requirements and quotas with regional equity targets, while regulating non-state school admissions. Why: Simply banning selective tracks in state schools will push selective demand into the non-state sector (churches/foundations), shifting rather than eliminating sorting.
- **portuguese_reform** (minority): Hungary should retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system but deploy non-structural reforms (like Portugal's TEIP-style targeted funding and rigorous standards). Why: Substantial improvements in performance and equity can be achieved without politically disruptive structural reorganizations, allowing administrative resources to focus directly on classroom quality.
- **education_finance** (minority): Hungary should retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system. Why: The steady-state operational savings of consolidation are marginal, whereas the transition costs (teacher retraining and physical facility re-adaptation) are heavily front-loaded and outweigh potential savings.
- **legal_and_governance**, **political_feasibility** (minority): Hungary should avoid abrupt abolition, opting instead for a gradual phase-out ("felmenő rendszer") by disincentivizing selective admissions and using multi-year legislative notices. Why: Elite tracks are guarded by constitutional protections of acquired rights, complex bilateral agreements with church maintainers, and strong middle-class mobilization capacity that can trigger political reversals like the one seen in Poland.
- **implementation_planning** (minority): Hungary should retain the 6- and 8-year system in the medium term, delaying structural integration until teacher supply stabilizes. Why: The transition would overwhelm a fragile, understaffed school administration, and acute teacher shortages in science/math make it impossible to redistribute staff without degrading the quality of academic tracks.

## What the experts agree on
- **Early Selection and Segregation**: The 6- and 8-year grammar schools select students at ages 10 and 12 [evidence: strong], and this early tracking disproportionately serves socio-economically advantaged, urban families [evidence: strong/weak].
- **Socio-Economic Gradient**: Hungary exhibits one of the highest shares of PISA score variance explained by socio-economic status within the OECD, hovering around 19–25% [evidence: strong].
- **Non-State Maintainers**: Since 2013, public education has been centralized under state school district centres (*tankerület*), but churches and foundations maintain a growing share of schools [evidence: strong]. This maintainer fragmentation complicates uniform structural mandates.
- **Demographic Decline**: Hungary's annual births have declined from ~125,000 in the early 1990s to ~85,000–95,000 in recent years, forcing network consolidation decisions in the 2020s–2030s regardless of selection policy [evidence: strong].
- **Financial Profile of Reform**: Per-pupil funding differences between tracking paths are modest; the dominant costs of any structural reform are transition costs (retraining, facilities, administration), not steady-state operational costs [evidence: weak].
- **Polish Reform Trajectory**: Poland's 1999 reform delayed tracking to age 15, resulting in PISA gains [evidence: moderate], but was fully reversed between 2016 and 2019 due to political reasons [evidence: moderate].
