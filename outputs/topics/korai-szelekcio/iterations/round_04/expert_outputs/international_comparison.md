# Expert analysis: international_comparison

## Findings (evidence)
- Cross-country difference-in-differences evidence indicates earlier between-school tracking increases inequality of outcomes without raising mean performance [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006, Economic Journal].
- In PISA, the share of reading and mathematics score variance explained by socio-economic status is among the highest in the OECD for Hungary, hovering around 19–25% across cycles compared to the OECD average of approximately 12–14% [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2012-2022 country notes].
- Cross-country evidence suggests that early selection at ages 10 and 12 (corresponding to Hungarian 8-class and 6-class grammar schools) segregates students by socio-economic status rather than cognitive ability [evidence: moderate — model knowledge].
- The academic performance gap between tracked paths in Central and Eastern European transition economies is highly correlated with parental education level rather than primary school academic progression [evidence: weak — model knowledge].

## Interpretation
Hungary's 6- and 8-year gimnazium system acts as an early tracking mechanism, sorting students at ages 10 and 12. Because international literature shows that early tracking exacerbates social inequality without improving average academic outcomes, the persistence of early selection in Hungary directly contributes to its exceptionally high PISA socio-economic gradient. Delaying tracking would likely mitigate this equity gap.

## Assumptions
- PISA socio-economic status gradients are valid proxies for long-term equity of opportunity in the labor market [assumption].
- The general cross-country findings on tracking apply to the specific institutional and cultural context of Hungary [assumption].

## Position
Hungary should phase out early selection into 6- and 8-year grammar schools and unify the lower-secondary education phase until age 16 to reduce socio-economic disparities.

## Uncertainties
- The precise proportion of the Hungarian PISA variance explained by the 6- and 8-year gimnazium systems versus standard tracking at grade 8 [megbízhatóság: közepes; csökkentené: Hungarian administrative student-level panel data matching track choices with primary school test scores].
- The potential flight of high-SES students to private or shadow education providers if early public tracking is abolished [megbízhatóság: alacsony; csökkentené: evaluation of private school enrollment trends in countries that recently postponed tracking, such as Poland].
