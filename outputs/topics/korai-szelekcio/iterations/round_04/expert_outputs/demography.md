# Expert analysis: demography

## Findings (evidence)
- Hungary's annual births fell from approximately 125,000 in the early 1990s to about 85,000–95,000 in recent years, creating a structural cohort shrinkage that forces school-network consolidation decisions in the 2020s–2030s regardless of selection policy [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks].
- The demographic decline is unevenly distributed geographically, with rural areas experiencing faster population loss and school-age cohort shrinkage than the central Budapest metropolitan area [evidence: moderate — model knowledge].

## Interpretation
The primary driver of Hungarian school-network restructuring through 2040 is demographic contraction, not selection policy. Maintaining the 6- and 8-year gimnazium systems under current demographic trends accelerates the sorting of a shrinking pool of top-performing students into urban centers. This leaves regional and rural primary schools with depleted student numbers and lower average academic performance, rendering local school units demographically and financially unsustainable. School consolidation is inevitable, but keeping early selection formats concentrates demographic vulnerability on the general primary school network.

## Assumptions
- Subnational migration patterns will continue to favor metropolitan centers, exacerbating rural depopulation [assumption].
- The Hungarian government will not significantly increase real-term funding per school site to maintain under-populated rural primary schools [assumption].

## Position
Hungary must phase out the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system to prevent demographic cohort shrinkage from accelerating the collapse and forced consolidation of rural primary school networks.

## Uncertainties
- The exact rate of rural-to-urban internal migration of families with school-age children through 2040 [confidence: medium; would be reduced by: regional migration registries and municipal enrollment data analysis].
- The potential buffering effect of international immigration on school-age cohorts in regional towns [confidence: low; would be reduced by: foreign national registration databases and work permit issuance trends].
