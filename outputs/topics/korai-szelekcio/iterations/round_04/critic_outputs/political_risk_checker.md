# Critique: political_risk_checker

## S1.political_risks
Objection: The risk list names "elite-school and middle-class parent opposition" and "church/private maintainer exemption claims" but omits the private tutoring / test-prep industry as an organized political actor. This is a multi-billion-forint sector with direct financial incentive to lobby against a less-coachable exam, and it appears only obliquely in the uncertainties section (as a technical "adaptation" risk), never as a political stakeholder capable of funding opposition campaigns or influencing the assessment agency's design choices. Without naming this actor, the mitigation plan (compliance audit in the pilot) targets school-level circumvention only, leaving the industry-lobbying channel completely unaddressed.
Severity: medium
Suggested revision: Add a named risk — "test-preparation industry lobbying against the new assessment format, including influence over the assessment agency's pilot design" — and pair it with a mitigation step (e.g., publishing assessment specifications openly to prevent capture, or an independent design-review board including no test-prep-industry members).

## S2.political_risks
Objection: The scenario correctly flags "a successor government can freeze or reverse the trajectory cheaply (Polish precedent)" but no entrenchment mechanism appears anywhere in implementation_steps or cost_categories — the only counter-move is deferred to uncertainties as "cross-party pact or statutory entrenchment attempts," i.e., an unresolved research question rather than a design commitment. A decade-long cap trajectory with zero legal or procedural entrenchment (no supermajority requirement, no multi-year budget lock, no independent monitoring body with statutory standing) is exactly the profile that was reversed in Poland at negligible political cost. Naming the risk without designing against it is not a mitigated risk, it is a documented vulnerability.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Add an implementation step requiring the cap trajectory to be enacted via a mechanism raising the reversal cost (e.g., a cardinal/supermajority law if constitutionally available, multi-year earmarked budget line requiring cross-party sign-off, or binding international commitment), and move "cross-party pact" from uncertainty into a concrete pre-legislation step with a named convening actor.

## S3.political_risks
Objection: "Highest-intensity opposition of all scenarios" is asserted but the response is limited to a pre-legislation "pact attempt" with no fallback if the pact fails — the scenario has no described contingency for what happens if government and opposition do not reach agreement before legislating (which the synthesis itself flags as historically likely, citing Polish reversal and closure politics). Given S3 carries the largest sunk transition costs (nationwide teacher retraining, network reconfiguration) and the political risk section acknowledges these could be "destroyed" by reversal, the absence of any entrenchment design (e.g., binding the felmenő rendszer guarantees in a way that can't be unwound mid-cohort, or sequencing sunk-cost-heavy steps only after the pact is secured) means the scenario's stated highest-benefit case rests on an unmitigated single point of political failure.
Severity: high
Suggested revision: Specify a sequencing rule — no irreversible spending (retraining at scale, network reconfiguration) proceeds until the cross-party framework is legally ratified — and add a named fallback path (e.g., defaulting to S2's slower cap mechanism) if the pact attempt fails, rather than leaving "negotiate a pact" as the sole risk control.
