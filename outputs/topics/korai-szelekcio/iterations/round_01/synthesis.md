## Overview
This synthesis integrates the findings, interpretations, assumptions, and positions of the expert analyses regarding the Hungarian education system's early academic tracking (the 6- and 8-year gimnaziums). The core policy debate centers on whether Hungary should phase out these early selective tracks (operating at ages 10 and 12) to reduce high socio-economic segregation and inequality, or retain them to avoid intense political resistance, legal obstacles, and high transition costs amidst a severe teacher shortage.

## Disagreement map
- **hungarian_education_system, international_comparison, finnish_reform, polish_reform, equity_and_social_mobility, demography, school_network_planning**: Early academic selection in 6- and 8-year gimnaziums must be phased out. Why: These tracks function primarily as socio-economic sorting mechanisms rather than academic value-adders, exacerbating inequality, draining regional school networks, and intensifying geographic segregation amidst demographic decline. [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics; OECD PISA 2012-2022 country notes; Hanushek & Woessmann 2006; Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009; Jakubowski et al. 2016; Horn (2013); Kertesi & Kezdi] [assumption: High sorting leads to suboptimal outcomes; test scores proxy potential; peer effects shape learning].
- **portuguese_reform, education_finance, political_feasibility, implementation_planning (minority)**: The 6- and 8-year gimnazium system should be retained. Why: Educational performance and equity can be improved within the existing structure through non-structural adjustments (targeted resources, curriculum standards), thereby avoiding massive front-loaded transition costs, intense middle-class resistance, legal liabilities, and administrative strain during an acute teacher shortage. [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020; domestic press/stakeholder analyses; OECD Education at a Glance] [evidence: weak — budget chapters] [assumption: Non-structural interventions can mitigate inequities; teacher supply is inelastic; Klebelsberg Center capacity is constrained].
- **legal_and_governance (minority)**: Academic selection should not be immediately abolished, but rather transitioned gradually via a phased transition (*felmenő rendszer*) over at least eight years while renegotiating maintainer agreements. Why: Rapid structural changes violate constitutional principles of legal certainty and acquired rights for enrolled pupils, and the split between state and non-state maintainers prevents uniform command-and-control changes. [evidence: strong — Nkt. analysis; Public education act amendments; Hungarian Constitutional Court practice].

## What the experts agree on
- **Early tracking and socioeconomic segregation**: The 6- and 8-year gimnazium tracks select 8-12% of a student cohort at ages 10 and 12 via a centralized exam, with participants strongly skewed toward high socio-economic status (SES), urban families, segregating students earlier than the standard age 14 transition. [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics; Public education act amendments; OECD PISA country notes; model knowledge].
- **Highly unequal outcomes**: Hungary’s share of reading/maths score variance explained by student SES is among the highest in the OECD, standing at ~19-25% compared to the OECD average of ~12-14%. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2012-2022 country notes].
- **Fragmented governance**: Since the 2013 public education act amendments, most state schools are run by centralized state school district centres (tankerület), while churches and foundations maintain a growing share of schools, which complicates uniform structural mandates. [evidence: strong — Public education act amendments; maintainer statistics].
- **Demographic pressures**: Shrinking cohort sizes (annual births down from ~125,000 in the early 1990s to ~85,000–95,000) will force school-network consolidation in the 2020s and 2030s regardless of the tracking policy chosen. [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks].
- **Teacher shortages**: The public education system faces a sustained teacher shortage and aging workforce, particularly concentrated in mathematics, science, and disadvantaged regions. [evidence: strong — OECD Education at a Glance; PSZ/PDSZ surveys].
- **Reform costs**: Per-pupil funding differences between track types are modest; the primary financial impacts of structural tracking reform are transitional (retraining, building adaptation, administrative reorganization) rather than long-run steady-state costs. [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates].
