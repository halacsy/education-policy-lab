# Critique: equity_checker

## S1.equity_impact
Objection: The claim of "moderately improves equity within selective tracks" is undermined by the scenario's own assumptions section, which flags that "middle-class parents will accept admissions quotas rather than seeking to bypass them" — a bare assumption with no evidence tag supporting compliance. The evidence tagged [strong — KSH/Oktatási Hivatal] only documents the *existing* SES skew in admissions, not that quota mechanisms survive elite bypass via secondary oral exams (which the scenario itself lists as a live risk in "Assumptions"). The equity_impact field should state that gains are conditional on an unverified compliance assumption, not present them as a settled moderate improvement.

## S2.equity_impact
Objection: The equity_impact field claims a "significant" improvement, but this directly conflicts with the scenario's own uncertainties, which note that "middle-class parents will bypass the reform by moving children to private or church schools that resist state mandates." Since church/foundation maintainers are explicitly described elsewhere (synthesis: fragmented governance) as outside uniform state control, a plausible outcome is that high-SES flight concentrates advantage in non-state early-selective schools while the state system absorbs a more homogeneously lower-SES population — this could leave residual sorting untouched or worsened for those unable to afford private/church alternatives. The equity_impact statement does not acknowledge this displacement risk (speculative, but grounded in the scenario's own listed uncertainty) and should not be rated "significant" without addressing it.

## S3.equity_impact
Objection: "Maximizes educational equity" overstates what the cited evidence supports. Hanushek & Woessmann 2006 and Jakubowski et al. 2016 (tagged strong/moderate) support that delayed tracking reduces SES-variance in outcomes *in the systems studied* (largely Nordic/Polish contexts with adequate teacher supply), not that Hungary's specific implementation — undertaken during an acknowledged severe teacher shortage (per synthesis, evidence: strong) — will replicate that effect. If teacher shortages force uneven implementation quality across regions, disadvantaged/rural schools are plausibly the first to receive under-resourced comprehensive classrooms during transition, producing an equity-negative transitional cohort effect that the equity_impact field entirely omits (speculative, but a foreseeable capacity-outcome mismatch already flagged in the scenario's own uncertainties).

## S4.equity_impact
Objection: The equity_impact field acknowledges that primary-level sorting is untouched, but fails to identify who specifically is excluded even within its own targeted-support logic: TEIP-style clustering (evidence: moderate — Crato 2020, Portuguese case) targets designated disadvantaged *schools/clusters*, not dispersed rural poor students who fall outside those cluster boundaries. Given Hungary's demography (KSH: shrinking, geographically uneven rural cohorts), a school-clustering mechanism modeled on Portugal's more urban-concentrated disadvantage pattern may systematically miss low-SES students scattered across small rural schools — this is a distinct equity gap from the "leaves sorting untouched" framing already given, and is unsupported by any Hungary-specific evidence tag in the mechanism section (weak — budget chapters/expert estimates).
