# Critique: political_risk_checker

## S1.political_risks
Objection: The listed risks (parent resistance, legal challenges from non-state maintainers) omit the most basic entrenchment problem: the mechanism operates via ministerial decree ("Amend the ministerial decree governing secondary school admissions"), not statutory law. A decree-level reform can be reversed by a future government with a single administrative act, with no legislative debate or public visibility required. Unlike S2/S3, which at least route through Parliament (Nkt. amendment), S1 has no entrenchment design at all — no proposal to codify quotas in law, sunset-proof the weighting formula, or require legislative supermajority to alter it. This makes S1's equity gains structurally the most reversible of all four scenarios, yet this is not named as a risk.

## S2.political_risks
Objection: The political risks list backlash and legal disputes but omits the specific reversal risk inherent to a felmenő (phase-in) design: because the reduction unfolds "over a multi-year period," an incoming government can simply halt the schedule mid-stream (e.g., freeze caps at 50% reduction) without repealing anything, producing a permanent patchwork of partially-reduced tracks rather than either the old or new steady state. This is a materially different and more likely failure mode than the "high risk of backlash" and "legal disputes" already listed, and the scenario proposes no locking mechanism (e.g., automatic non-renewal clauses, binding multi-year budget commitments) to prevent a future administration from arresting the phase-down partway.

## S3.political_risks
Objection: The scenario names "a high risk of a Polish-style political reversal" as its own central risk but the implementation_steps contain no entrenchment design whatsoever to counter it — no cross-party consensus-building step, no constitutional/supermajority anchoring, no irreversible-by-design sequencing (e.g., front-loading the reform so reversal costs exceed forward costs). Simply naming the Polish precedent without proposing any structural defense against it is an acknowledged-but-unmitigated risk, which is precisely the failure mode this scenario should be graded down for, not credited for candor.

## S4.political_risks
Objection: "Very low risk of middle-class backlash" is stated as near-certain but the scenario simultaneously has the weakest entrenchment of any option: it relies entirely on discretionary annual funding ("Design and fund a targeted support framework," [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates]) rather than legislative or constitutional change. A subsequent government (or even the same government facing budget pressure, given the acknowledged teacher shortage and fiscal constraints noted elsewhere) can simply defund TEIP-style programs in a single budget cycle with no legal challenge possible, since no rights or structures were changed. This is arguably a *higher* reversal risk than the structural scenarios, not a lower one — the claim inverts the actual entrenchment ordering across the four scenarios and is unsupported by the evidence tag it cites (moderate, for "improves performance without backlash," which says nothing about durability).
