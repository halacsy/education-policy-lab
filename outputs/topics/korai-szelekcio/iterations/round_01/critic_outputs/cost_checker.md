# Critique: cost_checker

## S1.cost_categories
Objection: The cost list ("admissions administration and test design adjustments," "monitoring and enforcement compliance costs") omits litigation/legal defense costs, even though the scenario's own political_risks section flags "potential legal challenges from non-state school maintainers regarding institutional autonomy in student selection." If church/foundation maintainers sue over quota mandates, defending or settling those actions is a distinct, potentially large recurring cost category that the evidence tags (moderate/strong, both administrative-statistics-based) never anticipate. This is a missing item, not a phrasing issue.

## S2.cost_categories
Objection: Cost categories list only "bilateral contract renegotiations" and "curriculum/staff adjustment costs," but the scenario's own uncertainties section states there is real exposure to "legal liability and compensation claims if bilateral agreements with church/foundation maintainers are unilaterally altered." Compensation payouts to maintainers are a plausible, potentially fiscally significant cost path that is absent from cost_categories entirely — the evidence tags supporting the mechanism (strong/moderate, both about tracking-age effects) say nothing about legal-liability magnitude, so this gap is unsupported by omission rather than merely underweighted.

## S3.cost_categories
Objection: This is a transition-vs-steady-state confusion. Both listed categories ("teacher retraining," "building reorganization") are one-time transition costs, but the expected_benefits claim ("substantial reduction in variance explained by SES" [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006]) implicitly requires that the comprehensive model sustain smaller/mixed-ability classes and differentiated instruction indefinitely. Hanushek & Woessmann's difference-in-differences evidence supports outcome effects of postponed tracking, not that these outcomes are achievable without a permanently higher per-pupil cost structure. No steady-state cost category (e.g., ongoing differentiated-instruction staffing) is listed, so the claimed benefit is costed only for the transition period, not for maintaining it.

## S4.cost_categories
Objection: The TEIP-style "targeted funding programs" category is presented as a bounded alternative to structural reform's "front-loaded" costs, but TEIP-style support is an open-ended recurring fiscal commitment with no scale, duration, or forint range given, while the mechanism itself is tagged "evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates." Given the synthesis explicitly states per-pupil funding differences are modest and reform costs are mostly transitional, this scenario's ongoing steady-state cost exposure is arguably understated relative to S1–S3, yet it is the one scenario whose cost_categories are least specified with any range or ceiling — a fiscal-sensitivity gap the weak evidence tag should have forced the scenario to flag explicitly. (speculative on ultimate magnitude, but the absence of any bound is a concrete flaw.)
