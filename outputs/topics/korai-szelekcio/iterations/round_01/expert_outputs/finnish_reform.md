# Expert analysis: finnish_reform

## Findings (evidence)
- Finland's peruskoulu (comprehensive school to age 16, phased in 1972-1977) abolished early academic tracking and is associated with high educational equity and reduced dependence of outcomes on family background [evidence: moderate — Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009].
- The equity effects of the Finnish comprehensive school reform are entangled with concurrently implemented teacher policies (requiring master's degrees) and robust special education support systems [evidence: moderate — Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009].
- Early tracking in Hungary (6- and 8-year gimnazium entry at ages 10 and 12) segregates students by socio-economic status, exacerbating the impact of family background on achievement [evidence: strong — model knowledge].

## Interpretation
The Finnish experience suggests that delaying academic selection until age 16, when bundled with systemic supports, raises the floor of student achievement and decouples outcomes from parental background. In Hungary, the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system functions as an early tracking mechanism that cream-skims privileged students. However, simply abolishing these schools without reproducing Finland's high teacher standards and targeted remediation systems will not automatically replicate Finnish equity outcomes.

## Assumptions
- Academic selection at ages 10 or 12 is too early to accurately reflect a child's long-term academic potential [assumption].
- The institutional prestige of Hungarian gimnaziums can be successfully redirected into a comprehensive model without triggering middle-class flight to private sectors [assumption].

## Position
Hungary should phase out early selection into 6- and 8-year gimnaziums in favor of a unified comprehensive system until age 16, conditionally paired with major investments in teacher training and special education.

## Uncertainties
- The precise degree to which Finland's equity gains were caused by the structural comprehensive school reform itself versus the parallel teacher education reforms (Pekkarinen et al. 2009).
- Whether Hungary currently possesses the fiscal headroom or teacher labor market capacity to implement Finnish-style pedagogical support systems.
