# Expert analysis: implementation_planning

## Findings (evidence)
- Hungary faces a sustained teacher shortage and an ageing teacher workforce, concentrated in disadvantaged regions and in maths/science; any structural reform competes for the same scarce teachers. [evidence: strong — OECD Education at a Glance; PSZ/PDSZ surveys]
- Per-pupil funding differences between track types are modest; the dominant costs of structural reform are transition costs (retraining, building use, administration), not steady-state costs. [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates]
- The 6- and 8-year gimnazium system creates early academic selection at ages 10 and 12, sorting students into elite tracks earlier than the standard age 14 transition. [evidence: strong — Model knowledge / Hungarian Public Education Act]

## Interpretation
The severe, localized shortages of qualified teachers—particularly in maths and science—imply that any structural change (such as phasing out 6- and 8-year schools to unify the lower-secondary curriculum) will trigger intense logistical friction. Disadvantaged regions already suffer from a lack of staff, and redistributing or retraining teachers during a transition will absorb significant administrative bandwidth. Because the dominant costs of modifying the track structure are front-loaded transition costs rather than long-term operational savings, a rapid phase-out would yield high immediate administrative stress and fiscal pressure for negligible long-term budgetary relief.

## Assumptions
- Teacher labor supply in Hungary is highly inelastic in the short-to-medium term, meaning higher administrative demands cannot be offset by rapid recruitment. [assumption]
- The administrative capacity of the state school maintenance agency (Klebelsberg Központ) is constrained, limiting its ability to manage complex school reorganizations and staff reassignments simultaneously. [assumption]

## Position
Hungary should retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium structures in the medium term, delaying any structural unification of public school tracks until the teacher supply crisis is stabilized through systemic compensation and recruitment reforms.

## Uncertainties
- The precise total cost of administrative transition (including physical building reallocations and localized teacher contract renegotiations) for a nationwide phase-out is unknown.
- The exact rate of teacher attrition over the next five years, which determines the baseline capacity to absorb administrative disruption, remains uncertain.
