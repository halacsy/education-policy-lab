# Expert analysis: demography

## Findings (evidence)
- Annual births in Hungary fell from approximately 125,000 in the early 1990s to approximately 85,000–95,000 in recent years [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks].
- This cohort shrinkage forces school-network consolidation and rationalization decisions throughout the 2020s and 2030s regardless of the specific selection policy chosen [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks].
- Rural regions in Hungary experience faster demographic decline and youth out-migration compared to the Budapest metropolitan area, creating severe spatial disparities in school-age population density [evidence: moderate — model knowledge].

## Interpretation
The demographic reality of shrinking student cohorts means Hungary cannot maintain its current school network footprint without incurring unsustainable per-capita costs and teacher shortages. Early academic selection (the 6- and 8-year gimnazium track) concentrates a dwindling pool of high-performing students into select urban centers. This demographic drainage hollows out standard 4-year regional schools, accelerating their decline and forcing closures. The demographic decline acts as a forcing function: maintaining early selection will inevitably lead to a highly segregated, geographically centralized elite network, while the periphery faces aggressive consolidation.

## Assumptions
- The birth rate in Hungary will remain below replacement levels (total fertility rate under 2.1) through 2040, preventing any natural cohort recovery [assumption].
- Internal migration patterns will continue to favor urban centers and the Budapest agglomeration at the expense of peripheral rural municipalities [assumption].

## Position
Hungary must phase out 6- and 8-year gimnaziums to prevent demographic cohort shrinkage from triggering the systemic collapse and forced consolidation of standard regional secondary schools.

## Uncertainties
- The exact rate of regional student population decline in specific micro-regions (kistérség) through 2040.
- The degree to which cross-border migration (emigration of families to other EU countries) will further accelerate cohort shrinkage.
