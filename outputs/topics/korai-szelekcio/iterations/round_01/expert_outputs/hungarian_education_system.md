# Expert analysis: hungarian_education_system

## Findings (evidence)
- Roughly 8-12% of a student cohort enters 6- or 8-year gimnazium tracks, selected at age 10 or 12 largely via the central written admission examination; these entrants are strongly skewed toward high socio-economic status (SES), urban families. [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics]
- Free school choice combined with early selective tracks produces strong between-school sorting already at the primary stage in urban areas. [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013; Berenyi-Berkovits-Erőss school choice studies]
- Abolition of early tracks alone would not eliminate school sorting, which also occurs through catchment boundaries and free school choice. [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013; Berenyi-Berkovits-Erőss school choice studies]
- Since the 2013 public education act amendments, most state schools are run by centralized state school district centres (tankerület), while churches and foundations maintain a growing share of schools. [evidence: strong — Public education act amendments; maintainer statistics]

## Interpretation
The Hungarian school system is highly segregated, with academic selection operating as early as age 10. The 6- and 8-year gimnazium tracks serve as an exit route for high-SES urban families, intensifying segregation before the standard secondary transition at age 14. However, because school choice and residential catchment sorting are already entrenched at the primary level, addressing the 6- and 8-year tracks in isolation will not resolve system-wide inequities. Furthermore, the fragmented landscape of school maintainers (state, church, and foundation) limits the government's ability to enforce uniform structural reforms, as non-state operators often bypass standard catchment or admissions constraints.

## Assumptions
- High levels of between-school sorting and early socioeconomic segregation lead to suboptimal and inequitable educational outcomes for the student cohort as a whole. [evidence: assumption]
- Church and foundation schools would resist or find legislative pathways to bypass centralized efforts to restrict early academic selection. [evidence: assumption]

## Position
Hungary should phase out early academic selection in 6- and 8-year gimnaziums while simultaneously reforming catchment regulations and funding formulas across all maintainers to curb primary-stage sorting.

## Uncertainties
- The precise proportion of the SES sorting variance that is attributable to early gimnazium tracks versus primary school choice and residential segregation. [evidence: weak — model knowledge]
- The legal and political feasibility of applying uniform structural mandates to church- and foundation-maintained schools under current constitutional arrangements. [evidence: weak — model knowledge]
