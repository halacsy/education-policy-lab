# Expert analysis: school_network_planning

## Findings (evidence)
- Annual births in Hungary fell from ~125,000 in the early 1990s to ~85,000–95,000, forcing school-network consolidation decisions in the 2020s–2030s regardless of selection policy. [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks]
- Since 2013, most state schools are run by school district centres (tankerület), while churches and foundations maintain a growing share of schools, complicating uniform structural mandates. [evidence: strong — Public education act amendments; maintainer statistics]
- Selective 6- and 8-year gimnaziums are concentrated in county capitals and larger towns, requiring younger students from smaller settlements to commute longer distances. [evidence: moderate — model knowledge]
- The departure of top-performing students at grades 5 and 7 reduces the demographic and academic viability of rural primary school upper-cycles, accelerating local school closures. [evidence: moderate — model knowledge]

## Interpretation
The dual pressure of demographic decline and early academic selection creates a spatial mismatch in the school network. When 8-year gimnaziums recruit students at age 10, they drain the academic top tier from local primary schools. This leaves primary schools in smaller towns with diminished class sizes and lower average performance, rendering them economically and instructionally unviable. Consequently, the state is forced to consolidate primary schools prematurely. Because school maintenance is fragmented among state, church, and foundation actors, coordinating this network consolidation is highly complex, often leaving state-run primary schools to bear the brunt of demographic decline while non-state actors maintain selective tracks.

## Assumptions
- Non-state school maintainers (churches and foundations) will continue to operate independently of state-led school consolidation initiatives. [assumption]
- Commuting times exceeding 45 minutes for children under 12 years old represent an unacceptable social and developmental burden for families. [assumption]

## Position
Hungary should phase out the 8-year gimnazium system to protect the viability of local primary school networks, while consolidating 6-year gimnaziums strictly within regional hubs that possess adequate transit infrastructure.

## Uncertainties
- The precise reserve classroom capacity in regional secondary school buildings to absorb students if local programs are consolidated.
- The willingness of church maintainers to voluntarily align their enrollment caps with regional demographic consolidation plans.
