# Expert analysis: education_finance

## Findings (evidence)
- Per-pupil funding differences between track types (e.g., standard secondary vs. 6- and 8-year gimnaziums) are modest [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates].
- The dominant costs of structural reform are transition costs (retraining, building use adjustment, administration), rather than long-run steady-state operational costs [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates].

## Interpretation
From a financial perspective, maintaining the status quo of early academic selection (6- and 8-year gimnaziums) versus restructuring to a unified lower-secondary system does not yield significant steady-state savings or cost increases. However, implementing structural reform to phase out or integrate these early-selection schools will trigger substantial front-loaded transition costs, particularly in teacher retraining, administrative reorganization, and building reallocation. Therefore, the fiscal decision to reform depends on whether the long-term socioeconomic benefits of reducing academic segregation outweigh these immediate transition costs.

## Assumptions
- Modest per-pupil funding differences imply that teacher salary structures and basic operational overhead are relatively uniform across Hungarian secondary tracks [assumption].
- Capital expenditure required for modifying building usage during a transition cannot be easily absorbed into existing maintenance budgets [assumption].

## Position
Hungary should retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system in the short term to avoid substantial transition costs, directing available educational funding instead toward target interventions within the existing structure.

## Uncertainties
- The precise order-of-magnitude cost for retraining teachers displaced or reassigned by a potential phase-out of the 6- and 8-year schools is unknown.
- The capacity of existing municipal and state infrastructure to absorb building reallocations without requiring new construction is unknown.
- The exact distribution of administrative costs associated with altering the tracking system at the national scale is unknown.
