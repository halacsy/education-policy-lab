# Disagreement map (final)

Preserved per the Habermas-machine principle: mapped, not collapsed into consensus. Minority positions also appear in the final brief.

## Disagreement map
### Does abolishing or delaying early selection by itself reduce inequality, or does sorting simply relocate?
- **polish_reform, finnish_reform, educational_psychology, international_comparison**: Delaying selection to age 14–15 causally improves both equity and learning, especially for lower-track pupils, as Poland's 1999 reform demonstrated with 0.25–0.28 SD gains. Why: Strong cross-country meta-analysis plus Poland's causally-identified natural experiment show extended common instruction and improved peer composition raise outcomes without lowering the mean.
- **portuguese_reform** (minority): Removing early academic tracking without simultaneously reforming school-choice admissions discretion, grade retention and resource allocation would relocate rather than eliminate sorting — Portugal abolished age-10 selection but substituted retention (26–31% of 15-year-olds) and equity still worsened. Why: Two-thirds of the age-15 gap is already visible at age 10, and Portugal's comprehensive model still worsened equity 2015–2022, showing the school-choice/resource architecture is the stronger lever than tracking age or existence.

### Is the ~10% value-added premium of selective-track pupils real pedagogy or composition/cream-skimming?
- **hungarian_education_system, equity_and_social_mobility, educational_psychology, education_finance**: The premium is predominantly a selection/peer-composition effect; once prior ability and background are controlled, genuine pedagogical value-added is modest or absent. Why: Hungarian value-added studies (Horn, HÉTFA), the minister's competence-test analysis, and the CEU null long-run study point to composition, not distinctive teaching.
- **political_feasibility** (minority): The ~10% gain is a contested but real finding that complicates a purely equity-based abolition case and will be weaponized by pro-selection stakeholders. Why: Whether a 10% gain justifies sorting costs is a value question, not settled empirics; the finding is tagged contested and cannot be dismissed as pure composition.

### When and how is structural reform feasible?
- **implementation_planning** (minority): Structural reform is feasible only after 2031, once teacher-salary reform concludes and administrative bandwidth frees up; near-term action is limited to incremental admissions-criterion revision. Why: Saturated administrative bandwidth, an aging workforce (half over 50, 7.2% under 30), and a rolled-back evaluation bureaucracy make capacity-intensive reform unrealistic before 2031.
- **legal_and_governance, political_feasibility**: A faster route exists now: admissions rules can be changed by EMMI ministerial decree without the 8-year felmenő phase-out, making near-term selection-mechanism reform legally and politically live. Why: Admissions are decree-level, not primary-legislation-level; the June 2026 debate and divided stakeholder coalitions open a real (if fragile) policy window for restriction short of abolition.

### Can non-state (church/foundation) maintainers be prevented from absorbing phased-out selective capacity?
- **finnish_reform, demography** (minority): With transparent allocation, anti-segregation safeguards and needs-based funding, migration to denominational/private selection can be contained as part of a coordinated reform design. Why: Finland's positive-discrimination model and coordinated consolidation-with-equity design suggest safeguards can work — though both flag this as an [assumption].
- **legal_and_governance, hungarian_education_system, education_finance**: Church and foundation maintainers, now ~25–30% of enrolment and constitutionally shielded with autonomous funding agreements, can freely select and form a de facto political-legal veto, so restriction cannot be assumed to hold. Why: Maintainer autonomy and church–state funding accords create a veto point requiring multi-party consent; abolishing state selective capacity likely shifts demand into freely-selecting non-state schools.

### Is early selection or demographic collapse the dominant force reshaping the system?
- **demography, school_network_planning**: Cohort collapse (30–35% decline, ~110k to ~72k pupils) — not selection design — is now the dominant structural force, forcing network consolidation regardless of selection policy. Why: Many schools already lack a first grade; uniform national selection rules are becoming operationally moot, so the real question is how to consolidate while protecting equity.
- **equity_and_social_mobility, international_comparison, educational_psychology**: Selection remains the primary equity-relevant lever; demographic decline does not erase segregation and, if consolidation concentrates intake in fewer selective sites, it may amplify sorting. Why: The SES–performance link and segregation persist independent of cohort size; the equity harm is a selection-mechanism property, not a demographic artefact.
