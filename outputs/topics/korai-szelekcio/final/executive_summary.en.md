Hungary's 6/8-year gimnázium system selects 8–12% of each cohort at age 10–12 via central exams that strongly favor high-SES, urban families. Hungary shows among the highest socio-economic segregation and achievement-gap levels in the OECD/EU, and cross-country evidence robustly links earlier tracking to wider inequality without gains in mean performance. Five scenarios are on the table. S1 keeps the 6/8-year structure but regulates admissions (SES-aware proxies, coaching-resistant exams). S2 shrinks state-sector selective capacity while leaving admission rules unchanged. S3 delays the tracking age toward 14+ (Finnish/Polish model), converting early entry into a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase with concurrent anti-sorting rules. S4 pursues non-structural compensatory reform (Portugal-style): curriculum clarity, targeted funding, retention reduction, without touching the selection age. S5 is passive demographic contraction — the no-new-policy baseline, as shrinking birth cohorts force network consolidation regardless of reform.

Experts do not agree on the root cause, and this disagreement is not resolved here. One group holds that early selection itself is a structural driver of segregation, distinct from school-choice effects, so delaying it (S3) is the lever. A minority counters that free school choice and catchment-area sorting are separable from tracking and would keep sorting pupils even without early selection — removing selection alone would relocate, not resolve, the problem. Experts also split on whether the ~10% value-added premium for selected pupils reflects composition/peer effects (the majority view, including the Ministry's own analysis) or remains genuinely undecomposed (a minority, including the original researcher). A further split concerns whether demographic collapse or the selection mechanism itself is now the dominant force shaping the school network, and whether structural reform (S3) is legally and administratively feasible given an 8-year statutory phase-out, church-maintainer autonomy, and an aging teaching workforce, or is currently unrealistic given the minister's political retreat in 2026.　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　　

Immediate no-regret moves, independent of which scenario prevails: publish a legal opinion on whether admission-equity or capacity rules can bind church and foundation maintainers; commission a teacher-workforce feasibility study covering retraining capacity and the 2024-2031 salary reform; begin independent monitoring of admission outcomes by SES and Roma status under current rules; and require any demographic-consolidation decision (S5) to include an explicit equity safeguard rather than proceeding as an unmanaged default.
