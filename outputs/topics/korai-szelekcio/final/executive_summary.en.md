Hungary's early academic selection at age 10–12, sorting pupils into 6- and 8-year gimnázium tracks, sits at the centre of the EU's highest socio-economic school segregation: a 53-point parent-education achievement gap (vs. 33 EU average), and a raw 116-point grammar/vocational gap that shrinks to 24 points once family background is controlled for — evidence that most of the apparent advantage is compositional, not pedagogical (strong evidence). Five scenarios are on the table. S1 keeps the track structure but regulates the entry gate with SES-aware quotas and exam redesign. S2 shrinks state-sector selective capacity via score thresholds, leaving admission rules untouched. S3 structurally delays selection to age 14+ (Finnish/Polish model), phasing 6/8-year entry into a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase with anti-sorting rules. S4 pursues non-structural compensatory reform — curriculum clarity, targeted funding, retention reduction — without touching the selection age. S5 is the passive baseline: demographic collapse forces network consolidation regardless of active policy.

Experts do not agree on whether early selection itself is the primary driver of segregation or merely one mechanism among several (school choice, catchment, church-maintainer growth) that would keep sorting pupils even if selection were abolished — a minority notes ~40% of general schools already send zero pupils to selective tracks, meaning the selection gate is not the only channel. This disagreement is preserved, not resolved: it shapes whether S1-style gate-fixing or S3-style structural delay is the more defensible lever. Poland's reform delivered real PISA gains (0.16-0.28 SD) yet was reversed after 18 years; Portugal achieved comparable gains (0.3 SD) through non-structural means alone — leaving open whether structural or non-structural reform is the more promising equity lever. Whether church/foundation maintainers can realistically be restricted from absorbing displaced selective capacity is also contested, given coalition commitments to protect their operating conditions.

No-regret moves, regardless of which scenario prevails: commission the KIFIR/Oktatási Hivatal disaggregation separating demographic contraction from active administrative levers; fund a front-loaded teacher retraining and recruitment plan given the aging, unevenly distributed workforce; and require any funding-floor commitment (TEIP-style or otherwise) to be published in the 2027 budget bill with explicit escape-clause conditions rather than left to unbudgeted announcement.
