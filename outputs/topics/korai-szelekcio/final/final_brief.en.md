# Policy brief — Early academic selection and the 6/8-year gimnázium

This brief synthesises expert evidence and a stakeholder stress test on reforming early academic selection (age 10-12) in Hungarian secondary education. It distinguishes what is well-evidenced from what is estimated, assumed, or a matter of values, and answers every argument raised by the modelled stakeholder voices. Five policy scenarios are compared; none is crowned as 'the' answer — the brief maps trade-offs, disagreements, and open questions so real deliberation can proceed on an honest footing.

## Scenario key (full detail: scenarios.en.md)
- S1 — S1 — Admissions and intake-equity regulation: keep the track structure, fix the entry gate with SES-aware quotas and exam redesign (full detail: scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md).
- S2 — S2 — Supply-side contraction of the selective track: shrink state-sector 6/8-year gimnázium capacity via score thresholds, leaving admission rules unchanged (full detail: scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md).
- S3 — S3 — Structural delay of the selection age to 14+ (Finnish/Polish model), phasing out 6/8-year entry into a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase with anti-sorting rules (full detail: scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md).
- S4 — S4 — Non-structural compensatory reform: curriculum clarity, TEIP-style targeted funding, retention reduction, without touching the selection age (full detail: scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md).
- S5 — S5 — Demographically-driven passive contraction: the no-new-policy baseline, in which shrinking birth cohorts force network consolidation regardless of active reform (full detail: scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md).

## What we know
- Hungary exhibits the EU's/OECD's highest socio-economic school segregation, with a 53-point parent-education achievement gap versus a 33-point EU average. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- The raw 116-point achievement gap between grammar-school and vocational-track students shrinks to 24 points once family background/SES is controlled for, indicating most of the gap is compositional rather than a school-quality effect. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- International difference-in-differences evidence shows early between-school tracking increases inequality of outcomes without raising mean achievement. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Poland's 1999 reform delaying tracking from age 12 to 15 produced PISA gains of 0.25 SD math, 0.28 SD reading, 0.16 SD science, with at-risk students gaining approximately 1 SD, but individual-level SES inequity did not close, and the reform was reversed after 18 years by a change of government. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Hungary's school-age population is shrinking rapidly (fertility rate down to 1.31 in 2025; births at ~77,500 in 2024, the lowest since 1949), forcing school-network consolidation regardless of selection policy. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Church-maintained schools' share of gimnázium provision grew from roughly 10.4% (2010) to about 27% (2020/21), concentrated disproportionately in elite 6/8-year tracks. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- There is a documented ~46% vs ~22% admission-rejection-rate gap between low- and high-poverty-share applicant classes at the 6/8-year gimnázium entry gate. [fact] (evidence: moderate)
- Hungary's teacher workforce is aging and unevenly distributed (roughly 50% aged 50+, only about 7.2% under 30), with acute shortages concentrated in rural and STEM areas. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Portugal's non-structural reforms (curriculum clarity, school autonomy, regular assessment, retention reduction) produced a documented ~31 PISA-point (≈0.3 SD) achievement gain between 2000-2018 without changing selection age, and its retention-reduction programme alone saved EUR 112 million over two years while cutting retained students by 26% across 663 schools. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Any structural change to Hungary's selection age must proceed via felmenő-rendszer (grade-by-grade, cohort-by-cohort) phase-in with a minimum lead time of roughly one year, per constitutional-doctrine and legislative practice. [fact] (evidence: moderate)
- 8-year gimnázium track applications fell more than 14% year-on-year while overall enrolment stayed flat, indicating the exam's original rationing function is eroding under demographic pressure. [fact] (evidence: moderate)
- Approximately 40% of general schools (60% in villages) currently send zero pupils to 6/8-year tracks, meaning admission-rule redesign alone cannot reach these settlements. [fact] (evidence: strong)

## What we consider likely
- SES-aware admission quotas and coaching-resistant exam redesign would reduce entry-gate gatekeeping to some degree, but no Hungarian evidence yet confirms the effectiveness of any specific proxy, quota, or exam design, and displacement effects on non-quota applicants are plausible but unmeasured. [estimate]
- Reduced state-sector selective capacity (S2) would likely be at least partly offset by church and foundation maintainer expansion, given their documented growth trajectory and coalition-level protection of their operating conditions. [estimate]
- Structural delay of selection (S3) would likely produce measurable equity gains only if anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment are enacted and enforced simultaneously; absent this, sorting would likely migrate into those channels, echoing Finland's post-2000 erosion pattern. [estimate]
- Non-structural compensatory reform (S4) is likely to produce real achievement and fiscal gains for the general-school majority, but its equity effect on access to selective tracks is likely to remain structurally bounded because the selection age and admission gatekeeping are left untouched. [estimate]
- TEIP-style targeted funding lines are likely to be among the first budget items cut during fiscal consolidation unless given statutory (cardinal-law) protection, given the projected 6.2% deficit in 2026 and documented past patterns of unfunded reform announcements. [estimate]
- A genuinely policy-neutral demographic baseline (S5) likely does not currently exist in practice, since active administrative levers (e.g. S2-style score thresholds) appear to already be operating alongside passive demographic contraction. [estimate]

## Where experts disagree
### Whether early selection (age 10-12) itself is the primary driver of Hungary's segregation, or one factor among several equally important structural mechanisms
- **hungarian_education_system, finnish_reform, portuguese_reform, education_finance**: Early selection at 10-12 is the primary structural cause and should be phased out; it is a codified, legally distinct exception whose removal is comparatively tractable. [value] Why: Hungary's own 2000s comprehensivization reform raised the floor without lowering the ceiling; Portugal achieved comparable gains with zero early tracking; Finland's mobility gains came specifically from delaying tracking to 16.
- **school_network_planning, demography, legal_and_governance** (minority): Early selection is numerically small (8-12% of cohort) and geographically concentrated; school choice, catchment rules, and maintainer shifts (church-school growth) operate largely independently of the 10-12 selection point and would continue sorting students even if it were abolished. [value] Why: ~40% of general schools (60% in villages) send zero pupils to 6/8-year tracks already; church-school enrollment share rose from 10.4% to 27% independent of formal selection-age law; demographic decline alone forces network consolidation regardless of selection policy.
### Whether the ~10% value-added observed in elite 6/8-year tracks justifies preserving some form of early selection
- **hungarian_education_system, education_finance**: The modest value-added is real but does not justify the system's equity cost; it does not offset the segregation and gap effects, and its causal robustness (peer effect vs. pedagogy) is itself uncertain. [value] Why: The raw achievement gap collapses from 116 to 24 points once SES is controlled, showing most of the apparent advantage is composition, not school quality; Hungary's own de-tracking history shows gains without ceiling loss.
- **equity_and_social_mobility** (minority): Value-added is heterogeneous by track type — positive and consistent for the 8-grade gimnázium but negative at grade 8 (turning positive only by grade 10) for the 6-grade gimnázium — meaning the pedagogical case differs materially by track and cannot be treated uniformly. [value] Why: HÉTFA Working Paper panel data (2008-2014) shows divergent value-added trajectories by track, meaning a single verdict on 'early selection's pedagogical value' oversimplifies the evidence.
### Whether non-state (church/foundation) maintainers can realistically be restricted from absorbing phased-out selective capacity
- **hungarian_education_system, implementation_planning**: Church and foundation maintainers can, in principle, be regulated or incentivized not to expand selective places to offset a state-sector reduction, treating maintainer behavior as policy-influenced and reversible. [value] Why: Legislative and funding levers (building-transfer rules, normatíva changes) are cited as the mechanisms by which church expansion occurred, implying they could equally be used to constrain it.
- **political_feasibility, legal_and_governance, school_network_planning** (minority): This restriction assumption is directly contradicted by observed political and legal constraints: church-maintained gymnasium share rose from 10.4% to 27% independent of formal selection-age law, the Tisza government's 12-point programme explicitly commits that church maintainers keep their operating conditions, and church/foundation admissions operate under partially autonomous concordat-based frameworks that a national reform cannot simply override. [value] Why: The coalition constraint (protecting religious institutions) and the demonstrated growth trend independent of law changes indicate maintainer behavior is not simply a policy dial that reform can turn; restricting it risks alienating a coalition partner or triggering legal challenge.
### Political risk profile of selection reform: what the dominant risks are and how tractable they are
- **hungarian_education_system, political_feasibility**: Elite-school resistance and middle-class flight (to church/private alternatives) are the dominant risks, but the demographic reframing (declining cohorts eroding the exam's rationing function) offers a genuine, untested political opening to lower this resistance. [value] Why: Central admission data show 8-year track applications falling >14% year-on-year while enrolment totals stay flat, supporting a narrative that the exam's scarcity-rationing rationale is eroding on its own.
- **political_feasibility** (minority): The scenario's own assumption that church and private maintainers will eventually comply with phased contraction is itself contradicted by the coalition's explicit commitment to protect their operating conditions, meaning the named risks (elite resistance, middle-class flight) understate the binding constraint, which is maintainer non-compliance backed by coalition politics, not merely public opinion. [value] Why: Tisza's 12-point programme explicitly commits to preserving church maintainers' prior operating conditions, a binding coalition-political fact that pre-empts any assumption of eventual compliance.
### Whether structural (selection-age) reform or non-structural reform (curriculum, assessment, retention policy) is the more promising lever for equity gains
- **portuguese_reform** (minority): Comparable or greater PISA gains (0.3 SD) are achievable through non-structural reform alone (curriculum clarity, school autonomy, assessment, retention reduction) without touching the selection age at all. [value] Why: Portugal improved PISA scores by ~31 points between 2000 and 2015-2018 while remaining fully comprehensive, and its retention-reduction programme alone saved €112 million while cutting retained students by 26%.
- **polish_reform, finnish_reform, international_comparison, hungarian_education_system**: Structural (selection-age) reform is necessary because international difference-in-differences evidence directly links early tracking architecture to inequality, and non-structural improvements alone (as in Poland pre-1999 or Finland pre-1972) did not resolve tracking-driven sorting. [value] Why: Poland's 1999 delayed-tracking reform produced 0.25-0.28 SD gains specifically attributable to the structural timing change; Finland's mobility gains were tied to the age-16 tracking delay itself, not merely parallel reforms.
### Whether political durability of any reform is achievable, given Poland's reversal precedent
- **political_feasibility, implementation_planning**: Cross-party durability is low-probability in the near term (2026-2028): Fidesz has already framed any reform as totalitarian, and the Tisza government's own pattern of rapid unilateral reversal of predecessor reforms (teacher evaluation) raises the base-rate expectation of future reversal. [value] Why: Poland's delayed-tracking reform, despite documented achievement gains, was reversed after 18 years by a change of government, demonstrating that structural reforms without cross-party consensus or constitutional entrenchment are vulnerable regardless of evidence quality.
- **legal_and_governance** (minority): Early selection's narrow, separately-codified legal status (as an exception to the age-15 structural default) makes it comparatively more tractable and potentially more durable to reform than Hungary's broader maintainer or curriculum architecture, since legislative change here does not require restructuring the entire secondary system. [value] Why: The Nkt. § 4 defines secondary education as beginning at grade 9 with 6/8-year gymnasium as the only statutory exception, meaning reform can proceed via a narrower legislative lever than a full system overhaul.

## What we don't know
- Whether any specific coaching-resistant exam format exists that meaningfully reduces the weight of paid test preparation without introducing new validity or reliability problems. [fact]
- Whether SES-proxy quotas would displace non-quota middle-SES or rural applicants without triggering compensating access mechanisms. [fact]
- Whether reduced state-sector selective capacity would actually improve peer composition in general schools, or whether displaced demand would simply migrate to church/foundation providers, private tutoring, or catchment relocation. [fact]
- What the precise fiscal cost of compensatory general-school investment, teacher retraining, facility conversion, and anti-sorting enforcement would be under any of S1-S4, since no numeric fiscal range has been published in Hungarian sources for most of these cost categories. [fact]
- Whether Poland's and Finland's structural-delay effect sizes would replicate in Hungary given differing demographic composition, baseline segregation levels, and school-choice intensity. [fact]
- Whether anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment can actually prevent sorting from migrating to those channels in the Hungarian context, given no domestic enforcement track record exists. [fact]
- Whether statutory (cardinal-law) funding-floor guarantees for TEIP-style or other targeted programmes are politically and fiscally achievable under projected consolidation, given Hungary has no directly comparable precedent of a cardinal-law-protected education budget line surviving multiple consolidation cycles. [fact]
- How many settlements would lose their last local secondary programme if demographic-driven consolidation continues at the current pace, absent any coordinated national plan. [fact]
- The magnitude of church-school funding advantage relative to state schools, which remains contested and unresolved by available sources. [fact]

## What could be done
- **S1 — Admissions and intake-equity regulation — keep structure, fix the gate**: Introduce SES-aware quotas or proxy indicators, a less coaching-sensitive exam format, and uniform score-threshold rules across maintainers to reduce family-capital-driven sorting at the point of entry into 6/8-year gimnázium tracks, without dismantling the track structure itself.
- **S2 — Supply-side contraction of the selective track — shrink the gate, not the rules**: Shrink the state-sector footprint of 6/8-year gimnázium places via score thresholds, capacity caps, or consolidation, while leaving admission criteria within remaining places largely unchanged.
- **S3 — Structural delay of the selection age — Finnish/Polish model**: Raise the statutory age of first academic tracking to 14+ by phasing out 6- and 8-year gimnázium entry points into a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase, paired with explicit anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment.
- **S4 — Non-structural compensatory reform — leave selection age unchanged**: Pursue equity and achievement gains through curriculum clarity, targeted (TEIP-style) funding for disadvantaged schools, retention reduction, and school-quality levelling, without altering the 10-12 selection age or track structure.
- **S5 — Demographically-driven passive contraction — status-quo baseline**: Serve as the no-new-policy counterfactual baseline against which S1-S4 are measured, in which shrinking birth cohorts force school-network consolidation and a natural decline in the selective track's absolute size and share regardless of any active equity or structural intervention.

## What each option costs
- **S1**: Winners: disadvantaged applicants gaining access via quotas; the track structure and its ~10% value-added is preserved for admitted students. Losers: middle-SES or rural applicants without qualifying SES-proxy status could be displaced from residual non-quota places with no proposed safeguard; smaller/less-resourced maintainers bear disproportionate compliance costs; no mechanism reaches the ~40% of general schools sending zero pupils to tracked programmes. [assumption]
- **S1**: Implementation via ministerial decree rather than cardinal law leaves the mechanism exposed to reversal within a single electoral cycle, with no proposed entrenchment design; SES-proxy data infrastructure, exam redesign, and cross-maintainer enforcement costs are all unbudgeted in published Hungarian sources. [assumption]
- **S2**: Winners: church/foundation maintainers positioned to absorb displaced demand and potentially expand market share; government avoids a legislative fight. Losers: families and pupils in the state sector facing arbitrary, discretionary capacity cuts with no compensatory placement guarantee; teachers in surviving urban schools facing workload intensification through larger classes; rural general schools facing disproportionate closure risk; disadvantaged applicants facing intensified competition for a smaller number of slots with no equity safeguard. [assumption]
- **S2**: Compensatory funding for general schools absorbing displaced demand is medium-to-high cost and currently unbudgeted, and its credibility is further undermined by fiscal consolidation pressure (deficit projected at 6.2% in 2026); the mechanism could be neutralized entirely if non-state expansion offsets the intended contraction, wasting the political capital spent on the reform. [assumption]
- **S3**: Winners: at-risk/low-SES pupils potentially gaining up to ~1 SD achievement improvement per Poland's precedent, and reduced between-school segregation; the comprehensive-sector majority. Losers/risks: church/foundation maintainers lose their fastest-growing entry points (grades 5, 7); teachers face a possible multi-year instructional-quality dip during transition given documented workforce shortages; families with children already in 6/8-year tracks risk disruption without ironclad felmenő-rendszer protection; the reform itself faces the highest political-reversal risk of all five scenarios. [assumption]
- **S3**: Costs include high, recurring spend on early diagnostic/remedial support, teacher retraining and reallocation (unquantified given the documented aging workforce and acute regional shortages), facility conversion, and an entirely new anti-sorting enforcement infrastructure with no prior Hungarian analogue or published estimate; the reform's central equity claim depends on this unproven, currently unfunded enforcement layer. [assumption]
- **S4**: Winners: general-school (non-tracked) pupils gaining from curriculum clarity, assessment reform, and retention reduction (net fiscally favorable per Portugal's EUR 112m/two-year precedent); politically least disruptive option, avoiding felmenő-rendszer obligations and the sharpest culture-war framing. Losers/risks: disadvantaged pupils already excluded from selective tracks gain nothing on access; the scenario may legitimize existing segregation by demonstrating 'action' without addressing the gatekeeping mechanism; low-SES high-performers may remain excluded from elite tracks despite compensatory investment, widening the gap between them and low-SES low/average performers. [assumption]
- **S4**: The central benefit-delivery mechanism (TEIP-style targeted funding) is named by the scenario's own risk assessment as a likely first-line fiscal-consolidation cut, making the durability of the expected equity gain highly uncertain; the scenario's political attractiveness as a 'lower-risk' option risks being reframed as policy avoidance given Hungary's EU-worst selectivity status. [assumption]
- **S5**: Winners: government, which avoids all legislative and reversal risk while claiming demographic forces are naturally reducing selectivity; urban elite gimnáziums, insulated by sustained demand. Losers: disadvantaged pupils, whose near-zero admission share into selective tracks remains fully unaddressed; rural and general-school communities facing uncoordinated, potentially arbitrary closure patterns; the near-total absence of active equity investment carries a substantial implicit cost in unaddressed segregation relative to any of S1-S4. [assumption]
- **S5**: Carries zero direct fiscal cost since no active compensatory spending occurs, but this apparent 'political safety' is itself risky: it allows policymakers to avoid the equity problem indefinitely, and the European Commission's explicit 2025 finding that Hungary's system amplifies social and economic inequalities creates funds-conditionality exposure regardless of demographic trends. [assumption]

## What research could resolve
- A piloted psychometric comparison of alternative exam formats with tracked family test-prep expenditure and outcome validity checks, to establish whether any coaching-resistant exam design actually exists (relevant to S1).
- A regression-discontinuity analysis of pilot-region admission outcomes comparing quota and non-quota applicant pools before and after implementation, to test whether SES-proxy quotas displace non-quota middle-SES or rural applicants (S1).
- A decomposition study tracking where displaced applicants actually enroll post-threshold-tightening, using linked admissions data across maintainer types, to test whether contraction actually improves peer composition or merely relocates sorting (S2).
- A dedicated Ministry of Finance costing study of general-school compensatory investment needs, published alongside the 2027 budget bill, to establish the fiscal magnitude and feasibility of S2's compensatory commitment.
- A regional pilot phase-out of 8-year gimnázium entry with propensity-score-matched comparison regions, tracking achievement and segregation outcomes over 5-7 years, to test whether Poland's/Finland's effect sizes replicate in Hungary (S3).
- A decomposition study isolating the marginal contribution of the 6/8-grade gate versus general school-choice segregation, combined with a rigorous ex-post evaluation of any enacted anti-sorting rules' actual enforcement effect (S3).
- A dedicated Ministry of Education costing study modeling teacher retraining, capital costs, and enforcement infrastructure across 1-, 3-, and 5-year phase-in scenarios, to establish S3's total fiscal cost and feasibility.
- A 5-8 year longitudinal matched-cohort study comparing TEIP-enrolled and non-enrolled students, controlling for baseline SES and prior achievement, tracking assessed competency gain rather than exam pass rates, to test whether TEIP-style funding closes achievement gaps (S4).
- The 2027 budget bill text showing either a cardinal-law funding-floor commitment or temporary, deficit-contingent compensation language, plus EC fiscal guidance on permissible spending commitments, to establish whether statutory funding floors are politically achievable (S4).
- A dedicated KSH or Oktatási Hivatal settlement-level school-viability projection under current birth-rate scenarios, to establish how many settlements would lose their last local secondary programme absent coordination (S5).
- Official KIFIR/Oktatási Hivatal admissions statistics disaggregated by track type and maintainer, cross-referenced against cohort-size trends, to isolate the policy-driven component of contraction from the purely demographic one (S5).

## What people must decide
- Whether Hungarian society values preserving parental choice and maintainer (especially church/foundation) autonomy over admissions more highly than closing the documented ~24-point SES-controlled achievement gap and near-zero disadvantaged-pupil access to selective tracks — this is a value trade-off, not a technical question the evidence can resolve alone. [value]
- Whether the political system is willing to accept the reversal risk and multi-year disruption of structural reform (S3) in exchange for the larger, evidence-backed equity and achievement gains it could deliver, or whether it prefers the lower-disruption, lower-certainty path of non-structural reform (S4) or targeted gate-fixing (S1). [value]
- Whether any reform's durability should be guaranteed via cardinal-law entrenchment or cross-party pacts (accepting the political cost of such negotiation) or left to ordinary legislation/decree with the accepted risk of single-cycle reversal — a decision about how much political capital to spend on durability versus speed of implementation. [value]
- Whether teacher-workforce capacity constraints should function as a hard veto that postpones any structural reform until fully resolved, or as a parallel-track precondition that must be funded and sequenced but does not block reform outright — this is a judgment about acceptable implementation risk, not a fact the evidence settles. [value]
- Whether non-state (church/foundation) maintainer autonomy over admissions and capacity should be treated as a negotiable policy lever subject to national equity goals, or as a protected constitutional/coalition commitment that constrains the feasible design space of any reform from the outset. [value]

## What to verify with real stakeholders
- A1 (SES-aware admission quotas and coaching-resistant exam redesign can reduce entry-gate gatekeeping, but only if exam validity, uniform cross-maintainer enforcement, and non-displacement safeguards for disadvantaged pupils are guaranteed.): needs_more_info — No Hungarian evidence yet confirms any specific coaching-resistant exam format or SES-proxy design actually works as intended, and no pilot-evaluation exists. This is a genuinely open empirical question, best resolved via the piloted psychometric comparison and regression-discontinuity analysis proposed above before national rollout.
- A2 (Implementing SES-proxy criteria and monitoring creates new administrative and workload burdens on schools and teachers that are not currently funded, and may fall disproportionately on smaller or less-resourced maintainers.): policy_design_fixable — This is a design flaw, not an inherent feature of SES-aware admission reform: dedicated, funded, timetabled administrative hours and a published compliance-cost budget across maintainer types would resolve the burden-shifting problem without abandoning the equity goal.
- A3 (Uniform admission quotas and thresholds risk overriding church and foundation maintainer autonomy over admissions, protected by subsidiarity principles and constitutional/coalition commitments.): value_conflict — This is a genuine collision between equity (uniform standards to widen opportunity) and subsidiarity/institutional autonomy (constitutionally and contractually protected). No technical redesign eliminates the tension; a political choice about how much autonomy to trade for uniformity is required.
- A4 (Shrinking state-sector gimnázium capacity via score thresholds without funding commitments or anti-sorting rules simply accelerates consolidation, worsens rural school viability, intensifies competition for remaining places, and does not address—may worsen—SES-based exclusion.): policy_design_fixable — The cascade of harms described depends on the absence of specific safeguards (funding floors, redeployment plans, anti-sorting enforcement, restraint on non-state offsetting expansion). If S2 is redesigned to include these explicitly and statutorily, the described harms are substantially containable — several stakeholder voices (pedagogus_erdekvedo, digitalisgazdasagi, egyhazi_fenntartoi, kormanyzati_reformrealizmus) explicitly revised toward this conclusion in the reciprocity round.
- A5 (State-sector selective-capacity contraction can be absorbed by growing church and foundation maintainer capacity, preserving overall selective-track access and institutional pluralism without a legislative fight.): needs_more_info — No published evidence exists on the elasticity or geographic patterning of church/foundation capacity expansion in response to state contraction. Whether non-state expansion would occur in the same regions and at the same pace as state losses is an open empirical question the independent research voice explicitly flags as unresolved.
- A6 (Arbitrary, discretionary capacity cuts disrupt families who already committed to a selective-track path and provide no predictable, published rationale or schedule for planning.): policy_design_fixable — This is squarely an implementation-design problem: a transparent, multi-year-published capacity schedule with guaranteed local-alternative placement, which several scenarios (S1, S2 as redesigned) already propose as the fix, would resolve the predictability concern without abandoning the contraction goal.
- A7 (Delaying academic tracking to age 14+ addresses the root sorting mechanism and is supported by strong cross-national evidence, but its equity gain depends entirely on simultaneously enacted and enforced anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment.): irreducible_tradeoff — Even with well-designed anti-sorting rules, Finland's post-2000 experience shows equity gains eroding via school choice and residential sorting after successful de-tracking — this is not a solvable implementation gap but a documented, recurring pattern where gains in one dimension (structural equity) are vulnerable to erosion in another (choice-driven sorting) that cannot be fully eliminated by design alone, only mitigated and monitored.
- A8 (Hungary's acute and aging teacher shortage is the binding constraint on any comprehensive-school transition: without front-loaded, statutory teacher retraining and recruitment guarantees, structural delay cannot deliver its promised gains and risks a multi-year instructional-quality dip.): policy_design_fixable — Multiple voices (digitalisgazdasagi, oktataspolitikai_reformmozgalom) explicitly argue this is a sequencing and funding problem, not a categorical blocker: a funded, time-bound workforce plan starting 18-24 months before phase-in, sequenced against existing CPD commitments, can close the retraining gap without abandoning structural reform.
- A9 (Abolishing the 6/8-year gimnázium entry points via top-down structural reform erases institutional pluralism, church/foundation maintainer autonomy, and parental choice, and carries high political-reversal risk as shown by Poland's 18-year reform being later reversed.): value_conflict — This combines a genuine value conflict (institutional pluralism/parental choice versus centrally engineered structural equity) with a documented political-durability risk that no design feature fully eliminates. The Polish precedent shows even well-evidenced reforms remain vulnerable to reversal; this is a political-feasibility fact, not merely a fixable design flaw.
- A10 (Families with children already in or entering the 6/8-year track need ironclad felmenő-rendszer protection and no lengthened commutes or unfamiliar catchment reassignment during any phase-out transition.): policy_design_fixable — Felmenő-rendszer (grade-by-grade phase-in) protections are already a legal requirement documented across all structural-reform scenarios; a detailed implementation design specifying zero commute-lengthening and zero catchment reassignment for already-enrolled cohorts is a concrete, achievable design commitment, not an unresolvable conflict.
- A11 (Non-structural reform can deliver real, evidence-backed achievement and fiscal gains with lower workload, disruption, and political-reversal risk than structural reform, without touching selection age.): irreducible_tradeoff — This is a genuine trade-off, not a fixable flaw: lower political risk and disruption necessarily comes at the cost of leaving the structural gatekeeping mechanism untouched, which multiple voices (eselyegyenlosegi_civil, oktataspolitikai_reformmozgalom) show directly bounds the equity effect. You cannot get the full structural equity gain and the lower-risk profile simultaneously from S4 alone.
- A12 (Non-structural compensation leaves the early-selection mechanism that produces near-zero disadvantaged-pupil admission to selective tracks untouched, structurally bounding its equity effect and risking legitimizing segregation while claiming to address it.): value_conflict — Whether non-structural compensation 'legitimizing' the status quo is an acceptable trade-off for its lower-risk, real gains for the general-school majority is fundamentally a value judgment about the purpose and adequacy of incremental reform — evidence establishes the structural boundedness as fact, but whether that boundedness is disqualifying is a political/ethical choice, not a technical one.
- A13 (Targeted (TEIP-style) funding for disadvantaged schools is politically fragile unless given statutory cardinal-law funding-floor status, since the scenario's own risk assessment names it as a likely first-line cut during fiscal consolidation.): policy_design_fixable — While full cardinal-law entrenchment may be politically difficult (as kormanyzati_reformrealizmus notes, given coalition budget-priority constraints), a middle path—an ordinary-law multi-year commitment with published escape-clause conditions and parliamentary oversight making any cut publicly visible and votable—is a concrete, achievable design fix that meaningfully improves durability without requiring the hardest-to-achieve entrenchment level.
- A14 (Passive demographic contraction without an active equity or network-planning lever is a policy abdication that entrenches existing SES-based gatekeeping, risks uncoordinated and unsafe school closures, and wastes scarce future talent in a shrinking labour-market cohort.): value_conflict — Whether S5's political convenience (avoiding all reversal risk and legislative cost) justifies its acceptance of unaddressed equity harm is a value choice about the government's obligation to act versus the political cost of acting — this is not something evidence alone resolves, since it turns on how much weight is placed on avoiding harm through inaction versus the political capital required for active intervention.
- A15 (A purely passive demographic baseline may not actually exist, since active administrative levers (such as S2's score thresholds) are already operating alongside demographic decline, making 'inaction' framing potentially a disguise for unnamed active policy choices.): evidence_answerable — This is a resolvable empirical question: official KIFIR/Oktatási Hivatal admissions statistics disaggregated by track type and maintainer, cross-referenced against cohort-size trends, could isolate the policy-driven component from the purely demographic one, settling whether a clean passive baseline exists.
- A16 (Demographically-driven contraction is politically convenient but leaves EU-level equity criticism of Hungary's highly selective school system unanswered unless paired with transparent reporting distinguishing demographic effects from active policy levers.): communication_fixable — The underlying demographic data is already collected by KSH and Oktatási Hivatal; the missing piece is transparent, published disaggregation distinguishing demographic from policy-driven effects — a reporting/communication fix, not a new data-collection or fundamental policy problem.

## Minority positions
- (school_network_planning, demography, legal_and_governance) Early selection is numerically small (8-12% of cohort) and geographically concentrated; school choice, catchment rules, and maintainer shifts operate largely independently of the 10-12 selection point and would continue sorting students even if it were abolished. — ~40% of general schools (60% in villages) send zero pupils to 6/8-year tracks already; church-school enrollment share rose from 10.4% to 27% independent of formal selection-age law; demographic decline alone forces network consolidation regardless of selection policy.
- (equity_and_social_mobility) Value-added is heterogeneous by track type — positive and consistent for the 8-grade gimnázium but negative at grade 8 (turning positive only by grade 10) for the 6-grade gimnázium — meaning the pedagogical case differs materially by track and cannot be treated uniformly. — HÉTFA Working Paper panel data (2008-2014) shows divergent value-added trajectories by track, meaning a single verdict on 'early selection's pedagogical value' oversimplifies the evidence.
- (political_feasibility, legal_and_governance, school_network_planning) The assumption that church and foundation maintainers can be restricted from absorbing phased-out selective capacity is directly contradicted by observed political and legal constraints: growth independent of law, coalition commitments to preserve operating conditions, and constitutional autonomy frameworks. — The coalition constraint (protecting religious institutions) and the demonstrated growth trend independent of law changes indicate maintainer behavior is not simply a policy dial that reform can turn; restricting it risks alienating a coalition partner or triggering legal challenge.
- (political_feasibility) The named political risks (elite resistance, middle-class flight) understate the true binding constraint on reform, which is maintainer non-compliance backed by binding coalition politics, not merely shifting public opinion. — Tisza's 12-point programme explicitly commits to preserving church maintainers' prior operating conditions, a binding coalition-political fact that pre-empts any assumption of eventual compliance.
- (portuguese_reform) Comparable or greater PISA gains (0.3 SD) are achievable through non-structural reform alone (curriculum clarity, school autonomy, assessment, retention reduction) without touching the selection age at all. — Portugal improved PISA scores by ~31 points between 2000 and 2015-2018 while remaining fully comprehensive, and its retention-reduction programme alone saved €112 million while cutting retained students by 26%.
- (legal_and_governance) Early selection's narrow, separately-codified legal status makes it comparatively more tractable and potentially more durable to reform than Hungary's broader maintainer or curriculum architecture. — The Nkt. § 4 defines secondary education as beginning at grade 9 with 6/8-year gymnasium as the only statutory exception, meaning reform can proceed via a narrower legislative lever than a full system overhaul.
- (fuggetlen_szakpolitikai_kutatomuhely, konzervativ_ertekvedo) A purely passive demographic baseline (S5) may not actually exist, since active administrative levers (such as S2's score thresholds) already appear to be operating alongside passive demographic contraction. — The scenario's own uncertainty section concedes this, but no side has produced the disaggregated data separating demographic contraction from active administrative levers, so presenting S5 as a clean counterfactual risks legitimizing inaction under a misleading frame.
