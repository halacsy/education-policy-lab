# Policy scenarios

## S1 — Admissions and intake-equity regulation (keep structure, fix the gate)
**Goal**
Reduce family-capital-driven sorting at the point of entry into 6/8-year gimnázium tracks, without dismantling the track structure itself.

**Mechanism**
- SES-aware quotas or proxy indicators reserve a share of places for disadvantaged applicants, directly countering the documented ~46% vs ~22% rejection-rate gap between low- and high-poverty-share classes. [evidence: moderate]
- A less coaching-sensitive exam format would reduce the weight of family test-prep resources in admission outcomes, though no Hungarian evidence confirms any specific alternative format's effectiveness. [evidence: weak]
- Uniform score-threshold rules across maintainers would reduce administratively inconsistent, tightening cutoffs (e.g., 70/100 points at some institutions) that currently vary by maintainer discretion. [evidence: weak]

**Evidence status**
moderate — Admission-gatekeeping SES bias is strongly documented, but the effectiveness of any specific proxy, quota, or coaching-resistant exam design in reducing it is only weakly or not yet evidenced in Hungary.

**Assumptions**
- Admission-equity rules can be designed and enforced consistently across state, church, and foundation maintainers — though the expert record treats maintainer plurality as a structural complication, not a solved design problem, and church/foundation maintainers operate under partial autonomy frameworks that may resist uniform national mandates.
- Self-selection out of the exam by disadvantaged-background pupils reflects information and financial barriers rather than genuine ability differences at age 10-12, and is therefore addressable by proxy/quota design rather than being an independent, unaddressed channel of exclusion.
- Regulatory change can be implemented via legislation or ministerial decree with sufficient enforcement capacity to prevent circumvention by maintainers, despite the felmenő-rendszer (grade-by-grade phase-in) legal requirement and the absence of any Alkotmánybíróság precedent on its binding status.

**Expected benefits**
- Reduced SES-skew in admitted cohorts at the 6/8-year gate, addressing the documented near-zero intake share of multiply-disadvantaged pupils. [evidence: moderate]
- Preserves the approximately 10% competency-gain advantage associated with 6/8-year tracks for admitted students, since the track structure itself is untouched. [evidence: moderate]
- Lower political disruption than structural reform, since it does not touch the statutory definition of secondary education or require felmenő-rendszer treatment of the whole system, though this should not be read as evidence the approach is free of reversal risk given the 2026 Fidesz culture-war framing of any selection-related change. [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
Likely reduces SES-skew within remaining selective places, but middle-SES or rural pupils without qualifying SES-proxy status could face reduced access if quotas displace them from the residual non-quota places, and the scenario offers no specific safeguard or compensating mechanism for this displaced group; it also does nothing to address the ~40% of general schools (60% in villages) that send zero pupils to tracked programs regardless of admission-rule design.

**Cost categories**
- SES-proxy data infrastructure (administrative data-linkage/register build); low-cost option (self-declared SES checklist) has minimal fiscal footprint, while the high-cost option (verified administrative register linkage, akin to Admin3-scale infrastructure) lacks a published fiscal range in current Hungarian costing studies.
- Exam redesign and psychometric validation costs, one-off plus recurring revalidation, magnitude unpublished in Hungarian sources.
- Enforcement and monitoring costs across state, church, and foundation maintainers, unbudgeted and structurally complicated by maintainer plurality.

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education / Oktatási Hivatal — Design SES-proxy criteria and draft legislative or decree text, incorporating felmenő-rendszer phase-in requirements and at least the ~1-year statutory lead time established by past Nkt. amendments. (timeline: year 1-2)
- Legislature (Országgyűlés) — Legislate uniform admission-equity rules applicable across maintainer types, with explicit negotiation provisions for church/foundation maintainer autonomy agreements; given documented litigation and consultation risks, this step is more realistically multi-year than a single legislative session. (timeline: year 2-4)
- Oktatási Hivatal + school maintainers — Pilot the SES-proxy admission mechanism and coaching-resistant exam format in a subset of regions/schools before national rollout, monitoring for circumvention. (timeline: year 3-5)
- Ministry of Education / national administration — National rollout with felmenő-rendszer cohort protections and published annual monitoring of admitted-cohort SES composition. (timeline: year 5-7)

**Political risks**
- Implementation by ministerial decree rather than cardinal law leaves the mechanism exposed to reversal by a subsequent government within a single electoral cycle; the scenario does not propose a specific entrenchment design (e.g., cardinal-law status, cross-party pact, or sunset-clause safeguard) to mitigate this.
- Fidesz's demonstrated framing of any selection-related change as ideologically totalitarian creates reversal risk even for a narrower admissions-only intervention, not only for structural abolition.
- Church and foundation maintainer coalition constraints (Tisza's 12-point program commitment to preserve prior operating conditions) may block uniform enforcement, creating a two-tier compliance landscape.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether any specific coaching-resistant exam format exists that meaningfully reduces the weight of paid test preparation without introducing new validity or reliability problems. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Piloted psychometric comparison of alternative exam formats with tracked family test-prep expenditure and outcome validity checks.)
- Whether uniform admission-equity rules can be legally and practically enforced across state, church, and foundation maintainers given documented maintainer plurality and partial autonomy agreements. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Legal mapping of maintainer autonomy clauses plus interviews with maintainer federation counsel on compliance willingness.)
- Whether SES-proxy quotas would displace non-quota middle-SES or rural applicants without triggering compensating access mechanisms. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Regression-discontinuity analysis of pilot-region admission outcomes comparing quota and non-quota applicant pools before and after implementation.)

## S2 — Supply-side contraction of the selective track (shrink the gate, not the rules)
**Goal**
Shrink the state-sector footprint of 6/8-year gimnázium places via score thresholds, capacity caps, or consolidation, while leaving admission criteria within remaining places largely unchanged.

**Mechanism**
- Minimum-score admission thresholds set by maintainers (e.g., the Klebelsberg Center) reduce the gimnázium track's share of cohort intake from approximately 40% toward 30% or lower, independent of demand. [evidence: moderate]
- Reduced state-sector selective capacity may incrementally improve peer composition in urban general-school upper grades, though the international evidence base for this specific compositional mechanism (capacity reduction rather than track abolition) is contested and cycle-dependent per PISA equity-trend reviews. [evidence: contested]
- Demographic decline compounds the score-threshold lever, since the absolute pool of eligible students is shrinking independently of policy, meaning capacity caps interact with an already-contracting applicant base. [evidence: strong]

**Evidence status**
contested — The score-threshold mechanism for shrinking capacity is moderately documented in current admissions practice, but whether this specific supply-contraction approach improves peer composition or equity outcomes (versus simply relocating advantage) is contested and cycle-dependent in the international literature.

**Assumptions**
- Church and foundation maintainers can be regulated or incentivized not to expand selective places to offset the state-sector reduction — though the expert record's minority position states these maintainers are institutionally incentivized to expand (having grown from 10.4% to 27% of gimnázium students 2010-2021) and Tisza's coalition commitment to preserve their prior operating conditions constrains any restriction on this expansion.
- Score-threshold-driven contraction, applied independent of demand, will not simply push excluded high-demand families toward church/foundation alternatives, private tutoring intensification, or catchment-area relocation.
- Maintainer-level discretionary score thresholds, currently applied inconsistently (binding at some institutions, non-binding then retroactively enforced at others), can be converted into a coherent, predictable national policy lever rather than remaining a source of administrative chaos.

**Expected benefits**
- Shrinks the selective track's footprint without requiring statutory redefinition of the secondary-education age boundary, potentially avoiding the felmenő-rendszer and ~1-year lead-time obligations that a structural reform would trigger. [evidence: weak]
- Aligns policy with the demographic reality that the exam's original rationing function is eroding, as evidenced by falling 8-year-track applications (-14% year-on-year) alongside flat overall enrolment. [evidence: moderate]
- May incrementally improve peer composition in urban general-school upper grades by retaining higher-performing students who would otherwise have exited into shrunk selective capacity. [evidence: contested]

**Equity impact**
Residual selective places are likely to become more SES-skewed as competition intensifies for a smaller number of slots, and lower-SES pupils lose access without compensating investment in general-school quality; the scenario does not offer any mechanism, safeguard, or funding commitment to mitigate this risk of intensified within-remaining-places sorting, nor does it address church/foundation-sector expansion that could absorb and even increase overall selective-track access despite state-sector contraction.

**Cost categories**
- Compensatory funding or capacity controls for general schools absorbing displaced demand — medium-to-high, multi-year, currently unbudgeted; no numeric fiscal range has been published in Hungarian sources, and fiscal consolidation pressure (deficit projected at 6.2% in 2026) further reduces the credibility of any future budget commitment.
- Regulatory design and legal-drafting costs for capacity-cap or threshold-setting instruments across maintainer types, magnitude unpublished.
- Administrative monitoring costs to track maintainer-level compliance and detect offsetting expansion by non-state maintainers, currently unbudgeted.

**Implementation steps**
- Klebelsberg Center / state school district maintainers — Formalize currently discretionary minimum-score thresholds into a consistent, published national capacity-reduction schedule to eliminate the current two-tier administrative chaos (binding vs. non-binding cutoffs). (timeline: year 1-2)
- Ministry of Education — Design and propose regulatory levers (funding conditions, licensing terms) aimed at discouraging offsetting capacity expansion by church and foundation maintainers, acknowledging the coalition-constraint and legal-autonomy barriers documented in the expert record; a credible design requires legal review of existing maintainer autonomy agreements before any lever can be enacted. (timeline: year 2-4)
- Oktatási Hivatal — Publish annual monitoring reports tracking selective-track share of cohort, SES composition of remaining places, and any offsetting non-state expansion. (timeline: year 2-6)
- General-school maintainers / Ministry of Finance — Allocate compensatory capacity/quality investment to general schools absorbing displaced demand, contingent on fiscal-space availability under consolidation pressure. (timeline: year 3-7)

**Political risks**
- Reversal requires legislative repeal since score thresholds are maintainer-level administrative practice rather than statute, offering more procedural friction than S1's decree-based mechanism, but a parliamentary majority change during the multi-year rollout still poses a significant reversal risk given the Fidesz framing of any selection-related change and the government's own pattern of rapidly reversing predecessor reforms.
- Church and foundation maintainers, protected by Tisza's coalition commitment to preserve prior operating conditions, may simply expand to absorb displaced demand, neutralizing the intended contraction and creating a policy-credibility problem.
- The lack of a clear normative justification (efficiency vs equity vs demography) for capacity reduction independent of demand creates messaging vulnerability, since parents may perceive it as arbitrary rationing rather than principled reform.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether reduced state-sector selective capacity actually improves peer composition in general schools, or whether displaced demand simply migrates to church/foundation providers, private tutoring, or catchment relocation, neutralizing the compositional benefit. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A decomposition study tracking where displaced applicants actually enroll post-threshold-tightening, using linked admissions data across maintainer types.)
- Whether church and foundation maintainers can be regulated or incentivized not to expand selective places, given their documented growth trajectory and coalition-level political protection. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A legal mapping of maintainer funding-conditionality levers available to government, tested against at least one enacted case of successful non-state capacity restraint.)
- What the precise fiscal cost of compensatory general-school investment would be, and whether it is achievable under projected fiscal consolidation. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A dedicated Ministry of Finance costing study of general-school compensatory investment needs published alongside the 2027 budget bill.)

## S3 — Structural delay of the selection age (Finnish/Polish model)
**Goal**
Raise the statutory age of first academic tracking to 14+ by phasing out 6- and 8-year gimnázium entry points into a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase, paired with explicit anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment.

**Mechanism**
- Cross-national difference-in-differences evidence associates earlier between-school tracking with greater inequality of outcomes without raising mean achievement, providing the core rationale for delaying selection. [evidence: moderate]
- Poland's 1999 reform (delaying selection from age 12 to 15) produced PISA gains of 0.25 SD math, 0.28 SD reading, 0.16 SD science, with at-risk students gaining approximately 1 SD, directly demonstrating the achievable effect-size range for structural delay. [evidence: strong]
- Sorting does not simply migrate to school choice, non-state maintainers, catchment effects, or private tutoring markets to the point of neutralizing the equity gain only if explicit anti-sorting rules for choice and catchment are implemented and enforced simultaneously with the structural delay; absent such rules, Kertesi & Kezdi (2013) and Berenyi-Berkovits-Erőss school-choice research directly document that free school choice plus early selective tracks already produce strong between-school sorting at the primary stage, and Finland's post-2000 experience shows equity gains eroding via school choice and residential sorting even after successful de-tracking. [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
moderate — The equity and achievement effects of delaying tracking are strongly supported cross-nationally (Poland, Finland), but whether these gains hold in Hungary's specific context — given school choice, maintainer plurality, and the risk of sorting migrating to other channels — is only moderately evidenced and explicitly contested by the scenario's own uncertainty and equity fields.

**Assumptions**
- Sorting does not simply migrate to school choice, non-state maintainers, catchment effects, or private tutoring markets to the point of neutralizing the equity gain — this assumption is in direct tension with this scenario's own mechanism, equity_impact, political_risks, and uncertainties fields, all of which document that anti-sorting rules for choice and catchment are a necessary, unproven, and politically contested co-requirement, not a guaranteed accompaniment to structural delay.
- Poland's and Finland's institutional and demographic contexts are sufficiently similar to Hungary's that their effect sizes and reform durability lessons transfer meaningfully, despite documented differences in maintainer structure, school-choice intensity, and baseline segregation levels.
- Teacher workforce capacity — despite the documented aging workforce (50% age 50+, only 7.2% under 30) and acute regional/subject shortages — can be sufficiently expanded or reallocated to staff a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase without compromising quality during the transition.

**Expected benefits**
- Potential achievement gains in the range of Poland's observed 0.16-0.28 SD across subjects, with the largest gains for at-risk/vocational-track-bound students, if Hungary's context proves sufficiently comparable. [evidence: moderate]
- Reduced intergenerational income elasticity, by analogy with Finland's observed 23% reduction (0.30 to 0.23) following its delay of tracking from 11 to 16, though Finland's effect was bundled with welfare-state expansion and teacher-education reform not isolated from the tracking-age effect itself. [evidence: moderate]
- Between-school equity improvement of the magnitude Poland achieved (moving from one of the OECD's largest between-school disparities to one of the smallest) is plausible given Hungary's currently even higher baseline segregation, offering a larger absolute gap to close. [evidence: moderate]

**Equity impact**
The scenario's largest identified risk is that sorting relocates into school choice, non-state maintainer expansion, catchment-area effects, and private tutoring markets, potentially neutralizing any equity gain from raising the selection age alone; Poland's own experience shows between-school equity improved sharply while individual-level SES gaps did not close, and Finland's post-2000 erosion via school choice demonstrates this is not a hypothetical risk but an observed pattern in both reference cases — the scenario's anti-sorting rules for choice and catchment are the load-bearing, currently unproven component without which the headline equity claim does not hold.

**Cost categories**
- Early diagnostic and remedial support system build-out — high, recurring; no numeric fiscal range has been published in Hungarian sources, though Portugal's grade-retention reduction (analogous compensatory investment) saved EUR 112 million over two years across 663 schools, offering one partial cross-national cost/benefit reference point of a different but related intervention.
- Teacher retraining and reallocation for a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase, high cost given the documented aging workforce and acute regional shortages, magnitude unquantified in published Hungarian costing studies.
- Facility conversion and capital costs for consolidating parallel 4/6/8-year track infrastructure into a unified structure, unquantified.
- Anti-sorting enforcement infrastructure for school-choice and catchment regulation, a wholly new cost category with no prior Hungarian analogue or published estimate.

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education + Legislature — Draft and negotiate legislation raising the statutory secondary-education age boundary (amending Nkt. §4), with cross-party consultation attempted given the documented reversal risk of single-party structural reforms (Poland's 18-year reversal precedent). (timeline: year 1-3)
- Ministry of Education / Oktatási Hivatal — Design felmenő-rendszer (grade-by-grade) phase-out schedule protecting already-admitted cohorts, plus anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment to be enacted simultaneously, not sequentially. (timeline: year 2-3)
- Ministry of Education + teacher-training universities — Staged, capacity-realistic teacher-retraining programme sequenced after — or explicitly coordinated with — the 2024-2031 salary-reform timeline, acknowledging that current CPD bandwidth is already committed to NAT and AI-integration reforms and that rural/STEM shortages are 2-3 times more severe, meaning full national rollout within this window remains ambitious and may require regional staggering beyond the stated end date. (timeline: year 2-9)
- Oktatási Hivatal + municipal/maintainer bodies — Enforce and monitor anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment, publishing annual segregation-index reports to detect displacement of sorting into unregulated channels. (timeline: year 3-10)

**Political risks**
- The scenario identifies a substantial reversal risk citing the Polish precedent (reform reversed after 18 years despite documented gains), and the only entrenchment device contemplated (a cross-party political pact) is not specified with statutory safeguards, independent monitoring triggers, or supermajority requirements, leaving the reform structurally exposed in the same way Poland's was.
- Fidesz's immediate framing of any 'unified model' as totalitarian ('no example since communism since') establishes this scenario as the highest-visibility target for culture-war opposition among all five, given it most closely matches the minister's initially floated (then retracted) unified four-year model idea.
- Church and foundation maintainer opposition is likely to be intense given the scenario would phase out the entry points (grades 5 and 7) that anchor their fastest-growing enrollment segment, and Tisza's coalition commitment to preserve prior church-maintainer operating conditions creates an internal contradiction with this scenario's core mechanism.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether Poland's and Finland's effect sizes would replicate in Hungary given differing demographic composition (Roma representation, migration patterns), baseline segregation levels, and school-choice intensity. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A regional pilot phase-out of 8-year gimnázium entry with propensity-score-matched comparison regions, tracking achievement and segregation outcomes over 5-7 years.)
- Whether anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment can actually prevent sorting from migrating to those channels, given that Kertesi & Kezdi and Finland's post-2000 experience both document this migration occurring even where such rules existed in some form. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A decomposition study isolating the marginal contribution of the 6/8-grade gate versus general school-choice segregation, using regional variation in kisgimnázium density, combined with a rigorous ex-post evaluation of any enacted anti-sorting rules' actual enforcement effect.)
- What the total fiscal cost of the combined teacher-retraining, facility-conversion, and anti-sorting enforcement package would be, and whether it is feasible within the current teacher-salary-reform funding envelope. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A dedicated Ministry of Education costing study modeling teacher retraining, capital costs, and enforcement infrastructure across 1-, 3-, and 5-year phase-in scenarios.)

## S4 — Non-structural compensatory reform (leave selection age unchanged)
**Goal**
Pursue equity and achievement gains through curriculum clarity, targeted (TEIP-style) funding for disadvantaged schools, retention reduction, and school-quality levelling, without altering the 10-12 selection age or track structure.

**Mechanism**
- Portugal's documented gains (approximately 31 PISA points / 0.3 SD between 2000-2018) resulted from curriculum standards clarification, increased school autonomy, and regular student assessment — not from changes to selection age — providing a template mechanism transferable in principle to Hungary's comprehensive-sector (non-tracked) majority. [evidence: strong]
- TEIP-style targeted funding reduces student dropout and grade retention, but does not close competency gaps relative to non-TEIP schools, and no rigorous longitudinal evaluation of its long-term student-outcome effects exists. [evidence: moderate]
- Grade-retention reduction alone saved Portugal EUR 112 million over two years while reducing retained students by 26% across 663 schools, demonstrating that this specific lever can be both fiscally and pedagogically favorable, independent of the TEIP funding question. [evidence: strong]
- Non-structural reform can operate independently of decisions about selection age and intensity only to a limited degree, since the mechanism field itself notes that early selection may offset some benefits of compensatory investment by continuing to remove the highest-performing, often highest-SES pupils from the general-school population that this scenario's interventions target — meaning the scenario's benefits are bounded by a structural factor it does not touch. [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
moderate — Portugal's non-structural achievement gains and the retention-reduction fiscal win are strongly evidenced, but TEIP's competency-gap-closing effect is only moderately evidenced, and the scenario's own mechanism field acknowledges its benefits are structurally bounded by the unaddressed selection age.

**Assumptions**
- Funding commitments for TEIP-style targeted programmes will be durable and not the first line cut during fiscal consolidation — this directly contradicts the scenario's own political_risks field, which identifies targeted funding lines as a likely first-line cut, and the fiscal-consolidation pressure documented in the political-feasibility record (6.2% deficit projection, 2026).
- Curriculum clarity, school autonomy, and regular assessment drive outcomes independently of selection structure, and their effects are not interactive with early tracking in ways that would reverse the ordering observed in Portugal's non-tracked system.
- Portugal's demographic and institutional context (school resources, teacher training, family educational-background distribution) is sufficiently comparable to Hungary's that its non-structural intervention outcomes are transferable, despite Hungary's markedly higher baseline SES segregation and different maintainer landscape.

**Expected benefits**
- Achievement gains in the range of Portugal's observed 0.3 SD improvement over roughly 15-18 years are plausible for the comprehensive (non-tracked) majority of Hungarian pupils if curriculum, autonomy, and assessment reforms are implemented with comparable fidelity. [evidence: moderate]
- Politically less disruptive than structural reform since it does not touch the statutory definition of secondary education, avoiding the felmenő-rendszer obligations and the direct Fidesz 'totalitarian unified model' framing risk that S3 faces — though this should not be read as evidence the package is politically risk-free, given that targeted funding for disadvantaged schools is independently a fiscal-consolidation target. [evidence: weak]
- Grade-retention reduction offers a documented dual fiscal-and-equity win independent of the more uncertain TEIP competency-gap question, based directly on the Portuguese EUR 112 million/two-year, 26%-reduction precedent. [evidence: strong]

**Equity impact**
The scenario could be equity-neutral or even equity-negative net of its entrenchment effect on the status quo, because it explicitly leaves the 10-12 selection age and the associated removal of the highest-performing, often highest-SES pupils from the general-school population untouched, legitimizing early selection by demonstrating that 'something is being done' without addressing its documented segregation effect (highest in the EU) or the near-zero admission share of multiply-disadvantaged pupils in elite tracks; there is also a risk of widening the gap between low-SES high-performers (who benefit from compensatory investment but may still be excluded from elite tracks by admission gatekeeping) and low-SES low/average-performers.

**Cost categories**
- TEIP-style targeted funding line for disadvantaged schools — no numeric fiscal range available in published Hungarian sources, and the scenario's own political-risk field identifies this as a likely first target for fiscal-consolidation cuts, making its effective, durable cost highly uncertain despite its nominal budget line existing.
- Curriculum-clarity and assessment-transparency reform costs, one-off design plus recurring implementation-support costs, magnitude unpublished.
- Grade-retention reduction programme costs, offset by documented savings (Portugal's EUR 112 million over two years across 663 schools), suggesting this specific line item may be net-cost-saving rather than cost-adding if implemented at comparable scale and fidelity.

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education / National Core Curriculum body — Design curriculum-clarity and assessment-transparency standards for general (non-tracked) schools, sequenced explicitly alongside the already-committed NAT and AI-integration reform timelines to avoid CPD-bandwidth conflict. (timeline: year 1-3)
- Ministry of Education + Ministry of Finance — Establish a TEIP-style targeted-funding mechanism with an explicit statutory (cardinal-law) funding floor rather than an annual-budget line, directly responding to the scenario's own political-risk finding that non-statutory targeted funding is a likely first-line cut; achieving statutory-floor status is itself uncertain given projected fiscal consolidation and should be treated as a contested legislative negotiation, not an administrative step, extending the realistic timeline beyond initial design. (timeline: year 2-6)
- Ministry of Education + school-level administration — Roll out grade-retention reduction programme at national scale, drawing on Portugal's documented model, with annual monitoring of both fiscal savings and retained-student outcomes. (timeline: year 2-5)
- Independent research institute / Oktatási Hivatal — Commission a longitudinal matched-cohort evaluation of TEIP-style programme effects on competency gains (not just dropout/retention), addressing the documented absence of rigorous long-term evaluation. (timeline: year 3-10)

**Political risks**
- Targeted TEIP-style funding lines are likely first-line cuts during fiscal consolidation (deficit projected at 6.2% in 2026, debt rising to 76.8% of GDP by 2027), making the central benefit-delivery mechanism budget-vulnerable in a way that significantly undermines the durability of any expected equity gain, even though the scenario avoids the structural-reform reversal risks facing S1-S3.
- The scenario's political attractiveness as a 'lower-risk' alternative may itself become a political liability if framed by reform advocates or the European Commission as inadequate given Hungary's documented status as the EU's most socially selective system, risking accusations of policy avoidance rather than genuine reform.
- Teacher-union (PSZ) framing that positions workforce adequacy (16,000+ shortage) as the binding, non-negotiable precondition applies to this scenario's curriculum and TEIP-delivery mechanisms as much as to structural alternatives, meaning workforce constraints could stall implementation regardless of the scenario's structural modesty.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether TEIP-type targeted support can close achievement/competency gaps rather than merely reducing dropout rates, since no longitudinal study has tracked student competency trajectories beyond retention reduction. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: A 5-8 year longitudinal matched-cohort study comparing TEIP-enrolled and non-enrolled students, controlling for baseline SES and prior achievement, tracking assessed competency gain rather than exam pass rates.)
- Whether statutory (cardinal-law) funding-floor guarantees for targeted programmes are politically and fiscally achievable under projected consolidation, or whether compensatory funding will remain contingent on annual budget cycles and vulnerable to cuts. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: The 2027 budget bill text showing either a cardinal-law funding-floor commitment or temporary, deficit-contingent compensation language, plus EC fiscal guidance on permissible spending commitments.)
- Whether curriculum clarity, autonomy, and assessment reforms would produce Portugal-comparable gains given Hungary's markedly higher baseline SES segregation and different maintainer landscape, or whether the untouched selection age would suppress the transferable effect size. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A matched cross-national comparison isolating the marginal contribution of curriculum/autonomy/assessment reform independent of tracking structure, ideally using regions or time periods within Hungary with varying implementation fidelity.)

## S5 — Demographically-driven passive contraction (status-quo baseline)
**Goal**
Serve as the no-new-policy counterfactual baseline against which S1-S4 are measured, in which shrinking birth cohorts force school-network consolidation and a natural decline in the selective track's absolute size and share regardless of any active equity or structural intervention.

**Mechanism**
- Annual births fell from ~125,000 (early 1990s) to 77,500 (2024) and ~72,000 (2025 estimate), the lowest recorded level since 1949, mechanically shrinking the pool of children available to enter any secondary track, selective or not. [evidence: strong]
- A measurable demographic cliff one cohort ahead (approximately 17,000 fewer one-year-olds than four-year-olds, equivalent to 600-800 kindergarten/class-groups) means at least one hundred small schools' worth of children will structurally vanish from the system within a few years, independent of any policy choice. [evidence: strong]
- Physical school-network capacity (880 secondary schools, underused dormitory capacity) is not the binding constraint on selective-track viability; the binding constraint is the shrinking and unevenly distributed child population itself, meaning passive demographic contraction alone will not resolve the geographic concentration of selection in a minority of (mostly urban, church-maintained) settlements. [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
strong — The demographic decline itself and its mechanical effect on cohort sizes are very strongly evidenced by KSH data; what remains uncertain is the distributional and equity consequence of passive contraction, which is only moderately or weakly evidenced.

**Assumptions**
- Cohort-size projections (declining under-20 population, from ~2 million in 2012 toward ~1.4 million by 2060) are stable across the planning horizon and do not assume major immigration reversing natural population decrease.
- School-maintainer sustainability thresholds (minimum ~45-student class-viability floors, per the Ukrainian cross-border analogue) are a binding constraint such that schools below such floors cannot operate financially or pedagogically, forcing consolidation regardless of policy stance on selection.
- Passive contraction is genuinely policy-neutral — i.e., no active government lever (such as the score-threshold mechanism documented in S2) is simultaneously operating to accelerate or shape the contraction beyond pure demographic effect — though the demography record itself notes government is currently using score thresholds as an additional supply-side lever, meaning a truly 'passive' baseline may not exist in the current policy environment and this scenario should be read as an analytical counterfactual rather than an achievable real-world state.

**Expected benefits**
- Provides a clean counterfactual baseline against which the incremental effects of S1-S4 can be measured, isolating policy-driven change from demographically-driven change. [evidence: strong]
- Reduces the absolute number of selective-track places over time simply through cohort shrinkage, without requiring any legislative or administrative action, avoiding all the implementation and reversal risks documented for S1-S4. [evidence: strong]
- May force some degree of network consolidation that incidentally reduces the number of dispersed, small selective-track offerings in thin rural markets, though this is a side effect of viability thresholds rather than a designed equity mechanism. [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
Passive contraction alone does not address, and may worsen, the concentration of remaining selective places among already-advantaged urban and church-maintained institutions, since consolidation pressure falls hardest on smaller, often rural or general-school providers while urban elite gimnáziums (44.8% of secondary enrollment nationally, concentrated in Budapest) retain sufficient demand to persist; without any active equity lever, the SES-skew of who accesses the shrinking number of selective places is likely to remain unchanged or worsen, and this scenario explicitly does nothing about the documented near-zero admission share of multiply-disadvantaged pupils.

**Cost categories**
- Network consolidation and school-closure administrative costs, driven by demographic necessity rather than policy design, magnitude unquantified at settlement level though KSH/Oktatási Hivatal aggregate trends exist.
- Teacher workforce redeployment or attrition costs associated with consolidation, potentially displacing experienced teachers from closing small schools, magnitude unquantified.
- Foregone equity-investment opportunity cost: since this baseline involves no active compensatory spending, it carries zero direct fiscal cost but a substantial implicit cost in unaddressed segregation relative to any of S1-S4.

**Implementation steps**
- KSH / Ministry of Education (monitoring only, no active policy design) — Continue publishing annual demographic and enrollment statistics tracking cohort decline and its mechanical effect on track sizes, without introducing new admission, funding, or structural levers. (timeline: year 1-10 (ongoing))
- Municipal and school-maintainer bodies — Respond reactively to falling enrollment through ad hoc, maintainer-level consolidation decisions as individual schools fall below viability thresholds, absent any coordinated national plan. (timeline: year 1-15 (ongoing))
- Independent research institutes / Oktatási Hivatal — Track and publish the counterfactual comparison data (selective-track share, SES composition, achievement gaps) needed to evaluate S1-S4 against this baseline. (timeline: year 1-10 (ongoing))

**Political risks**
- Politically the path of least resistance, since it requires no legislative action and therefore faces no direct Fidesz culture-war framing risk, but this apparent political safety is itself a risk: it allows policymakers to avoid the equity problem indefinitely while claiming demographic forces are 'naturally' solving selectivity, when the evidence shows government is simultaneously using score thresholds (S2) as an active supply lever layered atop passive contraction.
- Absence of active intervention risks EU-level criticism given the European Commission's explicit 2025 finding that Hungary's system amplifies social and economic inequalities, potentially triggering funds-conditionality pressure regardless of demographic trends.
- Reactive, uncoordinated maintainer-level consolidation (as opposed to planned network restructuring) risks arbitrary and inequitable closure patterns, disproportionately affecting rural general schools while urban elite gimnáziums are insulated by sustained demand.

**Uncertainties**
- How many settlements would lose their last local secondary programme if consolidation continues at the current demographic pace, absent any coordinated national plan. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A dedicated KSH or Oktatási Hivatal settlement-level school-viability projection under current birth-rate scenarios.)
- The extent to which government's active score-threshold lever (documented in S2) is already operating on top of passive demographic contraction, meaning a purely 'policy-neutral' demographic baseline may not currently exist in practice. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Official KIFIR/Oktatási Hivatal admissions statistics disaggregated by track type and maintainer for 2025/2026-2026/2027, cross-referenced against cohort-size trends to isolate the policy-driven component from the purely demographic one.)
- The causal magnitude of teacher-workforce disruption (displacement vs. efficiency gain) from consolidation-induced school closures under this passive scenario, since closing small schools may displace experienced teachers or may improve efficiency. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Longitudinal administrative data on teacher movements, school staffing and pupil-to-teacher ratios pre- and post-consolidation, and value-added models of school quality in rural vs. urban surviving schools.)
