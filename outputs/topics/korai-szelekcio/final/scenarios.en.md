# Policy scenarios

## S1 — Admissions and intake-equity regulation (keep structure, fix the gate)
**Goal**
Keep the 6/8-year gimnázium structure intact but regulate the admission mechanism itself — SES-aware quotas or proxies, uniform score-threshold rules, coaching-resistant exam design — to reduce family-capital-driven sorting at the point of entry.

**Mechanism**
- A coaching-resistant exam design and SES-aware admission proxies could reduce the correlation between private tutoring investment and admission success. [evidence: moderate]
- Uniform score-threshold rules across maintainers would reduce school-by-school discretion in admissions criteria, which currently varies across roughly 2,000+ institutions. [evidence: weak]
- Regulating admission mechanics alone will not eliminate sorting, since school choice and catchment-area dynamics operate as independent segregation channels alongside formal selection. [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
moderate — Admission-mechanism effects on sorting are moderately evidenced (Berényi, Eduline preparation-cost studies), but no Hungarian causal study isolates the effect of admission-rule redesign alone from parallel school-choice and catchment effects.

**Assumptions**
- Admission-equity rules (SES-aware quotas, uniform thresholds) can be designed and enforced with reasonable consistency across state, church, and foundation maintainers, notwithstanding documented maintainer plurality and church-school autonomy guarantees that are constitutionally and contractually protected. [assumption]
- Redesigning the exam to be coaching-resistant will not simply shift private investment toward new, harder-to-regulate preparation channels (e.g. informal networks, earlier-age tutoring). [assumption]
- Legal reform via ministerial decree (EMMI rendelet) is sufficient to implement these changes without requiring primary legislation, given that admissions rules currently sit at decree level rather than in the Nkt. itself. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Likely reduces the correlation between measured admission scores and family socio-economic capital at the point of entry, though the magnitude is not established for Hungary. [evidence: weak]
- Preserves the approximately 10% value-added gain associated with 6/8-year tracks for admitted students, but this figure is itself contested as a composition/peer effect rather than a pedagogical effect, and is measured relative to comparable-background peers, not as an aggregate system benefit. [evidence: contested]
- Avoids the legal, political and multi-year transition costs of structural abolition (felmenő rendszer, church-maintainer consent), since decree-level admission reform is procedurally faster than statutory track discontinuation. [evidence: moderate]

**Equity impact**
Likely produces a modest reduction in family-capital-driven sorting within remaining selective places, but this gain is bounded and possibly reversed by two documented offsetting channels: (1) church/foundation maintainers, whose share has grown to roughly 25-30% of gimnázium enrolment and who retain autonomous admission discretion, are not bound by the same decree-level rules as state schools and can absorb displaced advantaged demand; (2) private preparation investment may relocate to new, less regulable channels rather than disappear. Because the scenario leaves school choice and catchment-area sorting untouched, the equity gain is confined narrowly to the admission-scoring mechanism itself and should not be read as a system-wide segregation reduction.

**Cost categories**
- SES-proxy data infrastructure: a low-cost option (self-reported proxy on existing forms) versus a high-cost option (verified administrative-data linkage across tax/social registries); no numeric range is available in the current record.
- Exam redesign and coaching-resistance testing: magnitude unpublished; likely recurring cost for item-bank development and psychometric validation, not a one-off expense.
- Enforcement and compliance monitoring across state, church, and foundation maintainers: unquantified, but structurally multi-year and likely to require new inspection capacity given the kormányhivatal registration-power route documented for quality-conditioning precedents.

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education (EMMI) — Draft and consult on a decree amendment introducing SES-aware admission proxies and uniform score thresholds. (timeline: year 1)
- Oktatási Hivatal (education authority) — Pilot coaching-resistant exam items in a subset of schools and evaluate preparation-cost impact. (timeline: year 1-2)
- Kormányhivatal (county government offices) — Establish monitoring and registration-power enforcement mechanisms to check maintainer compliance with uniform thresholds, negotiating separately with church and foundation maintainers given their autonomy guarantees. (timeline: year 2-4)
- Independent research institute (e.g. TÁRKI, HÉTFA) — Evaluate whether private preparation investment has relocated to unregulated channels and whether SES-composition of admitted cohorts has shifted. (timeline: year 3-5)

**Political risks**
- Church and foundation maintainers, protected by constitutionally-backed autonomy and funding agreements, may simply decline to adopt uniform thresholds, and no concrete statutory instrument (e.g. cardinal-law entrenchment) has been specified to compel or incentivize their compliance, leaving the reform's reach confined to state schools.
- A decree-level reform can be reversed by a subsequent minister without primary legislation, giving it lower entrenchment than a statutory change and making its durability contingent on political continuity.
- Elite gimnázium directors have demonstrated coordinated collective-action capacity (documented joint petitioning) and are likely to resist score-threshold changes perceived as diluting selectivity, a mobilization risk not neutralized by consultation alone.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether SES-aware admission proxies would durably reduce family-capital-driven sorting or whether advantaged families would simply redirect investment toward new preparation or relocation strategies once the exam changes. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A discontinuity or matched-cohort analysis of admission outcomes and preparation-spending patterns before and after a pilot exam redesign, tracked over multiple admission cycles.)
- Whether church and foundation maintainers can be brought into a uniform threshold regime without a specified legal-political mechanism, given their documented autonomy guarantees and growing enrolment share. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Publication of the full text of church-state funding and autonomy accords, plus a legal opinion on the scope for mandating admission-rule compliance among non-state maintainers.)

## S2 — Supply-side contraction of the selective track (shrink the gate, not the rules)
**Goal**
Reduce the state-sector share of 6/8-year places via score thresholds, capacity caps, or demographically-driven consolidation to shrink the selective track's footprint, while leaving admission criteria within remaining places largely unchanged.

**Mechanism**
- State school district centres (Klebelsberg centre system) formalize minimum-score thresholds or capacity caps for state-maintained 6/8-year places, mechanically reducing the state-sector selective footprint. [evidence: weak]
- Church and foundation gimnázium enrolment share has grown from roughly 10% (2010) to approximately 25-30% (2022) of secondary enrolment, indicating a documented historical pattern of non-state expansion that would plausibly offset state-sector contraction absent specific countermeasures. [evidence: strong]
- Shrinking state-sector selective capacity may incrementally improve peer composition in urban general-school upper grades by retaining some higher-achieving students who would otherwise have exited via selection. [evidence: weak]

**Evidence status**
weak — No study directly tests whether shrinking state-sector selective capacity reduces net sorting rather than displacing it, and the scenario's own baseline (roughly 8-12% of a cohort enters 6/8-year tracks, not 40%) is far smaller than earlier drafts implied, so the achievable contraction magnitude is itself weakly specified.

**Assumptions**
- State-sector score thresholds or capacity caps can shrink selective provision without being fully offset by church/foundation or private-sector expansion, despite the documented historical growth of non-state selective enrolment share over the past decade. [assumption]
- Church and foundation maintainers can be regulated or incentivized not to expand selective places to absorb displaced demand, though no evidence-based pathway for such regulation or incentive design currently exists in the record. [assumption]
- Reducing state-sector selective capacity will translate into genuinely reduced overall selectivity rather than simply concentrating the same selectivity intensity into fewer, more competitive state places. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- May reduce the absolute number of children exposed to high-stakes early sorting within the state sector specifically, though the cohort-share baseline being reduced is roughly 8-12%, not a larger figure. [evidence: weak]
- Could reduce fiscal exposure to maintaining flat-capacity elite tracks amid demographic contraction, aligning selective-place supply with a shrinking cohort rather than holding capacity artificially high relative to demand. [evidence: weak]
- Does not require the multi-year statutory phase-out process (felmenő rendszer) that full structural abolition would trigger, since capacity caps and thresholds can operate within existing legal instruments. [evidence: moderate]

**Equity impact**
Correctly anticipated to intensify SES-skew within the remaining, more competitive state-sector selective places, but the primary countervailing displacement pathway is now made explicit: church and foundation maintainers, whose selective enrolment share has grown roughly threefold since 2010 and who are legally free to select students without catchment constraints, are structurally positioned to absorb displaced advantaged demand. This means supply-side contraction in the state sector alone is likely to relocate rather than reduce net selectivity and net segregation, unless paired with equivalent constraints on non-state maintainers — for which no current legal or political mechanism exists in the record.

**Cost categories**
- Compensatory funding or capacity controls for displaced demand: flagged as medium-to-high, multi-year, and currently unbudgeted, with no numeric estimate available and no modelling yet of the timing mismatch between state-sector contraction and any offsetting non-state regulation.
- Administrative cost of designing and enforcing thresholds/caps across ~200 state-maintained selective schools, unquantified but likely lower than full structural reform given reliance on existing tankerület governance structures.
- Potential transition costs if capacity reduction triggers teacher redundancy or redeployment in affected state schools, magnitude unpublished and contingent on the pace of demographic-driven consolidation already underway independent of this policy.

**Implementation steps**
- Klebelsberg Center (state school district system) — Formalize minimum-score thresholds and/or capacity caps for state-maintained 6/8-year places, informed by regional demographic forecasts. (timeline: year 1-2)
- Ministry of Education — Design and propose regulatory or fiscal levers (e.g. adjusted supplementary normative grants) aimed at discouraging offsetting expansion by church/foundation maintainers, while acknowledging the constitutional and contractual limits on compelling such maintainers. (timeline: year 2-4)
- Independent research institute — Monitor whether displaced demand migrates to church/foundation/private schools and measure net change in overall (not just state-sector) selective-track segregation. (timeline: year 2-6)

**Political risks**
- The scenario's core lever — discouraging church/foundation expansion — has no evidence-based enforcement pathway; church schools enjoy constitutionally-backed funding agreements and autonomy guarantees that make direct regulation politically and possibly legally costly.
- State-sector capacity reduction may be framed by opposition or parent groups as unilaterally disadvantaging state-school families relative to those who can access church/foundation alternatives, creating an equity-optics backlash distinct from the policy's intended equity goal.
- Demographic contraction is already driving informal, tankerület-level consolidation; layering an explicit contraction policy on top may create attribution confusion (is a closure demographic or policy-driven?) that complicates political accountability and could be exploited by either side of the debate.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether state-sector capacity contraction produces a net reduction in selective-track segregation or is substantially offset by church/foundation/private expansion, given the documented threefold growth in non-state selective share since 2010. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A multi-year tracking study of enrolment shifts across all maintainer types following a state-sector capacity policy change, using disaggregated KSH data by programme length and maintainer.)
- Whether any regulatory or fiscal lever could legally and politically constrain church/foundation maintainer expansion given their constitutionally-backed autonomy and funding agreements. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Legal opinion or Constitutional Court guidance on the permissible scope of fiscal/regulatory levers applied to non-state maintainers, plus publication of the underlying church-state financing accords.)

## S3 — Structural delay of the selection age (Finnish/Polish model)
**Goal**
Raise the statutory age of first academic tracking (e.g., to 14+ or later), phasing out or converting 6- and 8-year entry points into a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase, paired with explicit anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment.

**Mechanism**
- Delaying first tracking from age 10-12 to 14-15, as in Poland's 1999 reform, is associated with test-score gains, especially for lower-ability and vocational-track students, though two-thirds of Poland's gain was attributed to increased resources and improved peer composition rather than the delayed-tracking mechanism itself. [evidence: moderate]
- Delaying selection without simultaneous, enforceable anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment risks the socio-economic gradient re-manifesting at the later gatekeeping point, as suggested by the OECD's 2011 Poland re-evaluation. [evidence: moderate]
- Finland's peruskoulu reform's equity gains are not attributable to abolition of early selection alone but were bundled with a major, decades-long teacher-education professionalization investment (5-year Master's, ~10-15% acceptance) that Hungary has not undertaken and would need years to replicate. [evidence: strong]

**Evidence status**
moderate — Poland's 1999 reform evidence is tagged moderate in the curated registry, not strong, because subsequent OECD re-evaluation and the 2017 politically-driven reversal complicate a clean causal read, and no Hungarian natural experiment has directly tested a tracking-age delay.

**Assumptions**
- Sorting does not simply migrate from formal track assignment to school choice, catchment, or private-tutoring channels to the point of neutralizing the equity gain, provided anti-sorting rules are designed, enacted, and enforced concurrently with the structural delay — a co-requirement, not an optional add-on. [assumption]
- Hungary's teacher workforce, currently aging (half aged 50+, only 7.2% under 30) and mid-way through an unrelated multi-year salary reform (2024-2031), can absorb a comprehensive lower-secondary transition without a Finland-scale, decade-plus teacher-professionalization investment being completed first. [assumption]
- Statutory anti-sorting rules for choice and catchment can be made legally and politically enforceable in Hungary's decentralized admissions system, given that free school choice (szabad iskolaválasztás) is a statutorily entrenched, distinctive Hungarian feature not easily narrowed without explicit re-legislation. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- International evidence (Poland, cross-country meta-analysis of 21 PISA/TIMSS cycles across 75 countries) associates delayed tracking with reduced socio-economic achievement gaps without lowering mean performance. [evidence: strong]
- OECD explicitly identifies later tracking as a system feature associated with resilience and equity preservation in high-performing, high-equity PISA systems. [evidence: strong]
- Would reduce exposure to the documented big-fish-little-pond effect and performance-goal-orientation pressures associated with early ranking and labeling, per robustly replicated educational-psychology findings, though the Hungary-specific magnitude of this benefit is unmeasured. [evidence: moderate]

**Equity impact**
The scenario's central equity claim — that delaying selection will yield Poland/Finland-style gains — is now explicitly treated as conditional, not automatic: it depends entirely on contemporaneous, enforceable anti-sorting rules for school choice, catchment, and admissions discretion being designed, legislated, and operationally monitored alongside the age-delay itself. Absent such enforcement, Hungary's existing free-school-choice architecture and growing church/foundation selective capacity provide ready alternative channels through which SES-driven sorting could re-manifest at or before the new, later gatekeeping age — mirroring the pattern the OECD's 2011 Poland re-evaluation flagged. The equity benefit should therefore be read as achievable only under a specific, harder-to-implement joint design, not as a near-automatic consequence of raising the tracking age.

**Cost categories**
- Early diagnostic and remedial support for the extended comprehensive phase: no aggregate fiscal range published; magnitude unknown.
- Teacher retraining and reallocation for a comprehensive lower-secondary model: no per-teacher retraining-cost estimate available; feasibility is directly constrained by the documented aging workforce (half aged 50+) and concurrent salary-reform bandwidth absorption through 2031.
- Facility conversion for merging 6/8-year entry-point buildings into a unified comprehensive network: unquantified; likely offsettable in part by demographic-driven consolidation already underway independent of this policy, but concurrent conversion adds coordination cost.
- Anti-sorting enforcement infrastructure (monitoring school choice and catchment compliance): no cost estimate available; flagged as a load-bearing, currently unbudgeted requirement without which the scenario's equity claim does not hold.

**Implementation steps**
- Parliament (Országgyűlés) — Amend the Nkt. (Nemzeti köznevelési törvény) to raise the statutory first-tracking age, triggering the felmenő rendszer (ascending-system) principle that no currently enrolled cohort can be forced out. (timeline: year 1)
- Ministry of Education, jointly with Parliament — Legislate concurrent, enforceable anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment as a co-requirement of the tracking-age delay, not a later add-on. (timeline: year 1-2)
- Ministry of Education and teacher-training institutions — Begin a phased teacher-professionalization and reallocation programme, explicitly sequenced around the constraints of an aging workforce and the ongoing 2024-2031 salary reform rather than assuming parallel capacity. (timeline: year 2-10)
- State school district centres and maintainers (state, church, foundation) — Convert existing 6/8-year entry-point school buildings into a unified comprehensive lower-secondary network as cohorts age up under the felmenő rendszer. (timeline: year 3-9)

**Political risks**
- Church-maintained schools, protected by autonomy and funding-agreement guarantees, may resist conversion of their 6/8-year tracks into a comprehensive model, and no legally-tested mechanism for compelling their conversion (short of full Nkt. amendment with multi-party buy-in) currently exists.
- Poland's 2017 reversal of its own successful 1999 delayed-tracking reform, driven explicitly by political values rather than evidence, demonstrates that even a well-evidenced structural delay reform is not durable against a change of government; no cardinal-law or cross-party entrenchment mechanism is specified in this scenario to prevent an analogous Hungarian reversal.
- Elite gimnázium directors and a segment of parents (per the June 2026 controversy) have already mobilized against framings of reform as 'communist-style imposition'; a structural delay proposal is the most extreme version of that framing risk and would likely trigger the strongest version of this backlash.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment can be made simultaneously enforceable with the structural delay, or whether enactment will lag, allowing sorting to migrate before enforcement catches up. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Publication of a concrete legislative package pairing the tracking-age delay with named, operational anti-sorting enforcement mechanisms and named monitoring bodies, plus early-implementation compliance data.)
- Whether Hungary's teacher workforce, given its current age structure and the concurrent 2024-2031 salary reform, can realistically absorb a comprehensive lower-secondary transition within the 2-9 year timeline proposed, or whether this timeline is infeasible given documented workforce constraints. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A detailed teacher-workforce feasibility study modelling retirement/attrition rates, retraining capacity, and salary-reform bandwidth overlap, published before legislative enactment.)
- Whether the observed Polish 1999 test-score gains would replicate in Hungary's specific institutional context (school-choice system, maintainer plurality, teacher labor market), given that no Hungarian natural experiment has directly tested a tracking-age delay. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A staggered, regional natural experiment or pilot phase-in of delayed tracking in a subset of Hungarian districts, with matched administrative-data outcome tracking against non-pilot regions.)

## S4 — Non-structural compensatory reform (leave selection age unchanged)
**Goal**
Pursue Portugal-style equity and achievement gains through curriculum clarity, targeted (TEIP-style) funding for disadvantaged schools, retention reduction, and school-quality levelling, without changing the 10-12 selection age or track structure.

**Mechanism**
- Portugal's 2006-2015 PISA improvement occurred via curriculum-standards alignment, school-clustering reform, TEIP targeted support, and retention-rate reduction, without changing the selection age, suggesting resourcing and organization — not selection-age — may be a controllable determinant of equity and efficacy. [evidence: moderate]
- Portugal's TEIP targeted-support programme shows mixed results: early school-leaving rates fell faster in TEIP schools 2007-2011, but achievement gaps in mathematics and Portuguese widened, and TEIP schools remain above national averages for retention and dropout. [evidence: moderate]
- Removing early academic tracking's counterfactual — i.e. leaving tracking in place while pursuing non-structural reform — risks that admission-discretion and school-choice segregation, which is documented as structurally separable from formal tracking, continues to drive the majority of Hungary's segregation regardless of compensatory investment. [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
moderate — Portugal's non-structural approach is moderately evidenced for its 2006-2015 PISA gains but is contradicted by Portugal's own 2015-2022 equity worsening despite the same non-selective structure, indicating the mechanism is context- and time-dependent, not a settled model.

**Assumptions**
- TEIP-style targeted funding commitments will be durable and protected from fiscal consolidation pressure, despite the scenario's own political-risk analysis and the synthesis record citing a projected 6.2% deficit and rising debt that typically make targeted, non-universal funding lines among the first-line budget cuts. [assumption]
- Non-structural reforms (curriculum clarity, retention reduction, school-quality levelling) can meaningfully improve equity outcomes even while the admission-discretion and school-choice segregation channels — documented as the primary driver of Hungarian inter-school inequality — remain fully intact and unaddressed. [assumption]
- Portugal's grade-retention substitution effect (26.6-31.2% of 15-year-olds retained at least once) will not be replicated as an unintended consequence in Hungary, where retention rates are currently below OECD average (~6% vs ~9%), if compensatory reform inadvertently increases reliance on retention as a sorting mechanism. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Portugal's model demonstrates PISA improvement is achievable via resourcing and organizational reform without changing selection age, offering a lower-political-risk pathway than structural reform. [evidence: moderate]
- Portugal's TEIP schools showed faster reduction in early school-leaving rates 2007-2011 relative to non-TEIP public schools, suggesting targeted funding can move at least some outcome measures even without structural change. [evidence: moderate]
- Avoids the multi-year felmenő rendszer phase-out process, maintainer-consent negotiations, and teacher-reallocation burden that structural alternatives (S2, S3) would trigger, making it the fastest-to-implement scenario within existing legal instruments. [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
Plausibly equity-neutral-to-negative if pursued in isolation: non-structural reforms leave the admission-discretion and free-school-choice architecture — documented as the primary driver of Hungary's EU-highest segregation — fully intact, meaning high-SES families retain undiminished capacity to sort into selective 6/8-year tracks via preparation investment while disadvantaged schools receive compensatory resources that Portugal's own 2015-2022 experience shows can coexist with worsening equity trends (low-SES high-performer share fell from 21.0% to 16.0%). The scenario also risks a specific distributional failure documented in the record but not previously named here: low-SES high-performing pupils, who would benefit most from either admission-equity protection (S1) or delayed gatekeeping (S3), receive no structural protection against being screened out at the existing 10-12 age gate, so compensatory funding for their home schools does not substitute for access to the selective track itself.

**Cost categories**
- TEIP-style targeted funding for disadvantaged schools: Portugal's programme runs at roughly EUR 100 million annually for 15+ years; Hungary-specific per-school or per-pupil cost has not been estimated in the record.
- Retention-reduction measures: Portugal's own EUR 112 million two-year saving from retention reduction is cited as evidence one element can be net-cost-saving, but this figure has not been translated into a Hungary-specific net-cost estimate accounting for Hungary's already-below-OECD-average retention rate (~6% vs ~9%), which limits the scale of comparable savings available.
- School-quality levelling (resource and teacher-quality equalization across schools): magnitude unquantified; likely the largest and most fiscally exposed category given documented teacher shortages concentrated in disadvantaged schools.

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education — Design a TEIP-style targeted funding formula for disadvantaged schools, informed by Portugal's mixed results and Hungary's fiscal-consolidation context. (timeline: year 1)
- Ministry of Education and Parliament — Seek statutory (cardinal-law) protection for the targeted funding floor, explicitly addressing the documented risk that targeted lines are typically first-line fiscal-consolidation cuts. (timeline: year 1-2)
- Schools and local education authorities — Implement curriculum-clarity and retention-reduction measures, monitored against Hungary's already-below-OECD-average retention baseline to detect any unintended shift toward retention-based sorting. (timeline: year 1-4)
- Independent research institute — Track whether compensatory investment measurably narrows achievement gaps for low-SES high-performing pupils specifically, distinguishing this from general achievement-gap trends. (timeline: year 2-6)

**Political risks**
- Targeted funding lines are documented as likely first-line cuts under Hungary's projected 6.2% deficit and rising debt, making the cardinal-law entrenchment step politically difficult to secure and, even if secured, potentially subject to future renegotiation.
- Because this scenario leaves the visible, politically salient selection structure untouched, it may be perceived by equity-focused stakeholders as an insufficient response to a June 2026 controversy that explicitly framed the selection mechanism itself as the problem, risking a credibility gap with the reform coalition that formed around that debate.
- Portugal's own 2015-2022 equity worsening despite an intact non-structural, non-selective model provides a ready counter-narrative for critics arguing that non-structural reform alone is insufficient, undermining the scenario's political durability if outcomes lag expectations.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether TEIP-style targeted funding can be made durable against fiscal consolidation pressure in Hungary's specific budget environment, or whether it will be cut in the first consolidation round as historical patterns for targeted lines suggest. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A published multi-year budget commitment with cardinal-law or equivalent statutory protection, tracked against actual disbursement through at least one fiscal-consolidation cycle.)
- Whether non-structural reform can measurably narrow achievement gaps for low-SES high-performing pupils specifically, given that this subgroup's primary barrier (exclusion from the selective track at age 10-12) is left entirely unaddressed by compensatory funding to their home schools. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A longitudinal subgroup analysis tracking low-SES high-performing pupils' access to selective tracks and achievement trajectories before and after targeted-funding implementation, compared against a matched cohort under S1-style admission-equity reform.)

## S5 — Demographically-driven passive contraction (status-quo baseline)
**Goal**
Serve as the no-new-policy baseline in which shrinking birth cohorts force network consolidation and a natural decline in the selective track's absolute size and share regardless of any active equity or structural intervention, against which S1-S4 are measured.

**Mechanism**
- Annual births fell from ~125,000 (early 1990s) to 85-95,000 (early 2020s), with 2024 figures around 77,500 and 2025 estimated near 72,000; this mechanically shrinks demand for all secondary tracks including 6/8-year gimnázium regardless of selection-policy design, though the curated demography record frames the multi-decade trend (30-35% cohort collapse) as the strong-evidence claim, while the most recent single-year figures carry more measurement and projection uncertainty. [evidence: strong]
- System infrastructure calibrated for ~110,000 annual cohorts will need to serve only ~72,000 within a few years, forcing school-network consolidation decisions independent of any early-selection reform. [evidence: moderate]
- Passive demographic contraction is not policy-neutral in its equity effects: it interacts with active administrative levers already in use, including score-threshold and capacity-cap mechanisms (S2) and consolidation decisions currently proceeding informally through tankerület-level administrative action rather than announced central policy. [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
strong — The multi-decade demographic collapse trend is strongly evidenced by KSH data, but the scenario's precise recent-year birth figures (77,500 in 2024, ~72,000 in 2025) carry more uncertainty than the underlying strong-evidence trend, and the claim that this contraction is policy-neutral is contested by the scenario's own acknowledgment of interacting active levers.

**Assumptions**
- Cohort-decline projections through 2040 will not be reversed by a major unforeseen fertility rebound or policy-driven immigration surge. [assumption]
- Demographic contraction operates as a genuinely passive baseline force distinct from active policy choices, even though the record documents the government already using score thresholds (S2) and administrative consolidation decisions (proceeding via tankerület action) that actively interact with and shape how demographic pressure translates into network and equity outcomes — meaning 'passive' describes the demographic input, not the full policy environment it operates within. [assumption]
- Consolidation without deliberate equity safeguards will not systematically worsen SES sorting by concentrating selective capacity into fewer, more competitive, and more advantaged-skewed sites, even though the school-network-planning record explicitly flags this as a likely risk absent intervention. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Requires no new legislation, decree, or political capital expenditure, making it the only scenario with zero implementation political risk in the near term. [evidence: strong]
- Mechanically reduces the absolute number and share of children in the selective track over time simply as a function of shrinking cohorts, independent of any deliberate equity design. [evidence: strong]
- Provides a natural, policy-independent counterfactual baseline against which the incremental equity or efficiency contribution of S1-S4 can in principle be measured. [evidence: moderate]

**Equity impact**
Framing passive demographic contraction as politically neutral or as simply 'the path of least resistance' is itself contestable: the record documents that consolidation is already proceeding through active administrative channels (tankerület decisions, score-threshold and capacity-cap levers under S2) that shape whether shrinkage concentrates or disperses selectivity. Absent explicit equity safeguards, consolidation is documented as likely to concentrate selective capacity into fewer, more competitive, and more advantaged-skewed sites — worsening rather than passively resolving SES sorting — while extreme regional divergence in birth-rate decline (some districts down to 55-67 annual births) means uniform national assumptions about this baseline's equity trajectory are themselves unreliable across regions.

**Cost categories**
- Consolidation-related transition costs (building repurposing, staff redundancy or redeployment) proceeding informally via tankerület decisions, with no coordinated public costing or agenda despite union and government acknowledgment that consolidation is 'nearly certain' within 5-10 years.
- Foregone equity-investment opportunity cost: absent deliberate design, funds that could support integration safeguards during consolidation are not earmarked, though no specific budget line is documented as forgone versus simply not yet allocated.
- Regional-divergence management costs: extreme variation in birth-decline rates across districts implies uneven consolidation burdens that a purely passive approach does not allocate resources to address.

**Implementation steps**
- State school district centres (tankerület), by default rather than mandate — Continue current informal, case-by-case consolidation decisions (mergers, closures) as enrollment falls, without a coordinated national equity framework. (timeline: ongoing, year 1-10)
- KSH and Ministry of Education (monitoring only) — Continue publishing demographic and enrollment statistics without an accompanying policy-response framework. (timeline: ongoing)
- Independent researchers (if commissioned) — Track whether informal, demographically-driven consolidation is concentrating or dispersing selective-track segregation across regions, absent any government-initiated equity-safeguard design. (timeline: year 1-5)

**Political risks**
- Framing passive contraction as the 'path of least resistance' understates that the government is already exercising active administrative levers (score thresholds under S2, tankerület consolidation decisions) that shape equity outcomes; treating S5 as purely passive risks obscuring accountability for these active choices.
- No coordinated political agenda for managing consolidation exists despite union and government acknowledgment of its near-certainty within 5-10 years, creating a governance vacuum that could produce ad hoc, inconsistent, and potentially inequitable local decisions without central accountability.
- Extreme regional divergence in birth-rate decline means a purely passive national baseline will produce very different local outcomes (some districts facing near-collapse, others milder contraction), risking politically salient regional inequity claims without any policy mechanism to address them.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether Hungary's fertility rate will stabilize around 1.2-1.3 or continue falling below 1.1, directly affecting whether cohort size bottoms out near 72,000 or falls further toward 60,000 annually by 2035. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Longitudinal fertility surveys (five-year updates to Eurostat projections) and direct cohort-component demographic modelling with scenario branching.)
- Whether demographically-driven consolidation, absent explicit equity safeguards, will concentrate or disperse selective-track segregation across regions, given documented extreme regional divergence in birth-rate decline. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Detailed school-by-school enrollment forecasts and feasibility studies of mixed school models in low-density regions, plus segregation-index tracking before, during, and after consolidation.)
