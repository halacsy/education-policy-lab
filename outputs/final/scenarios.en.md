# Policy scenarios

## S1 — Admission reform within the current structure
**Goal**
Reduce the SES skew of 6/8-year gimnazium intake by standardising and de-coaching the admission process, while leaving the track structure itself in place.

**Mechanism**
- Centrally standardised admission assessment replaces teacher-level gatekeeping, reducing information-asymmetry advantages of high-SES families [evidence: moderate]
- A lottery weight applied above a qualifying threshold dampens the marginal return to test-preparation, lowering the coaching arms race — conditional on the lottery weight being set large enough to matter (a design variable fixed in step 2, not left free) [evidence: weak]
- Because entry advantage is largely a selection effect rather than value-added, admitting a broader SES mix does not reduce the value-added the tracks deliver [evidence: moderate]
- Maintainer-uniform admission rules prevent selective intake from migrating to church/foundation schools that are exempt from the reform [evidence: contested]

**Evidence status**
moderate overall — the SES-skew diagnosis is strongly evidenced, but the causal effect of admission redesign on composition is inferential and the anti-migration claim is contested.

**Assumptions**
- The 8–12% intake figure reflects a stable recent cohort [assumption]
- SES classification / coaching-resistance can be audited with ≥85% accuracy (the kisgyerekes_szuloi support condition) [assumption]
- Independent evaluation capacity insulated from political pressure exists in Hungary [assumption]
- A lottery-plus-threshold design is legally compatible with Nkt. admission provisions [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- More socio-economically representative intake into selective tracks [evidence: moderate]
- Lower teacher-level gatekeeping bias via central standardisation [evidence: moderate]
- Reduced test-preparation arms race — NOTE: weakest-evidenced benefit in this list, contingent on lottery weight [evidence: weak]
- No loss of value-added for admitted pupils, since the raw advantage is mostly selection [evidence: moderate]

**Equity impact**
Directly helps high-ability low-SES pupils who would otherwise be screened out; however, the non-selected ~88–92% majority sees NO direct benefit, since the track structure and peer-sorting of standard schools are untouched. Against the mission's equity criterion this is a narrow, targeted gain, not a system-wide equity improvement.

**Cost categories**
- One-off: design and validation of central assessment instrument (order-of-magnitude: low tens of HUF billion) [evidence: weak]
- Recurring: independent audit of SES-classification accuracy (modest annual) [evidence: weak]
- Political capital: moderate, front-loaded during the maintainer-uniformity negotiation

**Implementation steps**
- Oktatási Hivatal — design and pilot the standardised central assessment in 3–4 districts (timeline: year 1-2)
- Ministry + legislature — fix the lottery weight and qualifying threshold in regulation, closing the free-variable gap (timeline: year 2)
- Independent evaluator — publish SES-classification accuracy against the ≥85% threshold before scale-up (timeline: year 2-3)
- Ministry — negotiate and legislate maintainer-uniform admission rules covering church/foundation schools (timeline: year 2-4)
- Oktatási Hivatal — national rollout conditional on pilot passing the accuracy gate (timeline: year 4-5)

**Political risks**
- Maintainer-exemption risk (UNDERSTATED if unaddressed): if church schools are exempt, S1 creates an incentive to shift selective intake there, worsening the very sorting it targets — mitigation is the step-4 maintainer-uniform rule, without which the risk is severe
- Urban middle-class resistance to any perceived dilution of gimnazium prestige
- Pilot may fail the ≥85% accuracy gate, stalling rollout

**Uncertainties**
- What share of high-SES sorting reflects genuine merit vs. test-prep/information access? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: value-added or randomised admission study]
- Elasticity of non-state selective intake if state selectivity is constrained [confidence: low — would be reduced by: administrative data linkage tracking student flows, or pre-reform survey of non-state maintainer intentions]
- Whether the named ≥85% classification threshold is a dealbreaker or a negotiating position [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: structured interviews with kisgyerekes_szuloi leadership]

## S2 — Gradual phase-down of 6/8-year entry places
**Goal**
Shrink the selective-entry share of each cohort over a decade via declining annual caps, redirecting freed capacity toward strengthening general-school lower-secondary programmes — with an explicit implementation step securing the political capital the freed capacity requires.

**Mechanism**
- Annually declining entry caps reduce the fraction of the cohort sorted early, moving Hungary toward the later-tracking configuration associated with equity gains [evidence: moderate]
- A felmenő rendszer (phase-in) guarantee protects enrolled cohorts, lowering legal and parental resistance [evidence: strong]
- Advanced/enriched programmes inside general schools retain ambitious families who would otherwise exit — NOTE: this rests only on expert inference plus US magnet-school analogues that are NOT verified for Hungary [evidence: weak]

**Evidence status**
moderate-to-weak overall — the tracking-timing equity link is moderately evidenced, but the family-retention mechanism is weakly evidenced and relies on non-Hungarian analogues.

**Assumptions**
- Declining caps are legally implementable under Nkt. with adequate notice periods [assumption]
- Freed capacity is actually reinvested in strong programmes rather than absorbed as savings [assumption]
- Non-state maintainers can be bound so selective demand does not simply migrate [assumption]
- A dedicated planning authority exists to manage split-timetable mixed cohorts [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Progressive reduction in early sorting without abrupt structural shock [evidence: moderate]
- Preserved continuity for enrolled pupils via felmenő rendszer [evidence: strong]
- Retention of ambitious families in general schools via advanced programmes [evidence: weak]
- Smoother alignment with demographic contraction of cohorts [evidence: moderate]

**Equity impact**
Gradually widens the pool exposed to mixed-ability lower-secondary, benefiting a growing share of each cohort over the phase-down decade; but if non-state selective intake absorbs displaced demand, the equity gain is redistributed away from state general schools rather than realised.

**Cost categories**
- Recurring: reinvestment into general-school advanced programmes (scales with pace of phase-down) [evidence: weak]
- Transitional: split-timetable planning and administration for mixed cohorts over ~a decade [evidence: weak]
- Political capital: HIGH and SUSTAINED — must be actively secured, not assumed (see step 1)

**Implementation steps**
- Government + legislature — enact a multi-party pact / legislative lock explicitly securing sustained political capital and cap trajectory against reversal (timeline: year 1)
- Ministry — publish the declining annual cap schedule with felmenő rendszer guarantees (timeline: year 1)
- Klebelsberg Centre — designate a named planning authority to manage split timetables for schools with mixed cohorts (timeline: year 1-2)
- Ministry — negotiate binding non-state maintainer caps to prevent demand migration (timeline: year 1-3)
- Tankerület — stand up advanced/enriched programmes in general schools as caps decline (timeline: year 2-10)

**Political risks**
- Reversal risk — MITIGATION ADDED: the step-1 legislative lock / multi-party pact is the design feature that raises the cost of a successor government freezing the cap trajectory; without it reversal is cheap (cf. Poland 2016–2019)
- Non-state migration undermining the freed-capacity logic
- Sustained-high political-capital demand exhausting government appetite mid-phase

**Uncertainties**
- Do advanced in-school programmes actually retain ambitious families in the Hungarian context? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: Hungarian pilot tracking family enrolment choices, since magnet-school evidence is US-specific and unverified here]
- Elasticity of non-state selective intake under declining state caps [confidence: low — would be reduced by: administrative student-flow linkage in a comparable jurisdiction]
- Durability of a multi-party pact against electoral turnover [confidence: low — would be reduced by: analysis of what institutional locks preserved Poland's 1999–2015 gains]

## S3 — Comprehensive school to age 14 (structural reform)
**Goal**
Delay between-school selection to age 14 by converting lower secondary to mixed-ability comprehensive provision, bundled with teacher and support investment.

**Mechanism**
- Delaying selection to age 14+ reduces SES-driven outcome variance, per cross-national tracking evidence [evidence: strong]
- Mixed-ability grouping exposes lower-SES pupils to high-achieving peers, whose effect is assumed asymmetric (helping non-selected pupils more than it costs selected pupils) — this asymmetry is the LOAD-BEARING claim of S3; if peer effects are symmetric, S3 redistributes learning rather than creating it [evidence: contested]
- Equity gains depend on concurrent teacher-quality and diagnostic-support upgrades, without which within-class variance rises and middle-class flight follows [evidence: moderate]
- Choice and catchment redesign is required alongside track removal, or high-SES families re-sort through neighbourhood and non-state channels [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
contested overall — the delayed-selection equity effect is strongly evidenced internationally, but the asymmetric-peer-effect assumption it depends on for Hungary is contested and transfer is unverified.

**Assumptions**
- Peer effects are asymmetric rather than zero-sum [assumption — this is the contested load-bearing assumption; if false, S3's core benefit collapses]
- A published 10-year teacher-supply forecast shows stabilisation before national rollout (pedagogus_erdekvedo condition) [assumption]
- Differentiation-retraining throughput is achievable despite the shortage being worst in the schools that most need it [assumption — capacity asserted, not shown]
- Choice and catchment rules are reformed concurrently [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Reduced SES gradient in outcomes (0.15–0.25 SD equity gain range in comparable systems) [evidence: moderate]
- Broader peer exposure for previously non-selected pupils [evidence: contested]
- No mean-performance loss where late-tracking evidence holds [evidence: moderate]
- Reduced rural-network hollowing from early cream-skimming [evidence: moderate]

**Equity impact**
Potentially the largest system-wide equity gain of the four, reaching the whole cohort rather than a selected slice; BUT becomes equity-NEGATIVE past a middle-class-exit threshold — proposed decision threshold: if state-general-school enrolment of the top-SES quartile falls below ~two-thirds of its pre-reform level within three cohorts, the reform is redistributing exit rather than integrating, and should be paused. Below that threshold it is equity-positive.

**Cost categories**
- One-off: nationwide differentiation retraining — HIGH; published order-of-magnitude range requested by education_finance not yet available, pending pedagogical-development budget analysis (placeholder: high tens of HUF billion) [evidence: weak]
- One-off: school-network reconfiguration — HIGH; order-of-magnitude pending national infrastructure audit (placeholder: high tens–low hundreds of HUF billion) [evidence: weak]
- Recurring: diagnostic / remedial support systems (Finnish-style) [evidence: moderate]
- Political capital: very high

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry — publish the 10-year teacher-supply forecast as a rollout precondition (timeline: year 1)
- Ministry — pilot comprehensive intake rebalancing in 3–4 districts with independent evaluation (timeline: year 1-5)
- Klebelsberg Centre — stage differentiation retraining, sequencing shortage-worst schools with named throughput capacity rather than assumed (timeline: year 1-4)
- Ministry — reform choice and catchment rules concurrently with track removal (timeline: year 2-4)
- Legislature — legislate binding non-state caps and teacher-protection guarantees before national rollout (timeline: year 3-5)
- Ministry — national rollout only if pilot clears SES-accuracy, teacher-supply, and middle-class-exit gates (timeline: year 6+)

**Political risks**
- Middle-class flight to non-state/private selective schools past the equity-negative threshold
- Reversal under electoral turnover despite measurable gains (Poland precedent)
- Teacher-shortage collapse in disadvantaged regions if retraining throughput is over-assumed
- Note: the framing of teacher shortage as fixed and the political window as absent are contested political judgments, not settled evidence

**Uncertainties**
- Are peer effects asymmetric or symmetric in Hungarian mixed-ability classes? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: longitudinal peer-effect studies in Hungarian lower-secondary following selection removal; this directly tests S3's load-bearing claim]
- Magnitude of the equity gain under Hungary's demographics [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: regional quasi-experimental delay of selection with pre/post tracking]
- Differentiation-retraining throughput capacity in shortage regions [confidence: low — would be reduced by: workforce-exit tracking and regional retraining-capacity audit]
- Durability of pilot findings against political turnover [confidence: low — would be reduced by: analysis of institutional locks that preserved Poland's 1999–2015 gains]

## S4 — Keep the structure, compensate general schools (Portuguese-style package)
**Goal**
Leave the 6/8-year structure in place and neutralise its equity deficit through non-structural investment (TEIP-style targeted funding, curriculum standards, school clustering) in general schools. NOTE: the premise that early selection is 'politically fixed' is itself a political judgment, not evidence; treating it as fixed forecloses options and is flagged as such.

**Mechanism**
- Targeted funding and intensive support to disadvantaged schools raise their outcomes without structural change, as in Portugal's TEIP trajectory [evidence: moderate]
- Rigorous national curriculum standards lift the floor across general schools [evidence: moderate]
- School clustering pools administrative and pedagogical capacity for small/rural schools [evidence: moderate]
- Compensation can OFFSET the segregation effect, not merely mask it — this is the load-bearing claim; NOTE: no country evidence exists at Hungary's selection age (10/12), so it is UNVERIFIABLE ex ante [evidence: weak]

**Evidence status**
moderate-to-weak overall — Portugal's non-structural gains are moderately evidenced, but the claim that compensation offsets rather than masks selection at Hungary's early age is unverifiable ex ante.

**Assumptions**
- Non-structural interventions are politically easier and more durable than structural ones [assumption]
- TEIP/curriculum mechanisms transfer to the Hungarian institutional context [assumption]
- Compensation can offset, not just mask, segregation — UNVERIFIABLE ex ante: no country evidence at Hungary's 10/12 selection age exists to confirm or refute this [assumption]
- Sustained multi-year targeted funding survives budget cycles [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Improved outcomes in disadvantaged general schools without structural disruption [evidence: moderate]
- Raised floor via national curriculum standards [evidence: moderate]
- Administrative efficiency from clustering under demographic contraction [evidence: moderate]
- Politically lower-friction than S2/S3 (contingent on the contested 'fixed structure' premise) [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
Improves absolute outcomes for the non-selected majority in disadvantaged schools, but leaves the early-sorting mechanism and its SES skew intact; equity gain is compensatory rather than structural, and its adequacy hinges on the unverifiable offset-vs-mask assumption.

**Cost categories**
- Recurring: the targeted-funding package itself — the dominant, ongoing cost [evidence: moderate]
- Recurring: curriculum-standards development and monitoring [evidence: weak]
- Fiscal sensitivity: at −30% budget (S4's own top political risk), TEIP-style targeting loses the intensity threshold Portugal relied on, and the offset mechanism degrades toward masking — S4 is highly budget-elastic [evidence: weak]
- Political capital: moderate but recurring (annual budget defence)

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry — define disadvantaged-school targeting criteria and TEIP-style intervention design (timeline: year 1)
- Legislature + Ministry of Finance — commit multi-year ring-fenced funding with a floor clause guarding against the −30% sensitivity case (timeline: year 1)
- Oktatási Hivatal — roll out national curriculum standards and monitoring (timeline: year 1-3)
- Klebelsberg Centre — implement school clustering for small/rural units (timeline: year 2-4)
- Independent evaluator — publish annual outcome tracking to test whether compensation offsets or merely masks segregation (timeline: year 2 onward)

**Political risks**
- Budget vulnerability — targeted funding is the first cut in fiscal downturns, degrading the whole mechanism (see −30% sensitivity)
- Selection skew persists, exposing the reform to the charge of legitimising rather than fixing stratification
- Legal exposure under constitutional equality-of-opportunity review remains, since the selective mechanism is untouched
- The 'structure is politically fixed' premise may be wrong, meaning a lower-cost structural window could be foreclosed unnecessarily

**Uncertainties**
- Does compensation offset or merely mask segregation at Hungary's 10/12 selection age? [confidence: low — would be reduced by: longitudinal outcome tracking of disadvantaged general schools under the package, since no analogue at this selection age exists]
- Do TEIP/curriculum mechanisms transfer given Hungarian teacher shortages? [confidence: high — would be reduced by: comparative teacher-supply and qualification studies vs. historical Portuguese levels]
- Can Hungarian budgetary commitment sustain targeted funding without reversal? [confidence: medium — would be reduced by: analysis of legislative continuity and ring-fencing precedent in Hungarian education budgets]
