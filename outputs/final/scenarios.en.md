# Policy scenarios

## S1 — Admission reform within the current 6-/8-year gimnazium structure
**Goal**
Retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium tracks but rewrite entry rules and quotas so that access to selective places is less strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families, while acknowledging that fragmented governance and school-choice sorting may blunt this goal [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics on current skew].

**Mechanism**
- Replacing the central written admission exam with a less preparation-sensitive assessment (e.g. ability/aptitude tasks, quota-adjusted scoring) weakens the advantage that paid test-prep confers on high-SES applicants [evidence: contested — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013 and Horn 2013 show raw track advantage is mostly a selection effect, which cuts against assuming a redesigned exam will change who applies or is chosen].
- Introducing SES-aware quotas or bonus points at application time reweights admission toward under-represented groups, conditional on a workable SES indicator existing [evidence: weak — no Hungarian administrative SES register usable at application is confirmed to exist].
- Reformed admission rules operate inside unchanged multi-maintainer governance, so church/foundation schools retain independent admission discretion, limiting reach of any state-mandated rule change [evidence: strong — Public education act amendments; maintainer statistics].

**Evidence status**
Mixed — the SES-skew problem is strongly evidenced, but the core mechanism (a less-coachable, SES-aware admission process) rests on weak/contested evidence and an unresolved feasibility gap (no usable SES indicator).

**Assumptions**
- A workable SES proxy indicator can be constructed and validated for use at application time [assumption — contradicted by the absence of any administrative student-level SES register usable at application; flagged as unverifiable ex ante without a pilot].
- Non-state maintainers will adopt or tolerate the reformed admission rules voluntarily [assumption].
- A redesigned assessment can be made meaningfully less coachable within the political timeline available [assumption].

**Expected benefits**
- Reduced test-preparation arms-race pressure on age-10 children insofar as the new assessment resists coaching [evidence: weak — model knowledge; this sits in tension with the political-risks item below on informal selection re-emerging].
- Modest increase in low-SES, high-ability pupils gaining access to selective seats, if the SES indicator functions as intended [evidence: weak — contingent on an indicator that does not yet exist].
- Preserves existing school brand identities and staffing, minimizing administrative disruption relative to structural reform [evidence: moderate — education_finance and implementation_planning experts confirm per-pupil cost differences are modest and disruption is lower than full restructuring].

**Equity impact**
Gains would accrue to high-ability, low-SES pupils who already apply and are reweighted into selective seats; pupils who do not apply at all (often for information or geographic reasons) see no benefit. Because a validated SES indicator does not currently exist, the equity gain is speculative and contingent on a governance and measurement capacity that has not been demonstrated. Reformed but still scarce selective channels risk informal selection (interviews, reputation, discretionary criteria) reproducing the SES skew the reform intends to fix.

**Cost categories**
- Assessment redesign, psychometric validation and multi-year piloting — cost likely higher than 'medium' given the multi-year psychometric development described; provisional label: medium-to-high, pending a costed pilot plan.
- Development and validation of an SES-proxy indicator and associated data infrastructure — high, novel system with no existing base to build from.
- Ongoing administration of a modified admission and quota system — low-to-medium, recurring.

**Implementation steps**
- Oktatási Hivatal — design and psychometrically validate a less-coachable admission assessment (timeline: year 1-3).
- Ministry of Education / statistical office — develop and pilot an SES-proxy indicator usable at application, in a limited number of districts (timeline: year 1-2).
- Legislator — amend Nkt. admission-rule provisions to permit SES-aware quotas, respecting transitional guarantees for enrolled cohorts (timeline: year 2-3).
- Oktatási Hivatal / maintainers — negotiate voluntary alignment of church/foundation admission practices with new rules (timeline: year 2-4).
- Ministry of Education — nationwide rollout of reformed admission process contingent on pilot results (timeline: year 4-5).

**Political risks**
- Reformed/scarcer selection channels may invite informal selection (interviews, reputation) that reproduces SES sorting; this is a plausible but evidence-free extrapolation from general school-choice literature, not a documented Hungarian finding [evidence: weak — no direct Hungarian evidence cited].
- Urban middle-class parents, who currently benefit from the status quo admission process, may perceive SES-aware quotas as a threat and mobilize against them, echoing historical opposition patterns [evidence: moderate — domestic press and stakeholder analyses, 1990s-2010s, political_feasibility expert].
- Non-state maintainers may simply opt out of the reformed rules, undermining uniform impact [evidence: strong — governance fragmentation is well documented].

**Uncertainties**
- Whether a valid, gameable-resistant SES proxy can be built and adopted in time for rollout [confidence: low; would be reduced by: a costed pilot generating proxy-validity and gaming-resistance data before nationwide rollout].
- Whether informal selection mechanisms would actually emerge at meaningful scale in response to reformed rules [confidence: low; would be reduced by: monitoring admission practices in early-pilot districts for interview/discretionary-criteria use].
- Whether a less-coachable assessment format exists that is also psychometrically valid at national scale [confidence: low; would be reduced by: piloting candidate assessment formats against coaching-sensitivity metrics].

## S2 — Gradual phase-down of 6-/8-year entry places
**Goal**
Reduce over time the number of seats available through 6- and 8-year gimnazium entry, shifting more of the cohort into the common track for longer, without a single abolition event.

**Mechanism**
- Capping and progressively reducing state-maintained 6-/8-year intake quotas keeps more high-achievers in the common track for longer, which may improve peer composition in urban general-school upper grades [evidence: contested — the asymmetric peer-effect mechanism cited (equity_and_social_mobility expert) is itself tagged weak/model-knowledge].
- Grandfathering enrolled cohorts under old rules while new entrants face reduced quotas limits legal exposure and honors transitional guarantees [evidence: strong — Nkt. analysis confirms multi-year notice periods and transitional guarantees are legally required].
- As state-maintained caps tighten, non-state (church/foundation) maintainers may expand their own selective intake to absorb demand, replacing rather than eliminating early selection [evidence: strong — governance fragmentation and maintainer expansion are well documented; this directly undercuts the mechanism's aggregate effect].

**Evidence status**
Mixed — the legal/administrative mechanics of grandfathered phase-down are well evidenced, but the intended equity mechanism (improved peer composition) rests on a contested, weak causal link, and the caps' aggregate effect is undermined by likely non-state offset.

**Assumptions**
- Caps on state-maintained schools are not simply offset by non-state maintainers expanding selective intake [assumption — directly contradicted by political-risk and uncertainty findings that identify this offset as a near-certain response; flagged as a load-bearing but weak assumption].
- Demand for selective places among high-SES families remains constant rather than migrating to alternative selective channels [assumption].
- A decade-long, multi-parliamentary-term implementation window can retain political ownership across changes of government [assumption].

**Expected benefits**
- Stronger peer composition in urban general-school upper grades as more high-achievers remain in the common track [evidence: contested — link is weak/model-knowledge per equity_and_social_mobility expert, and is undercut by likely non-state offset].
- Lower legal and political risk than abrupt abolition, since grandfathering respects existing enrollment guarantees [evidence: strong — Nkt. analysis on transitional guarantee requirements].
- Gradual demographic alignment: shrinking caps track Hungary's declining birth cohorts, potentially easing the school-network consolidation pressure identified independently of selection policy [evidence: moderate — demography and school_network_planning experts confirm cohort shrinkage is a structural driver regardless of policy].

**Equity impact**
As caps tighten, residual selective places become even more SES-skewed absent an S1-type admission-rule change — this residual-skew effect is mechanical and near-certain (fewer seats, same or worsening applicant skew) [evidence: strong — follows directly from KSH admission statistics on current skew]. If non-state maintainers expand to absorb displaced demand, the overall equity gain may be minimal or even negative, since non-state selective seats are outside state quota control and harder to regulate for SES balance.

**Cost categories**
- Parallel-regime administration while grandfathered cohorts run under old rules alongside capped intake — likely medium-to-high and sustained for most of the decade-long transition, not merely 'transitional,' given the scenario's own decade-long timeline.
- Monitoring and potential regulatory response to non-state maintainer expansion — medium, recurring, currently unbudgeted.
- Staff reallocation as state-maintained selective-track positions shrink — medium, one-off per phase.

**Implementation steps**
- Legislator — amend Nkt. to set a multi-year schedule of quota reductions with grandfathering guarantees for enrolled cohorts (timeline: year 1-2).
- Klebelsberg Centre (KK) / tankerulet — implement year-on-year intake caps in state-maintained schools per the legislated schedule (timeline: year 2-10).
- Ministry of Education — monitor non-state maintainer intake for offsetting expansion and assess regulatory options (timeline: year 2-5, ongoing).
- Ministry of Education — periodic (biennial) review of peer-composition and equity indicators in affected urban general schools (timeline: year 3, 5, 7, 9).

**Political risks**
- Middle-class parents perceive shrinking state-maintained seats as a threat to a mobility/insurance asset and mobilize, echoing the entrenched opposition pattern documented for prior curtailment attempts [evidence: moderate — political_feasibility expert, domestic press and stakeholder analyses 1990s-2010s].
- Non-state maintainers absorbing displaced demand could shift political conflict from the state sector to church/foundation governance disputes [evidence: strong — governance fragmentation documented; specific conflict dynamic is plausible extrapolation, not directly evidenced].
- A decade-long implementation window spans multiple electoral cycles, and the political_feasibility expert's analysis indicates no clear precedent for such reforms surviving that long in Hungary without reversal pressure [evidence: moderate — Polish reversal precedent, Jakubowski et al. 2016/OECD, cited as analogous risk].

**Uncertainties**
- Whether non-state maintainers will in fact expand selective intake to offset state caps, and at what scale [confidence: low; would be reduced by: tracking church/foundation admission numbers year-on-year following initial cap implementation].
- Whether the peer-composition benefit materializes given the underlying mechanism is tagged weak/contested [confidence: low; would be reduced by: longitudinal peer-effect studies in Hungarian primary/general schools pre- and post-cap].
- Whether political ownership of a decade-long phase-down survives changes in government [confidence: low; would be reduced by: cross-party pact commitments and monitoring of legislative continuity].

## S3 — Comprehensive school to age 14 (structural reform)
**Goal**
Abolish 6- and 8-year gimnazium entry points and postpone academic tracking until age 14-15, moving Hungary toward the delayed-tracking model associated internationally with lower SES-outcome gradients.

**Mechanism**
- Removing early selective entry points keeps all pupils in a common track through age 14, extending exposure to mixed-ability peer groups [evidence: strong — OECD/Eurydice structural comparisons confirm Hungary currently selects earlier than most OECD systems].
- Cross-country difference-in-differences evidence indicates earlier tracking increases outcome inequality without raising mean performance, implying the reverse (delaying tracking) should reduce inequality without a mean-performance cost [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006, though this is a cross-country generalization, not a Hungary-specific estimate].
- A weaker SES-outcome gradient can emerge within a decade via broader access to high-achieving peers [evidence: contested — the asymmetric peer-effect mechanism is tagged weak/contested by the equity_and_social_mobility expert, undercutting the confidence of the equity-gradient claim].
- Structural abolition alone does not eliminate sorting: free school choice combined with maintainer plurality can relocate selection to inter-school choice or non-state enrollment rather than removing it [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013; Berenyi-Berkovits-Erőss school choice studies].

**Evidence status**
Mixed and internally tense — the cross-national case for delayed tracking reducing inequality without harming mean performance is strong, but the Hungary-specific equity mechanism (peer effects) is weak/contested, and the scenario's own equity_impact section identifies a major migration risk that partly offsets the mechanism's promise.

**Assumptions**
- Sorting does not simply migrate to school choice between general schools and to non-state maintainers [assumption — directly contradicted by this scenario's own equity_impact assessment, which identifies exactly this migration as its largest risk; retained here as an assumption that is known to be in tension with the evidence, not resolved].
- Political ownership of the reform survives two electoral cycles [assumption — contradicted by the political_feasibility expert's position that no such durability window currently exists in Hungary; retained as an unresolved tension].
- Teacher training and support infrastructure in comprehensive schools can be upgraded fast enough to handle increased heterogeneity, per the Finnish precedent [assumption — the finnish_reform expert explicitly flags Hungary as currently lacking Finland's equivalent teacher quality and support systems].

**Expected benefits**
- A weaker SES-outcome gradient within a decade via broader access to high-achieving peers [evidence: moderate on cross-country pattern generally, but contested/weak on the specific Hungarian peer-effect mechanism — evidence status intentionally mixed, not upgraded].
- No demonstrated loss of mean academic performance from delaying tracking, per cross-national comparison [evidence: moderate — cross-country PISA/DiD literature, general pattern known but exact effect sizes not available for Hungary].
- Alleviation of demographically driven rural school-network collapse, since abolishing selective tracks stops draining top performers from local schools into urban selective hubs [evidence: moderate — demography and school_network_planning experts].

**Equity impact**
This scenario carries the largest expected aggregate equity gain of the four scenarios, but also the largest risk: middle-class and church/foundation-school flight relocates sorting to inter-school choice and non-state enrollment rather than removing it. That exit does not necessarily harm the exiting families and may reconcentrate disadvantage in the remaining public comprehensive schools, potentially reproducing much of the current SES gradient in a different institutional form. The net equity effect therefore depends on choice-architecture and maintainer-transparency reforms accompanying the structural change — reforms not guaranteed by this scenario alone.

**Cost categories**
- Nationwide teacher retraining in differentiated instruction for increased classroom heterogeneity — high; no precise per-cohort/per-district unit cost is available, and the absence of a costed figure is itself a material planning risk rather than a neutral gap.
- Facility and curriculum re-adaptation (converting gimnazium infrastructure and re-timetabling) — high, front-loaded in initial budget cycles per education_finance expert.
- Legal/administrative transition costs (multi-year notice periods, grandfathering enrolled cohorts) — medium-to-high, mandated by Nkt. provisions.

**Implementation steps**
- Legislator — amend Nkt. to remove the legal basis for 6-/8-year entry selection, with multi-year notice and grandfathering provisions (timeline: year 1-2).
- Ministry of Education — commission an independent equity and cost audit of the transition before full rollout (timeline: year 1).
- Klebelsberg Centre / teacher training institutes — design and deliver nationwide differentiated-instruction retraining, prioritizing shortage subjects (math/sciences) (timeline: year 1-4).
- Ministry of Education — pilot comprehensive-track conversion in a limited set of districts, monitoring inter-school choice and non-state enrollment shifts (timeline: year 2-4).
- Ministry of Education — nationwide rollout of comprehensive structure to age 14, contingent on pilot results and Go/No-Go review (timeline: year 5-8).
- Ministry of Education — establish choice-architecture and maintainer-transparency safeguards to limit sorting migration (timeline: year 3-6, concurrent with rollout).

**Political risks**
- Middle-class and church/foundation-school flight relocates sorting rather than removing it, echoing the Finnish-reform expert's flagged risk of middle-class exit from public education absent adequate support-system upgrades [evidence: moderate — Finnish reform analogy and Hungarian school-choice literature; no direct Hungarian quantitative estimate available].
- The Polish precedent shows even a pedagogically successful delayed-tracking reform (rising PISA scores) can be politically reversed years later under middle-class and institutional pressure [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD, political_feasibility expert].
- Teacher unions may withdraw conditional support if the reform introduces compliance audits rather than guidance-based standards [evidence: moderate — stakeholder discourse per political_feasibility expert, rounds 1-6].

**Uncertainties**
- Whether sorting migration to school choice and non-state maintainers would substantially offset the equity gain, and by how much [confidence: low; would be reduced by: simulation modeling or comparative case studies of similar abolitions, e.g., Finland, some German Länder].
- Whether political ownership can survive two electoral cycles given no current evidence of such a durability window in Hungary [confidence: low; would be reduced by: cross-party pact commitments and tracking of legislative continuity post-implementation].
- Whether teacher retraining can be delivered at the scale and speed required given existing teacher shortages [confidence: low; would be reduced by: a costed national retraining capacity assessment against current shortage data].
- The precise magnitude of the achievement-inequality tradeoff specific to the Hungarian curriculum and labor market [confidence: low; would be reduced by: a Hungary-specific replication of Hanushek & Woessmann-style analysis].

## S4 — Keep the structure, compensate general schools (Portuguese-style package)
**Goal**
Retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system unchanged in structure, but neutralize its socioeconomic inequities through non-structural interventions: targeted funding for disadvantaged schools (TEIP-style), rigorous curriculum standards, and reduced grade retention.

**Mechanism**
- Targeted, sustained funding to disadvantaged general schools (TEIP-style) raises resource levels and instructional quality for students not selected into gimnazium tracks [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020, describing Portugal's own trajectory, not a Hungarian replication].
- Rigorous national curriculum standards and school clustering can raise floor performance without altering selection age, as demonstrated in Portugal's 2006-2015 PISA gains [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020].
- Compensation is intended to offset, not merely mask, the effects of early sorting [evidence: weak — no country has demonstrated offsetting at Hungary's selection age (10-12); the Portuguese case did not involve early tracking of this kind, limiting transferability].

**Evidence status**
Moderate on the Portuguese precedent for non-structural gains generally, but weak and contested on whether the same package can offset (rather than mask) inequities specifically produced by selection as early as age 10-12, which Portugal did not have.

**Assumptions**
- Compensation can meaningfully offset, not just mask, the effects of early sorting [assumption — contradicted by the scenario's own evidence status, which notes no country has demonstrated offsetting at Hungary's early selection age; flagged as unverifiable ex ante].
- Non-structural interventions are politically easier to implement and sustain than structural changes like altering tracking age [assumption — portuguese_reform expert].
- Portugal's TEIP and curriculum-standards mechanisms are transferable to Hungary's socio-economic and institutional context [assumption — portuguese_reform expert].
- Sustained multi-year funding commitments will not be reversed in fiscal consolidation cycles [assumption].

**Expected benefits**
- Raises floor performance and resource levels in disadvantaged general schools without the political and administrative disruption of structural reform [evidence: moderate — Portuguese PISA gains 2006-2015, though achieved without Hungary's early-selection context].
- Politically less disruptive to implement than S2 or S3, since it avoids altering admission rules or tracking age [evidence: contested — this claim is undercut by the scenario's own political-risks section, which flags budget vulnerability and the risk of permanently legitimizing early selection as a form of political and institutional disruption in its own right].
- Avoids the transition costs (retraining, facility repurposing) associated with structural consolidation, per education_finance expert findings that transition costs, not steady-state costs, dominate structural reform budgets [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates].

**Equity impact**
Narrows the SES gradient at best partially, since it leaves the sorting mechanism itself intact and only compensates downstream; the root allocation of high-achieving peers and resources into selective tracks continues, meaning compensated general schools may still lag on peer-effect dimensions unaddressed by funding alone. Whether compensation offsets or merely masks the underlying stratification cannot be determined from available evidence, since no comparator country combines Hungary's early selection age with a Portuguese-style compensation package.

**Cost categories**
- TEIP-style targeted funding for disadvantaged schools — high; no numeric range is available, and this line is explicitly identified as a likely first cut in any fiscal consolidation, making its long-run exposure a serious, not merely notional, risk.
- Curriculum standards development and school clustering administration — medium, largely a one-off design cost with lower recurring burden.
- Reduced grade-retention program (diagnostic and remedial support) — medium-to-high, recurring, scale dependent on current retention rates.

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education — design a TEIP-equivalent targeted funding formula for disadvantaged general schools (timeline: year 1).
- Ministry of Education — develop and legislate rigorous national curriculum standards, informed by Portuguese design principles (timeline: year 1-2).
- Klebelsberg Centre — implement school clustering administration to share resources across disadvantaged school networks (timeline: year 2-3).
- Ministry of Education — roll out grade-retention reduction measures with diagnostic and remedial support (timeline: year 2-4).
- Ministry of Finance — secure a multi-year, ring-fenced budget commitment for TEIP-style funding to reduce reversal risk (timeline: year 1, renewed at year 4 and year 7).

**Political risks**
- Legitimising early selection permanently: compensation may entrench rather than dismantle the sorting engine, since it removes political pressure for structural reform while leaving selection itself untouched [evidence: weak — plausible extrapolation from the scenario's own design logic, not a documented Hungarian or Portuguese finding].
- Budget vulnerability: targeted funding programs are historically the first line item cut during fiscal consolidation, threatening the sustained, multi-year commitment the mechanism requires [evidence: weak — general pattern asserted, no Hungary-specific budget-cut history cited].
- Teacher supply constraints may limit the ability to deliver differentiated remedial support at the quality level Portugal achieved [evidence: high confidence per portuguese_reform expert's own uncertainty note — teacher shortages are a documented, high-confidence constraint].

**Uncertainties**
- Whether compensation can offset, rather than merely mask, the effects of early sorting at Hungary's selection age [confidence: low; would be reduced by: a Hungary-specific pilot comparing compensated vs. non-compensated general schools on medium-term SES-outcome gradients].
- Whether sustained, long-term funding for targeted interventions can survive Hungarian budgetary and legislative cycles without reversal [confidence: medium; would be reduced by: analysis of Hungarian budgetary commitments and legislative continuity in education policy, per portuguese_reform expert].
- The precise impact of existing Hungarian teacher shortages on the viability of delivering Portuguese-style standards and support [confidence: high; would be reduced by: comparative studies on teacher supply in Hungarian disadvantaged schools versus historical Portuguese levels, per portuguese_reform expert].
