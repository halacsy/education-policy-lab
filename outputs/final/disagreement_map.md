# Disagreement map (final)

Preserved per the Habermas-machine principle: mapped, not collapsed into consensus. Minority positions also appear in the final brief.

## Disagreement map

- **international_comparison, equity_and_social_mobility, hungarian_education_system, demography, school_network_planning**: Hungary should phase out early selection (delay to age 14–16 / comprehensive schooling). Why: early tracking amplifies existing SES stratification without raising mean performance [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006], value-added for selected pupils is modest [evidence: moderate — Horn 2013], and demographic shrinkage makes parallel selective tracks unsustainable while hollowing rural primary networks [evidence: strong — KSH].

- **portuguese_reform, education_finance, implementation_planning**: Hungary should retain the 6/8-year system and pursue non-structural or delayed alternatives. Why: Portugal improved PISA outcomes without structural change via TEIP targeting and curriculum standards [evidence: moderate — Crato 2020]; structural consolidation yields no steady-state savings and is dominated by transition costs [evidence: weak/strong — education_finance]; and the teacher shortage [evidence: strong — OECD/PSZ/PDSZ] makes dismantling functionally impossible without degrading delivery in disadvantaged regions.

- **hungarian_education_system (minority)**: Track abolition must be coupled with binding intake rules (randomized/balanced admission) AND maintainer-governance consolidation. Why: abolition alone will not reduce inequality because free choice and residential catchment already fragment urban schools [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013]; without eliminating denominational/foundation choice-compatible selectivity, sorting persists.

- **finnish_reform (minority)**: Phase-out is endorsed only if bundled with standardized teacher training and universal diagnostic support. Why: Finnish equity gains cannot be isolated from teacher-quality and early special-education bundling [evidence: moderate — Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009]; unbundled abolition risks within-classroom variance and middle-class flight.

- **polish_reform, political_feasibility (minority on durability)**: Even a well-designed phase-out will be reversed unless anchored in multi-party pacts / irreversible decoupling and compensatory funding. Why: Poland's 1999 reform produced measurable PISA gains yet was fully reversed in 2016–2019 under political pressure [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD]; organized urban middle-class opposition has blocked prior Hungarian attempts [evidence: moderate].

- **legal_and_governance (minority)**: No legislated closure should be attempted before an independent equity audit; reform should be a graduated, audit-justified pathway. Why: no recent Hungary-specific impact study exists [evidence: weak], and early selection already carries constitutional equality-of-opportunity exposure (Art. 66/a) and EU equal-treatment risk [evidence: moderate]; legislative entrenchment plus maintainer plurality impose multi-year windows.

- **school_network_planning vs. hungarian_education_system (contradiction on maintainer response)**: school_network_planning holds that non-state (church/foundation) maintainers *can and will absorb* phased-out selective capacity and bypass consolidation [assumption]; hungarian_education_system's position depends on maintainer governance being *consolidatable* to prevent exactly this. Why: unresolved — both cite the same strong maintainer-fragmentation finding but draw opposite feasibility conclusions. This contradiction remains open from rounds 02–03 and is not resolved here.
