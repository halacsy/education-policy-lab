# Disagreement map (final)

Preserved per the Habermas-machine principle: mapped, not collapsed into consensus. Minority positions also appear in the final brief.

## Disagreement map

- **equity_and_social_mobility, international_comparison, demography, finnish_reform, polish_reform**: Hungary should phase out early tracking (delay selection to age 14–16) to reduce SES segregation. Why: early tracking raises inequality without lifting mean achievement [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006], the value-added gain to selected pupils is modest/mostly a selection effect [evidence: moderate — Horn 2013], and cross-national delayed-tracking reforms show 0.15–0.25 SD equity gains [evidence: moderate — Brunello & Checchi 2007].
- **finnish_reform, polish_reform, international_comparison**: Phase-out only works if bundled — with teacher-training upgrades and early support (Finland), and with multi-party pacts and compensatory funding to prevent reversal (Poland). Why: Finnish equity gains cannot be isolated from teacher-quality and special-education bundling [evidence: moderate — Pekkarinen et al. 2009]; Poland's evidence-backed reform was fully reversed 2016–2019 for political reasons [evidence: moderate — OECD].
- **portuguese_reform (minority)**: Hungary should retain the tracks and instead deploy non-structural reforms (TEIP-style targeted funding, rigorous curriculum standards). Why: Portugal raised PISA outcomes 2006–2015 without changing selection age, focusing on classroom quality and disadvantaged-school support [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020].
- **education_finance (minority)**: Hungary should retain the system because transition costs (teacher retraining, facility re-adaptation) outweigh marginal steady-state savings. Why: per-pupil costs are flat across tracks and funding is centralized, so consolidation yields no structural savings [evidence: strong — model knowledge; weak — budget chapters].
- **implementation_planning (minority)**: Hungary should retain the tracks in the medium term until teacher supply stabilizes. Why: the acute maths/science teacher shortage makes staff redistribution across comprehensive schools infeasible without degrading delivery in disadvantaged regions [evidence: strong — OECD; PSZ/PDSZ surveys].
- **hungarian_education_system (minority)**: Retain tracks *only if* coupled with simultaneous reform of school choice and catchment rules; standalone removal would redistribute rather than reduce segregation. Why: choice- and catchment-based sorting persist independently of tracks [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013].
- **legal_and_governance (minority)**: Do not legislate closure; first commission an independent equity audit, then co-design a graduated pathway respecting legal timelines and maintainer plurality. Why: no recent Hungary-specific equity impact study exists [evidence: weak], and legislative entrenchment plus maintainer fragmentation impose multi-year procedural cost [evidence: strong — Nkt. analysis].
- **political_feasibility (minority)**: Do not roll out nationally; pilot in 3–4 districts over 5 years gated on SES-classification accuracy (≥85%), teacher-supply forecasts, and binding non-state caps. Why: stakeholder support is conditional not binary, and reversal risk under the next government is high without irreversible institutional lock-in [evidence: strong — episodic memory rounds 6–7; moderate — Poland precedent].
- **school_network_planning, demography**: Demographic contraction, not selection policy, is the primary driver of network restructuring; keeping early selection concentrates demographic vulnerability on rural primary schools. Why: births fell from ~125,000 to 85,000–95,000, forcing consolidation regardless of policy [evidence: strong — KSH yearbooks].
- **school_network_planning, political_feasibility (minority on maintainer absorption)**: Non-state maintainers (churches/foundations) can absorb phased-out selective capacity and bypass consolidation unless bound by law. Why: non-state maintainers hold a growing, decentralized share and can prioritize selective tracks [evidence: strong — maintainer statistics]. This directly contradicts the assumption elsewhere that non-state maintainers can be administratively restricted absent binding legal architecture.
