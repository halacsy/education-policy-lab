# Disagreement map (final)

Preserved per the Habermas-machine principle: mapped, not collapsed into consensus. Minority positions also appear in the final brief.

## Disagreement map

- **international_comparison, equity_and_social_mobility, education_finance**: Early between-school selection should be delayed or moved within-school; the international causal evidence (Hanushek–Woessmann) and SES-gradient data indicate the system amplifies inequality without raising mean performance. Why: cross-country difference-in-differences and Hungarian value-added studies suggest the track advantage is largely a selection effect, so deferral reduces stratification at low performance cost.

- **finnish_reform (minority)**: Hungary should NOT adopt comprehensive schooling on the Finnish model unless bundled with costed teacher professionalization and integrated support. Why: the Finnish equity effect is entangled with teacher policy and support systems; structure-only transfer replicates a known failure mode.

- **portuguese_reform (minority)**: Hungary can reduce equity costs WITHOUT abolishing gimnaziums, via curriculum standardization, TEIP-style targeted support, and retention reduction. Why: Portugal achieved large PISA gains without raising selection age, showing a non-structural pathway exists provided targeted investment is substantial and sustained.

- **polish_reform**: Hungary should pilot delayed selection (to age 15) in selected regions with embedded monitoring and legitimacy-building. Why: Poland's 1999 reform correlated with PISA gains, but its 2016–2019 reversal shows reforms fail without sustained political coalition.

- **implementation_planning, school_network_planning**: Do not implement structural reform nationwide first; pilot mixed-ability lower-secondary in well-resourced districts alongside teacher recruitment. Why: teacher shortages are acute and geographically uneven, and premature nationwide change concentrates selection pressure and worsens regional inequality.

- **legal_and_governance (minority)**: Commission a Hungary-specific evaluation BEFORE committing to structural reform, negotiating voluntary alignment with non-state maintainers. Why: no curated Hungary-specific outcome data exists, and statutory anchoring plus maintainer fragmentation create high switching costs.

- **political_feasibility**: Durable reform requires either a supermajority absorbing 3–5 years of parent mobilization or a choice-protecting reframe that risks Poland-style reversal. Why: gimnazium places function as signalling/insurance assets for a mobilisable urban middle class.

- **hungarian_education_system**: Track removal alone will not reduce stratification without simultaneous school-choice and catchment reform. Why: catchment design, choice asymmetries, and maintainer diversity sustain between-school sorting independently of track existence.

- **demography**: Selection policy does not alter total cohort contraction (~25–30% by 2035–2040); it only determines who bears consolidation. Why: birth-cohort shrinkage is structurally locked in through 2040 and independent of selection policy.
