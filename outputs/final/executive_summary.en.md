This brief synthesises nine expert analyses and a stakeholder stress test on early selection into Hungary's 6- and 8-year gimnázium tracks (entry at ages 12 and 10). It presents four scenarios without ranking them: **S1** — admission reform within the current structure; **S2** — gradual phase-down of 6/8-year entry places; **S3** — comprehensive school to age 14 (structural reform); **S4** — keep the structure, compensate general schools (Portuguese-style package). Each carries distinct winners, losers, and risks, and decisive Hungary-specific evidence is largely weak or absent.

Experts agree: entrants to 6/8-year tracks are strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families (evidence: strong); Hungary's SES-achievement gradient (19–25%) far exceeds the OECD average (12–14%) (strong); no recent Hungarian study isolates these tracks' true pedagogical value-added from pure selection effects (weak); teacher shortages and cohort decline constrain any path (moderate/strong); and church/foundation maintainers' growing enrolment share complicates any uniform national mandate (strong).

The central disagreement, left unresolved: whether international evidence (Finland, Poland, Portugal) is mechanistically transferable to justify phasing out early selection in Hungary, or whether those countries' equity gains were inseparably bundled with resource investments and institutional contexts (notably free school choice) that Hungary lacks or differs from — a dispute Poland's own 2016–2019 political reversal of its reform does not settle either way. A related fault line concerns method: some experts favour a monitored local pilot before any national commitment, given the missing value-added evidence; others (political_feasibility, demography) argue that without a binding, cross-party statutory pact, any decree-based or pilot-first reform is reversible within one electoral cycle and should not be the primary vehicle. A third dispute concerns whether non-state maintainers can realistically be prevented from absorbing displaced selective demand, or whether they function as an independent veto-player that undermines any equity rationale.

No-regret moves available now, regardless of which scenario is eventually chosen: commission the missing Hungarian value-added study separating school quality from selection effects; build a workforce-demand and teacher-retraining capacity model by region and subject; establish administrative tracking of non-state maintainer enrolment to detect offsetting expansion; and begin scoping a gaming-resistant SES-proxy design and its legal defensibility, since all four scenarios require this evidence base to proceed responsibly.
