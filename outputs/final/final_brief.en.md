# Policy brief — early selection and the 6/8-year gimnázium

This brief synthesises nine expert analyses and a stakeholder stress test on Hungary's early selection into 6- and 8-year gimnázium tracks (ages 10 and 12). It sets out four policy scenarios, what evidence supports each, where experts and stakeholders disagree, and which choices are genuine value decisions rather than technical questions. No single scenario is crowned as the answer: each carries distinct trade-offs, and much of the decisive evidence is weak or absent for the Hungarian context specifically.

## Scenario key (full detail: scenarios.en.md)
- S1 — Admission reform within the current structure
- S2 — Gradual phase-down of 6/8-year entry places
- S3 — Comprehensive school to age 14 (structural reform)
- S4 — Keep the structure, compensate general schools (Portuguese-style package)

## What we know
- Hungary's 6- and 8-year gimnáziums select pupils at ages 10 and 12 via a central written admission examination, and entrants are strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Hungary's socio-economic-status gradient in PISA achievement (19–25%) is markedly steeper than the OECD average (12–14%). [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Cohort decline (births falling from ~125,000 in the early 1990s to 85,000–95,000 by the 2020s) forces school-network consolidation regardless of any selection-policy choice. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Since 2013 most state schools are maintained by state school district centres (tankerületi központok), while church and foundation maintainers hold a growing share (government-dependent private enrolment near 25.5%), structurally complicating any uniform national reform mandate. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- A sustained teacher shortage (pedagógushiány) and an aging teacher workforce, concentrated in disadvantaged regions and specific subjects, limit the feasibility of rapidly scaling comprehensive-school capacity regardless of the chosen path. [fact] (evidence: moderate)
- Cross-country difference-in-differences evidence indicates earlier between-school tracking increases inequality of outcomes without raising mean achievement. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Portugal reduced early school leavers from 14.7% to 6.2% (2007–2023) via curriculum standards, school clustering, targeted support (TEIP), and retention reduction, without changing the age of initial selection. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Poland's 1999 postponement of tracking to age 15 correlated with PISA gains by 2012, but the reform was reversed in 2016/19 for political, not evidence-based, reasons. [fact] (evidence: moderate)
- No recent, comprehensive Hungarian study isolates the causal value-added (pedagógiai hozzáadott érték) of 6/8-year gimnáziums from pupil-composition (selection) effects. [fact] (evidence: weak)
- No detailed, publicly available cost-benefit or transition-cost estimate specific to a Hungarian phase-out of early selection currently exists. [fact] (evidence: weak)

## What we consider likely
- Because free school choice and catchment-area effects independently drive segregation, admission-rule changes alone would not eliminate sorting even if they reduce the SES skew of exam-based entry. [estimate]
- If early selection is abolished, sorting is likely to migrate into school choice, non-state maintainers, and residential/catchment mechanisms rather than disappear. [estimate]
- Under a state-sector cap, church and foundation maintainers may absorb displaced demand, so cohort-wide early-sorting exposure may not fall proportionally with the state-sector reduction. [estimate]
- Residual selective places are likely to become more SES-skewed as competition intensifies for a shrinking pool. [estimate]
- Compensatory (TEIP-style) targeted funding lines are likely to be among the first cut during fiscal consolidation unless protected by a statutory funding floor. [estimate]
- Structural reforms of this kind are politically reversible absent binding, cross-party, statutory (not decree-based) commitments, as the Polish reversal suggests. [estimate]

## Where experts disagree
### Whether international evidence (Finland, Poland, Portugal) is directly transferable to justify phasing out early selection in Hungary
- **international_comparison, finnish_reform, polish_reform, equity_and_social_mobility, demography**: Cross-country evidence on early tracking's inequality effects is mechanistically transferable because the underlying causal channels operate similarly across institutional contexts. [value] Why: Difference-in-differences and PISA gradient studies show consistent patterns across many systems; Hungary's exceptionally high SES gradient is itself evidence the mechanism is active domestically.
- **finnish_reform, portuguese_reform, polish_reform** (minority): Transferability is conditional and likely overstated: Finnish and Portuguese equity gains were bundled with resource and support investments inseparable from the structural change, and Hungary's free-school-choice regime differs structurally. [value] Why: Analytically, structure and bundled investment cannot be decomposed in the source studies; Poland's reversal shows documented gains did not guarantee political durability.
### Whether Hungary should pilot reform locally first or commit to a legally binding national phase-out schedule
- **hungarian_education_system, legal_and_governance, school_network_planning**: A monitored multi-year pilot in one or two large urban districts, with transparent value-added measurement, should precede any system-wide mandate. [value] Why: No recent Hungarian value-added study exists to distinguish school quality from selection effect; piloting generates the missing evidence before irreversible political capital is committed.
- **political_feasibility, demography** (minority): Without a multi-party statutory pact — binding timelines, teacher-retraining funding, and budget-floor guarantees — any decree-based or pilot-first reform remains reversible within one electoral cycle and should not be the primary vehicle. [value] Why: Hungarian education policy is delivered predominantly via ministerial decree, faster to reverse than statute; the Polish reversal shows even evidence-backed reforms fail without durable cross-party commitment.
### Whether non-state (church/foundation) maintainers can realistically be restricted from absorbing phased-out selective capacity
- **hungarian_education_system, legal_and_governance, school_network_planning**: A phase-out design can, with cross-maintainer coordination and negotiated transitional guarantees, prevent church and foundation schools from simply absorbing displaced selective demand. [value] Why: Legislative amendment plus negotiated maintainer agreements are the standard mechanism for structural education reform in Hungary's plural-maintainer system.
- **political_feasibility** (minority): This restriction assumption is not credible: church and foundation maintainers constitute an independent veto-player likely to absorb high-achieving pupils regardless of state-track phase-down. [value] Why: Church/foundation growth is structural and not subject to state legislative control in the same way as state-school-district-centre schools; the maintainer-share evidence contradicts the restriction assumption.

## What we don't know
- Whether any alternative assessment design (aptitude, portfolio, lottery-among-qualified) actually resists SES advantage in the Hungarian context, or merely relocates it to portfolio/interview coaching markets. [fact]
- Whether a usable, legally defensible, gaming-resistant SES proxy can be built given the current absence of a student-level SES register. [fact]
- The magnitude of non-state maintainer offsetting expansion in response to a state-sector cap — real but currently unquantified. [fact]
- Whether teacher-retraining capacity can realistically meet an abolition timeline given the documented aging workforce and acute regional shortages. [fact]
- Whether within-track compensatory support can narrow SES-achievement gaps once early selection has already separated cohorts by prior attainment. [fact]
- Whether a cross-party statutory pact with credible entrenchment mechanisms can be negotiated in Hungary's fragmented political environment. [assumption]

## What could be done
- **S1 — Admission reform within the current structure**: Reduce the SES skew of entrants to 6/8-year tracks without changing the tracks' existence or entry ages, by piloting a less coaching-sensitive assessment and/or an SES-weighted admission mechanism — while acknowledging the reform lever itself is untested in Hungary.
- **S2 — Gradual phase-down of 6/8-year entry places**: Progressively reduce state-sector 6- and 8-year entry places over a multi-year statutory timetable, while acknowledging non-state maintainers may absorb displaced demand and cohort-wide early-sorting exposure may not fall proportionally.
- **S3 — Comprehensive school to age 14 (structural reform)**: Abolish 6/8-year gimnázium entry and consolidate academic preparation into a comprehensive structure to age 14, moving Hungary's selection age closer to later-tracking systems — without claiming this alone guarantees the cross-national equity effect in Hungary's distinct context.
- **S4 — Keep the structure, compensate general schools (Portuguese-style package)**: Retain the 6/8-year system while implementing curriculum standardisation, targeted resource allocation to disadvantaged schools, and grade-retention reduction — without claiming this neutralizes the inequities produced by age-10/12 selection.

## What each option costs
- **S1**: Winners: disadvantaged applicants who qualify under new rules; the government (low entrenchment, reversible within a cycle). Losers: middle-SES or rural pupils without qualifying proxy status who may be displaced; non-applicants deterred by information/distance gaps gain nothing. Risk: an unvalidated proxy may be gamed or simply relocate advantage to portfolio/interview coaching, and litigation over quota/proxy mechanisms is a real contingent cost. [value]
- **S2**: Winners: comprehensive-stream pupils if displaced high-achievers remain (peer-effect is contested); the state gains a directly monitorable lever. Losers: lower-SES pupils who lose access to selective seats without compensating investment; gimnázium teachers whose posts shrink with no stated retraining path; general-school teachers absorbing larger, more heterogeneous classes on unbudgeted compensatory funding. Risk: if non-state maintainers offset the reduction, cohort-wide exposure does not fall at all — a cost-shift, not an equity gain. [value]
- **S3**: Winners: disadvantaged pupils if the exam-based sorting gate is removed and sorting does not migrate; potentially a broader talent base long-term. Losers: families and church/foundation maintainers forced to unwind years of planning and investment; teachers carrying a retraining burden under documented shortage; pupils sitting in classrooms during an unproven multi-year transition. Risk: the largest identified — sorting relocates into school choice, non-state expansion, and catchment effects, and the reform is politically reversible (Polish precedent) absent statutory entrenchment. [value]
- **S4**: Winners: general-school pupils and families who keep their pathway; church/foundation maintainers (no structural disruption); the government (fiscally flexible, reversible). Losers: disadvantaged pupils if compensatory funding is cut first in consolidation and if the mechanism legitimizes and stabilizes early selection; teachers asked to deliver an ambitious bundle on fragile funding and short staffing. Risk: could be equity-neutral or even equity-negative net of its entrenchment effect on the status quo — Portugal's own TIMSS 2023 decline in low-SES high performers is a warning. [value]

## What research could resolve
- A multi-year Hungarian pilot comparing admission outcomes and SES composition under alternative assessment formats versus the current exam, with independent evaluation of gaming-resistance and displacement.
- A funded feasibility study and small-scale data-linkage pilot assessing accuracy, gaming-resistance, and legal defensibility of candidate SES-proxy designs.
- Annual administrative tracking of non-state maintainer enrollment by track type during the first cohort-cap cycles, compared against a pre-registered counterfactual, to quantify offsetting expansion.
- A recent Hungarian value-added study isolating school quality from selection effect, to settle whether the tracks add pedagogical value beyond intake composition.
- A workforce-demand model by subject and region linked to teacher-preparation pipeline and retraining-capacity estimates, to test whether an abolition timeline is realistic.
- Longitudinal value-added analysis in early-selection systems testing whether within-track support narrows gaps or whether between-track gaps dominate long-term inequality.
- A comparative case study of durable cross-party education pacts in other Central European states, plus structured interviews on concrete entrenchment safeguard conditions.

## What people must decide
- Whether reducing the SES-achievement gap (méltányosság) justifies disrupting a trusted, established gimnázium pathway that many families have planned their lives around — a value choice between equity and institutional continuity that no evidence can settle. [value]
- How much weight to give political reversibility risk: whether to prefer a reversible, decree-based lever (S1/S4) that a future government can undo, or a statutorily entrenched commitment (S2/S3) that binds successors but is harder to build. [value]
- Whether to constrain church and foundation maintainer autonomy through regulatory levers to prevent offsetting expansion — a genuine value conflict between maintainer plurality/family choice and system-wide equity enforcement. [value]
- Whether to commit statutory funding floors to protect equity spending from fiscal consolidation, accepting reduced budgetary flexibility in exchange for durable delivery. [value]
- Whether to act on strong cross-national association evidence now, or to wait for Hungary-specific causal evidence from a pilot before committing irreversible political capital. [value]

## What to verify with real stakeholders
- A1 (Admission-reform mechanisms are untested in Hungary and should be piloted and independently evaluated before national rollout rather than legislated on faith.): policy_design_fixable — The scenario's own implementation steps already build in a pilot-then-evaluate-then-legislate sequence with independent evaluation before codification; the design concern is met by insulating pilot evaluation triggers (composition shift, gaming, gap-narrowing) published before any national commitment.
- A2 (An SES-proxy or quota mechanism risks becoming a new, opaque gatekeeping tool that relocates coaching advantage to portfolio or interview preparation instead of genuinely widening access.): needs_more_info — Whether a gaming-resistant proxy is achievable is an open empirical question the scenario labels weak-evidence; only a published, independently evaluated multi-year pilot testing whether composition shifts without advantage relocating can settle it.
- A3 (Teachers, admissions staff, and church/foundation maintainers who must design, administer, and defend any new instrument need a formal design role, funded training, and respect for their autonomy before it reaches schools.): policy_design_fixable — Co-design, funded preparation, and workload allowance can be written into the pilot design phase and budgeted before any instrument reaches schools — a scheduling and budget-line matter, not a value conflict.
- A4 (Any new admission mechanism must apply prospectively with multi-year advance notice so families and children currently in the exam-preparation pipeline are not caught by unanticipated rules.): policy_design_fixable — Prospective application with a defined multi-year notice period and transitional protection for pupils already preparing is a standard phase-in design choice that fully addresses the concern.
- A5 (Admission-rule tinkering within the existing selective-track structure leaves early selection itself intact and does not serve child-centred comprehensive schooling or free teachers from sorting pressure.): value_conflict — This is a legitimate values collision between incremental reform within the structure and structural abolition; it is already reflected as the S1-versus-S3 choice and cannot be resolved technically, only by a decision on reform ambition.
- A6 (A less coaching-sensitive admission mechanism could widen the effective talent pool available to employers and higher education, since the current exam mainly rewards coachability.): needs_more_info — Whether a redesigned instrument measures aptitude rather than merely shifting the coachable margin to a new format is untested in Hungary; a pilot with downstream tracking of admitted-cohort composition would settle it.
- A7 (Because admission reform stays within ministerial authority and can be piloted, adjusted, or reversed within one electoral cycle, it carries lower political and legal entrenchment risk than structural reform.): value_conflict — Reversibility is a genuine value trade-off, not a fact: it lowers entrenchment risk for the government but reduces durability and credibility for families and schools who invest in the reform — the same reversibility that reformers see as a defect the government sees as a virtue.
- A8 (A state-sector cap risks being neutralised or worsening equity if non-state maintainers expand to absorb displaced demand, since no enforceable mechanism currently prevents this offsetting.): policy_design_fixable — The offsetting risk is real but the design levers exist — statutory caps on non-state selective places, capital-grant conditionality, licensing review, per-pupil funding rules with monitoring and escalation triggers; the honest conclusion is a cap without such safeguards is unfinished design, not that caps are unworkable. Whether the levers survive is itself contested.
- A9 (Regulatory levers discouraging non-state maintainer expansion treat church schools' growth as a policy problem rather than a legitimate exercise of maintainer autonomy and family choice.): value_conflict — This is a genuine collision between maintainer autonomy/family choice and system-wide equity enforcement; no technical fix dissolves the tension over whether public funds should be used to discourage selective expansion.
- A10 (A statutory multi-year admission-cap schedule locks in a policy a successor government has strong incentive to reverse, as Poland shows, creating reversal risk without entrenchment safeguards.): value_conflict — Whether the Polish reversal generalises to Hungary is an assumption-heavy analogy, but the underlying choice — reversibility versus durable entrenchment — is a value decision about how to weigh political-durability risk, not a fact to be settled.
- A11 (The phase-down must be paired with a statutory, pre-secured funding and staffing floor for general schools receiving displaced and retained pupils, not resources promised only after each cap reduction.): policy_design_fixable — Legislating and disbursing funding ahead of each cap reduction is a sequencing and budget-guarantee design choice; whether such a statutory floor survives budget cycles is a separate durability uncertainty.
- A12 (A gradual, monitored phase-down is preferable to abrupt abolition because it gives families, employers, and schools time to adjust, provided a segregation-tracking dashboard and lead-time guarantees exist.): policy_design_fixable — Lead-time guarantees and an annually published segregation dashboard with escalation triggers are design elements that can be built in; the residual doubt about offsetting magnitude is empirical, addressed by monitoring.
- A13 (Shrinking gimnázium cohort caps reduces teaching posts for gimnázium staff with no stated transition or retraining plan for the affected teachers.): policy_design_fixable — A named, funded retraining and redeployment path for gimnázium teachers can be legislated alongside the cap schedule; the net employment impact is currently undetermined and could be partly absorbed by attrition, which planning must quantify.
- A14 (Abolishing the 6/8-year structure would outrun realistic teacher-retraining and workforce capacity given Hungary's aging workforce and acute regional shortages, risking burnout and overloaded classrooms.): needs_more_info — The teacher shortage is a documented fact, but whether a phased, region-sequenced retraining plan could keep pace with a staged rollout is a low-confidence, untested implementation question; a workforce-demand model linked to retraining capacity would resolve it.
- A15 (The scenario's own analysis admits sorting may migrate into school choice, non-state maintainers, and catchment areas rather than disappearing, so abolition might not deliver the promised equity gain.): needs_more_info — Whether sorting migration would neutralize the equity gain is the decisive open empirical question; a decomposition analysis of segregation-index changes under separately varied exam abolition and school-choice restriction, using administrative pupil-flow data, would resolve it.
- A16 (Abolishing early selection removes the single most powerful, statutorily enforced sorting mechanism driving the achievement gap, aligning with cross-national evidence that later tracking is associated with lower SES gradients.): irreducible_tradeoff — Removing the exam-based gate is the necessary lever for interrupting the segregation cycle, but doing so necessarily trades institutional continuity and predictability, and its equity gain is conditional on containing sorting migration — improving one goal costs another and the causal transfer is not guaranteed.
- A17 (Abolishing the 6/8-year tracks would force families and church/foundation maintainers to abandon years of planning, institutional investment, and a proven pathway built on parental choice and subsidiarity.): value_conflict — This pits subsidiarity and parental choice against equity and reduced early selection; whether institutional continuity should be preserved absent Hungary-specific proof of net equity gain is an irreducible value judgment.
- A18 (A structural abolition legislated without a credible, statutorily entrenched cross-party pact is politically vulnerable to reversal, as the Polish 1999-2016/19 precedent shows.): policy_design_fixable — Entrenchment mechanisms — supermajority reversal requirements, automatic budget locks, independent monitoring triggers — can be designed to raise reversal cost; whether they can be negotiated in Hungary's fragmented environment is the remaining open question.
- A19 (Government-dependent private/church schools represent a substantial, well-organised veto-player bloc with incentive and capacity to resist structural abolition of early selection.): value_conflict — Whether maintainer resistance can actually block implementation is partly unknown, but the underlying question of whether veto-player autonomy should be accommodated or overridden for equity is a political-value decision, not a technical one.
- A20 (Retaining the selection mechanism while compensating its 'losers' legitimizes and entrenches segregation rather than addressing its structural cause, treating symptoms instead of removing the sorting gate.): value_conflict — This is a foundational values collision between structural equity (removing the cause) and pragmatic amelioration (compensating within an unchanged system); the empirical premise (whether within-track support can narrow gaps post-sorting) is contested but the core objection is a value stance.
- A21 (Portuguese TIMSS-2023 data show a decline in low-SES high-performing pupils (21% to 16%) despite 16 years of TEIP-style support, raising doubt that compensatory investment offsets early selection's effects once cohorts are sorted.): needs_more_info — The 21%-to-16% decline is a documented data point, but causal attribution (versus pandemic, demographic, or curriculum factors) is not established, and Portugal lacks Hungary's early-selection structure; a Hungary-specific longitudinal study is needed to settle transferability.
- A22 (TEIP-style targeted funding is identified by the scenario itself as a likely first-line cut during fiscal consolidation, making the compensatory package's central benefit-delivery mechanism fragile absent a statutory funding floor.): policy_design_fixable — A statutory, multi-year, revenue-protected funding floor with independent monitoring can address the fragility; whether the floor actually survives a consolidation cycle in practice is an untested Hungarian counterfactual.
- A23 (The Portuguese TEIP precedent shows real system-level gains (early school leaving cut from 14.7% to 6.2%) are achievable through targeted support, curriculum standards, and retention reduction without abolishing selective tracks.): evidence_answerable — The early-school-leaving reduction is a documented outcome (strong evidence), but its transfer to a Hungarian system that retains early selection is unverified, and the TIMSS 2023 decline qualifies whether the approach closes achievement gaps — the fact is real, its applicability is bounded.
- A24 (S4 is the only one of the four that improves conditions for pupils in general schools without forcing families who chose or plan the selective gimnázium pathway to abandon that plan.): value_conflict — Whether preserving the selective pathway alongside additive investment is a legitimate equity strategy or an entrenchment of segregation is exactly the value dispute between S4 supporters and the structural-reform movement; it cannot be resolved technically.
- A25 (S4 avoids the statutory entrenchment, cross-party consensus precondition, and reversal risk that structural reform (S2, S3) requires, making it more fiscally and politically durable within a single electoral cycle.): value_conflict — Reversibility as durability is a governing-majority value: the same non-statutory flexibility that the government prizes exposes disadvantaged pupils to funding collapse — whether that is durability or fragility depends on whose perspective and values are weighted.
- A26 (Teacher-shortage constraints strongly undermine credible delivery of grade-retention-reduction and diagnostic-support rollout at the Portuguese pace, and teachers would carry added expectations without secure resources.): policy_design_fixable — Explicit capacity-gating of rollout to regional teacher-staffing levels plus a statutory funding floor addresses the delivery-feasibility concern; whether the Portuguese pace is achievable under Hungary's staffing base remains an untested extrapolation.
- A27 (Church and foundation maintainers should not have a uniform admission-equity mandate imposed without a design voice, since it disregards their constitutionally protected pedagogical and admissions autonomy.): value_conflict — The scope of maintainers' constitutional autonomy versus permissible uniform equity regulation is a legal-value question not settled by data; consultation can be designed in, but the core autonomy-versus-uniformity tension is a genuine conflict.
- A28 (Without a statutory workload floor and matching staffing investment, general-school teachers will absorb larger, more heterogeneous classes as displaced and retained pupils arrive, amounting to a de facto phase-out of public teaching capacity.): policy_design_fixable — A statutory workload floor (maximum class sizes linked to disadvantage indicators) and legislated compensatory staffing disbursed ahead of each cap reduction directly address the concern; the actual class-size and heterogeneity impact is unverifiable until phase-down begins.

## Where the red herrings are
- A5: The objection that admission-rule reform leaves early selection intact is already answered by treating S1 as a within-structure lever, not a substitute for the S3 structural debate — it restates the S1-versus-S3 value choice rather than moving the decision. It consumes attention because it is rhetorically forceful, but resolving it would not change which scenario is chosen; that turns on the prior value decision about reform ambition.

## Minority positions
- (finnish_reform, portuguese_reform, polish_reform) International transferability is conditional and likely overstated: Finnish and Portuguese equity gains were bundled with resource and support investments inseparable from the structural change, and Hungary's free-school-choice regime differs structurally, so structural change alone would not replicate the gains. — Analytically, structure and bundled investment cannot be decomposed in the source studies; Poland's own reversal shows even documented gains did not guarantee political durability, undermining simple transfer logic.
- (political_feasibility, demography) Without a multi-party statutory pact — binding timelines, teacher-retraining funding, and budget-floor guarantees — any decree-based or pilot-first reform remains politically reversible within a single electoral cycle and should not be the primary reform vehicle. — Hungarian education policy is delivered predominantly via ministerial decree, faster to reverse than statute; the Polish 2016-2019 reversal demonstrates that even evidence-backed reforms fail without durable cross-party commitment.
- (political_feasibility) The assumption that non-state maintainers can be restricted from absorbing phased-out selective capacity is not credible: church and foundation maintainers (government-dependent private enrolment near 25.5%) are an independent veto-player likely to absorb high-achieving pupils regardless of state-track phase-down, undermining the equity rationale. — Church/foundation growth is structural and not subject to state legislative control in the same way as state-school-district-centre schools; this was explicitly flagged as an unresolved assumption contradicted by the maintainer-share evidence.
- (oktataspolitikai_reformmozgalom) S4 is not a defensible equity reform at all: compensatory support within retained early selection is a contradiction in terms that legitimizes and stabilizes the very sorting mechanism driving the SES-achievement gap; no evidence condition would change this, only the political impossibility of S3 would shift the movement toward S2 (never S4). — Selection by definition separates cohorts; compensatory support cannot unseparate them, so the objection is one of intellectual and moral honesty rather than evidence or implementation fidelity.
- (eselyegyenlosegi_civil) S2 in its unconditional form is opposed as a cost-shift that shrinks opportunity for disadvantaged pupils without expanding fair access, because it reduces state-sector selective places without any enforceable mechanism to prevent non-state offsetting or any guaranteed compensatory investment in general schools. — Equity demands expanding fair access and supporting comprehensive schools; S2 does the first part (shrinks opportunity) while only hypothetically doing the second (general-school support appears only in S4).
- (konzervativ_ertekvedo, egyhazi_fenntartoi) Structural abolition (S3) and statutory caps (S2) should be rejected in favour of preserving the gimnázium as a proven institution; any equity mechanism should be opt-in for maintainers, preserve an aptitude-based core, avoid a centralized SES register, and respect church/foundation constitutional autonomy. — Subsidiarity, parental primacy, and institutional continuity outweigh acting on cross-national correlational evidence the scenario itself labels contested for Hungary, especially where sorting migration and reversal risk make the equity gain uncertain.
