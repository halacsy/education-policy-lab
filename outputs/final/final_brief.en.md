# Deliberation Brief: Early Academic Selection in Hungary (6/8-Year Gimnázium Tracks)

## What we know

- Entry to the 6/8-year gimnázium tracks is skewed toward high-SES, urban families [fact — evidence: strong; KSH / Oktatási Hivatal intake statistics].
- Hungary's SES–achievement gradient (19–25% of PISA variance) sits well above the OECD average of 12–14% [fact — evidence: strong; OECD PISA].
- Earlier tracking raises inequality without raising mean achievement [fact — evidence: strong; Hanushek & Woessmann 2006].
- Since 2013 the maintainer landscape is fragmented between state (tankerület/KK) and a growing church/foundation share, which complicates any uniform mandate [fact — evidence: strong; public education act amendments; maintainer statistics].
- Non-state maintainers can absorb selective tracks and bypass state consolidation; a state-only cap can be offset by them [fact — evidence: strong; maintainer-plurality and school-network findings].
- Demographic cohort contraction (births down from ~125,000 to ~85,000–95,000) forces school-network consolidation regardless of selection policy [fact — evidence: strong; KSH].
- The selective share of each cohort is 8–12%, so capping it mechanically retains more pupils in the general track [fact — evidence: moderate; Hungarian intake statistics].
- The value-added of selective tracks for admitted pupils is modest; the raw advantage is largely a selection effect [fact — evidence: moderate; Horn 2013; Kertesi & Kézdi 2013].
- Hungary has a sustained, binding teacher shortage [fact — evidence: strong; OECD; PSZ/PDSZ].
- Structural-reform costs are dominated by front-loaded transition costs (retraining, facilities, administration), not steady-state operational savings [fact — evidence: weak; budget chapters].
- Hungary has no student-level SES register usable at application time [fact — evidence: strong; legal/administrative finding].

## What we consider likely

- Postponing formal selection to age 14–15 would reduce the degree to which family resources at age 10–12 predict track placement [estimate — indirect; Hanushek & Woessmann 2006 generalised to Hungary].
- Delayed selection can raise mean and equity outcomes, as in Poland's 1999 reform [estimate — evidence: moderate; Jakubowski et al. 2016; OECD].
- A phased phase-down with grandfathering can respect statutory notice periods [fact — evidence: strong; Nkt. analysis], but the mechanical retention benefit is likely to be offset by non-state maintainers absorbing displaced demand [estimate — near-certain; derived from the strong maintainer-plurality finding].
- Residual selective places become more SES-skewed as caps tighten (fewer seats, same applicant pool) [estimate — mechanical, near-certain].
- Portugal raised PISA outcomes without changing selection age via TEIP and standards [fact — evidence: moderate; Crato 2020], but its transfer to Hungary's earlier selection age is untested [assumption — flagged unverifiable ex ante].
- Free school choice and catchment-based sorting produce strong between-school segregation independent of formal tracks, so track abolition alone would be insufficient [estimate — evidence: moderate; Kertesi & Kézdi 2013].
- Equity gains from comprehensive reform would take 10–15 years to emerge and only if bundled with teacher-quality and early-support investment [estimate — evidence: moderate/contested; Finnish reform; diff-in-diff timing].

## Where experts disagree

- **Reform direction (structural vs. non-structural).** equity_and_social_mobility, international_comparison, demography, school_network_planning argue for phasing out early tracking because early selection amplifies SES segregation without raising mean achievement [fact — evidence: strong]. portuguese_reform, education_finance, implementation_planning argue for retaining the structure and pursuing non-structural compensation, because Portugal improved without structural change [fact — evidence: moderate] and structural consolidation yields no steady-state savings [fact — evidence: weak]. The reason for divergence is not the diagnosis (shared) but the weight given to implementation constraints.
- **Whether the teacher shortage is a veto or a parameter.** pedagogus_erdekvedo, pedagogus_szakszervezeti_hang, digitalisgazdasagi treat the shortage as a binding precondition that structural reform does not resolve [fact — evidence: weak that it makes reform "infeasible"; strong that a shortage exists]. Others (egyhazi_fenntartoi, kormanyzati_reformrealizmus, reform movement) hold the shortage is a fiscal/policy variable movable before scale-up [assumption — contested]. This is a genuine empirical-plus-political split.
- **Whether abolition removes or merely relocates sorting.** konzervativ_ertekvedo and hungarian_education_system hold that removing the state gate relocates sorting into opaque school choice and non-state maintainers, possibly worsening transparency [estimate — the scenario's own "largest risk"]. Equity advocates treat relocation as an implementation risk to be managed, not a design flaw [value + estimate]. Unresolved.
- **Whether compensation offsets or masks.** portuguese_reform/finance camp treats downstream compensation as capable of offsetting early sorting [assumption — unverifiable ex ante]; equity advocates and fuggetlen_szakpolitikai_kutatomuhely hold it only masks while entrenching the sorting engine [value + estimate].
- **Maintainer response (school_network_planning ⟷ hungarian_education_system).** Whether non-state maintainers can be restricted from absorbing phased-out capacity. hungarian_education_system: they cannot be assumed to comply [fact — evidence: strong]. school_network_planning/political_feasibility rely on containment this finding contradicts. Carried forward unresolved.

## What we don't know

- Whether a genuinely less-coachable assessment instrument can be designed and psychometrically validated [known-unknown — resolvable by a coaching-sensitivity pilot].
- Whether any SES proxy would survive gaming at application [known-unknown — no student-level SES register exists; resolvable by proxy-robustness audit in a comparable system].
- Whether documented outreach materially raises low-SES/Roma application rates [known-unknown — resolvable by pilot with uptake-elasticity data].
- Whether non-state maintainers would absorb displaced selective demand, and whether parallel intake limits on them are legally enforceable [known-unknown — the legal enforceability is contested at low confidence].
- How large and how fast equity gains would be in Hungary specifically [known-unknown — resolvable by a single-county pilot with 10-year tracking or synthetic control].
- What fraction of between-school sorting is formal selection vs. choice and catchment [known-unknown — resolvable by enrolment-flow decomposition].
- Whether teacher supply can be stabilised on any reform's timeline [known-unknown — resolvable by workforce exit-tracking and retraining-cost data].
- Whether compensation can offset rather than mask early-sorting effects at Hungary's selection age [known-unknown — no comparable-system evidence exists].
- That political ownership survives two electoral cycles is currently an **assumption**, not a known-unknown: the political-feasibility expert states no durable window exists absent cross-party or constitutional insulation [assumption — contradicted by an expert].

## What could be done

The four alternatives are genuine options, not a ranked list. A do-nothing baseline is included.

- **S0 — Do nothing (baseline).** Retain current admission rules and structure. The intake-skew problem persists at strong-evidence level; demographic contraction still forces network consolidation [fact].
- **S1 — Admission reform within the current structure.** Rewrite entry rules/quotas, replace or supplement the central exam with a less-coachable instrument, add SES-conscious reweighting and outreach. Lowest legislative friction [fact — evidence: moderate]; every equity lever rests on weak/unverified evidence [assumption].
- **S2 — Gradual phase-down of 6/8-year entry places.** Cap and annually reduce state-maintained selective seats over a decade with grandfathering. Mechanical retention is well-grounded [fact — evidence: moderate]; net equity gain depends entirely on containing non-state offset [estimate — near-certain risk].
- **S3 — Comprehensive school to age 14 (structural reform).** Abolish 6/8-year entry, postpone selection to 14–15, bundled with teacher-quality and support investment. Strongest causal evidence base [fact — evidence: strong on direction]; largest expected equity gain but also largest relocation risk and weakest political durability [estimate].
- **S4 — Keep the structure, compensate general schools (Portuguese-style).** Route TEIP-style targeted funding and curriculum standards to disadvantaged general schools. Lowest structural disruption [fact — evidence: moderate]; core "offset not mask" claim is unverifiable ex ante [assumption].

Several actors (kormanyzati_reformrealizmus, reform movement, eselyegyenlosegi_civil) explicitly propose **bundles** — S1+S4 as a two-pillar approach, or S4/S2 as interim steps toward S3 — rather than single scenarios [value].

## What each option costs

- **S0 (do nothing):** No new outlay. Trade-off: the strongly-evidenced intake skew and steep SES gradient persist. Winners — middle-class/urban families holding the mobility-insurance asset; non-state maintainers. Losers — high-ability low-SES and Roma pupils [value + fact].
- **S1:** Assessment redesign and multi-year psychometric validation [cost: medium-to-high]; outreach and uptake tracking [cost: medium, recurring]; proxy-gaming monitoring [cost: medium, recurring]. Trade-off: risks relabelling rather than reducing sorting if the less-coachable instrument fails [assumption]; may invite informal selection (interviews, reputation) that reproduces SES sorting [estimate]. Workload risk falls on teachers/school leaders if auditing and outreach are unfunded [value — pedagogus_erdekvedo, union]. Winners — high-ability low-SES applicants already applying; reform-minded government seeking a survivable step. Losers — potentially low-SES families if gatekeeping goes informal.
- **S2:** Parallel-regime administration across most of a decade [cost: medium, persistent]; general-school capacity expansion [cost: medium]; non-state monitoring [cost: low-to-medium]. Trade-off: residual selective seats become more SES-skewed [estimate — near-certain]; a decade of administrative friction and middle-class mobilisation for a benefit contingent on containing near-certain offset. Winners — non-state maintainers (absorb demand); Losers — general-track teachers (workload up 20–35% per union estimate [estimate]), high-ability low-SES pupils if offset materialises.
- **S3:** Nationwide teacher retraining [cost: high]; facility/curriculum realignment [cost: high, front-loaded]; early-diagnostic and special-education systems [cost: high, recurring]. Trade-off: largest equity upside but hostage to teacher supply (strong constraint), political reversal (Poland precedent), and sorting relocation to non-state/private schools. Winners (if durable and contained) — low-SES and rural pupils, rural primary networks. Losers — families losing early selective access; teachers if reform is imposed unbundled or reversed mid-course; institutional autonomy of non-state maintainers.
- **S4:** TEIP-style targeted funding [cost: high, budget-vulnerable — first cut in fiscal consolidation]; curriculum standards [cost: medium]; teacher recruitment/retention incentives [cost: high]. Trade-off: leaves the sorting gate intact, compensates symptoms; may entrench early selection permanently [estimate]; teacher shortage blocks staffing in exactly the targeted schools [fact — evidence: strong]. Winners — middle-class families (track preserved), non-state maintainers, government avoiding mobilisation. Losers — disadvantaged pupils if funding is cut mid-stream (doubly harmed); teachers blamed for inequality they cannot solve alone [value].

## What research could resolve

- **A coaching-sensitivity psychometric pilot** of the S1 assessment — would move S1's central lever from weak to moderate evidence and is a precondition named by nearly every S1-conditional voice.
- **A SES-proxy robustness audit** in a comparable system (Czech Republic, Poland) — resolves whether S1/S2 reweighting is gameable.
- **Registry data on selective seats by maintainer and settlement type, plus an audit of church/foundation admission response** — directly resolves the S2/S3 non-state offset question, the single most decision-pivotal uncertainty.
- **An enrolment-flow decomposition** across urban districts — resolves how much sorting is formal selection vs. choice/catchment (bears on whether abolition suffices).
- **A single-county S3 pilot with comparison-group design and 10-year outcome tracking** — resolves Hungary-specific size and speed of equity gains and whether sorting relocates.
- **An offset-vs-mask outcome evaluation** in a system that compensates without altering an early-selection age — the only thing that would validate S4's core claim.
- **Teacher workforce exit-tracking and retraining-cost data** — resolves whether the shortage is a veto or a movable parameter (the A1 dispute).

## What people must decide

These are value and political choices; naming them is the honest answer, not a failure to resolve.

- **Equity vs. parental choice / meritocratic selection.** Whether reducing SES sorting justifies constraining families' access to selective tracks [value — irreducible].
- **Whether to bind non-state maintainers.** Any effective cap or abolition requires either compelling church/foundation schools (contested legality, autonomy conflict) or accepting offset [value + political choice].
- **How much to spend, and how durably.** Whether to constitutionally or supermajority-protect equity funding against fiscal consolidation — a political commitment no scenario can manufacture technically [political choice].
- **Whether to accept relocation risk.** Whether an equity gain that may relocate sorting to opaque channels is preferable to a transparent status quo [value].
- **Sequencing and durability.** Whether to accept interim/bundle strategies (S1/S4 as bridges to S3) knowing Hungarian legislative commitments to future reform have been reversed [political choice].
- **Who bears transition burden.** Whether teachers can be asked to deliver reform without prior supply stabilisation, wage, and workload guarantees [value].

## Minority positions

Every dissenting position is carried here in proportion, never resolved away.

- **portuguese_reform, education_finance, implementation_planning:** Retain the structure; non-structural reform can address equity [fact — evidence: moderate; Crato 2020]; transition costs outweigh flat steady-state savings [fact — evidence: weak/strong]; teacher shortage makes structural redistribution unviable medium-term [fact — evidence: strong]. Not resolved: the majority treats the teacher constraint as a design condition rather than rebutting it.
- **legal_and_governance:** Reject legislated closure as a first move; require an independent equity audit and a graduated, maintainer-respecting pathway first, because no recent Hungary-specific equity impact study exists [fact — evidence: weak] and legal entrenchment imposes multi-year windows [fact — evidence: strong; Nkt.].
- **hungarian_education_system:** Track abolition addresses only one layer; choice- and catchment-based sorting would persist [estimate — evidence: moderate]. Qualifies every phase-out position without endorsing retention.
- **finnish_reform:** Phase-out without simultaneous teacher-training and diagnostic-support investment is counterproductive [estimate — evidence: moderate; Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009].
- **konzervativ_ertekvedo (stakeholder):** Central capping and comprehensive mandates violate subsidiarity and will fail by offset [value]; revised position concedes the SES-sorting effect is real but maintains S3 relocates rather than solves it [value].
- **digitalisgazdasagi (stakeholder):** No position on S4; opposes S3 on credential-opacity grounds [value].
- **Unresolved contradiction (school_network_planning ⟷ hungarian_education_system):** whether non-state maintainers can be restricted from absorbing phased-out capacity. Carried forward unresolved.

## What to verify with real stakeholders

Stakeholder voices are a stress test, not a prediction; each cluster is answered by id.

- **A1** (teacher shortage makes near-term structural reorganisation infeasible): needs_more_info — the shortage is settled [fact — strong], but whether it can be stabilised on the reform timeline is contested and pivotal; workforce data would decide it.
- **A2** (early selective tracks sort by status more than ability): evidence_answerable — the sorting direction is strongly evidenced; whether abolition improves net equity vs. relocates it is the genuinely open part, refinable by enrolment-flow and pilot data.
- **A3** (parents have the right to choose an academically intensive track): value_conflict — parental autonomy vs. system equity collide; no technical fix, though non-state intake guardrails could narrow the displacement it causes.
- **A4** (restricting state selective tracks shifts demand to church/private schools): policy_design_fixable — the offset mechanism is strongly evidenced, but simultaneous, negotiated parallel intake limits (or sector-wide agreement) could reduce it; enforceability remains legally contested.
- **A5** (investing in general schools lifts the whole cohort, as Portugal showed): needs_more_info — TEIP improvement is moderately evidenced, but transfer to Hungary's earlier selection age (offset vs. mask) is unverifiable without a targeted study.
- **A6** (admission reform is a low-risk, reversible first step that builds trust): value_conflict — whether a modest step builds momentum or substitutes for larger reform is a political-judgement disagreement, not evidence-settleable; the technical levers underneath are separately needs_more_info.
- **A7** (the central exam measures tutoring-driven preparation more than ability): evidence_answerable — a coaching-sensitivity psychometric pilot would settle how much score reflects coaching vs. ability and whether a less-coachable instrument is feasible.
- **A8** (Finland's gains came from a bundle, not structure alone): communication_fixable — the pro-S3 camp already treats bundling as a non-negotiable condition; the concern is answered in substance but must be made legible so structure-only adoption is not mistaken for the reform.
- **A9** (phase-down acceptable only with enforceable teacher guarantees): policy_design_fixable — statutory notice, funded class-size caps, retraining and salary protection are concrete design guarantees that address it; residual dependence on non-state limits remains contested.
- **A10** (compensation without structural change entrenches a two-tier system while appearing to fix it): value_conflict — whether S4 is a bridge or a trap turns on contested values and political durability, not a technical fix; a binding S3-trajectory commitment could partly reduce it but cannot dissolve the value clash.
