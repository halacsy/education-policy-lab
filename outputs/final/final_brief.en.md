## What we know

- The 6-/8-year gimnazium tracks select ~8–12% of a cohort at ages 10 or 12 via centralized examination, with strong skew toward high-SES, urban families [fact — strong, multiply corroborated KSH/Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics].
- Hungary's PISA SES-outcome gradient (~19–25% of variance) is an OECD outlier on the high-inequality end [fact — strong, OECD PISA country notes, cited independently by two experts].
- Hungary's system is fragmented across state (tankerület), church, and foundation maintainers, and this fragmentation constrains the feasibility of any uniform top-down structural mandate; non-state maintainers retain independent admission discretion [fact — strong, corroborated by three experts and Public education act / maintainer statistics].
- Cross-nationally, earlier tracking is associated with higher inequality without a demonstrated aggregate-performance cost — the tradeoff is distributional, not about mean achievement [fact — strong/moderate, Hanushek & Woessmann 2006, corroborated directionally by Finnish and Polish cases; note the registry does not fully back the "without harming mean performance" clause for Hungary specifically].
- No Hungary-specific causal (quasi-experimental) estimate of the selection effect on inequality currently exists; all causal claims about Hungary rely on cross-national generalization or value-added studies with acknowledged limits [fact — evidential absence, flagged independently by three experts].
- Any structural reform faces material, front-loaded transition costs and multi-year timelines regardless of the reform's ultimate merit [fact — moderate, finance/implementation/legal/political experts independently].
- Demographic cohort shrinkage (births down from ~125,000/yr to ~85,000–95,000) is an independent pressure on school-network sustainability operating on a 2020s–2030s timescale that will force some consolidation regardless of selection policy [fact — strong, KSH demographic yearbooks].
- Portugal raised PISA outcomes substantially (2006–2015) through non-structural reform without altering selection age [fact — moderate, OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020].
- Hungary has documented teacher shortages, especially in math/science and in marginalized regions [fact — high confidence per portuguese_reform and implementation_planning experts].

## What we consider likely

- A redesigned "less-coachable" admission assessment would only modestly change who is admitted, because the tracks' advantage is largely a selection effect rather than a coaching-gain effect [estimate — contested; Kertesi & Kezdi 2013 and Horn 2013 cut against strong effect].
- Under a state-only cap regime (S2), non-state maintainers would expand selective intake to absorb displaced demand, relocating rather than eliminating early selection [estimate — the scenario's own documentation tags this "well documented" and "near-certain," though the registry does not directly back the near-certain/negative-equity prediction].
- Under structural abolition (S3), middle-class and church/foundation-school flight would relocate sorting to inter-school choice and non-state enrollment, potentially reconcentrating disadvantage in remaining public comprehensive schools [estimate — moderate; Kertesi & Kezdi 2013, Berényi–Berkovits–Erőss; no Hungary-specific quantitative estimate].
- Compensation (S4) at Hungary's selection age would at best partially narrow the SES gradient and may mask rather than offset stratification [estimate — weak/contested; no comparator combines Hungary's age-10–12 selection with a Portuguese-style package].
- Urban middle-class parents would perceive SES-aware quotas or shrinking selective seats as a threat and mobilize against them [estimate — moderate; domestic press and stakeholder analyses 1990s–2010s].
- A decade-long or two-electoral-cycle reform has no clear Hungarian precedent for surviving without reversal pressure [estimate — moderate; Polish reversal precedent, Jakubowski et al. 2016/OECD].

## Where experts disagree

- **Whether to curtail the tracks at all.** A majority (equity_and_social_mobility, international_comparison, finnish_reform, polish_reform, demography, school_network_planning) favors phasing out or curtailing early selection, citing cross-national tracking-inequality evidence and Hungarian value-added studies [value + estimate]. Against this, portuguese_reform holds the structure should be retained and inequities addressed non-structurally [value]; education_finance and implementation_planning argue for retention at least medium-term because structural consolidation yields no steady-state savings (per-pupil costs are flat across tracks) and teacher shortages make simultaneous restructuring and redistribution "functionally impossible" [fact/estimate].
- **Sequencing and process.** legal_and_governance opposes legislated closure as a first move, requiring an independent equity audit first, because no recent Hungary-specific outcome study grounds the equity claims and maintainer entrenchment makes swift uniform closure unrealistic [value + fact].
- **Causal certainty.** hungarian_education_system holds the amplification-vs-reflection question "cannot be determined from available evidence" (low confidence), while equity/comparison experts treat cross-national DiD patterns as sufficient to justify a policy position despite conceding no Hungary-specific causal estimate exists [assumption vs. estimate].
- **Are non-state maintainers a controllable lever or a structural escape valve?** school_network_planning assumes non-state maintainers will keep selecting and can bypass consolidation; hungarian_education_system holds fragmentation constrains rather than enables predictable behaviour. Unresolved from round 2–3 [assumption vs. assumption].
- **Bundling precondition.** finnish_reform treats teacher-training/diagnostic-support investment as a hard precondition for abolition; polish_reform treats the binding precondition as political (multi-party pacts, compensatory funding), reflecting different diagnoses of Poland's reversal [value + estimate].

## What we don't know

- Whether a valid, gaming-resistant SES proxy usable at application can be built to acceptable false-positive/false-negative rates [known-unknown — no Hungarian administrative student-level SES register usable at application is confirmed to exist; resolvable only by a costed pilot].
- Whether a psychometrically valid, meaningfully less-coachable assessment format exists at national scale [known-unknown — low confidence].
- The magnitude of non-state offset under caps or abolition [known-unknown — depends on maintainer behaviour and enrolment shares not measured under any reformed regime].
- The magnitude and Hungary-specificity of the peer-effect mechanism underlying all equity claims [known-unknown — tagged weak/contested].
- The precise per-cohort/per-district cost of nationwide differentiated-instruction teacher retraining [known-unknown — costed as "high," no unit figure; itself a material planning risk].
- Whether compensation offsets or merely masks early-sorting effects at age 10–12 [known-unknown — "unverifiable ex ante"; no comparator country].
- Whether political ownership survives across electoral cycles [known-unknown — no clear precedent].
- That non-state maintainers will voluntarily align (S1) or not expand (S2) is an *assumption* the arguments need, not a known-unknown — it is contradicted by the governance-fragmentation evidence [assumption].

## What could be done

These are the real alternatives; none is crowned as the answer.

- **S0 — Do nothing (baseline).** Retain current structure and admission rules unchanged. Demographic contraction still forces some network consolidation independently [fact].
- **S1 — Admission reform within the current structure.** Replace the coachable written exam with a less preparation-sensitive assessment and introduce SES-aware quotas/bonus points, operating inside unchanged governance [assumption-dependent on a workable SES proxy].
- **S2 — Gradual phase-down of 6-/8-year entry places.** Cap and progressively reduce state-maintained selective intake with grandfathering, over roughly a decade [estimate — legal mechanics strong; equity mechanism contested].
- **S3 — Comprehensive school to age 14.** Abolish 6-/8-year entry and postpone tracking, ideally bundled with choice-architecture and maintainer-transparency safeguards and a costed teacher-retraining program [fact on cross-national pattern; contested on Hungarian mechanism].
- **S4 — Keep the structure, compensate general schools (Portuguese-style).** TEIP-style targeted funding, curriculum standards, reduced grade retention [moderate on Portuguese precedent; weak on transferability to age-10–12 selection].
- **Cross-cutting options usable with several above:** a costed multi-year pilot with a go/no-go gate; an independent equity audit before legislating; a cross-party pact; statutory/constitutional ring-fencing of targeted funding; a legislated sunset clause converting S4 into a bridge to S1 or S3.

## What each option costs

- **S0:** No transition cost; the SES skew and OECD-outlier gradient persist [fact]. Loser: low-SES/Roma high-ability pupils. Winner: high-SES families and non-state maintainers under the status quo [value].
- **S1:** Assessment redesign and psychometric validation (medium-to-high); SES-proxy infrastructure (high, novel); ongoing admin (low-to-medium) [estimate]. Trade-off: even if it works, gains accrue to low-SES pupils who *already apply*; those who do not apply see no benefit, and reformed-but-scarce channels risk informal selection reproducing the skew [estimate]. Winners: reweighted low-SES applicants; losers: high-SES applicants currently advantaged, and state schools if they carry the reform alone while non-state schools opt out [value].
- **S2:** Parallel dual-regime administration (medium-to-high, sustained most of a decade); monitoring of non-state expansion (medium, currently unbudgeted); staff reallocation (medium) [estimate]. Trade-off: residual selective seats become *more* SES-skewed (mechanical, near-certain) and aggregate equity gain may be minimal or negative if non-state offset occurs [estimate]. Losers: state general-track teachers (workload, job insecurity), public-education families; winners: non-state maintainers absorbing demand [value].
- **S3:** Nationwide teacher retraining (high, uncosted per unit); facility/curriculum re-adaptation (high, front-loaded); legal/transition costs (medium-to-high) [estimate]. Trade-off: largest expected aggregate equity gain *and* largest risk — flight can reconcentrate disadvantage; net effect depends on safeguards not guaranteed by the structural change alone [estimate]. Losers: families relying on early specialized instruction, the intensive talent pipeline, teachers absorbing unfunded heterogeneity; winners: disadvantaged pupils kept in mixed-ability settings *if* safeguards hold [value].
- **S4:** TEIP-style funding (high, likely first fiscal-consolidation cut); curriculum standards/clustering (medium); grade-retention reduction (medium-to-high) [estimate]. Trade-off: leaves the sorting mechanism intact and may legitimize early selection permanently while shifting indefinite remedial workload onto general-school teachers; fiscally fragile [estimate/value]. Winners: selective gimnaziums, non-state maintainers, institutional autonomy; losers: general-track teachers and disadvantaged pupils if funding is cut [value].

## What research could resolve

- A **costed multi-district pilot** constructing and validating an SES proxy and testing gaming-resistance, plus empirical coaching-sensitivity testing of a redesigned assessment — would most change the S1 decision [most decision-relevant].
- **Year-on-year tracking of church/foundation admission numbers** following any cap or abolition — would resolve the magnitude of non-state offset central to S2 and S3 [decision-relevant].
- A **Hungary-specific replication of Hanushek & Woessmann-style analysis** and longitudinal peer-effect studies in Hungarian general schools — would resolve the contested causal mechanism [decision-relevant].
- A **costed national teacher-retraining capacity assessment against current shortage data** — would resolve S3's binding feasibility constraint [decision-relevant].
- A **Hungary-specific compensated-vs-non-compensated pilot on medium-term SES-outcome gradients** — would resolve whether S4 offsets or merely masks [decision-relevant].

## What people must decide

These are value and political choices, not research gaps — naming them is the honest answer.

- **How much equity gain justifies how much disruption and cost** — a distributional value choice no study settles [value].
- **Whether early selection is legitimate-if-bounded or inherently to be removed** — the merit/excellence-vs-equity value clash between konzervativ/digitalisgazdasagi and the reform coalition [value].
- **Whether to constrain non-state maintainer autonomy** to close the offset channel — pits institutional/associational autonomy against system-wide fairness; politically infeasible per kormanyzati_reformrealizmus without ending non-state admission independence [value + political].
- **Whether to accept reversibility (S1/S4) or irreversibility (S2/S3) as a virtue** — a risk-tolerance and political-durability judgment [value].
- **Whether to legislate cross-party pacts, sunset clauses, and constitutional ring-fencing** — the political mechanisms on which most stakeholder conditions depend [political].

Rationale: Portugal's non-structural PISA gains suggest the tracking-age debate misallocates reform attention. Held against a majority favoring curtailment [value].
