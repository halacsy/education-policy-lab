# Policy brief — early selection and the 6/8-year gimnázium

This brief addresses the question: what should Hungary do with early academic selection and the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system? It separates evidence, interpretation, assumptions, recommendations and open questions, preserves expert disagreement, and marks every important claim with an evidence status. It is decision support: no recommendation below is a decision.

## Scenario key (full detail: scenarios.en.md)
- S1 — Admission reform within the current structure
- S2 — Gradual phase-down of 6/8-year entry places
- S3 — Comprehensive school to age 14 (structural reform)
- S4 — Keep the structure, compensate the general schools

## Evidence
- In PISA, the share of reading/maths score variance explained by socio-economic status is among the highest in the OECD for Hungary (~19-25% across cycles vs ~12-14% OECD average). [evidence: strong]
- Cross-country difference-in-differences evidence indicates earlier between-school tracking increases inequality of outcomes without raising mean performance. [evidence: strong]
- Roughly 8-12% of a cohort enters 6- or 8-year gimnazium tracks, selected at age 10 or 12 largely via the central written admission examination; entrants are strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families. [evidence: strong]
- Poland's 1999 reform postponed tracking to age 15 (gimnazjum); PISA scores rose substantially by 2012, with analyses attributing part of the gain to delayed selection. The reform was reversed in 2016-2019 for political reasons. [evidence: moderate]
- Annual births fell from ~125k (early 1990s) to ~85-95k; cohort shrinkage forces school-network consolidation decisions in the 2020s-2030s regardless of selection policy. [evidence: strong]

## Interpretation
- The Hungarian SES gradient is consistent with the causal tracking evidence, but country-level causality is an interpretation, not a measured fact. [interpretation]
- The gimnázium tracks' raw advantage is mostly selection effect; their causal value added is modest. [interpretation, based on moderate evidence]

## Assumptions
- Institutional capacity for any structural reform can be built within a decade. [assumption]
- Published intake data would be accurate and politically survivable. [assumption]

## Recommendations
- Do not choose between scenarios yet: commission the S1 admission pilot and the S4 compensation pilot immediately — they are cheap, reversible, and their results discriminate between the structural and non-structural paths.
- Treat S3 (comprehensive school to 14) as the reference structural option, but gate any legislation on the pilot evaluations, a teacher-capacity audit, and a cross-party feasibility test.
- Whatever path is chosen, publish annual intake-composition data by track and maintainer: every scenario's evaluation depends on it and it costs almost nothing.

## Minority positions
- (portuguese_reform, political_feasibility) A non-structural package (standards, targeted support) is preferable: comparable gains, far lower political risk. — Portugal's improvement without changing selection age; Poland's reversal.
- (equity_and_social_mobility) Cap new entries immediately; every delayed cohort pays the equity cost. — Each admission cycle sorts ~10% of a cohort irreversibly.
- (political_feasibility, hungarian_education_system) Even if gains are partly positional, the forms answer a real demand for demanding academic tracks; suppressing them shifts demand to private exit. — Parental attachment evidence; growth of non-state maintainers.
- (political_feasibility) Bundling couples the reform to unpopular school closures and doubles the opposition coalition. — Closure politics historically dominates local media cycles.

## Open questions (for human judgment)
- How should equity gains be weighed against risks to high-achiever outcomes and against parental choice? This weighting determines the S3-vs-S4 ranking and is a values question the system must not answer.
- Is a Polish-style reversal risk acceptable if the expected equity gain is large? Only an accountable decision-maker can accept this risk.
- Is early selection genuinely politically immovable, or is that assumption itself a choice? The devil_advocate flagged this as a foreclosing premise.
