## Evidence

- Early selection to 6/8-year gimnazium tracks (entry age 10–12) is heavily concentrated among high-SES, urban families [evidence: strong]
- Hungary's SES-achievement gradient is exceptionally high: 19–25% of PISA variance vs. 12–14% OECD average [evidence: strong]
- Much of the measured gimnazium achievement advantage is a selection effect rather than causal value-added [evidence: moderate]
- Maintainer fragmentation (state tankerület vs. church/foundation) prevents uniform top-down structural mandates [evidence: strong]
- No Hungary-specific study isolates the causal contribution of track existence net of choice/catchment effects [evidence: absent/low — noted across multiple expert inputs]
- Structural reform carries high transition costs (staff retraining, building repurposing, curriculum alignment) that international experience suggests exceed steady-state savings by 3–5x over 5–10 years, dominating modest steady-state per-pupil funding differences [evidence: moderate]
- Any structural reform requires a 6–8 year minimum implementation timeline due to statutory anchoring and enrolled-pupil guarantees [evidence: strong]
- Early between-school tracking causally increases SES achievement gradients without raising mean performance, in cross-country designs [evidence: strong]
- Poland postponed tracking from age 11 to 15 in 1999; PISA scores rose substantially in post-reform cohorts, though this is confounded with concurrent changes [evidence: moderate]
- Poland reversed delayed tracking in 2016–2019 for political/ideological reasons despite the PISA evidence [evidence: moderate]
- Finnish equity gains under comprehensive schooling are entangled with teacher policy and support systems, so structure alone is not a transparent cause [evidence: moderate]
- Portugal achieved +49 PISA points (reading) and +40 (math), 2006–2015, without raising selection age, via curriculum standardisation, school clustering, and targeted support (TEIP) [evidence: strong]
- Portugal reduced grade retention from ~35% (2006) to ~15% (2015) via diagnostic early intervention rather than system-wide streaming [evidence: moderate]
- No randomised trial compares delayed selection vs. early selection + targeted support in Central European contexts [evidence: weak]
- Affluent urban parents treat gimnazium places as mobility/insurance assets and are likely to mobilise against changes reducing perceived certainty of access [evidence: moderate]
- Total birth-cohort contraction of ~25–30% by 2035–2040 is structurally locked in and independent of selection policy choice [evidence: strong, per demography expert input]

## Interpretation

- The descriptive stratification pattern is settled across expert inputs, but causal attribution of that stratification to track existence (as opposed to choice, catchment, or maintainer effects) is not [interpretation]
- The four scenarios (S1 admission reform, S2 gradual phase-down, S3 comprehensive schooling to 14, S4 Portuguese-style compensation) represent genuinely different bets on where leverage lies — entry criteria, pace of structural change, full structural removal, or non-structural compensation — and expert inputs disagree on which bet is best supported, not merely on implementation detail [interpretation]
- International analogues (Finland, Poland, Portugal) are offered as partial, non-substitutable evidence: each supports a different pathway (S3/structure, S2/pacing+reversal-risk, S4/non-structural), and none has been validated for Hungary's specific institutional configuration [interpretation]
- Structural reform (S3) and phased reform (S2) share the same underlying causal logic (early tracking amplifies SES gradients) but diverge on political durability — the phased approach may reduce the abruptness of conflict or may simply prolong it, and expert inputs treat this as genuinely open rather than resolved [interpretation]
- Non-structural compensation (S4) and admission-only reform (S1) share exposure to a "durability trap": both add mechanisms or investments on top of the existing structure without removing the selective gatekeeper, risking absorption of political capital without proportionate equity gains [interpretation]
- Maintainer fragmentation is a cross-cutting constraint on all four scenarios, not specific to any one: church/foundation non-cooperation could blunt S1 (admission criteria), S2 (cap enforcement), and S3 (conversion negotiation) alike [interpretation]
- Demographic contraction is best read as a constraint that determines who bears consolidation pressure under each scenario, not as an independent argument for or against any specific reform pathway [interpretation]

## Assumptions

- SES-proportional or validity-improved admission criteria can be defined and enforced across fragmented maintainers (state, church, foundation) [assumption]
- Test-prep inequality is a substantial component of the SES gap in selection success, rather than underlying prior achievement [assumption — unverified]
- Staged reform (S2) reduces reversal risk relative to abrupt change; this is untested and may instead extend the period of conflict [assumption]
- Teacher recruitment/retraining can be front-loaded during the 2025–2035 retirement window at unquantified cost [assumption]
- Elite pupils will not exit to private/international pathways at a rate that undermines the equity aim of S2 or S3 [assumption]
- The Hanushek & Woessmann timing findings generalise to Hungary despite differences in school-quality variance, teacher composition, and curriculum coherence [assumption — untested inference]
- Political ownership of a structural reform (S3) survives two electoral cycles — in tension with the political-feasibility view that no durable supermajority/consensus window is assured [assumption]
- Hungary's teacher workforce and support capacity can reach sufficiency with necessary complementary investment under S3 [assumption]
- Catchment and school-choice rules are reformed simultaneously with any track-side reform (S1, S2, S3), without which stratification is displaced rather than removed [assumption]
- Compensation under S4 can offset, rather than merely mask, the equity costs of early selection — this is unverifiable ex ante since no country evidence exists at Hungary's selection age [assumption]
- Portugal's TEIP model is transferable to Hungarian institutional contexts despite different labour markets and teacher supply [assumption]
- Hungary's higher intergenerational persistence may require stronger dosing than Portugal's higher-equity baseline under S4 [assumption]

## Recommendations

These recommendations address process and evidence-generation, not a choice among S1–S4; expert inputs disagree on which scenario is preferable and that disagreement is preserved rather than resolved here.

- Commission Hungary-specific evaluation before committing to any structural pathway: no curated dataset currently isolates the causal contribution of track existence net of choice/catchment effects, and this gap constrains confidence in S1, S2, and S3 alike.
- Whatever pathway is chosen, pair any track-side reform (S1 admission changes, S2 phase-down, or S3 abolition) with simultaneous catchment and school-choice reform; expert inputs converge that track reform alone will likely displace rather than reduce stratification.
- Address maintainer fragmentation explicitly and early: negotiate church/foundation participation (or document non-cooperation) before finalising design of S1, S2, or S3, since all three depend on maintainer cooperation that is not currently assured.
- If a non-structural pathway (S4) is pursued, commission a comparative costing study of a Hungarian TEIP-equivalent programme before scale-up, since dosage and unit cost are currently unknown and the compensatory claim is unverified.
- If a phased or structural pathway (S2 or S3) is pursued, budget and pilot teacher retraining with cost accounting before nationwide commitment; current cost estimates are absent or unquantified.
- Build in monitoring by SES quartile and region from the outset of any reform, so that displacement effects (e.g., migration to exempt maintainers or private schools) are detected early rather than discovered after the 6–8 year minimum implementation window.
- Treat demographic contraction as a planning input for capacity and staffing across all scenarios, not as a justification for any particular selection policy.
- Where political durability is uncertain (S2, S3), consider design features that raise the cost of reversal for a successor government; no scenario currently includes such a feature, and this gap should be treated as a design task rather than resolved by assumption.

## Open questions

- How much of the SES gap in selection success reflects test-prep inequality vs. underlying prior achievement? [confidence: low]
- What is the causal contribution of track existence to overall inequality, net of choice and catchment effects? [confidence: low]
- Will churches/foundations cooperate with a common admission or cap framework, or resist and fragment the system further? [confidence: medium]
- What proportion of middle-class parent preference for gimnazium is pedagogically motivated vs. signalling-driven? [confidence: low]
- Does staged reform (S2) reduce reversal risk, or does it merely extend the conflict period? [confidence: low]
- What is the elasticity of parental exit to private/international schools under phase-down or abolition? [confidence: medium]
- What mechanism actually drove the Finnish equity effect — structure, teacher policy, or support systems? [confidence: low]
- Can mixed-ability teaching at scale maintain academic rigor for high achievers in the Hungarian context? [confidence: medium]
- Can political ownership of a structural reform survive two electoral cycles given documented reversal risk (Poland)? [confidence: low]
- What is the actual transition-phase budget requirement for structural or phased reform (estimated ~7–15 billion HUF, unconfirmed)? [confidence: low]
- Does Hungarian early selection causally lower performance in non-selected students, or is selection endogenous to prior attainment? [confidence: medium]
- What is the unit cost and required dosage of a Hungarian TEIP-equivalent programme? [confidence: low]
- Which stakeholder coalition would fund and sustain a multi-year targeted-support programme if selection is preserved (S4)? [confidence: low]
- How would regional heterogeneity in selection intensity and demographic contraction interact with any chosen reform? [confidence: medium]

These questions require human judgment — political-economy weighting of reversal risk, willingness to accept unquantified transition costs, and the relative value placed on structural certainty versus incremental, evidence-generating steps are not resolvable from the evidence base alone.
