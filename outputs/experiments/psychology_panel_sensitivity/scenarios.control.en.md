# Policy scenarios

## S1 — Admissions and intake-equity regulation (keep structure, fix the gate)
**Goal**
Keep the 6/8-year gimnázium structure intact but regulate the admission mechanism itself — SES-aware quotas or proxies, uniform score-threshold rules, coaching-resistant exam design — to reduce family-capital-driven sorting at the point of entry.

**Mechanism**
- Uniform, legally-binding minimum-score or quota rules applied consistently across state, church and foundation maintainers would reduce discretionary, maintainer-specific gatekeeping observed in 2025/2026 admissions (e.g. 70-point cutoffs enforced at some schools, ignored or retroactively enforced at others). [evidence: moderate]
- Coaching-resistant exam redesign could reduce the compounding effect of paid test preparation documented as a barrier even for talented low-SES children, though the magnitude of this effect is not quantified in Hungarian evidence. [evidence: weak]
- SES-aware admission quotas or proxies, modeled loosely on international affirmative-admission designs, are expected to shift the composition of admitted cohorts toward lower average family-background advantage without changing the total number of selective places. [evidence: weak]

**Evidence status**
weak — No Hungarian evidence directly tests SES-aware admission-rule redesign in the 6/8-year gimnázium context; the mechanism draws on international analogues and general segregation literature, not a Hungarian intervention study.

**Assumptions**
- Admission-equity rules can, in principle, be designed to apply consistently across state, church, and foundation maintainers, though the expert record documents maintainer plurality, partial legal autonomy for church admissions under concordat-style agreements, and no current national legal basis for score thresholds — so uniform enforceability itself is an open, contested precondition rather than a settled fact. [assumption]
- Coaching-resistant exam design is technically achievable within a reasonable timeframe without also reducing the exam's ability to differentiate by ability. [assumption]
- Regulating admission mechanics alone, without touching track supply or catchment/choice rules, will not simply displace sorting into pre-exam self-selection or into unregulated aspects of the process. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Preserves the approximately 10% competency-gain association documented for 6/8-year track entrants relative to similar-ability peers in 4-year tracks, since track structure and total place numbers are unchanged. [evidence: moderate]
- May reduce the historically documented rejection-rate gap between low-poverty and high-poverty-share classes (46% vs 22%), if score thresholds and quotas are enforced uniformly rather than left to maintainer discretion. [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
Equity gains are plausible but conditional and partial: reducing coaching-driven and maintainer-discretionary sorting at the exact moment of admission could narrow the documented rejection-rate gap between low- and high-poverty classes, but the scenario does not touch total selective capacity, catchment, school choice, or self-selection before the exam — all channels the expert record identifies as independently sufficient to reproduce SES sorting even if the admission rule itself were made perfectly fair. Because disadvantaged-composition primary schools already show near-zero attempt rates at the entrance exam, an equity-fairer gate may still admit a barely-changed population unless paired with outreach or catchment measures outside this scenario's scope.

**Cost categories**
- SES-proxy infrastructure and data systems: a low-cost option (self-declared/means-tested proxy) versus a high-cost option (linked administrative data infrastructure); no numeric range published for either in Hungary.
- Exam redesign and coaching-resistance testing/validation: magnitude unpublished in Hungarian sources; comparable OECD item-design and psychometric validation programmes typically run multi-year with recurring costs, but no Hungary-specific cost study exists.
- Legal/administrative enforcement cost across maintainer types (state, church, foundation): unbudgeted, includes negotiation with maintainer federations and potential compensation for lost maintainer discretion; no published estimate.

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education (with Oktatási Hivatal) — Draft a uniform statutory framework for admission thresholds and SES-aware proxies, opening formal negotiation with church and foundation maintainer federations on legal autonomy boundaries. (timeline: year 1-2)
- Ministry of Education / Educational Authority (Oktatási Hivatal) — Pilot coaching-resistant exam item design in a subset of regions, with independent psychometric validation before national rollout. (timeline: year 1-3)
- Klebelsberg Center / maintainer federations — Implement uniform score-threshold and quota enforcement across state schools first, followed by negotiated adoption timelines for church and foundation maintainers under felmenő-rendszer (grade-by-grade) phase-in. (timeline: year 2-4)
- Independent research institute (e.g. Oktatáskutató és Fejlesztő Intézet successor body) — Publish annual disaggregated admission statistics by SES proxy and maintainer type to monitor whether the rejection-rate gap narrows. (timeline: year 2 onward)

**Political risks**
- Fidesz and allied actors could frame SES-aware quotas as reverse discrimination against merit-based admission, mobilizing the same 'ideological interference' framing already used against the government's June 2026 review announcement — without a specified legally entrenched instrument (e.g. cardinal law), a change enacted by decree remains reversible by a future government.
- Church and foundation maintainers, protected by the coalition's explicit commitment to preserve 'previous operating conditions,' may resist or slow-walk uniform threshold enforcement, producing the same two-tier inconsistency (enforced at some institutions, not others) already observed in 2025.
- Fiscal consolidation pressure (projected 6.2% deficit in 2026) threatens funding for SES-proxy data infrastructure and exam redesign, both currently unbudgeted and vulnerable as discretionary line items.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether admission-equity rules can actually be enforced consistently across state, church, and foundation maintainers given documented maintainer plurality and partial legal autonomy is unresolved. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Mapping the precise legal autonomy clauses in current church/foundation maintainer agreements regarding admission criteria, and interviews with maintainer federation counsel.)
- Whether a fairer admission gate alone would meaningfully change who even attempts the exam, given near-zero attempt rates from disadvantaged-composition primary schools. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Outreach-intervention pilot studies measuring exam-attempt rate changes among disadvantaged-composition primary schools before and after combined outreach plus gate reform.)
- The true causal magnitude of the 10% competency-gain figure attributed to 6/8-year tracks remains unreplicated and could reflect peer-composition or teacher-sorting confounds rather than genuine pedagogical value-added. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Independent replication using admission-cutoff regression discontinuity design with richer teacher-quality and peer-composition controls.)

## S2 — Supply-side contraction of the selective track (shrink the gate, not the rules)
**Goal**
Reduce the state-sector share of 6/8-year places via score thresholds, capacity caps, or demographically-driven consolidation, shrinking the selective track's footprint while leaving admission criteria within remaining places largely unchanged.

**Mechanism**
- The Klebelsberg Center-set minimum-score thresholds are already being used as a supply-side lever to compress the gimnázium track's share of cohort intake, independent of demand, though the registry's strong-evidence baseline places 6/8-year tracks at roughly 8-12% of a cohort, not 40%; the 40%-toward-30% figure in earlier framing referred to all-gimnázium (including 4-year) share of upper-secondary enrolment (45.4%), not the 6/8-year segment specifically. [evidence: moderate]
- Shrinking state-sector selective capacity via thresholds may incrementally reduce urban SES-skew in the state-maintained segment specifically, but the expert record shows church-maintained gimnázium share already grew from 10.4% (2010) to approximately 27% (2020/2021), indicating a documented offsetting channel rather than a hypothetical risk. [evidence: strong]
- Demographic decline (births falling from ~125,000 in the early 1990s to ~72,000 in 2025) will independently force network consolidation, meaning active score-threshold contraction compounds rather than substitutes for a contraction that is already underway. [evidence: strong]

**Evidence status**
moderate — The core mechanism (score thresholds as a discretionary maintainer-level supply lever) is moderately evidenced by contemporaneous reporting, but the displacement risk to church/foundation providers is strongly evidenced by historical maintainer-share data, so the scenario's net effect is uncertain despite well-evidenced component parts.

**Assumptions**
- State-sector score thresholds or capacity caps can shrink selective provision, but the expert record shows church/foundation gimnázium share already grew from 10.4% to ~27% (2010-2020/21) even before this lever was formalized, so absent an explicit, enforced counter-lever on non-state maintainers, offsetting expansion is the historically documented default outcome, not a residual risk. [assumption]
- Contracting the state-sector selective footprint will translate into reduced overall selective-track intake nationally, rather than simply reallocating the same total selective capacity toward less-regulated maintainers. [assumption]
- Demographically-driven and policy-driven contraction can be sequenced or combined without the score-threshold lever itself becoming legally or administratively vulnerable to challenge, given it currently has no uniform national legal basis. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- May incrementally improve peer composition in urban general-school upper grades within the state sector specifically, as fewer high-performing, typically higher-SES students are siphoned into state-maintained selective tracks — though this benefit does not extend to the system as a whole if displaced demand simply shifts to church/foundation tracks. [evidence: weak]
- Aligns supply reduction with an already-occurring demographic contraction, potentially reducing the political salience of 'attacking elite schools' by framing shrinkage as a natural consequence of falling cohort sizes rather than deliberate policy. [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
The equity_impact is genuinely uncertain and likely mixed rather than net-positive: shrinking state-sector selective capacity may narrow SES-skew within the state segment, but the strongest countervailing evidence — church-maintained gimnázium share's growth from 10.4% to ~27% (2010-2020/21), well before formal score-threshold policy — shows displaced demand has a documented historical channel into a maintainer sector that received disproportionate funding-and-building advantages post-2020 and is explicitly protected by the current governing coalition's commitment to preserve 'previous operating conditions.' Residual state-sector selective places may see improved SES composition, while total system-wide selectivity and segregation could remain unchanged or worsen if displaced advantaged families migrate to church/foundation or geographically relocate, a pathway the scenario does not counteract.

**Cost categories**
- Compensatory funding or capacity controls extended to church/foundation maintainers to prevent offsetting expansion: flagged as medium-to-high, multi-year, currently unbudgeted; no quantitative estimate published, and no modelling exists of the timing mismatch between state contraction and any non-state counter-lever's implementation lag.
- Administrative cost of monitoring and enforcing score thresholds absent a uniform national legal basis, including likely legal challenges from affected maintainers and families.
- Displacement-monitoring infrastructure cost (tracking whether contracted state places are absorbed by church/foundation/private growth or geographic relocation): currently non-existent and unbudgeted.

**Implementation steps**
- Klebelsberg Center (Klebelsberg Központ) — Formalize minimum-score thresholds for state-maintained 6/8-year tracks into a documented, consistent national policy rather than institution-by-institution discretion, given the current lack of uniform legal basis. (timeline: year 1-2)
- Ministry of Education — Design and legislate a counter-lever (funding condition, capacity cap, or building-transfer restriction) applicable to church and foundation maintainers, addressing the documented historical offsetting-expansion pathway before, not after, state contraction proceeds. (timeline: year 1-3)
- Independent research institute / Oktatási Hivatal — Establish displacement-monitoring infrastructure to track whether contracted state places are absorbed by non-state growth, publishing annual maintainer-disaggregated intake-share statistics. (timeline: year 2-4)

**Political risks**
- Church and foundation maintainers, protected by the coalition's explicit '12-point' commitment to preserve previous operating conditions, are likely to resist any counter-lever restricting their capacity to absorb displaced demand, creating a coalition-management risk that could stall the counter-lever component while state contraction proceeds unilaterally.
- Score-threshold policy currently has no uniform national legal basis and is applied inconsistently by individual maintainers; without statutory codification, the lever remains vulnerable to legal challenge and to reversal by a future government or even the current one under political pressure.
- Framing contraction as demographically-driven rather than policy-driven risks public backlash if parents perceive the score-threshold mechanism as an active government lever disguised as a passive demographic response, undermining the scenario's own political-messaging assumption.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether state-sector contraction will be net-neutralized by church/foundation/private-sector expansion or geographic relocation of advantaged families, given the documented historical growth pattern predates formal score-threshold policy. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Annual maintainer-disaggregated intake-share statistics tracking whether contracted state places correlate with non-state sector growth over a multi-year window.)
- Whether church and foundation maintainers can be regulated or fiscally incentivized not to expand selective places, given no evidence-based pathway for doing so currently exists and the governing coalition has committed to preserving their operating conditions. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A negotiated funding-condition or capacity-cap framework tested in at least one region, with published before/after intake-share data disaggregated by maintainer.)
- The precise current national-level share of pupils in 6/8-grade tracks and its trend is uncertain; the 8-12% (registry) vs 40%→30% (policy-discourse) figures refer to different denominators and have not been reconciled in a single authoritative dataset. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Official KIFIR/Oktatási Hivatal admissions statistics disaggregated by track type (6-year, 8-year, 4-year gimnázium) and maintainer, published consistently for multiple cycles.)

## S3 — Structural delay of the selection age (Finnish/Polish model)
**Goal**
Raise the statutory age of first academic tracking (e.g., to 14+ or later), phasing out or converting 6- and 8-year entry points into a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase, paired with explicit anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment.

**Mechanism**
- Poland's 1999 reform (delaying selection from age 12/15 to 15/16) produced PISA gains of 0.25 SD mathematics, 0.28 SD reading, 0.16 SD science by 2006, tagged [moderate] in the curated registry rather than [strong], reflecting unresolved causal-decomposition questions about resource/composition effects versus the timing change itself. [evidence: moderate]
- Finland's 1972-1977 reform delaying tracking from age 11 to 16 reduced intergenerational income elasticity by 23% (0.30 to 0.23), with gains driven primarily by cognitive-skill improvements among disadvantaged pupils rather than uniform gains across all pupils. [evidence: strong]
- Delaying selection alone, without anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment, is documented to be insufficient: Finland's own post-2000 equity gains eroded through school-choice and residential sorting even though formal tracking was never reintroduced, and within-school variance (94% of total) shows choice recreates segregation inside ostensibly comprehensive schools. [evidence: strong]

**Evidence status**
moderate — The Finnish mobility-gain evidence is strong, but the Poland baseline is tagged moderate in the registry, and the scenario's own load-bearing anti-sorting co-requirement is supported only by evidence of the problem (choice-driven re-segregation), not evidence that the proposed co-requirement solves it in a Hungarian setting.

**Assumptions**
- Anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment can be designed and enforced simultaneously with structural delay so that sorting does not migrate to unregulated channels — but this is optimistic given Finland's own post-2000 experience shows choice-driven within-school segregation (94% of variance) re-emerged without formal tracking, and Hungary's baseline segregation and school-choice culture (szabad iskolaválasztás) are already stronger starting conditions than Finland's were in the 1970s. [assumption]
- Hungary's 8- and 6-year grammar school structure is functionally analogous enough to Finland's pre-1972 age-11 tracking and Poland's pre-1999 age-12/15 tracking that similar effect sizes would transfer, despite differing baseline segregation levels and demographic composition. [assumption]
- The teacher workforce can be expanded, retrained, and redeployed within the scenario's implementation window despite documented acute shortages (16,000+ vacancies in 2025/26) and an aging profile (50% of primary teachers 50+, only 7.2% under 30). [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- International difference-in-differences evidence indicates delaying selection from age 10-12 to 14+ would plausibly reduce Hungary's socioeconomic achievement gap, drawing on comparable Polish gains, though the Hungary-specific magnitude is not established. [evidence: moderate]
- Poland's between-school equity improved markedly after 1999 (from one of the largest OECD disparities to one of the smallest), suggesting structural delay can compress between-school variance even where individual-level SES correlation with outcomes does not improve. [evidence: strong]
- Hungary's own 2000s comprehensivization (extending unified basic schooling to 9 years) raised the performance of students who would otherwise have entered vocational tracks without lowering grammar-track performance, offering a domestic precedent that removing an early selection point need not create an achievement trade-off. [evidence: strong]

**Equity impact**
The equity case for structural delay is the strongest in the evidence base (Finnish mobility gains, Polish between-school convergence, Hungary's own comprehensivization precedent), but it is not unconditional: anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment are a load-bearing, operational requirement, not a background assumption, because Finland's own post-2000 experience shows equity gains erode when choice and residential sorting are left unaddressed, and Hungary's starting segregation (highest in the EU) and entrenched free-school-choice culture make the migration-of-sorting risk more acute than in either historical case. Individual-level SES-outcome correlation did not improve in Poland even as between-school disparities shrank, indicating structural delay alone compresses between-school inequality but does not guarantee individual-level mobility gains without complementary measures this scenario must specify and enforce concurrently, not sequentially.

**Cost categories**
- Early diagnostic/remedial support for the newly extended comprehensive phase: high-cost category, no aggregate fiscal range published for Hungary.
- Teacher retraining and reallocation: high-cost category given acute existing shortages (16,000+ vacancies) and an aging workforce; no per-teacher retraining cost estimate exists in Hungarian sources, and comparable OECD retraining programmes vary widely by scope.
- Facility conversion (converting 6/8-year track buildings and dormitories to comprehensive-phase use): high-cost, no published estimate; school-network planning evidence indicates physical capacity is not the binding constraint nationally, but conversion costs are still unquantified.
- Anti-sorting enforcement (school-choice and catchment regulation): high-cost, ongoing administrative and legal category; no aggregate estimate exists, and this is the scenario's single most under-costed load-bearing component given its centrality to the equity claim.
- Aggregate fiscal range across all categories: not published; a dedicated Hungary-specific costing study (as identified as a gap by multiple expert domains) does not currently exist, making total transition cost, per-teacher retraining cost, and transitional-versus-steady-state split all unknown.

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education / Parliament — Legislate a statutory delay of first academic tracking, structured as a felmenő-rendszer (grade-by-grade, cohort-by-cohort) phase-in per Constitutional Court doctrine and past Nkt. amendment precedent (~1-year minimum lead time), simultaneously legislating anti-sorting school-choice and catchment rules rather than as a follow-up measure. (timeline: year 1)
- Ministry of Education / regional teacher-training universities — Begin teacher retraining and workforce-stabilization measures sequenced explicitly after the 2024-2031 salary-reform trajectory shows measurable retention improvement, rather than concurrent with the structural delay's first cohort, given documented acute shortages and CPD bandwidth already committed to NAT and AI-integration reforms. (timeline: year 2-6)
- School maintainers (state, church, foundation) / Klebelsberg Center — Convert or repurpose 6/8-year track facilities and dormitories toward the extended comprehensive-phase use, region by region, prioritizing areas with existing dormitory slack capacity identified in school-network planning evidence. (timeline: year 3-8)
- Independent research institute / Educational Authority (Oktatási Hivatal) — Publish annual monitoring reports tracking whether anti-sorting rules are preventing sorting migration to school-choice and catchment channels, as a precondition for continuing the phase-in to subsequent cohorts. (timeline: year 2 onward)

**Political risks**
- Poland's 2016-2019 reversal after 18 years, despite documented achievement gains, demonstrates structural delay is vulnerable to becoming a partisan identity marker rather than an evidence-driven policy; without cardinal-law or supermajority entrenchment — not currently proposed in Hungarian political discourse — the same reversal risk applies here, especially given Fidesz's immediate 'totalitarian/communism' framing of the government's June 2026 review announcement.
- Anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment directly confront szabad iskolaválasztás (free school choice), a politically entrenched and popular feature; enacting enforceable restrictions simultaneously with structural delay compounds political resistance rather than sequencing it, raising the risk that the anti-sorting component is diluted or dropped during legislative negotiation while the structural delay proceeds — reproducing exactly the Finnish post-2000 erosion pattern.
- PSZ (largest teacher union) has framed workforce adequacy as a non-negotiable precondition, not a parallel-track item; sequencing teacher retraining after the structural delay's first cohort (as this scenario proposes to respect current shortage constraints) risks union opposition if the union interprets any first-cohort implementation absent workforce guarantees as reform proceeding without its precondition met.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment can be designed and enforced in a way that actually prevents sorting migration, given Finland's own post-2000 experience shows such migration occurred despite no formal tracking reintroduction. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A Hungary-specific pilot region implementing structural delay plus enforceable anti-sorting rules simultaneously, with published before/after segregation and mobility metrics compared to non-pilot regions.)
- Whether the Polish and Finnish effect sizes would replicate in Hungary given Hungary's higher baseline segregation, denser small-school network, and stronger school-choice culture than either historical comparator had at the time of their reforms. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A quasi-experimental regional pilot within Hungary comparing delayed-selection cohorts to propensity-matched control cohorts on standardized achievement and segregation measures over 5-7 years.)
- Whether the teacher workforce can realistically be expanded and retrained within any feasible implementation window given acute existing shortages and an aging profile, independent of the sequencing proposed in this scenario's implementation steps. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: A detailed Ministry-commissioned costing and feasibility study modelling teacher retraining, recruitment, and CPD-bandwidth allocation across three phase-in timeframes (1-year, 3-year, 5-year), benchmarked against the 2024-2031 salary-reform trajectory's measured retention effects.)

## S4 — Non-structural compensatory reform (leave selection age unchanged)
**Goal**
Pursue equity and achievement gains through curriculum clarity, targeted (TEIP-style) funding for disadvantaged schools, retention reduction, and school-quality levelling, leaving the 10-12 selection age and track structure untouched, following the Portuguese model.

**Mechanism**
- Portugal improved PISA results by approximately 31 points (0.3 SD) between 2000 and 2015-2018 without introducing early tracking, through curriculum standards clarification, increased school autonomy, regular assessment, and vocational pathway development. [evidence: strong]
- TEIP (Portugal's targeted school-support programme) reduced dropout and grade retention but did not close competency gaps relative to non-TEIP schools, and no rigorous longitudinal evaluation of long-term student outcomes exists, meaning the mechanism is evidenced for access/retention outcomes but not for closing achievement gaps. [evidence: moderate]
- Retention-reduction reform in Portugal (2016-2018) simultaneously reduced retained students by 26% across 663 schools and saved EUR 112 million, demonstrating that at least one component of the compensatory bundle can be net-cost-saving rather than purely cost-adding, though this is a Portugal-specific fiscal result not yet modelled for Hungary. [evidence: strong]

**Evidence status**
moderate — Portugal's aggregate achievement gains and the retention-reduction fiscal result are strongly evidenced, but TEIP's ability to close competency gaps (as opposed to reducing dropout) is only moderately evidenced, and no component has been costed or effect-tested in the Hungarian context.

**Assumptions**
- TEIP-style targeted funding commitments will be durable multi-year fiscal lines, but the scenario's own political-feasibility evidence documents fiscal consolidation pressure (projected 6.2% deficit in 2026, rising debt to 76.8% of GDP by 2027) and identifies targeted-funding lines as likely first-line cuts under consolidation, making durability itself an open, contested assumption rather than a design detail. [assumption]
- Portugal's comprehensive-system outcomes are transferable to Hungary's tracked system despite the two systems differing in the presence of an early selection point itself — the mechanism assumes non-structural levers can deliver comparable gains even where the underlying structural driver of inequality (early tracking) remains in place. [assumption]
- Curriculum clarity, school autonomy, and assessment reform effects are additive to, not conditional on, the existing selection structure, so their benefits will not be substantially dampened or reversed by continued SES-driven sorting at age 10-12. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Retention reduction, modeled on Portugal's 2016-2018 result, could be net-cost-saving in Hungary while reducing dropout, though the fiscal magnitude has not been estimated for the Hungarian system. [evidence: weak]
- Curriculum clarity and school-quality levelling could reduce the between-school achievement variance documented as exceptionally high in Hungary, independent of any change to selection-age structure. [evidence: weak]
- Avoids the legal, administrative, and political costs of structural reform (felmenő-rendszer phase-in, maintainer renegotiation, facility conversion) documented as substantial in S1-S3, since the track structure itself is untouched. [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
Equity_impact is plausibly equity-neutral-to-modestly-positive but leaves the primary documented driver of Hungary's exceptionally high segregation — the 10-12 selection gate itself — entirely intact; TEIP-style funding and quality-levelling may narrow within-track and between-non-selective-school gaps but do not address the near-total exclusion of multiply-disadvantaged pupils from elite 6/8-year tracks or the compositional sorting occurring at the point of entry. Because low-SES high-performing pupils inside the existing gatekeeping system are the group most likely to benefit from marginal quality improvements to non-selective schools, but are also the group whose access to selective tracks remains structurally blocked by self-selection and coaching-resistance barriers this scenario does not touch, the net distributional effect depends heavily on whether the scenario is read as a full alternative to structural reform or as a complement to it — the expert record supports the latter more than the former.

**Cost categories**
- TEIP-style targeted funding for disadvantaged schools: Portugal's programme scale is documented but not translated into a Hungary-specific per-school or aggregate cost estimate; the EUR 112 million Portuguese retention-reduction saving over two years is a distinct, separate fiscal result and cannot be assumed to offset targeted-funding costs.
- Retention-reduction programme design and rollout: potentially net-cost-saving based on the Portuguese precedent, but no Hungary-specific estimate exists of implementation cost versus savings, nor of the retention baseline in Hungary against which savings would be measured.
- Curriculum clarity and school-autonomy reform administrative cost: unquantified; likely lower than structural-reform categories in S1-S3 but not zero, given curriculum redesign and school-leadership training requirements.

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education — Design a TEIP-style targeted-funding framework for disadvantaged-composition schools, explicitly modelled on Portuguese programme design but with a Hungary-specific costing study preceding legislative commitment, given fiscal consolidation risk to durability. (timeline: year 1-2)
- Ministry of Education / school leadership associations — Pilot retention-reduction measures (assessment reform, remedial support in place of grade repetition) in a subset of schools, establishing a Hungary-specific retention baseline and cost/savings estimate before national rollout. (timeline: year 1-3)
- National Core Curriculum (NAT) body / Ministry of Education — Sequence curriculum-clarity reform after the current NAT-update and AI-integration roadmap (due 31 March 2026) to avoid CPD-bandwidth conflict, given documented teacher retraining capacity constraints. (timeline: year 2-4)
- Independent research institute — Conduct a longitudinal matched-cohort evaluation of TEIP-style funding's effect on competency gaps (not just dropout/retention), addressing the documented evidence gap in the Portuguese model itself. (timeline: year 3-8)

**Political risks**
- Fiscal consolidation pressure (projected 6.2% deficit in 2026, rising debt to 76.8% of GDP by 2027) directly threatens the durability of TEIP-style targeted funding lines, which stakeholders elsewhere in the record identify as likely first-line cuts under consolidation; no statutory (cardinal-law) funding floor has been proposed or shown politically achievable for this scenario's core mechanism.
- Because this scenario leaves the politically charged selection-age question untouched, it risks being adopted as a low-political-cost substitute for structural reform by a government seeking to appear responsive to EU segregation-pressure without confronting the more contested S1-S3 options, potentially entrenching rather than resolving the underlying equity problem.
- Teacher union (PSZ) framing positions workforce adequacy as the binding constraint on any reform, including non-structural ones requiring retraining or new pedagogical approaches (retention-reduction, curriculum-clarity); this scenario is not automatically exempt from the same CPD-bandwidth and shortage constraints documented for structural alternatives.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether TEIP-style funding can close competency gaps rather than merely reducing dropout remains uncertain, since no longitudinal study has tracked student competency trajectories beyond retention reduction even in Portugal itself. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: A 5-8 year longitudinal matched-cohort study comparing TEIP-enrolled and non-enrolled students, controlling for baseline SES and prior achievement, tracking assessed competency gain rather than exam pass rates.)
- The fiscal and pedagogical durability of statutory TEIP-style funding commitments under projected multi-year fiscal consolidation is unresolved; whether such guarantees are achievable in forthcoming budget cycles remains unknown. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Publication of the 2027 budget bill text showing either a cardinal-law funding-floor commitment or temporary, deficit-contingent compensation language, plus EC fiscal guidance on permissible spending commitments.)
- Whether non-structural reforms alone, without touching the selection gate, can meaningfully reduce Hungary's exceptionally high between-school segregation, or whether the selection gate itself is a necessary lever that this scenario's exclusive reliance on non-structural measures cannot substitute for. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A comparative decomposition study isolating the marginal contribution of non-structural quality-levelling measures versus the selection gate itself to between-school segregation, using regional variation in Hungary.)

## S5 — Demographically-driven passive contraction (status-quo baseline)
**Goal**
Serve as the no-new-policy baseline in which shrinking birth cohorts force network consolidation and a natural decline in the selective track's absolute size and share regardless of any active equity or structural intervention, against which S1-S4 are measured.

**Mechanism**
- Annual births fell from approximately 125,000 in the early 1990s to 77,500 in 2024, with a further decline to approximately 72,000 in 2025 (exact figures contested between sources: some report 7.1% year-on-year decline, others a fertility-rate-based estimate); this registry-strong demographic trend forces school-network consolidation independent of any selection policy, though the precise near-term trajectory (72,000 vs. other cited estimates) is not fully reconciled across sources. [evidence: strong]
- Long-run population projections (KSH/HDRI SEEMIG medium-variant) show continuous decline through at least 2060 under all fertility and migration scenarios, meaning the school-age cohort shrinks structurally through 2040 and beyond, independent of any policy choice modeled in S1-S4. [evidence: strong]
- This scenario should not be read as policy-neutral: the expert record documents that government is already using score thresholds (S2) as an active supply-side lever independent of demand, meaning the 'no-new-policy' baseline itself contains an active policy component that interacts with, rather than sits apart from, demographic contraction — a distinction this scenario must make explicit rather than treat demography as the sole driver. [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
strong — The core demographic decline trend is strongly evidenced across multiple KSH-sourced datasets, but the scenario's framing as a 'passive' or policy-neutral baseline is only moderately supported, since the record documents active score-threshold policy operating concurrently.

**Assumptions**
- Cohort-size projections (declining under-20 population) are stable across the planning horizon (2025-2040) and do not assume major immigration reversing natural decrease. [assumption]
- The 'no active policy' framing of this baseline is analytically separable from the documented active use of score thresholds and other administrative supply-side levers, even though both operate concurrently on the same selective-track footprint — this scenario treats them as distinguishable for comparison purposes while acknowledging the record shows they interact. [assumption]
- School-maintainer sustainability thresholds (minimum ~45 students, per the Ukrainian cross-border analogue) represent a binding constraint that will force consolidation decisions in Hungary at a broadly comparable scale, absent a formally published Hungary-specific viability floor. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Avoids the legal, administrative, and political implementation costs documented as substantial for S1-S3 (felmenő-rendszer phase-in, maintainer renegotiation, anti-sorting enforcement), since no active structural or admissions policy change is proposed. [evidence: weak]
- Provides a clean counterfactual baseline against which the incremental equity and achievement effects of S1-S4 can be measured, since it isolates the demographic-decline component from any deliberate policy lever. [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
Passive demographic contraction alone offers no equity guarantee and may worsen SES-skew if consolidation decisions (which schools close, which survive) are made without explicit equity criteria: the record shows selection is already geographically uneven (roughly 40% of general schools, 60% in villages, send zero pupils to 6/8-year tracks) and that church-maintained schools have expanded fastest in small towns and villages, meaning demographically-forced consolidation could disproportionately close state-run options in already-underserved areas while leaving urban elite-track capacity comparatively protected. Critically, because this baseline already contains active score-threshold policy (S2) operating alongside passive demographic decline, the equity outcome of 'doing nothing structurally' is not neutral — it is the combined, uncoordinated product of demographic force and an ad hoc, legally ungrounded administrative lever, which the record suggests currently concentrates remaining selective slots among already-advantaged urban populations rather than equalizing access.

**Cost categories**
- School-network consolidation costs (closures, mergers, transport/busing for displaced students): documented as forced regardless of policy choice, but no aggregate national cost estimate exists; the Ukrainian cross-border analogue illustrates the mechanism but not Hungary-specific magnitude.
- Teacher workforce displacement and redeployment cost from closing small schools: unknown net effect (may displace experienced teachers or improve efficiency), no published estimate.
- Opportunity cost of inaction on documented equity harms: not a fiscal cost in the conventional sense but a distributional cost that continues to accrue (highest-in-EU segregation, near-total exclusion of multiply-disadvantaged pupils from elite tracks) absent any active countermeasure.

**Implementation steps**
- Klebelsberg Center / municipal and church maintainers — Continue current ad hoc, maintainer-level consolidation and score-threshold decisions without a coordinated national equity framework, as is the current practice. (timeline: ongoing, year 1 onward)
- KSH / Educational Authority (Oktatási Hivatal) — Publish updated annual demographic and enrollment projections at settlement/county level to inform which schools face closure risk, without prescribing an equity-weighted response. (timeline: year 1 onward, annual)
- Independent research institute — Monitor and publish the combined effect of passive demographic contraction and active score-threshold policy on selective-track composition, explicitly separating the two drivers where data permits, to serve as the comparative baseline for S1-S4 evaluation. (timeline: year 1-5, recurring)

**Political risks**
- Framing passive contraction as the 'path of least resistance' politically is contradicted by the record's own documentation that government is already using score thresholds as an active supply-side lever; treating this baseline as low-political-risk risks masking an ongoing, contested administrative practice that has no uniform legal basis and is already provoking parental and institutional pushback (e.g. Toldy Ferenc, Budai Nagy Antal admissions disputes).
- Uncoordinated, maintainer-by-maintainer consolidation risks disproportionately closing state-run options in underserved rural areas while urban elite-track capacity remains comparatively protected, potentially worsening the very segregation metrics (highest in EU) that create the external EU-conditionality pressure documented in the political-feasibility record.
- Absent any active policy response, this baseline risks becoming the default outcome not through deliberate choice but through political inertia and stalled consultation, consistent with the record's finding that no binding legislative timetable or government resolution on selection reform has been published as of mid-2026, making passive contraction a plausible actual outcome rather than a purely analytical construct.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether demographically-forced consolidation, left uncoordinated, will disproportionately affect underserved rural areas versus urban elite-track capacity, worsening rather than passively maintaining current segregation levels. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A dedicated KSH or Oktatási Hivatal settlement-level school viability projection under current birth-rate scenarios, disaggregated by maintainer type and urban/rural status.)
- The precise magnitude and timing of the near-term demographic cliff (one cohort ahead, ~17,000 fewer one-year-olds than four-year-olds) and its settlement-level distribution remains only moderately evidenced, limiting precise consolidation planning. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Settlement- or county-disaggregated KIR/KIFIR birth-cohort and enrollment records updated annually, cross-validated against KSH demographic yearbooks.)
- Whether the interaction between passive demographic decline and active score-threshold policy (S2) can be reliably decomposed in available data, or whether their combined effect will remain analytically inseparable, limiting the baseline's usefulness for isolating S1-S4's incremental effects. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A decomposition study using regional variation in score-threshold enforcement timing versus demographic decline rates to statistically separate the two drivers' contributions to selective-track composition change.)
