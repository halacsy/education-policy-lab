# Policy scenarios

## S1 — Admissions and intake-equity regulation (keep structure, fix the gate)
**Goal**
Keep the 6/8-year gimnázium structure intact but regulate the admission mechanism itself — SES-aware quotas or proxies, uniform score-threshold rules, coaching-resistant exam design — to reduce family-capital-driven sorting at the point of entry.

**Mechanism**
- Uniform, nationally binding minimum-score or quota rules replace the current maintainer-by-maintainer discretionary thresholds, closing the two-tier admissions gap documented between institutions. [evidence: moderate]
- SES-aware quotas or proxies at intake would reduce, but not eliminate, the family-background gradient in admission, since self-selection out of the applicant pool occurs before the formal exam. [evidence: weak]
- Coaching-resistant exam redesign could narrow the preparation-investment advantage that currently lets some talented children fail and less-talented but coached children pass. [evidence: weak]

**Evidence status**
moderate — Historical rejection-rate gaps by poverty share and documented two-tier discretionary thresholds are moderate evidence for the sorting problem, but no Hungarian study has evaluated an SES-aware quota or coaching-resistant exam design in operation.

**Assumptions**
- Admission-equity rules (quotas, proxies, uniform thresholds) can be designed with sufficient legal and technical specificity to be enforced consistently across state, church, and foundation maintainers, despite documented maintainer plurality and partial autonomy frameworks for church admissions.
- Families will not fully offset intake-point regulation by shifting investment further upstream (earlier coaching, primary-school choice) in ways that reproduce the same SES gradient one step earlier.

**Expected benefits**
- Preserves the roughly 10% competency-gain advantage associated with 6/8-year tracks for admitted students, since track structure and instructional model remain unchanged. [evidence: moderate]
- Likely reduces SES-skew within the remaining selective places compared to the current discretionary two-tier system, though the size of the effect is untested in Hungary. [evidence: weak]
- Is politically lower-risk than structural delay because it does not touch the statutory selection age or track existence, reducing the 'totalitarian unified-model' framing risk seen in current debate. [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
Equity gains are plausible but bounded: uniform admission rules would reduce the currently documented two-tier discretionary gap between maintainers, yet the scenario leaves untouched the pre-exam self-selection of disadvantaged pupils out of the applicant pool and the compounding effect of free school choice and catchment sorting; absent parallel action on those channels, the measured SES-skew within remaining selective places may fall only modestly while segregation migrates to choice-driven sorting at the general-school level.

**Cost categories**
- SES-proxy infrastructure and data systems: low-cost option (self-declared SES indicators) vs. high-cost option (linked administrative data matching, as used in Admin3-style research) — magnitude unpublished for either option, and no Hungary-specific costing exists.
- Coaching-resistant exam redesign and enforcement: development, piloting, and annual administration costs are unquantified; comparable OECD item-design and psychometric-validation programmes suggest a multi-year, specialist-staffed effort, but no published Hungarian estimate exists.
- Legal and administrative negotiation costs across state, church, and foundation maintainers to establish uniform, enforceable admission rules, including possible compensation or transition agreements with autonomous maintainers — magnitude unpublished.

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education / Oktatási Hivatal — Commission a legal-design study mapping enforceable admission-rule options across maintainer types, including church-concordat constraints, and draft the statutory instrument. (timeline: year 1)
- Klebelsberg Center and maintainer federations (church, foundation) — Negotiate a binding cross-maintainer agreement on uniform score thresholds or quotas, including transition terms for maintainers currently applying informal cutoffs. (timeline: year 1-2)
- Oktatási Hivatal — Pilot a coaching-resistant exam design in a subset of institutions and evaluate its differential impact by SES before national rollout. (timeline: year 2-3)
- Parliament — Enact the statutory admission-equity framework with felmenő-rendszer (grade-by-grade) phase-in, respecting the roughly one-year minimum lead time established by past Nkt. practice. (timeline: year 3)
- Oktatási Hivatal / independent monitor — Publish annual disaggregated admissions statistics by maintainer and SES proxy to monitor whether sorting migrates to pre-exam self-selection or school choice. (timeline: year 3 onward)

**Political risks**
- Fidesz has already framed any perceived move against elite-track admissions as 'totalitarian'; even admission-only reform risks being folded into that culture-war framing despite leaving track structure untouched.
- Church and foundation maintainers retain partial autonomy over admissions under concordat-style agreements; the scenario's central enforcement mechanism has no demonstrated legal pathway that compels uniform compliance without renegotiating those agreements.
- A decree-level threshold (as currently used by the Klebelsberg Center) can be reversed by a future government as easily as it was introduced, unless entrenched in cardinal law — and no cross-party consensus for such entrenchment currently exists.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether admission-equity rules can be legally and operationally enforced consistently across state, church, and foundation maintainers given documented maintainer plurality and partial autonomy. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A legal mapping of church and foundation maintainer autonomy clauses on admissions, combined with negotiated pilot agreements tested in at least one maintainer type before national rollout.)
- Whether SES-aware quotas or proxies would measurably reduce the family-background gradient in admission once pre-exam self-selection and coaching-driven preparation gaps are accounted for. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A regression-discontinuity or matched-pairs evaluation comparing admission outcomes before and after a piloted SES-aware quota, controlling for prior achievement and coaching investment.)
- Whether a decree-based or statutory admission-equity rule would survive a change of government, given the demonstrated pattern of rapid reversal of predecessor education reforms. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Explicit cardinal-law entrenchment requiring supermajority to reverse, or cross-party negotiated safeguard clauses, neither of which currently exists.)

## S2 — Supply-side contraction of the selective track (shrink the gate, not the rules)
**Goal**
Reduce the state-sector share of 6/8-year places (via score thresholds, capacity caps, or demographically-driven consolidation) to shrink the selective track's footprint, while leaving admission criteria within remaining places largely unchanged.

**Mechanism**
- The Klebelsberg Center's minimum-score thresholds are used as a supply-side lever to compress the gimnázium track's share of cohort intake, independent of demand, from its current level toward a lower target. [evidence: moderate]
- Reduced state-sector selective capacity may incrementally improve peer composition in urban general-school upper grades by retaining higher-performing pupils who would otherwise have exited at grade 4 or 6. [evidence: weak]
- Contraction implemented only in the state sector, without matching regulation of church and foundation maintainers, risks displacing demand toward non-state selective provision rather than reducing selection overall. [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
moderate — The Klebelsberg score-threshold mechanism and its supply-reduction intent are moderately evidenced by contemporaneous reporting, but the current gimnázium 6/8-year track's actual cohort share (~8-12% per the curated registry) is much smaller than the ~40% figure cited in some demography reporting, which appears to conflate the full gimnázium track (all entry ages) with just the 6/8-year entry points; this scenario's baseline figures should be read with that distinction in mind.

**Assumptions**
- State-sector score thresholds or capacity caps can shrink selective provision without being fully offset by expansion in church, foundation, or private selective provision, even though church/foundation gimnázium share has already grown from 10.4% (2010) to approximately 27% (2020/2021).
- Church and foundation maintainers can be regulated or fiscally incentivized not to expand selective places to absorb displaced demand, despite the scenario providing no evidence-based enforcement pathway for such regulation given maintainer autonomy.

**Expected benefits**
- Reduces the absolute number of pupils exposed to early selection nationally, lowering the system-wide footprint of the sorting mechanism without requiring admission-criteria redesign. [evidence: weak]
- Politically simpler to implement than structural delay, since it uses an existing administrative lever (Klebelsberg Center thresholds) rather than requiring new primary legislation. [evidence: weak]
- Aligns with, and could be framed as accelerating, an already-underway demographic contraction (S5), potentially reducing political salience of the change. [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
Equity_impact correctly flags intensified SES skew within the remaining, smaller selective sector, but the primary displacement risk is not merely hypothetical: church/foundation gimnázium share has already grown from 10.4% to approximately 27% over a decade, alongside post-2020 legislation easing church acquisition of school buildings — a documented pathway by which state-sector contraction could be offset by non-state expansion, leaving overall selection intensity and SES-skew unchanged or worse if state contraction proceeds without matching regulation of non-state maintainers.

**Cost categories**
- Compensatory funding or capacity controls for church/foundation maintainers to prevent offsetting expansion: flagged as medium-to-high, multi-year, currently unbudgeted, with no published estimate of the fiscal transfer or regulatory-compliance cost required.
- Administrative cost of redesigning and monitoring score-threshold or capacity-cap enforcement nationally, including data systems to track displacement to non-state providers in real time — magnitude unpublished.
- Transitional costs for state-sector schools and teachers affected by capacity reduction (reallocation, potential redundancy, facility repurposing), with timing mismatch risk against the multi-year teacher-salary reform (2024-2031) already underway.

**Implementation steps**
- Klebelsberg Center — Formalize minimum-score thresholds into a transparent, published national schedule with an explicit multi-year glide path toward the target intake share. (timeline: year 1-2)
- Ministry of Education — Design and negotiate levers (funding conditions, building-transfer review, capacity-cap agreements) to discourage offsetting expansion by church/foundation maintainers, given their documented autonomy and growth trend. (timeline: year 2-4)
- Oktatási Hivatal / KSH — Establish a real-time monitoring system tracking cross-maintainer enrollment shifts to detect and quantify displacement as state contraction proceeds. (timeline: year 1 onward)
- Parliament / Ministry of Finance — Review and adjust normatíva/feladatfinanszírozás funding rules for church and foundation maintainers if monitoring shows material displacement, subject to the contested nature of current per-student funding comparisons. (timeline: year 3-5)

**Political risks**
- The scenario's core assumption — that church and foundation maintainers can be regulated or incentivized not to expand — has no evidence-based enforcement pathway; without one, the entire equity rationale for supply contraction could be neutralized by documented, ongoing non-state expansion.
- Using an administrative decree (Klebelsberg Center thresholds) rather than statute means the mechanism has no national legal basis and remains vulnerable to inconsistent enforcement or reversal by a future maintainer or government.
- Parent and school-community opposition (Szülői Hang and similar stakeholders) already documents distress at inconsistent threshold application across institutions, which could intensify if contraction is perceived as arbitrary or state-sector-only.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether state-sector contraction via score thresholds will be substantially offset by expansion in church, foundation, or private selective provision, given the documented decade-long growth trend in non-state gimnázium share. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Real-time, disaggregated enrollment monitoring by maintainer type across at least 3-5 years following threshold implementation, with a published displacement-rate estimate.)
- Whether the current ~40%-toward-30% cohort-share framing used in some reporting is measuring the same population as the 8-12% 6/8-year-entry figure used elsewhere, and which is the correct policy-relevant baseline. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: An official Oktatási Hivatal/KSH clarification disaggregating gimnázium track share by entry type (4-year, 6-year, 8-year) on a consistent annual basis.)
- Whether a decree-level administrative lever without national legal basis can produce durable supply contraction, or whether inconsistent maintainer-level application will undermine the policy's stated equity goal. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A statutory basis for the threshold mechanism combined with a published multi-year compliance audit across maintainer types.)

## S3 — Structural delay of the selection age (Finnish/Polish model)
**Goal**
Raise the statutory age of first academic tracking (e.g., to 14+ or later), phasing out or converting 6- and 8-year entry points into a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase, paired with explicit anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment.

**Mechanism**
- Cross-national difference-in-differences evidence (75 countries/regions, 20 years) shows early tracking increases test-score dispersion; delaying tracking to 14+ is expected to reduce this dispersion. [evidence: strong]
- Poland's 1999 delayed-tracking reform produced PISA gains of 0.25 SD (math), 0.28 SD (reading), 0.16 SD (science) within 3-7 years, providing an empirical baseline effect-size, though the curated registry tags this evidence as moderate given confounding with bundled curriculum and teacher-qualification reforms. [evidence: moderate]
- Delaying tracking without simultaneous anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment would likely see equity gains substantially eroded by within-system sorting, as observed in Finland post-2000 where school choice and residential sorting re-widened SES gaps despite no reintroduction of formal tracking. [evidence: strong]

**Evidence status**
moderate — Poland's 1999 reform evidence is tagged moderate, not strong, in the curated registry because effect sizes are confounded with bundled curriculum, examination, and teacher-qualification reforms, and the reform was later politically reversed with associated PISA decline; precise SD figures should be read as directional, not firmly isolated causal estimates.

**Assumptions**
- Sorting does not simply migrate from formal 6/8-year tracking to school choice, catchment, or within-school streaming to the point of neutralizing the equity gain — an assumption directly contradicted by Finland's own post-2000 experience, where 94% of outcome variation is now within-school and SES gaps widened again via choice and residential sorting despite no reintroduction of formal tracking.
- Anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment can be designed, enacted, and enforced with sufficient rigor and simultaneity to function as a credible co-requirement, not merely an aspirational add-on, given that Hungary's segregation is administratively embedded in both selection and free-choice rules codified separately.

**Expected benefits**
- International DiD evidence suggests delaying selection from age 10-12 to 14 would likely reduce Hungary's socioeconomic achievement gap by approximately 0.15-0.25 SD, based on Polish comparable gains, though this transfer estimate itself carries uncertainty given differing baseline segregation levels. [evidence: moderate]
- Hungary's own 2000s comprehensivization reform (extending unified basic schooling to 9 years) demonstrated domestically that removing an earlier selection point raised the floor for would-be vocational-track students without lowering the ceiling for grammar-track students, providing within-country precedent. [evidence: strong]
- Reduced between-school achievement variance, as observed in Poland where disparities fell from among the largest in the OECD to among the smallest, though individual-level SES-achievement correlation did not improve in the Polish case. [evidence: strong]

**Equity impact**
Equity_impact treats anti-sorting rules as 'load-bearing' but this scenario now makes explicit that their enforceability is not an open design detail but an operational co-requirement without which the projected 0.15-0.25 SD equity gain is unlikely to materialize: Hungary's segregation is embedded in both selection and free-choice rules as separately codified legal features, and Finland's post-2000 experience shows equity gains from de-tracking erode within a generation once school choice and residential sorting are left unaddressed. Delaying selection alone, without simultaneous, enforceable catchment and choice regulation phased in on the same timeline, risks reproducing the current SES gradient through a different sorting channel rather than reducing it.

**Cost categories**
- Early diagnostic and remedial support for pupils previously destined for early tracking: no aggregate fiscal range published for Hungary; Portugal's TEIP programme offers a partial cost analogue but is not directly transferable.
- Teacher retraining and reallocation for a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase: costs are unquantified; Hungary's teacher workforce is already aging (approximately 50% age 50+) and contracting (over 21,000 fewer teachers since 2003/2004), constraining feasible retraining capacity and timeline.
- Facility conversion costs to merge or restructure 6/8-year gimnázium buildings into a unified lower-secondary network: no per-facility or aggregate estimate published; the dense 880-school network and existing dormitory slack capacity may reduce but not eliminate this cost.
- Anti-sorting enforcement infrastructure (catchment-area monitoring, school-choice audit systems): magnitude unpublished; this is a new administrative function without a clear existing budget line.

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education — Commission a Hungary-specific costing study (teacher retraining, facility conversion, transport/logistics, administrative overhead) across three phase-in schedules (1-year, 3-year, 5-year), since no such study currently exists. (timeline: year 1)
- Parliament — Enact statutory delay of the selection age with simultaneous, not sequential, enactment of enforceable anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment, respecting felmenő-rendszer phase-in and roughly one-year minimum lead time. (timeline: year 2)
- Ministry of Education / teacher training universities — Begin teacher retraining and reallocation for the unified lower-secondary phase, sequenced after — not concurrent with — the National Core Curriculum update and AI-integration roadmap to avoid CPD-bandwidth conflict; given workforce contraction and aging, this stage realistically requires a longer window than initially planned. (timeline: year 3-9)
- Oktatási Hivatal / independent monitor — Publish annual monitoring reports on catchment and school-choice sorting patterns to detect whether equity gains are being neutralized by displacement to other sorting channels. (timeline: year 3 onward)
- Cross-party parliamentary committee — Negotiate cardinal-law or supermajority entrenchment of both the selection-age delay and the anti-sorting rules together, given Poland's 18-year reversal precedent and the risk that partial entrenchment (delay without anti-sorting protection) would be more politically durable but less equity-effective. (timeline: year 2-4)

**Political risks**
- Enacting selection-age delay and anti-sorting rules simultaneously, as this scenario now requires, is more legislatively complex and politically exposed than sequential reform, increasing the risk of partial implementation where the popular delay proceeds but the harder-to-sell anti-sorting rules stall or are diluted.
- Fidesz has already framed a 'unified model' proposal as totalitarian; a structural delay scenario is the most exposed of the five frames to this culture-war attack line, raising high reversal risk on government change, as demonstrated by Poland's own 18-year reversal after a change in ruling party.
- Teacher workforce constraints (shortage of at least 16,000 in 2025/26 per PSZ) are framed by the largest teacher union as a non-negotiable precondition for any structural reform; proceeding without resolving workforce shortage first risks union opposition strong enough to stall implementation regardless of legislative success.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment can be enforced with sufficient rigor and simultaneity to prevent sorting from migrating to those channels, given Hungary's embedded, separately codified school-choice legal architecture. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A decomposition study separating the marginal contribution of the 6/8-year gate from general school-choice segregation, using variation across regions with different kisgimnázium densities, plus a pilot region where both delay and anti-sorting rules are enacted simultaneously and monitored for at least 5 years.)
- Whether Poland's 0.25-0.28 SD effect sizes would replicate in Hungary given higher baseline segregation, different demographic composition, and the confounding of the Polish reform with bundled curriculum and teacher-qualification changes. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A dedicated Hungarian pilot or quasi-experimental regional rollout with propensity-matched controls, tracking achievement and segregation measures over 5-7 years, isolating the timing-of-selection effect from curriculum and teacher-qualification changes.)
- Whether the teacher-retraining and reallocation timeline (now extended to year 3-9) is feasible given Hungary's aging, contracting teacher workforce and already fully committed CPD bandwidth for concurrent NAT and AI-integration reforms. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: A detailed Ministry of Education costing and capacity study modelling teacher retraining timelines against current CPD commitments and workforce-attrition projections through 2035.)

## S4 — Non-structural compensatory reform (leave selection age unchanged)
**Goal**
Pursue Portugal-style equity and achievement gains through curriculum clarity, targeted (TEIP-style) funding for disadvantaged schools, retention reduction, and school-quality levelling, leaving the 10-12 selection age and track structure untouched.

**Mechanism**
- Portugal improved PISA results by approximately 31 points (0.3 SD) between 2000 and 2015-2018 without introducing early tracking, through curriculum standards clarification, increased school autonomy, and regular student assessment. [evidence: strong]
- TEIP-style targeted school support reduces dropout and grade retention but does not close competency gaps relative to non-TEIP schools; no rigorous longitudinal evaluation of long-term student-outcome effects exists. [evidence: moderate]
- Retention reduction is both fiscally and pedagogically favorable when implemented systematically, evidenced by Portugal's EUR 112 million two-year saving alongside a 26% reduction in retained students across 663 schools. [evidence: strong]
- Non-structural reform leaves the admission gatekeeping mechanism (which historically shows ~46% rejection in low-poverty-share classes vs. ~22% in high-poverty-share classes) fully intact, meaning it does nothing to address SES-driven sorting at the point of entry into 6/8-year tracks. [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
moderate — Portugal's overall achievement and retention-reduction results are strongly evidenced, but the transferability of its specific TEIP-style compensatory mechanisms to Hungary's much higher-segregation, 6/8-year selective-track context is untested and thus only moderately supported.

**Assumptions**
- TEIP-style targeted funding commitments will remain durable and adequately budgeted over the multi-year horizon needed to show results, despite the scenario's own political_risks and the wider fiscal-consolidation pressure (projected 6.2% deficit, rising debt) that make targeted funding lines a likely first-line budget cut.
- Curriculum clarity, school autonomy, and assessment reform can produce Portugal-comparable achievement gains within Hungary's much higher-segregation starting point (53-point vs. 33-point EU-average SES gap), even though these non-structural levers do not directly address the compositional sorting that the segregation figures primarily reflect.

**Expected benefits**
- Achievable without touching the statutory selection age or track structure, making it the lowest-legislative-complexity and lowest culture-war-exposure scenario among the five. [evidence: weak]
- Retention reduction alone offers a plausible net-fiscal-saving component, following Portugal's documented EUR 112 million two-year saving, though a Hungary-specific translation of this figure has not been produced. [evidence: weak]
- Curriculum and assessment reform could improve average system performance somewhat independent of the selection-structure debate, consistent with Portugal's achievement gains achieved via non-structural levers. [evidence: moderate]

**Equity impact**
Equity_impact plausibly flags an equity-neutral-to-negative outcome relative to structural alternatives, and the missing distributional mechanism is now made explicit: because this scenario leaves admission gatekeeping fully intact (with its documented ~46% vs ~22% rejection-rate gap by poverty share), any curriculum, funding, or retention-reduction gains accrue primarily to pupils who remain in the non-selective majority of the system, while the smaller, already advantaged 6/8-year selective population continues to be protected from — and to benefit from — the existing sorting mechanism; TEIP-style support therefore risks becoming a parallel-track compensatory layer that coexists with, rather than reduces, the core selection-driven segregation this brief is assessing.

**Cost categories**
- TEIP-style targeted funding for disadvantaged schools: Portugal's EUR 112 million two-year retention-reduction saving is cited as precedent for net-cost-saving potential, but no Hungary-specific net-cost estimate exists translating this figure to Hungary's school population, cost structure, or disadvantaged-school count.
- Curriculum-clarity and assessment-transparency reform development and rollout: costs are unquantified in available sources; comparable OECD curriculum-reform programmes suggest multi-year specialist development costs, but no Hungary-specific figure has been published.
- School-quality levelling investment (facilities, staffing parity between advantaged and disadvantaged schools): no aggregate estimate published; Hungary's rural/disadvantaged-area teacher shortage (2-3 times higher unqualified-teacher share) suggests this cost category could be substantial but remains unmodelled.

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education — Design a TEIP-style targeted funding formula for disadvantaged schools, drawing on Portugal's design but adapted to Hungary's maintainer-plurality context, with an explicit statutory (not merely annual-budget) funding-floor commitment. (timeline: year 1)
- National Core Curriculum authority (NAT) — Revise curriculum-clarity standards and assessment transparency measures, sequenced to avoid conflict with the concurrent NAT update and AI-integration roadmap already committed through 2026. (timeline: year 1-2)
- School maintainers (state, church, foundation) and Klebelsberg Center — Implement retention-reduction protocols and school-quality-levelling investment in disadvantaged schools, monitoring dropout and retention rates against Portugal's 26% reduction benchmark. (timeline: year 2-5)
- Parliament / Ministry of Finance — Negotiate cardinal-law entrenchment of the TEIP-style funding floor to protect it from being a first-line cut under fiscal consolidation, given the projected 6.2% deficit and rising debt. (timeline: year 1-3)
- Oktatási Hivatal / independent monitor — Publish a longitudinal matched-cohort evaluation of TEIP-enrolled versus non-enrolled students tracking competency gain, not only dropout/retention, to test whether compensatory measures close achievement gaps. (timeline: year 3-8)

**Political risks**
- The scenario assumes a statutory (cardinal-law) funding floor for TEIP-style programmes is politically achievable, yet the same evidence record documents that targeted funding lines are a likely first-line cut under projected fiscal consolidation (6.2% deficit), creating direct tension between the scenario's central mechanism and its own political-feasibility record.
- Because this scenario leaves selection-gate admissions untouched, it may be politically attractive precisely because it avoids the culture-war exposure of S1-S3, but this same feature means it cannot credibly claim to resolve the segregation problem that is the brief's central concern, risking a 'looks like action, changes nothing structural' critique from equity advocates.
- Teacher-workforce constraints (rural/disadvantaged-area shortages 2-3x higher) directly limit the feasibility of school-quality-levelling investment, since the schools most needing levelling are also the ones facing the most acute staffing shortages.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether TEIP-style targeted support can close achievement/competency gaps rather than merely reducing dropout rates, since no longitudinal study has tracked student competency trajectories beyond retention reduction. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: A 5-8 year longitudinal matched-cohort study comparing TEIP-enrolled and non-enrolled students, controlling for baseline SES and prior achievement, tracking assessed competency gain, not just exam pass rates.)
- Whether a statutory funding floor for TEIP-style programmes is achievable in the 2027 budget bill given projected fiscal consolidation pressure, or whether compensatory funding will remain contingent on annual budget cycles and vulnerable to cuts. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Publication of the 2027 budget bill text showing either a cardinal-law funding-floor commitment or temporary, deficit-contingent compensation language, plus EC fiscal guidance on permissible spending commitments.)
- Whether non-structural reform can produce Hungary-comparable equity gains to Portugal given Hungary's much higher starting segregation (53-point vs 33-point EU-average SES gap) and the fact that this scenario leaves the primary segregation-producing mechanism (selective admissions) untouched. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A Hungary-specific pilot evaluation comparing TEIP-style compensatory measures against a matched comparison group, run in parallel with continued selective-track admissions, to isolate the non-structural-reform effect from the selection-driven segregation baseline.)

## S5 — Demographically-driven passive contraction (status-quo baseline)
**Goal**
Serve as the no-new-policy baseline in which shrinking birth cohorts force network consolidation and a natural decline in the selective track's absolute size and share regardless of any active equity or structural intervention, against which S1-S4 are measured.

**Mechanism**
- Annual births fell from approximately 125,000 in the early 1990s to 77,500 in 2024, with a further decline to approximately 72,000 in 2025 per the demography expert's estimate, though other cited figures in the record (5,979 births in October 2025 alone, fertility rate 1.31) indicate the decline is continuing at an accelerating, not stabilizing, pace; forcing school-network consolidation independent of selection policy. [evidence: strong]
- Long-run population projections (KSH/HDRI SEEMIG medium-variant) show continuous population decline through at least 2060 under all fertility and migration scenarios, meaning the school-age cohort available for all secondary tracks shrinks structurally through 2040 and beyond. [evidence: strong]
- Passive demographic contraction is not policy-neutral in practice, because it interacts with the government's already-active use of score thresholds (S2) as a supply-side lever; treating S5 as a pure counterfactual baseline understates the degree to which current administrative levers are already shaping which selective places disappear and where. [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
strong — The core demographic decline trend (births, fertility rate, population projections) is strongly evidenced across multiple KSH and independent sources, though the precise near-term birth-count figures vary somewhat between cited sources and should be read as directionally consistent rather than a single precise number.

**Assumptions**
- Cohort-size projections (declining under-20 population) are stable across the planning horizon (2025-2040) and do not assume major immigration reversing natural decrease.
- Treating demographic contraction as a policy-neutral baseline understates that the government is already using active administrative levers (score thresholds under S2) that interact with and partly determine how demographic decline translates into selective-track contraction; S5 should be read as 'no new policy beyond currently active levers,' not as a genuinely passive counterfactual.

**Expected benefits**
- Requires no legislative action, political negotiation, or cross-party consensus, making it the only scenario with zero implementation or reversal risk in the conventional political sense. [evidence: weak]
- Naturally reduces the absolute number of pupils exposed to the selective track over time as cohorts shrink, without requiring any redesign of admission rules or track structure. [evidence: moderate]
- Provides the counterfactual baseline against which the incremental equity and achievement effects of S1-S4 can be measured, a methodologically necessary function even if the scenario itself does not solve the segregation problem. [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
Passive contraction does not address, and may worsen, SES-based sorting: as the absolute number of selective places shrinks under demographic pressure, competition for the remaining places likely intensifies, and given documented ~46% vs ~22% rejection-rate gaps by poverty share and the near-total exclusion of multiply-disadvantaged pupils from elite tracks, a shrinking supply without any admission-equity intervention would plausibly concentrate remaining selective places even more heavily among already-advantaged, urban, coached applicants — meaning this baseline should not be read as equity-improving merely because the track's footprint declines.

**Cost categories**
- Small-school and dispersed-track closure costs (facility repurposing, teacher redeployment) forced by demographic decline, occurring regardless of policy choice; magnitude linked to the ~600-800 kindergarten/class-group cliff one cohort ahead, but not separately costed for the selective-track subset.
- Administrative cost of network-consolidation planning and decision-making across state, church, and foundation maintainers as enrollment falls below viability thresholds (e.g., the 45-student floor precedent from the Beregszász cross-border analogue) — magnitude unpublished for the Hungarian case.
- No new compensatory or equity-targeted spending is budgeted under this baseline by definition, meaning any equity harm from intensified competition for shrinking selective places (see equity_impact) carries an implicit, unaddressed cost that this scenario does not fund a remedy for.

**Implementation steps**
- KSH / Ministry of Education — Continue publishing annual demographic and enrollment projections to inform maintainer-level consolidation decisions, without introducing new admission-equity or structural policy. (timeline: ongoing)
- School maintainers (state, church, foundation) — Make individual, decentralized decisions on closing or merging schools/tracks as enrollment falls below viability thresholds, absent national coordination on equity impact. (timeline: year 1 onward)
- Klebelsberg Center — Continue applying score-threshold levers (as under S2) even in the absence of a dedicated demographic-baseline policy, since this administrative practice is already active and will continue to interact with passive contraction. (timeline: ongoing)

**Political risks**
- Framing passive demographic contraction as the 'path of least resistance' politically is undermined by the fact that the government is already using active administrative levers (score thresholds) that interact with and shape demographic contraction, meaning S5 is not a genuinely inert policy stance but a default that already contains an active, undebated administrative choice.
- Absence of active equity intervention under this baseline risks becoming politically costly if EU conditionality (already citing Hungary's highest-in-EU segregation) treats inaction on segregation as non-compliance, independent of whether contraction is 'natural' or policy-driven.
- Decentralized, maintainer-by-maintainer consolidation decisions without national coordination risk uneven regional outcomes (as already seen in rural teacher-shortage concentration), potentially deepening the very rural-urban and SES divides this brief is meant to assess.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether the interaction between passive demographic contraction and the government's active score-threshold lever (S2) will accelerate selective-track contraction beyond what demographic decline alone would produce, and whether this compound effect is being measured or monitored at all. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A joint KSH/Klebelsberg Center analysis decomposing selective-track enrollment change into a demographic-decline component and an administrative-threshold component over the 2024-2030 period.)
