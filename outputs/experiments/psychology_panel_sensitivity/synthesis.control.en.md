# Synthesis

## Overview
The expert record converges on a set of well-evidenced empirical facts about Hungary's early-selection system (korai szelekció into 6- and 8-year gimnázium tracks at ages 10-12) while leaving open substantial normative and implementation disagreements. Demographically, Hungary's school-age cohort is shrinking sharply and structurally through at least 2040-2060, forcing school-network consolidation regardless of selection policy; this is layered with a policy-driven compression of grammar-track intake share via minimum-score admission thresholds. Comparative evidence (Finland, Poland, Portugal) shows early tracking raises between-school inequality without raising average achievement, and that delayed-tracking reforms (Poland 1999, Finland 1972-77) produced measurable equity gains, though Poland's reform was later politically reversed after 18 years, illustrating durability risk. Hungary already has the EU's highest socio-economic school segregation, and the raw achievement gap between tracks shrinks drastically once family background is controlled for, suggesting selection sorts pre-existing advantage more than it creates value. Yet Hungarian-specific research finds a modest (~10%) competency-gain advantage for students admitted to elite 6/8-year tracks compared to similar peers in 4-year tracks, complicating any simple abolitionist reading. Legally, early selection is a narrow, separately-justified statutory exception rather than the structural default, making it more tractable to reform than the broader maintainer architecture, but constitutional-doctrine and precedent require a felmenő-rendszer (grade-by-grade phase-in) with roughly one-year minimum lead time. Politically, the landscape shifted with the 2026 change of government: reform is now live policy, with ministerial framing of the system as elite self-selection rather than pedagogy, EU tailwind via segregation-linked country reports, but also immediate opposition framing of any change as an ideological attack, unresolved stakeholder preconditions (teacher-workforce stabilization, statutory funding floors), and a demonstrated pattern of rapid reversal of predecessor reforms — all suggesting durability is currently low-probability absent cross-party consensus or constitutional entrenchment. Where experts genuinely diverge is on the role and treatability of non-state (church, foundation) maintainers in absorbing or resisting phased contraction of selective capacity, on how much of the segregation problem is attributable to the 6/8-year gate specifically versus school choice and catchment sorting more broadly, and on whether the modest measured value-added inside elite tracks is a genuine pedagogical effect or a confound of peer composition and teacher sorting that would disappear under matched analysis.

## Disagreement map
### Whether non-state (church, foundation) maintainers can be restricted from absorbing the phased-out selective capacity
- **hungarian_education_system, implementation_planning**: A phase-out of state-maintained 6/8-year tracks is a coherent policy lever because church-school expansion and parallel-track multiplication are policy-influenced (not purely demand-driven) and thus reversible through legislative and funding changes. Why: Church-maintained schools' share of all gimnázium students rose from 10.4% (2010) to ~27% (2020/2021), coinciding with post-2020 legislation permitting free building transfers to churches — evidence interpreted as policy-driven rather than autonomous demand growth.
- **political_feasibility, legal_and_governance** (minority): Any national reform faces a binding coalition and legal constraint: the current government's own 12-point programme explicitly commits that church maintainers will keep their previous operating conditions, and church-maintained admissions operate under concordats with partial autonomy — meaning non-state maintainers are structurally positioned to absorb or resist contraction of state-sector selective capacity, not merely comply with it. Why: The Tisza government's published programme (April 2026) explicitly protects church maintainers' operating conditions as a coalition-preserving commitment, and legal_and_governance notes church-maintained admissions retain partial legal autonomy under concordat-style agreements requiring separate negotiation.

### How much of Hungary's school segregation is attributable specifically to the 6/8-year selection gate versus broader school-choice and catchment sorting
- **hungarian_education_system, international_comparison, equity_and_social_mobility**: Early selection at age 10-12 is a documented, non-trivial and primary structural driver of Hungary's exceptionally high segregation; the raw 116-point achievement gap between grammar and vocational students shrinks to 24 points once family background is controlled, indicating selection itself (not just choice) accounts for most of the observed gap. Why: PISA 2022-based analysis (Taní-tani Online) and cross-national compositional-effect literature (Sciffer et al.) directly link earlier tracking ages to larger between-school SES sorting; Hungary's social selectivity index deteriorated by 5.3 points 2018-2022, the largest in Europe alongside Poland's tracking reintroduction.
- **hungarian_education_system, equity_and_social_mobility** (minority): Free school choice plus catchment-area sorting produce strong between-school segregation even without early selective tracks; abolishing the 6/8-year gate alone would not eliminate sorting, since it is unclear how much of the compositional damage is caused specifically by the gate versus by choice/catchment mechanisms operating independently. Why: Kertesi & Kezdi (2013) and Berenyi-Berkovits-Erőss studies document strong sorting at the primary stage via choice and catchment independent of tracks; equity_and_social_mobility explicitly flags this decomposition as an unresolved uncertainty requiring dedicated study.

### Whether the measured ~10% competency-gain advantage in elite 6/8-year tracks reflects genuine pedagogical value or unmeasured confounds
- **education_finance, hungarian_education_system**: Students in 6/8-year gimnázium show roughly 10% larger competency gains than similar-ability, similar-background peers in 4-year gimnázium, suggesting a real peer-effect or pedagogical value-added distinct from pure selection. Why: Horn Dániel's (Corvinus) statistical-model research explicitly controls for family background and prior ability, distinguishing this from raw achievement-gap comparisons; the finding is treated as evidence, not merely an assumption.
- **equity_and_social_mobility, education_finance** (minority): Whether the ~10% value-added estimate reflects genuine school effect versus unmeasured peer-composition or teacher-sorting confounds remains unresolved; value-added estimates diverge sharply by track type (positive for 8-grade, negative at grade 8 for 6-grade, turning positive only by grade 10), undermining a uniform 'value-added' narrative. Why: HÉTFA Working Paper 2015/11 (Országos Kompetenciamérés panel data) documents divergent, track-specific value-added trajectories; both education_finance and equity_and_social_mobility flag low confidence in the causal interpretation absent regression-discontinuity or matched replication.

### Whether structural reform durability requires cross-party consensus or can proceed via unilateral government action
- **political_feasibility, polish_reform, implementation_planning**: Durable reform requires either cross-party consensus or constitutional/cardinal-law entrenchment; absent this, any selection-structure reform enacted unilaterally faces high-probability reversal on the next government change, as demonstrated by Poland's 18-year reform reversal and Hungary's own pattern of rapidly reversing predecessor education policies (e.g., the teacher performance-evaluation system). Why: Poland's PiS government reversed the 1999 reform after 18 years despite documented achievement gains; Fidesz immediately framed the 2026 Hungarian minister's review statement as 'totalitarian,' establishing high reversal risk absent negotiated consensus.
- **legal_and_governance** (minority): Early selection is codified as a narrow, separately-justified statutory exception (not the structural default of secondary education), making legislative reform of the selection age more legally tractable via ordinary legislative process with felmenő-rendszer phase-in, without necessarily requiring cross-party negotiation as a legal precondition — the KEKVA precedent shows entire maintainer models can be legally dismantled within weeks when the government has the will. Why: The 2026 KEKVA dismantling (foundation-run public institutions) occurred within weeks following a constitutional amendment, demonstrating legal tractability of rapid unilateral structural change when political will exists, independent of durability considerations.

### Whether the pedagogical value-added of elite tracks should carry equal normative weight to their equity cost
- **equity_and_social_mobility, hungarian_education_system, finnish_reform, international_comparison**: Given Hungary's already-highest-in-EU segregation, the equity case against early selection is stronger than the achievement case for it; policy alternatives should be judged primarily by whether they reduce SES-based sorting at intake, not by whether they preserve the modest measured value-added for admitted students. Why: International DiD evidence (75 countries, 20 years) and Hungary's own comprehensivization-era experience (2000s reform) show removing early selection raises the floor for previously-tracked students without lowering the ceiling for grammar-track students, undercutting the necessity claim for selection.
- **education_finance** (minority): The ~10% learning-gain finding for admitted students represents a genuine tension, not a settled verdict: any policy alternative must weigh the demonstrated (if unreplicated) pedagogical value-added against equity harms, rather than treating equity as automatically dominant, because parental demand and the value-added evidence are not merely artefacts to be dismissed. Why: education_finance explicitly frames Hungary's 8%-of-cohort elite-track structure, entrenched parental demand, and contested church-funding architecture as making reform politically costly, not merely technically straightforward — implying the value-added finding cannot be dismissed outright.

## What the experts agree on
- Hungary's school-age cohort is shrinking structurally and will continue to do so at least through 2040-2060, forcing school-network consolidation regardless of selection policy choices. [evidence: strong]
- Hungary has the highest socio-economic school segregation in the EU, with a parent-education achievement gap (53 PISA points) far above the EU average (33 points). [evidence: strong]
- The raw achievement gap between grammar-track and vocational-track students shrinks drastically (from 116 to 24 points) once family background is statistically controlled, indicating composition/selection effects dominate the raw gap. [evidence: strong]
- International difference-in-differences evidence across dozens of countries shows early academic tracking increases between-school and socio-economic inequality without raising average achievement. [evidence: strong]
- Poland's 1999 delayed-tracking reform produced measurable PISA gains (0.25 SD math, 0.28 SD reading, 0.16 SD science) and reduced between-school inequality, but individual-level socio-economic inequity did not improve and the reform was politically reversed after 18 years. [evidence: strong]
- Hungary's teacher workforce is structurally aging and contracting, with acute shortages concentrated in rural areas and STEM subjects, constituting a binding constraint on any staffing-dependent selection-structure reform. [evidence: strong]
- Legally, early selection (the 6/8-year gimnázium track) is codified as a narrow, separately-justified statutory exception rather than the structural default of secondary education, and any reform touching enrolled cohorts is expected to require a felmenő-rendszer (grade-by-grade phase-in) with roughly one-year minimum lead time. [evidence: moderate]
- Physical school-network infrastructure (buildings, dormitories, transport) is not currently the binding constraint on delivering early selection almost anywhere in Hungary; the real feasibility bottleneck is the shrinking and unevenly distributed child population. [evidence: moderate]
- Church-maintained schools' share of gimnázium provision has grown substantially (from ~10.4% in 2010 to ~27% in 2020/2021), concentrated disproportionately among elite 6/8-year tracks. [evidence: strong]
- As of mid-2026, no binding legislative timetable, formal government resolution, or Oktatási Hivatal review mandate on selection reform has been published; the government's commitment remains consultative rather than statutory. [evidence: strong]
