# Policy scenarios

## S1 — Admissions and intake-equity regulation (keep structure, fix the gate)
**Goal**
Keep the 6/8-year gimnázium structure intact but regulate the admission mechanism itself — SES-aware quotas or proxies, uniform score-threshold rules, coaching-resistant exam design — to reduce family-capital-driven sorting at the point of entry.

**Mechanism**
- Uniform, nationally-binding minimum-score thresholds (rather than maintainer-discretionary ones) would reduce the two-tier admissions inconsistency currently observed across institutions. [evidence: moderate]
- Coaching-resistant exam redesign could reduce the test-preparation advantage that currently allows paid coaching to compound family-background sorting. [evidence: weak]
- SES-aware quotas or proxies at intake would directly counteract the documented pattern whereby admissions gatekeeping tracks socio-economic status more than ability alone. [evidence: moderate]
- Because church- and foundation-maintained gimnáziums operate under partial autonomy frameworks and separate agreements, uniform enforcement of admission-equity rules across all maintainers is not guaranteed by legislative change alone. [evidence: contested]

**Evidence status**
moderate — Admissions gatekeeping's SES-skew is well documented (OFI rejection-rate data, moderate), but no Hungarian evaluation exists of whether SES-aware quotas or coaching-resistant exam redesign would work as intended, and enforceability across maintainer types is unresolved.

**Assumptions**
- Admission-equity rules can be designed with sufficiently precise legal and operational definitions to be enforced consistently across state, church, and foundation maintainers, despite documented maintainer plurality and partial autonomy frameworks; enforcement consistency is not guaranteed and would require dedicated negotiation with maintainer federations. [assumption]
- SES-proxy measures (e.g., postcode, parental education, free/reduced meal status) are administratively available and legally usable for admissions purposes in Hungary. [assumption]
- Reducing family-capital-driven sorting at intake would not simply be offset by increased investment in coaching for whichever new admission criteria are introduced. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Preserves the approximately 10% competency-gain advantage associated with 6/8-year tracks for admitted students, based on a single recent statistical-model study not yet independently replicated. [evidence: moderate]
- May reduce, but is not proven to eliminate, SES-skew within remaining selective places, since the admissions gate is only one of several sorting channels (school choice, catchment) documented in the evidence. [evidence: weak]
- Avoids the transition costs and political conflict associated with structural abolition or delay of the selection age, since the track structure itself is untouched. [evidence: moderate]

**Equity impact**
Equity gains are plausible but bounded and unevenly distributed: SES-aware admission rules could reduce sorting at the specific admissions gate they target, but the evidence shows children from disadvantaged-composition primary schools already self-select out before reaching the formal exam (information and financial barriers), so a fairer gate may still admit a similar population if outreach and pre-exam barriers are not addressed simultaneously. Families with resources to navigate new criteria (e.g., relocating within a catchment, contesting SES-proxy classification) may partially recapture advantage, and residual selective places — even under reformed admission rules — will remain concentrated in urban, higher-resource areas given the geographic distribution of 6/8-year provision. The reform does not touch school-choice or catchment sorting, which the evidence identifies as an independent channel that can substitute for admissions-gate sorting.

**Cost categories**
- SES-proxy data infrastructure and verification system: low-cost option (using existing administrative registries, magnitude unpublished) vs. high-cost option (new means-testing or verification bureaucracy, magnitude unpublished); no Hungarian costing study exists.
- Exam redesign and coaching-resistance testing: development, piloting and psychometric-validation costs, magnitude unpublished in any cited source.
- Cross-maintainer enforcement and legal negotiation costs (state, church, foundation) to achieve uniform rule application: no numeric estimate exists; likely multi-year, given documented maintainer autonomy frameworks.

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education / Oktatási Hivatal — Draft a national legal framework defining SES-aware admission criteria and uniform score-threshold rules, and begin formal consultation with state, church, and foundation maintainer federations, given documented partial-autonomy constraints. (timeline: year 1-2)
- Educational Authority (Oktatási Hivatal) and exam-design contractors — Pilot and psychometrically validate a coaching-resistant exam design in a subset of institutions, respecting the felmenő-rendszer (grade-by-grade phase-in) legal norm requiring roughly one-year minimum lead time for enrolled cohorts. (timeline: year 2-4)
- Ministry of Education with maintainer federations — Negotiate and legally formalize uniform enforcement mechanisms across maintainer types; absent this step, uniform application cannot be assumed given documented maintainer autonomy. (timeline: year 2-5)
- Oktatási Hivatal — Publish annual disaggregated admissions statistics by track, maintainer, and SES-proxy category to allow independent monitoring of whether the reform reduces sorting. (timeline: year 3 onward)

**Political risks**
- Fidesz or other opposition actors could frame SES-aware quotas as reverse discrimination against merit, mobilizing the same culture-war framing already documented in response to structural-selection proposals, without any specified statutory entrenchment path currently proposed to resist reversal.
- Church and foundation maintainers, protected by partial-autonomy frameworks and coalition commitments (e.g., 'previous operating conditions' guarantees), may resist or slow-walk uniform enforcement, and no evidence-based pathway currently exists for compelling compliance.
- Decree-level implementation (as used for current score thresholds) versus cardinal-law entrenchment carries different reversal risk; no specific statutory instrument has been proposed or modelled for durability against a future government reversing the rule via decree.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether SES-aware quotas or proxies would survive legal challenge or be judged compatible with equal-treatment constitutional doctrine in Hungary is untested. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A legal opinion or Constitutional Court test case assessing SES-based admission criteria against equal-treatment and non-discrimination provisions.)
- Whether uniform enforcement across state, church, and foundation maintainers is achievable without a multi-year negotiated settlement with maintainer federations remains unresolved. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Structured interviews or a negotiated framework agreement with maintainer federations specifying binding compliance mechanisms and their legal basis.)
- Whether reducing family-capital-driven sorting at the admissions gate alone (without touching school choice/catchment) produces a measurable equity gain, given documented pre-exam self-selection by disadvantaged pupils, is unknown. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A decomposition study separating the admissions-gate contribution from school-choice and catchment-driven segregation, using regional variation in kisgimnázium density.)

## S2 — Supply-side contraction of the selective track (shrink the gate, not the rules)
**Goal**
Reduce the state-sector share of 6/8-year places (via score thresholds, capacity caps, or demographically-driven consolidation) to shrink the selective track's footprint, while leaving admission criteria within remaining places largely unchanged.

**Mechanism**
- Government is already using minimum-score admission thresholds set at the maintainer level (e.g., Klebelsberg Center) to shrink the state-sector gimnázium track's share of cohort intake, independent of demand, from roughly 8-12% toward a lower share, though the precise national target and baseline are contested across sources. [evidence: moderate]
- Reducing state-sector selective capacity may incrementally improve peer composition in urban general-school upper grades as fewer top performers depart after grade 4 or 6, though this is documented to also produce mildly negative average effects and unplannable class-size volatility for sending schools during transition. [evidence: moderate]
- Score-threshold-driven contraction in the state sector is not proven to reduce the overall selective-track footprint, because church- and foundation-maintained gimnáziums — whose share of gimnázium students rose from 10.4% (2010) to approximately 27% (2020/2021) — are not bound by the same thresholds and have historically expanded capacity independent of state-sector policy. [evidence: strong]
- Demographic decline (births falling from ~125,000 in the early 1990s to 77,500 in 2024 and an estimated ~72,000 in 2025) will independently shrink the absolute size of the selective track's intake pool regardless of any active score-threshold policy, meaning some contraction is not attributable to this scenario's mechanism. [evidence: strong]

**Evidence status**
moderate — The score-threshold policy mechanism and its demand-independence are moderately evidenced by contemporaneous reporting, but the precise baseline share figure is contested across sources (8-12% per hungarian_education_system vs ~40% per demography), and the displacement risk to church/foundation providers is strongly evidenced but not yet quantified for net effect.

**Assumptions**
- State-sector score thresholds or capacity caps can shrink the selective track's overall footprint without being substantially offset by expansion in church, foundation, or private provision; this is contradicted by documented historical growth of church/foundation gimnázium share (10.4% to 27%, 2010-2020/21) operating independently of state-sector policy, and no mechanism is currently specified to prevent similar offsetting expansion going forward. [assumption]
- Church and foundation maintainers can be regulated or fiscally incentivized not to expand selective places in response to state-sector contraction, though no evidence-based regulatory or incentive pathway for achieving this is currently documented, and coalition commitments to preserve church maintainers' 'previous operating conditions' constrain the available policy levers. [assumption]
- Reduced state-sector selective capacity translates into improved peer composition for remaining general-school students rather than merely relocating the same sorting dynamic to church/foundation or private channels. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- May incrementally improve peer composition in urban general-school upper grades as fewer high performers exit after grade 4 or 6, though the magnitude is not quantified and is contested by the equity_impact concern about residual selective places remaining concentrated among advantaged families. [evidence: weak]
- Does not require legislative restructuring of the track system itself, since it operates through existing maintainer-level administrative levers (score thresholds, capacity caps) that have already been demonstrated as operationally feasible in the 2025/2026 cycle. [evidence: moderate]
- Aligns with, and could be framed as accelerating, an already-occurring demographic contraction, potentially reducing political resistance relative to a structural-delay reform. [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
The equity_impact is genuinely ambiguous and likely negative on the primary displacement pathway: shrinking state-sector selective capacity without a parallel lever over church/foundation or private providers is more likely to intensify SES-skew than reduce it, because the strongest and best-documented growth channel for selective places (church/foundation expansion, 10.4% to 27% of gimnázium share 2010-2020/21) is not addressed by this scenario's mechanism and has historically absorbed demand independent of state-sector policy. Families with the resources to navigate application to church/foundation alternatives, relocate, or pursue private provision would likely retain access to selective education, while families without such resources would face a genuinely shrunk set of options — meaning the policy could concentrate remaining state-sector selective places among a smaller, similarly or more advantaged population, rather than de-selecting overall. Any equity claim for this scenario is contingent on either regulating the church/foundation growth channel or accepting that net segregation may not improve.

**Cost categories**
- Compensatory funding or capacity controls for church/foundation offsetting expansion: flagged as medium-to-high, multi-year, currently unbudgeted; no quantitative estimate exists, and the timing mismatch between state-sector contraction (administratively rapid, achievable within one admissions cycle) and any regulatory response to offsetting expansion (requiring multi-year negotiation with maintainer federations) is not modelled.
- Administrative cost of setting and monitoring nationally-consistent (vs. maintainer-discretionary) thresholds: magnitude unpublished.
- Transition costs for sending general schools coping with class-size volatility and peer-drain effects during the contraction period: magnitude unpublished, though documented as a real operational burden.

**Implementation steps**
- Klebelsberg Center (state school district maintainer) — Formalize minimum-score thresholds into a nationally consistent (rather than institution-discretionary) rule, replacing the current inconsistent two-tier practice, while a parallel legal basis is established since current thresholds have no national legal basis. (timeline: year 1-2)
- Ministry of Education — Design and propose regulatory or fiscal levers (e.g., funding-normatíva adjustments, capacity registration requirements) intended to discourage offsetting expansion by church/foundation maintainers, while negotiating within the constraint of coalition commitments protecting church maintainers' existing operating conditions; feasibility of this step is not established by any cited precedent. (timeline: year 2-4)
- Oktatási Hivatal — Publish disaggregated admissions and capacity data by maintainer type annually to monitor whether state-sector contraction is being offset by church/foundation/private expansion. (timeline: year 2 onward)
- Ministry of Education and sending general schools — Provide transitional staffing and class-composition support to general schools experiencing peer-drain and class-size volatility as the selective track contracts. (timeline: year 2-5)

**Political risks**
- No evidence-based pathway currently exists for regulating or incentivizing church and foundation maintainers not to expand selective places in response to state contraction; this contradicts the scenario's core assumption and is not resolved by any cited precedent, meaning the central equity claim of this scenario currently lacks a credible implementation mechanism.
- Score-threshold policy currently operates via maintainer-level discretionary decision with no national legal basis, creating litigation and legitimacy risk (as already observed with retroactive rejection of borderline students in 2025), and formalizing it nationally would require legislative action subject to the same felmenő-rendszer and coalition constraints as structural reforms.
- Tisza's coalition commitment that church maintainers retain 'previous operating conditions' directly constrains the availability of regulatory or fiscal levers against church/foundation offsetting expansion, creating an internal political contradiction within this scenario's mechanism.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether any regulatory or fiscal lever can effectively discourage church/foundation offsetting expansion without violating coalition commitments or maintainer autonomy protections is unresolved. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A policy-design study or negotiated framework with church/foundation maintainer federations specifying enforceable, mutually acceptable capacity-control mechanisms.)
- The net equity effect of state-sector contraction — whether it reduces overall selective-track footprint or merely redistributes selective access toward church/foundation/private channels — is not measured. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A longitudinal tracking study comparing aggregate selective-track enrollment share (all maintainer types combined) before and after state-sector threshold tightening.)
- The precise current baseline share of cohort intake attending 6/8-year tracks is contested across sources (8-12% vs approximately 40%), undermining confidence in stated contraction targets. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: An authoritative, single KSH/Oktatási Hivatal published figure disaggregating 6/8-year track share from total gimnázium enrollment share, reconciling the divergent baseline estimates.)

## S3 — Structural delay of the selection age (Finnish/Polish model)
**Goal**
Raise the statutory age of first academic tracking (e.g., to 14+ or later), phasing out or converting 6- and 8-year entry points into a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase, paired with explicit anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment.

**Mechanism**
- Poland's 1999 reform, which delayed tracking from age 12/15 to age 15/16, is associated with PISA gains of approximately 0.25 SD in mathematics, 0.28 SD in reading and 0.16 SD in science measured 2000-2006, though the registry tags this evidence as moderate rather than strong, and roughly one-third of the gain is attributed to instructional time with the remainder to compositional/resource changes. [evidence: moderate]
- Finland's 1972-1977 comprehensive reform, delaying tracking from age 11 to age 16, reduced intergenerational income elasticity by 23% (0.30 to 0.23), with mobility gains driven primarily by cognitive-skill improvements among disadvantaged pupils — but this occurred as part of a policy bundle including university-based teacher-education reform and welfare-state expansion, not from delayed tracking in isolation. [evidence: strong]
- Delaying selection alone, without simultaneous anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment, does not guarantee equity gains, since Finland's own post-2000 experience shows equity gains eroded due to school choice and residential sorting creating within-school segregation (94% of outcome variance within-school) even without formal tracking being reintroduced. [evidence: strong]
- Poland's 1999 reform was politically reversed after approximately 18 years (2017-2020 phaseout) despite documented achievement gains, and PISA 2022 results fell to levels last seen in 2003, indicating structural reforms lacking durable multi-party consensus are vulnerable to reversal that can erase gains within a single cohort cycle. [evidence: strong]

**Evidence status**
moderate — The Polish reform's effect-size evidence is tagged moderate (not strong) in the curated registry despite being widely cited with precise figures, and transferability to Hungary's higher-baseline-segregation context, plus dependence on simultaneous anti-sorting enforcement, remain unresolved, so this scenario's core equity claim is more contingent than the headline PISA numbers suggest.

**Assumptions**
- Sorting does not simply migrate from the formal 6/8-year admission gate to school choice, catchment, or streaming within comprehensive schools to the point of neutralizing the equity gain, provided anti-sorting rules are implemented; however, Finland's own post-2000 experience (94% within-school variance driven by choice and residential sorting) and Hungary's currently unregulated school-choice system make this a strong, unverified assumption rather than an established design feature. [assumption]
- Anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment can be designed, legislated, and enforced concurrently with the structural delay itself, rather than lagging it; no Hungarian implementation precedent or costed design for such simultaneous enforcement currently exists. [assumption]
- Polish and Finnish effect sizes (0.16-0.28 SD, 23% mobility-elasticity reduction) would transfer to Hungary's institutional and demographic context despite Hungary's substantially higher baseline segregation (highest in EU) and denser rural small-school network. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Poland-comparable achievement gains of approximately 0.16-0.28 SD across subjects are a plausible upper-bound reference, though Hungary-specific transferability is unverified and the underlying Polish evidence is tagged moderate, not strong. [evidence: moderate]
- Finland's mobility gains (23% reduction in intergenerational income elasticity) suggest a credible mechanism by which delayed tracking benefits disadvantaged pupils' long-run outcomes, though this was achieved within a broader welfare-and-teacher-education policy bundle, not from tracking delay alone. [evidence: strong]
- Would eliminate the specific inconsistency of parallel 4-, 6-, and 8-year admission tracks running simultaneously, a configuration otherwise found in very few OECD systems, simplifying the admissions landscape structurally. [evidence: moderate]

**Equity impact**
Delaying selection is the structurally strongest equity lever in the evidence base (Finland, Poland both show substantial gains), but the scenario's own mechanism and assumptions sections establish that anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment are a load-bearing, not supplementary, requirement — without them, sorting is documented (in Finland's own post-2000 experience) to migrate into within-school streaming and residential/choice-driven segregation, potentially neutralizing the headline equity gain. Hungary's starting point of already-highest-in-EU segregation and an entrenched, legally protected free-school-choice system makes this co-requirement harder to satisfy than in either historical case, and no Hungarian-specific design or costing for simultaneous anti-sorting enforcement currently exists. The equity claim should therefore be read as conditional and unproven for Hungary, not as an established transferable result.

**Cost categories**
- Early diagnostic/remedial support for the extended comprehensive phase: high-cost category, no aggregate fiscal range published for Hungary.
- Teacher retraining and reallocation to staff a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase: high-cost category; no per-teacher retraining estimate or aggregate figure exists for Hungary, and this cost interacts directly with the documented workforce constraint (approximately 50% of primary teachers aged 50+, only 7.2% under 30, teacher shortage of at least 16,000 in 2025/26).
- Facility conversion of dedicated 6/8-year gimnázium buildings into comprehensive-phase capacity: high-cost category, magnitude unpublished; interacts with the dense 880-school network and generally sufficient physical capacity noted by school-network-planning evidence, so facility need may be lower than in systems with capacity shortfalls.
- Anti-sorting enforcement infrastructure for school choice and catchment (monitoring, legal mechanisms, appeals processes): high-cost, ongoing category; no Hungarian costing exists, and this is identified as the load-bearing but least-costed element of the scenario.

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education and Parliament — Draft and pass legislation amending the Nkt. § 4 structural definition of secondary education to raise the statutory tracking age, with a minimum ~1-year statutory lead time and felmenő-rendszer (grade-by-grade phase-in) protections for already-admitted cohorts, and simultaneously draft anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment as a co-requirement, not a follow-on. (timeline: year 1-2)
- Ministry of Education, teacher-training universities, PDSZ/teacher unions — Sequence teacher retraining and workforce-capacity building after (or in careful parallel with, not before) the 2024-2031 salary-reform timeline, explicitly acknowledging that acute STEM and rural teacher shortages constrain the feasible pace of region-by-region rollout; the previously stated year 2-9 window is revised to reflect that full workforce readiness is not assured within this horizon given documented shortage severity. (timeline: year 3-10, contingent on workforce stabilization)
- Oktatási Hivatal and municipal/maintainer authorities — Design, legislate, and begin enforcing anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment concurrently with the tracking-age change, not as a subsequent phase, given that delay of enforcement is documented (via Finland's experience) to allow sorting to migrate to these channels. (timeline: year 1-3, concurrent with legislation)
- Ministry of Education / independent monitoring body — Publish annual equity and achievement monitoring reports (segregation index, SES-achievement gap, anti-sorting rule compliance) to track whether gains materialize and whether sorting has migrated to unregulated channels. (timeline: year 4 onward)

**Political risks**
- Poland's own 18-year reversal despite documented gains demonstrates that structural reforms without durable multi-party consensus are vulnerable to erasure within a single cohort cycle; no cross-party negotiation platform or statutory entrenchment mechanism (e.g., cardinal-law status, supermajority-reversal requirement) has been proposed for this scenario, and Fidesz has already framed comparable proposals as 'totalitarian' abolition, signalling high reversal risk on any future change of government.
- Simultaneous enactment of structural delay and anti-sorting rules is treated as feasible in this scenario's design, but no concrete enforcement mechanism, legal instrument, or monitoring body has been specified with authority over school choice and catchment, and Hungary's free-school-choice system is legally entrenched and politically popular, making rapid co-enactment optimistic relative to documented implementation capacity.
- Church-maintained gimnázium expansion and coalition commitments to preserve church maintainers' operating conditions create a specific political veto risk against any structural change that would convert or phase out 6/8-year entry points at church-run institutions.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment can in practice be enforced strongly enough, and fast enough, to prevent sorting migration to the degree observed in Finland post-2000, given Hungary's higher baseline segregation and legally entrenched school-choice system, is unresolved. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A Hungary-specific pilot (regional or phased) measuring segregation and sorting-migration indicators before and after simultaneous structural delay and anti-sorting rule enforcement.)
- Whether Polish and Finnish effect sizes would transfer to Hungary's specific institutional and demographic context (higher baseline segregation, denser rural small-school network, different maintainer structure) remains untested. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A quasi-experimental regional pilot phase-out of 8-year gimnázium entry with propensity-score-matched comparison regions, tracking achievement and segregation indicators over 5-7 years.)
- Whether teacher workforce capacity (given documented aging, rural/STEM shortages, and CPD bandwidth already committed to NAT and AI-integration reforms) can support full-scale rollout within any politically plausible timeline is highly uncertain. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: A detailed Ministry-commissioned costing and capacity study modelling teacher retraining, reallocation, and recruitment timelines against the current shortage severity and salary-reform trajectory.)
- Whether cross-party or constitutional entrenchment sufficient to prevent an 18-year-style reversal (as in Poland) is politically achievable in Hungary's current environment is unknown. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Explicit cross-party negotiation records, a cardinal-law proposal, or a supermajority-reversal legal safeguard formally tabled in Parliament.)

## S4 — Non-structural compensatory reform (leave selection age unchanged)
**Goal**
Leave the 10-12 selection age and track structure untouched but pursue equity and achievement gains through curriculum clarity, targeted (TEIP-style) funding for disadvantaged schools, retention reduction, and school-quality levelling, following the Portuguese model.

**Mechanism**
- Portugal improved PISA results by approximately 31 points (0.3 SD) between 2000 and 2015-2018 without introducing early tracking, through curriculum standards clarification, increased school autonomy, regular student assessment, and vocational pathway development. [evidence: strong]
- Portugal's TEIP targeted school-support programme reduced student dropout and grade retention, but did not close competency gaps relative to non-TEIP schools, and no rigorous longitudinal evaluation of its long-term student-outcome effects exists. [evidence: moderate]
- Portugal's grade-retention reduction initiative saved EUR 112 million over two years (2016-2018) while reducing retained students by 26% across 663 schools, demonstrating a fiscally self-funding compensatory mechanism, though this precedent has not been translated into a Hungary-specific net-cost estimate given differing retention rates, school-funding formulas, and per-pupil costs. [evidence: strong]
- Non-structural reform leaves the existing 6/8-year admissions gate and its documented SES-skewed gatekeeping (46% rejection in low-poverty-share classes vs. 22% in high-poverty-share classes) fully in place, meaning the primary sorting mechanism identified in the evidence base is not addressed by this scenario's mechanism at all. [evidence: strong]

**Evidence status**
moderate — Portugal's aggregate achievement gains and the fiscal savings from retention reduction are strongly evidenced, but TEIP's competency-gap-closing effect is only moderately evidenced (dropout reduction only, no gap closure shown), and no evidence exists on whether the same compensatory levers would work absent Hungary's specific selection architecture untouched by this scenario.

**Assumptions**
- Curriculum clarity, school autonomy, and regular assessment drive outcomes independently of selection structure, without interactive effects with early tracking that would reverse the ordering observed in Portugal (a system with no early tracking at all), which is an untested transferability assumption given Hungary's specific 6/8-year gate. [assumption]
- TEIP-style targeted funding commitments will be durable and statutorily protected, despite the scenario's own political_risks and the synthesis-level evidence citing fiscal consolidation pressure (projected 6.2% deficit in 2026, 5.8% in 2027, debt rising to 76.8% of GDP by 2027) and identifying targeted funding lines as likely first-line cuts under consolidation; this durability assumption is directly contradicted by cited fiscal evidence and is not resolved by any specified statutory funding-floor mechanism. [assumption]
- Retention reduction and school-quality levelling can be pursued without simultaneously addressing the existing 6/8-year admissions gate's SES-skewed gatekeeping, and will not simply leave the highest-performing, best-resourced pupils continuing to sort into the untouched selective track regardless of compensatory investment elsewhere. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Portugal-comparable achievement gains (approximately 0.3 SD) are a plausible reference point for what curriculum clarity, assessment reform, and autonomy measures could achieve in Hungary, though no Hungary-specific modelling of this transfer exists. [evidence: moderate]
- Retention reduction is documented as potentially net-cost-saving (Portugal's EUR 112 million two-year saving), offering a rare compensatory-reform element that could be fiscally self-justifying even under consolidation pressure, though a Hungary-specific costed estimate does not yet exist. [evidence: strong]
- Avoids the legal, administrative, and political costs of restructuring the selection-age architecture (felmenő-rendszer phase-ins, maintainer negotiations, cross-party entrenchment), since the track structure remains untouched. [evidence: moderate]

**Equity impact**
This scenario's equity impact is plausibly positive for the general-school population (reduced dropout, better-resourced disadvantaged schools) but is equity-neutral-to-negative with respect to the specific segregation mechanism this whole problem area is about, because it leaves the 6/8-year admissions gate and its documented SES-skewed gatekeeping (46% vs 22% rejection rates by poverty-share) completely untouched. The scenario does not specify how compensatory investment in general schools will interact with the existing gatekeeping to protect or improve outcomes for low-SES high-performing pupils who are currently filtered out before or at the exam stage; without such a mechanism, the highest-resourced families likely continue to access the untouched selective track while disadvantaged high performers continue to be excluded, meaning this path could improve average outcomes for those remaining outside the selective track without narrowing the specific selective-track access gap that drives Hungary's segregation ranking.

**Cost categories**
- TEIP-style targeted funding for disadvantaged schools: Portugal's programme scale and design are documented, but no Hungary-specific net-cost estimate exists; Portugal's EUR 112 million two-year retention-reduction saving is not yet translated into a Hungary-specific projection given differing baseline retention rates and per-pupil funding formulas.
- Curriculum-clarity and assessment-reform development costs: magnitude unpublished for a Hungary-specific rollout.
- School-quality levelling investment (facility, staffing, resource equalization across schools): high-cost, multi-year category with no aggregate figure; interacts with the same teacher-shortage and rural/urban distribution constraints documented for structural scenarios.

**Implementation steps**
- Ministry of Education and National Core Curriculum (NAT) review body — Revise curriculum standards for clarity and coherence, sequenced carefully against the already-committed NAT-update and AI-integration-roadmap timelines to avoid CPD-bandwidth conflicts documented as a binding constraint. (timeline: year 1-3)
- Ministry of Education and Ministry of Finance — Design a TEIP-style targeted funding mechanism for disadvantaged schools, and seek to establish it on a statutory (cardinal-law) rather than annual-budget basis to address the durability risk documented under fiscal consolidation pressure — while acknowledging this statutory entrenchment itself faces uncertain achievability given the 2027 budget bill has not yet been published. (timeline: year 1-2 (design), year 2 onward (implementation, contingent on funding-floor achievability))
- Ministry of Education and school-level administrators — Implement grade-retention-reduction measures (assessment reform, remedial support prioritized over automatic retention), modelled on Portugal's approach and its documented net-cost-saving potential. (timeline: year 2-5)
- Oktatási Hivatal / independent research institute — Commission a longitudinal evaluation tracking whether compensatory measures close competency gaps (not just reduce dropout/retention), and monitor whether the untouched 6/8-year gate continues to absorb disproportionately high-SES pupils despite general-school improvements. (timeline: year 3 onward)

**Political risks**
- Statutory (cardinal-law) funding floors for TEIP-style targeted programmes are assumed achievable, yet fiscal consolidation pressure (projected 6.2% deficit 2026, rising debt to 76.8% of GDP by 2027) makes targeted funding lines a likely first-line budget cut, and no 2027 budget bill language has yet confirmed such a floor; this creates a direct contradiction between the scenario's durability assumption and the documented fiscal environment.
- Leaving the selective-track admissions gate untouched risks being framed by segregation-focused critics (including EU country-report pressure and domestic researchers like Berényi and Nahalka) as an insufficient response that avoids the primary documented driver of Hungary's segregation ranking, undermining political credibility of the reform as an equity measure.
- PSZ and other teacher-union stakeholders have framed workforce adequacy (16,000-teacher shortage) as the binding, non-negotiable precondition for any reform; compensatory measures requiring additional school-level staffing and CPD bandwidth compete directly with this already-strained capacity and with concurrent NAT/AI-integration commitments.

**Uncertainties**
- Whether TEIP-style compensatory measures can close competency gaps rather than merely reduce dropout is unresolved because no longitudinal study has tracked student competency trajectories beyond retention reduction. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: A 5-8 year longitudinal matched-cohort study comparing TEIP-enrolled and non-enrolled students, controlling for baseline SES and prior achievement, tracking assessed competency gain.)
- The fiscal and pedagogical cost of Hungary-specific compensatory-reform rollout (curriculum, TEIP-equivalent funding, retention reduction, quality-levelling) has not been modelled in published Hungarian research. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: A scenario analysis prepared by the Ministry of Education or a major research institute modelling capital reallocation, staffing, and transition costs over 10-15 years under different rollout schedules.)
- Whether leaving the 6/8-year admissions gate untouched while investing in general schools produces any measurable narrowing of the specific selective-track access gap, or only improves outcomes for pupils who remain outside the selective track, is unknown. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A decomposition study tracking selective-track admission composition by SES before and after compensatory investment in sending general schools, holding admissions rules constant.)

## S5 — Demographically-driven passive contraction (status-quo baseline)
**Goal**
The no-new-policy baseline in which shrinking birth cohorts force network consolidation and a natural decline in the selective track's absolute size and share regardless of any active equity or structural intervention, serving as the counterfactual against which S1-S4 are measured.

**Mechanism**
- Annual births fell from approximately 125,000 in the early 1990s to 77,500 in 2024, with a further 7.1% decline estimated to roughly 72,000 in 2025 (a specific figure the demography record itself flags as an estimate, not a confirmed count, while the underlying strong-evidence claim is the multi-decade decline trend and its consolidation-forcing effect, not the precise most-recent-year count). [evidence: moderate]
- Long-run population projections (KSH/HDRI SEEMIG medium-variant) show continuous population decline through at least 2060 under all fertility and migration scenarios, with the under-20 population declining from ~2 million (2012) to approximately 1.4 million by 2060, meaning the school-age cohort available for all secondary tracks shrinks structurally through 2040 and beyond, independent of any selection policy. [evidence: strong]
- Passive demographic contraction is not policy-neutral in practice, because the government is simultaneously and actively using score-threshold levers (documented in S2) to shrink the selective track's share beyond what demographic decline alone would cause; treating S5 as a pure counterfactual therefore risks conflating a genuinely passive baseline with an already-interventionist trajectory. [evidence: strong]
- Physical school-network capacity (the dense 880-school grid, underused dormitory capacity) is not currently the binding constraint on selective-track viability almost anywhere in Hungary; the binding constraint is the shrinking and unevenly distributed child population itself, meaning consolidation pressure will fall disproportionately on already-thin rural markets rather than uniformly across the network. [evidence: moderate]

**Evidence status**
moderate — The multi-decade demographic decline trend and its consolidation-forcing effect are strongly evidenced, but this scenario's framing as a clean policy-neutral counterfactual is itself contested, since the same evidence base documents active government use of score thresholds (S2) operating simultaneously with and independent of pure demographic contraction.

**Assumptions**
- Demographic contraction can be modelled and presented as a 'passive,' policy-neutral baseline distinct from S1-S4, despite the same evidence base documenting that the government is already using active administrative levers (score thresholds, S2) that interact with and amplify demographic effects; treating S5 as policy-neutral risks understating how much of any observed contraction is attributable to active choices rather than demography alone. [assumption]
- Cohort-size projections (declining under-20 population) are stable across the planning horizon (2025-2040) and do not assume major immigration reversing natural decrease. [assumption]
- School-maintainer sustainability thresholds (minimum viable class/school size) are a binding constraint that will force consolidation in a predictable, modellable way, rather than producing ad hoc, politically-contingent closures that deviate substantially from demographic projections. [assumption]

**Expected benefits**
- Requires no new legislative, administrative, or fiscal action, making it the default trajectory absent any deliberate policy choice among S1-S4. [evidence: strong]
- Will mechanically reduce the absolute size of the selective track over time simply through cohort shrinkage, though this is a scale reduction, not a demonstrated equity improvement, since the evidence does not show that passive contraction changes who is selected, only how many are. [evidence: weak]
- Provides a clean counterfactual baseline against which the incremental equity and achievement effects of S1-S4 can in principle be measured, though this benefit is analytical/methodological rather than a direct policy outcome for students. [evidence: weak]

**Equity impact**
Passive demographic contraction reduces the absolute number of selective-track places but provides no mechanism to change who is selected among the shrinking pool; without active intervention, existing SES-skewed admissions gatekeeping, church/foundation expansion patterns, and school-choice sorting all continue operating on a smaller population, meaning segregation intensity (the share-based and compositional measures, not just absolute counts) could remain unchanged or even worsen if the shrinking pool becomes more geographically and socioeconomically concentrated in surviving urban institutions. Critically, this baseline is not the clean equity-neutral counterfactual it is often treated as: because the government is simultaneously running an active score-threshold policy (S2) atop the demographic trend, any observed contraction in practice reflects a blend of passive and active forces, and using S5 as if it isolates the 'demography-only' effect risks systematically understating the active policy's independent contribution.

**Cost categories**
- School-network consolidation and closure costs (facility repurposing, transport/busing for consolidated hub schools) driven by demographic decline: documented as a real cost category via cross-border analogue (Ukraine's 45-student viability floor forcing busing), but no Hungary-specific aggregate figure exists.
- Teacher workforce redeployment or displacement costs from closing small schools: unquantified; interacts directly with the documented aging/shortage workforce profile.
- Opportunity cost of forgoing active equity intervention: not a fiscal cost category per se, but represents the unaddressed segregation and achievement-gap costs that continue to accrue under a no-new-policy trajectory.

**Implementation steps**
- KSH and Ministry of Education (monitoring only, no active policy action) — Continue publishing demographic and enrollment projections to inform maintainer-level consolidation decisions, without introducing new selection-structure or admissions policy. (timeline: ongoing, year 1 onward)
- Individual school maintainers (state, church, foundation, municipal) — Make case-by-case consolidation, merger, or closure decisions as enrollment falls below sustainability thresholds, absent any coordinated national framework specific to selective-track viability. (timeline: year 1-15, continuous as thresholds are crossed)
- Oktatási Hivatal / independent researchers — Track whether passive contraction changes the compositional (SES, geographic) profile of the selective track, to distinguish genuinely demographic effects from the simultaneous active score-threshold policy (S2). (timeline: year 2 onward)

**Political risks**
- Framing passive demographic contraction as the 'path of least resistance' politically is complicated by the fact that the same government is simultaneously using active score-threshold levers (S2); presenting S5 as a politically inert baseline risks obscuring that the status quo already includes an active, contested policy lever, undermining its use as a neutral comparator in public deliberation.
- Rural and small-town consolidation driven purely by demographic thresholds (as in the Ukraine/Beregszász analogue) risks provoking local political backlash over school closures without any compensatory framework, since this scenario by design includes no compensatory or transition-support mechanism.
- Absence of any active equity mechanism in this baseline leaves Hungary's EU-highest segregation ranking and associated EU country-report pressure unaddressed, creating reputational and funding-conditionality risk if S5 is allowed to persist as the default outcome by political inaction rather than deliberate choice.

**Uncertainties**
- The precise magnitude of the most recent annual birth-count decline (e.g., the ~72,000 2025 estimate) is flagged by the source itself as an estimate, and the relative contribution of passive demographic decline versus active score-threshold policy (S2) to observed selective-track contraction is not decomposed in any cited study. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A decomposition analysis using KSH/Oktatási Hivatal administrative data separating the demographically-attributable share of selective-track contraction from the policy-attributable share (score-threshold effects), before and after the threshold policy's introduction.)
- Whether consolidation-induced school closure displaces experienced teachers or improves efficiency, and how this interacts with pedagogical quality in surviving small schools, is unknown. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Longitudinal administrative data on teacher movements, school staffing and pupil-to-teacher ratios pre- and post-consolidation, and value-added models of school quality in rural vs. urban surviving schools.)
- Whether the shrinking selective-track pool will become more or less geographically and socioeconomically concentrated as consolidation proceeds — i.e., whether passive contraction intensifies or dilutes segregation intensity — is not measured in any cited source. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A settlement-level longitudinal tracking study of selective-track compositional indicators (SES, geography) as cohort sizes shrink, ideally isolating periods without concurrent active score-threshold policy changes.)
