# Synthesis

## Overview
Ten expert analyses converge on a well-evidenced empirical core — Hungary combines an unusually early, multi-point selection architecture (age 7, then the 6/8-year gimnázium gate at 10–12) with the EU's highest socioeconomic school segregation, a 53-point PISA parent-education gap versus a 33-point EU average, and a raw grammar-vocational achievement gap (116 points) that collapses to 24 points once family background is controlled — while simultaneously undergoing an accelerating demographic contraction (fertility at 1.31 in 2025, births at their lowest since 1949) that will force network consolidation regardless of selection policy. International comparators (Finland's 1972–77 reform, Poland's 1999 reform and its 2017 reversal, Portugal's non-structural gains) show that delaying tracking can raise equity without an average-achievement cost, but also that such gains are fragile — vulnerable to school-choice-driven within-school sorting (Finland) and to partisan reversal within a single electoral cycle (Poland). Politically, the landscape shifted concretely after Tisza's April 2026 victory and the education minister's June 2026 statement framing the 6/8-year tracks as elite self-selection rather than pedagogy, but this opened a live culture-war fight (Fidesz's 'totalitarian' framing), a narrowed reform scope (church-maintainer protections, review-not-abolition), and unresolved stakeholder preconditions (teacher-workforce stabilization, statutory funding floors) under fiscal-consolidation pressure. Legally, the selection age is a codified exception rather than the structural default, making it more tractable to amend than the broader maintainer architecture, but any change must proceed via felmenő-rendszer (grade-by-grade) phase-in with roughly a one-year minimum lead time. The experts disagree sharply, however, on how much weight the modest (~10%) within-track value-added evidence should carry against the equity case, on whether non-state maintainers can be feasibly restrained from absorbing displaced selective demand, and on the near-term political feasibility and durability of any structural reform.

## Disagreement map
### Whether the ~10% within-track pedagogical value-added justifies preserving early selection
- **education_finance, hungarian_education_system**: 6/8-year gimnázium students show ~10% larger competency gains than similar-ability peers in 4-year tracks, a genuine (if unreplicated) pedagogical/peer-effect benefit that must be weighed against equity costs, not dismissed. Why: Horn Dániel's statistical-model research (moderate evidence) controls for family background and finds a real gain; the researcher himself does not conclude this justifies the system, but the finding is treated as a genuine trade-off input.
- **equity_and_social_mobility, international_comparison, portuguese_reform**: The value-added evidence is heterogeneous (negative at grade 8 for 6-year tracks, positive only later), confounded by unmeasured peer/teacher sorting, and in any case does not outweigh Hungary's status as the EU's most segregated system; the equity case against early selection is stronger than the achievement case for it. Why: HÉTFA panel data show divergent value-added by track type; cross-national DiD evidence shows early tracking raises dispersion without raising the mean; Portugal achieved comparable PISA gains without any tracking.

### Whether non-state (church/foundation) maintainers can be restricted from absorbing phased-out selective capacity
- **implementation_planning, legal_and_governance** (minority): A national reform can, in principle, be designed to constrain non-state maintainers' selective-track expansion through coordinated legislative and funding-agreement mechanisms, provided consensus-building with maintainer federations occurs. Why: Legal analysis finds the selection age is a codified, narrow exception amendable by statute; implementation planning treats maintainer coordination as a solvable multi-lever problem given adequate lead time.
- **hungarian_education_system, political_feasibility**: Non-state maintainers are structurally positioned to absorb any phased-out selective capacity: church-run gimnázium share already rose from 10.4% (2010) to ~27% (2020/21), post-2020 legislation gave churches free building transfers, and Tisza's own 12-point programme commits that church maintainers retain their prior operating conditions — making restriction politically and institutionally unlikely. Why: Empirical maintainer-share trend data plus the explicit coalition commitment in Tisza's published programme directly contradict the assumption that restriction is a viable, cost-free lever.

### Near-term political feasibility and durability of structural selection-age reform
- **political_feasibility**: Reform is now live government policy with genuine EU tailwind, but cross-party durability is low-probability in the near term; feasibility exists only if the government narrows scope to pilot-phase rebalancing with explicit quality-levelling and abandons abolition rhetoric. Why: Fidesz's immediate 'totalitarian' framing within 48 hours of the minister's statement, the pattern of rapid reversal of predecessor reforms (teacher evaluation), and unresolved fiscal-consolidation pressure on funding guarantees all point to high reversal risk.
- **implementation_planning** (minority): Structural reform is not feasible before 2030 at all without prior teacher-workforce stabilization, explicit sequencing away from concurrent NAT/AI reforms, and multi-party legislative consensus — only the first precondition is even in progress. Why: Teacher shortages are acute and geographically uneven (2–3× higher unqualified-teacher rates in disadvantaged/rural schools), CPD bandwidth is already committed to other reforms through 2026, and Poland's reversal after 18 years shows durability cannot be assumed.

### Whether abolishing the admission examination alone would meaningfully reduce segregation
- **hungarian_education_system, school_network_planning**: Abolishing early selection without simultaneously reforming school choice and catchment rules would leave between-school sorting substantially unchanged, since free school choice and residential/catchment segregation operate independently of the formal exam gate. Why: Kertesi & Kézdi and Berényi-Berkovits-Erőss school-choice studies document strong between-school sorting already at the primary stage; Finland's post-2000 experience shows choice-driven within-school segregation persisting after formal de-tracking.
- **political_feasibility** (minority): Researcher Nahalka argues abolishing the exam without levelling within-system school-quality variance would simply reproduce an equally inequitable system via other selection channels, while Szülői Hang leader Miklós argues school-quality gaps, not the exam, are the root problem — both converge that a single-lever abolition is insufficient, but disagree on where the primary lever should be. Why: Both positions are drawn from named Hungarian stakeholders in the current live policy debate (June 2026), representing a genuine within-country disagreement on causal priority, not a resolved empirical question.

## What the experts agree on
- Hungary has the EU's highest socioeconomic school segregation and one of the OECD's largest SES-achievement gaps (53 PISA points vs. 33 EU average). [evidence: strong]
- The raw achievement gap between grammar and vocational-track students shrinks dramatically (from 116 to 24 points) once family background is controlled for, indicating selection/composition rather than pure school-quality effects drive most of the observed gap. [evidence: strong]
- Hungary's accelerating demographic decline (record-low fertility and births) will force school-network consolidation over the 2025–2040 horizon independent of any selection-policy choice. [evidence: strong]
- International difference-in-differences evidence shows early tracking increases inequality of outcomes without raising average achievement, while delaying tracking (Poland 1999, Finland 1972–77) can produce equity gains without an achievement penalty. [evidence: strong]
- Structural education reforms that lack durable multi-party political consensus are vulnerable to reversal within a single electoral cycle, as demonstrated by Poland's 2016–2019 rollback of its 1999 reform. [evidence: strong]
- Physical school-network infrastructure (buildings, transport, dormitories) is not currently the binding constraint on delivering or reforming early selection; the binding constraints are demographic (shrinking cohorts), workforce-related (teacher shortages), and political. [evidence: strong]
- Any structural change to the selection age must, for legal and legitimacy reasons, proceed via a felmenő-rendszer (grade-by-grade, cohort-by-cohort) phase-in with a minimum lead time of roughly one year to protect already-admitted students. [evidence: moderate]
- Teacher shortages are acute, aging, and geographically concentrated in rural and disadvantaged areas, constraining the pace and sequencing of any region-by-region selection-structure reform. [evidence: strong]
