# Policy brief — Early academic selection and the 6/8-year gimnázium

This brief synthesizes the expert evidence base and a stakeholder stress test on reforming early academic selection (age 10-12 entry to 6/8-year gimnázium tracks) in Hungary. Five policy scenarios are compared. Every claim below is tagged by kind — fact, estimate, assumption, or value — and by evidence strength where applicable, following the discipline that a value judgment must never be presented as a settled fact. Disagreement among experts and among stakeholder voices is preserved throughout, not resolved into false consensus.

## Scenario key (full detail: scenarios.en.md)
- S1 — S1 — Admissions and intake-equity regulation: keep the 6/8-year gimnázium structure, but regulate the admission gate (SES-aware quotas, uniform thresholds, coaching-resistant exams). See scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md.
- S2 — S2 — Supply-side contraction of the selective track: shrink the state-sector share of 6/8-year places via score thresholds and capacity caps, leaving admission criteria largely unchanged. See scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md.
- S3 — S3 — Structural delay of the selection age (Finnish/Polish model): raise the statutory tracking age to 14+ via a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase, paired with anti-sorting rules for choice and catchment. See scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md.
- S4 — S4 — Non-structural compensatory reform (Portuguese model): leave the selection age untouched; pursue equity via curriculum clarity, TEIP-style targeted funding, and retention reduction. See scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md.
- S5 — S5 — Demographically-driven passive contraction: the no-new-policy baseline in which shrinking birth cohorts force network consolidation regardless of active policy choice. See scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md.

## What we know
- Hungary's school-age cohort is shrinking structurally and will continue to do so at least through 2040-2060, forcing school-network consolidation regardless of selection-policy choices. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Hungary has the highest socio-economic school segregation in the EU, with a parent-education-linked achievement gap (53 PISA points) far above the EU average (33 points). [fact] (evidence: strong)
- The raw achievement gap between grammar-track (gimnázium) and vocational-track students shrinks from 116 to 24 points once family background is statistically controlled, indicating that composition and selection effects dominate the raw gap. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- International difference-in-differences evidence across dozens of countries shows early academic tracking increases between-school and socio-economic inequality without raising average achievement. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Poland's 1999 delayed-tracking reform produced measurable PISA gains (0.25 SD math, 0.28 SD reading, 0.16 SD science) and reduced between-school inequality, but individual-level socio-economic inequity did not improve, and the reform was politically reversed after 18 years. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Finland's 1972-1977 reform, which delayed tracking from age 11 to 16, reduced intergenerational income elasticity by 23% (from 0.30 to 0.23), with gains driven primarily by cognitive-skill improvements among disadvantaged pupils. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Hungary's teacher workforce is structurally aging and contracting, with acute shortages concentrated in rural areas and STEM subjects — roughly 16,000+ vacancies in 2025/26, about 50% of primary teachers over 50, and only 7.2% under 30. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Church-maintained schools' share of gimnázium provision has grown substantially, from approximately 10.4% in 2010 to approximately 27% in 2020/2021, concentrated disproportionately among elite 6/8-year tracks. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Portugal improved its PISA results by approximately 31 points (0.3 SD) between 2000 and 2015-2018 without introducing early tracking, through curriculum standards clarification, increased school autonomy, regular assessment, and vocational pathway development. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Portugal's 2016-2018 retention-reduction reform simultaneously reduced retained (grade-repeating) students by 26% across 663 schools and saved EUR 112 million, showing at least one compensatory-bundle component can be net-cost-saving rather than purely cost-adding. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- As of mid-2026, no binding legislative timetable, formal government resolution, or Oktatási Hivatal review mandate on selection reform has been published; the government's commitment remains consultative rather than statutory. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Legally, early selection (the 6/8-year gimnázium track) is codified as a narrow, separately-justified statutory exception rather than the structural default of secondary education, and any reform touching already-enrolled cohorts is expected to require a felmenő-rendszer (grade-by-grade phase-in) with roughly one-year minimum lead time. [fact] (evidence: moderate)
- Hungarian-specific research finds a modest (~10%) competency-gain advantage for students admitted to elite 6/8-year tracks compared to similar-ability peers in 4-year tracks, though the value-added estimate diverges sharply by track type and grade. [fact] (evidence: moderate)

## What we consider likely
- Anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment are the load-bearing, currently unproven component of structural-delay reform (S3): without them, sorting is likely to migrate into choice/catchment/private-tutoring channels, echoing Finland's post-2000 erosion pattern. [estimate]
- Contracting state-sector selective capacity (S2) without an explicit counter-lever on non-state maintainers will likely be substantially offset by continued church/foundation-sector expansion, given the documented growth pattern already predates formal score-threshold policy. [estimate]
- A fairer admission gate alone (S1), without outreach measures, is likely to leave the applicant pool largely unchanged, since disadvantaged-composition primary schools already show near-zero attempt rates at the entrance exam. [estimate]
- Durable structural reform likely requires either cross-party consensus or constitutional/cardinal-law entrenchment; absent this, unilateral structural change faces a high probability of reversal on the next change of government, mirroring Poland's 18-year reversal and Hungary's own recent pattern of reversing predecessor education policies. [estimate]
- TEIP-style targeted funding for disadvantaged schools (S4) is likely to be vulnerable to being a first-line cut during fiscal consolidation unless it receives statutory, multi-year protection, given projected fiscal pressure (6.2% deficit in 2026, debt rising to 76.8% of GDP by 2027). [estimate]
- Non-structural compensatory reform (S4) is likely to be equity-neutral to modestly positive within the general-school sector, but is unlikely to meaningfully change the near-total exclusion of multiply-disadvantaged pupils from elite selective tracks, since it does not alter the selection gate itself. [estimate]

## Where experts disagree
### Whether non-state (church, foundation) maintainers can be restricted from absorbing phased-out selective capacity
- **hungarian_education_system, implementation_planning**: A phase-out of state-maintained 6/8-year tracks is a coherent policy lever, because church-school expansion and parallel-track multiplication are policy-influenced (not purely demand-driven) and thus reversible through legislative and funding changes. [value] Why: Church-maintained schools' share of all gimnázium students rose from 10.4% (2010) to ~27% (2020/2021), coinciding with post-2020 legislation permitting free building transfers to churches — evidence interpreted as policy-driven rather than autonomous demand growth.
- **political_feasibility, legal_and_governance** (minority): Any national reform faces a binding coalition and legal constraint: the current government's own 12-point programme explicitly commits that church maintainers will keep their previous operating conditions, and church-maintained admissions operate under concordats with partial autonomy — meaning non-state maintainers are structurally positioned to absorb or resist contraction, not merely comply with it. [value] Why: The Tisza government's published programme (April 2026) explicitly protects church maintainers' operating conditions as a coalition-preserving commitment, and legal_and_governance notes church-maintained admissions retain partial legal autonomy under concordat-style agreements requiring separate negotiation.
### How much of Hungary's school segregation is attributable specifically to the 6/8-year selection gate versus broader school-choice and catchment sorting
- **hungarian_education_system, international_comparison, equity_and_social_mobility**: Early selection at age 10-12 is a documented, non-trivial and primary structural driver of Hungary's exceptionally high segregation; the raw 116-point achievement gap shrinks to 24 points once family background is controlled, indicating selection itself accounts for most of the observed gap. [value] Why: PISA 2022-based analysis (Taní-tani Online) and cross-national compositional-effect literature (Sciffer et al.) directly link earlier tracking ages to larger between-school SES sorting; Hungary's social selectivity index deteriorated by 5.3 points 2018-2022, the largest deterioration in Europe alongside Poland's tracking reintroduction.
- **hungarian_education_system, equity_and_social_mobility** (minority): Free school choice plus catchment-area sorting produce strong between-school segregation even without early selective tracks; abolishing the 6/8-year gate alone would not eliminate sorting, since it is unclear how much of the compositional damage is caused by the gate itself versus choice/catchment mechanisms operating independently. [value] Why: Kertesi & Kezdi (2013) and Berenyi-Berkovits-Erőss studies document strong sorting at the primary stage via choice and catchment independent of tracks; equity_and_social_mobility explicitly flags this decomposition as an unresolved uncertainty requiring dedicated study.
### Whether the measured ~10% competency-gain advantage in elite tracks reflects genuine pedagogical value or unmeasured confounds
- **education_finance, hungarian_education_system**: Students in 6/8-year gimnázium show roughly 10% larger competency gains than similar-ability, similar-background peers in 4-year gimnázium, suggesting a real peer-effect or pedagogical value-added distinct from pure selection. [value] Why: Horn Dániel's (Corvinus) statistical-model research explicitly controls for family background and prior ability, distinguishing this from raw achievement-gap comparisons; the finding is treated as evidence, not merely an assumption.
- **equity_and_social_mobility, education_finance** (minority): Whether the ~10% value-added estimate reflects genuine school effect versus unmeasured peer-composition or teacher-sorting confounds remains unresolved; value-added estimates diverge sharply by track type, undermining a uniform value-added narrative. [value] Why: HÉTFA Working Paper 2015/11 (Országos Kompetenciamérés panel data) documents divergent, track-specific value-added trajectories; both education_finance and equity_and_social_mobility flag low confidence in the causal interpretation absent regression-discontinuity or matched replication.
### Whether structural reform durability requires cross-party consensus or can proceed via unilateral government action
- **political_feasibility, polish_reform, implementation_planning**: Durable reform requires either cross-party consensus or constitutional/cardinal-law entrenchment; absent this, any selection-structure reform enacted unilaterally faces high-probability reversal on the next government change. [value] Why: Poland's PiS government reversed the 1999 reform after 18 years despite documented achievement gains; Fidesz immediately framed the 2026 Hungarian minister's review statement as 'totalitarian,' establishing high reversal risk absent negotiated consensus.
- **legal_and_governance** (minority): Early selection is codified as a narrow, separately-justified statutory exception, making legislative reform of the selection age more legally tractable via ordinary legislative process with felmenő-rendszer phase-in, without necessarily requiring cross-party negotiation as a legal precondition — the KEKVA precedent shows entire maintainer models can be legally dismantled within weeks when the government has the will. [value] Why: The 2026 KEKVA dismantling (foundation-run public institutions) occurred within weeks following a constitutional amendment, demonstrating legal tractability of rapid unilateral structural change when political will exists, independent of durability considerations.
### Whether the pedagogical value-added of elite tracks should carry equal normative weight to their equity cost
- **equity_and_social_mobility, hungarian_education_system, finnish_reform, international_comparison**: Given Hungary's already-highest-in-EU segregation, the equity case against early selection is stronger than the achievement case for it; policy alternatives should be judged primarily by whether they reduce SES-based sorting at intake. [value] Why: International DiD evidence (75 countries, 20 years) and Hungary's own comprehensivization-era experience (2000s reform) show removing early selection raises the floor for previously-tracked students without lowering the ceiling for grammar-track students.
- **education_finance** (minority): The ~10% learning-gain finding for admitted students represents a genuine tension, not a settled verdict: any policy alternative must weigh the demonstrated pedagogical value-added against equity harms, rather than treating equity as automatically dominant. [value] Why: education_finance explicitly frames Hungary's 8%-of-cohort elite-track structure, entrenched parental demand, and contested church-funding architecture as making reform politically costly, not merely technically straightforward.

## What we don't know
- Whether admission-equity rules (S1) can actually be enforced consistently across state, church, and foundation maintainers given documented maintainer plurality and partial legal autonomy remains unresolved. [assumption]
- Whether state-sector contraction (S2) will be net-neutralized by church/foundation/private-sector expansion or geographic relocation of advantaged families is a known unknown, given the historical growth pattern predates formal score-threshold policy. [estimate]
- The precise current national-level share of pupils in 6/8-grade tracks and its trend is uncertain: the 8-12% (registry) versus 40%→30% (policy-discourse) figures refer to different denominators and have not been reconciled in a single authoritative dataset. [fact]
- Whether anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment (S3's load-bearing co-requirement) can actually be designed and enforced in a way that prevents sorting migration is unknown, since Finland's own post-2000 experience shows such migration occurred despite no formal tracking reintroduction. [assumption]
- Whether the teacher workforce can realistically be expanded and retrained within any feasible implementation window for structural reform (S3), given acute existing shortages and an aging profile, has not been established by a detailed feasibility study. [assumption]
- Whether TEIP-style targeted funding (S4) can close competency gaps rather than merely reducing dropout remains unknown, since no longitudinal study has tracked student competency trajectories beyond retention reduction even in Portugal itself. [fact]
- The true causal magnitude of the ~10% competency-gain figure attributed to elite tracks remains unreplicated and could reflect peer-composition or teacher-sorting confounds rather than genuine pedagogical value-added. [assumption]
- Whether the passive-demographic (S5) and active score-threshold (S2) contributions to selective-track composition change can be reliably decomposed in available data is unresolved, limiting the baseline's usefulness for isolating S1-S4's incremental effects. [assumption]

## What could be done
- **S1 — Regulate the admission gate, keep the structure**: Keep the 6/8-year gimnázium structure intact but regulate the admission mechanism — SES-aware quotas or proxies, uniform score-threshold rules across all maintainer types, and coaching-resistant exam design — to reduce family-capital-driven sorting at the point of entry, without touching total selective capacity or the age of first tracking.
- **S2 — Shrink the gate, not the rules**: Reduce the state-sector share of 6/8-year places via score thresholds, capacity caps, or demographically-driven consolidation, shrinking the selective track's footprint while leaving admission criteria within remaining places largely unchanged.
- **S3 — Delay the selection age structurally (Finnish/Polish model)**: Raise the statutory age of first academic tracking to 14+, phasing out 6- and 8-year entry points into a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase, paired with explicit, simultaneously-enforced anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment.
- **S4 — Leave the gate alone, fix everything else (Portuguese model)**: Pursue equity and achievement gains through curriculum clarity, TEIP-style targeted funding for disadvantaged schools, retention reduction, and school-quality levelling, leaving the 10-12 selection age and track structure untouched.
- **S5 — Let demographics do the work (status-quo baseline)**: Serve as the no-new-policy baseline in which shrinking birth cohorts force network consolidation and a natural decline in the selective track's absolute size, regardless of any active equity or structural intervention, against which S1-S4 are measured.

## What each option costs
- **S1**: Preserves the ~10% competency-gain advantage for track entrants since structure is unchanged, but equity gains are conditional and partial: without touching total selective capacity, catchment, choice, or pre-exam self-selection, a fairer gate may still admit a barely-changed population — the disadvantaged families who would benefit most already show near-zero attempt rates. [estimate]
- **S1**: Costs fall on: SES-proxy infrastructure and data systems, coaching-resistance exam redesign and psychometric validation, and legal/administrative enforcement across maintainer types — none quantified in Hungarian sources; church and foundation maintainers bear a compliance burden that may fall disproportionately on smaller, less-resourced providers. [estimate]
- **S2**: Winners: potentially the state-sector's remaining SES composition improves. Losers: church/foundation maintainers absorb displaced advantaged demand (already documented historical pattern), state-sector families face unpredictable rationing, and rural communities risk disproportionate school closure while urban elite-track capacity remains comparatively protected. [assumption]
- **S2**: No aggregate cost estimate exists for compensatory funding to counter offsetting non-state expansion, displacement-monitoring infrastructure, or legal enforcement absent a uniform national legal basis for score thresholds; this remains one of the most under-costed scenarios. [estimate]
- **S3**: The strongest equity case in the evidence base (Finnish mobility gains, Polish between-school convergence), but at the highest cost and risk: felmenő-rendszer phase-in, teacher retraining against an acute 16,000+ vacancy shortage, facility conversion, and anti-sorting enforcement are all high-cost, mostly unquantified categories; church/foundation maintainer autonomy and parental early-choice preferences bear the sharpest institutional and political cost. [estimate]
- **S3**: Individual-level SES-outcome correlation did not improve in Poland even as between-school disparities shrank — meaning this option compresses between-school inequality but does not guarantee individual-level mobility gains without concurrently enforced anti-sorting measures, which are themselves the scenario's most under-costed load-bearing component. [fact]
- **S4**: Lower political, legal and administrative cost than S1-S3 since the track structure is untouched, and retention-reduction may be net-cost-saving (Portugal precedent). But this option leaves the primary documented driver of segregation — the selection gate itself — entirely intact; the group most likely to benefit (low-SES high performers) remains structurally blocked from elite tracks by self-selection and coaching-resistance barriers this scenario does not touch. [assumption]
- **S4**: TEIP-style funding cost is unquantified for Hungary and, per the scenario's own risk assessment, is a likely first-line fiscal-consolidation casualty; if adopted as a substitute for structural reform rather than a complement, it risks entrenching rather than resolving the underlying equity problem while consuming reform political capital. [assumption]
- **S5**: Avoids all active implementation cost (legal, administrative, political) of S1-S3, but offers no equity guarantee: uncoordinated consolidation could disproportionately close state-run options in already-underserved rural areas while urban elite-track capacity remains comparatively protected, and this baseline is not truly policy-neutral since active score-threshold levers (S2) already operate concurrently. [assumption]
- **S5**: Opportunity cost of inaction: the highest-in-EU segregation and near-total exclusion of multiply-disadvantaged pupils from elite tracks continues to accrue as a distributional cost absent any active countermeasure, even though this is not a fiscal cost in the conventional sense. [value]

## What research could resolve
- Outreach-intervention pilot studies measuring exam-attempt rate changes among disadvantaged-composition primary schools before and after combined outreach plus admission-gate reform (S1) would show whether a fairer gate alone changes who even applies.
- Annual maintainer-disaggregated intake-share statistics tracking whether contracted state places (S2) correlate with non-state sector growth over a multi-year window would resolve whether offsetting expansion is real and at what scale.
- A Hungary-specific pilot region implementing structural delay (S3) plus enforceable anti-sorting rules simultaneously, with published before/after segregation and mobility metrics compared to non-pilot regions, would test whether the load-bearing anti-sorting co-requirement can actually work in the Hungarian context.
- A Ministry-commissioned costing and feasibility study modelling teacher retraining, recruitment, and CPD-bandwidth allocation across multiple phase-in timeframes (1-year, 3-year, 5-year), benchmarked against the 2024-2031 salary-reform trajectory's measured retention effects, would resolve whether the workforce can realistically support any structural reform.
- A 5-8 year longitudinal matched-cohort study comparing TEIP-enrolled and non-enrolled students in Hungary, controlling for baseline SES and prior achievement, tracking assessed competency gain rather than exam pass rates, would resolve whether targeted funding (S4) can close, not merely reduce, achievement gaps.
- Independent replication of the ~10% elite-track competency-gain finding using an admission-cutoff regression discontinuity design with richer teacher-quality and peer-composition controls would resolve whether the value-added figure reflects genuine pedagogical effect or unmeasured confounds.
- A decomposition study using regional variation in score-threshold enforcement timing versus demographic decline rates could statistically separate S2's and S5's contributions to selective-track composition change, resolving the currently unreconciled 'passive versus active' framing.

## What people must decide
- Whether the primary policy goal is reducing SES-based sorting at the point of entry (favouring S1/S3), reducing the absolute footprint of the selective track (favouring S2), improving quality system-wide without touching selection (favouring S4), or accepting demographic forces as the primary driver (S5) — these are not technically resolvable choices but value choices about which form of inequality is most urgent to address. [value]
- Whether preserving church and foundation maintainer autonomy over admissions (a constitutionally and coalition-protected value) should take precedence over uniform, cross-maintainer equity enforcement, or whether equity enforcement justifies renegotiating autonomy guarantees — a genuine value conflict the evidence cannot resolve. [value]
- Whether the modest (~10%) measured pedagogical value-added inside elite tracks should be weighed as a genuine competing good against equity harms, or treated as secondary to equity given Hungary's EU-worst segregation — a normative weighting question, not an empirical one. [value]
- Whether political durability (cross-party consensus, constitutional entrenchment) should be a precondition for launching any structural reform, even at the cost of delaying action on a documented, worsening equity problem, or whether unilateral action with reversal risk is preferable to continued inaction. [value]
- Whether teacher-workforce stabilization must fully precede any structural reform touching selection age (as unions insist), or whether workforce investment can be credibly sequenced alongside implementation without risking a multi-year instructional-quality dip — a political-sequencing decision with no risk-free option. [value]

## What to verify with real stakeholders
- A1 (SES-aware admission quotas and coaching-resistant exam redesign (S1) can reduce entry-gate gatekeeping, but only if exam validity, uniform cross-maintainer enforcement, and non-displacement safeguards for disadvantaged pupils are guaranteed.): needs_more_info — The mechanism itself is not registry-backed: no Hungarian evidence tests whether SES-proxy quotas or coaching-resistant exam design actually reduce gatekeeping or avoid displacement. A pre-registered pilot with published validity and displacement metrics is the precondition every voice in this cluster names before their conditional support firms up.
- A2 (Implementing SES-proxy criteria and monitoring creates new administrative and workload burdens on schools and teachers that are not currently funded, and may fall disproportionately on smaller or less-resourced maintainers.): policy_design_fixable — This is not an inherent flaw in SES-aware admission design but a funding-and-sequencing gap: dedicated, timetabled, funded administrative hours and a published capacity-cost mapping across maintainer types would directly address the concern, as several voices already propose.
- A3 (Uniform admission quotas and thresholds risk overriding church and foundation maintainer autonomy over admissions, protected by subsidiarity principles and constitutional/coalition commitments.): value_conflict — This pits equity (uniform standards to widen opportunity) directly against constitutionally and contractually protected institutional autonomy. No technical redesign resolves the underlying tension between mandating uniformity and preserving maintainer self-governance; co-design and opt-in pilots can soften but not eliminate the conflict.
- A4 (Shrinking state-sector gimnázium capacity via score thresholds (S2) without funding commitments or anti-sorting rules simply accelerates consolidation, worsens rural school viability, intensifies competition for remaining places, and does not address — may worsen — SES-based exclusion.): policy_design_fixable — Multiple stakeholder voices (including government reform-realists and church maintainers on reciprocity) explicitly acknowledge the harm is real if safeguards are missing, but agree it is containable with statutory class-size maxima, redeployment plans with wage continuity, and enforced anti-sorting rules — this is a design gap, not an irreducible tension, though the political will to fund these safeguards remains contested.
- A5 (State-sector selective-capacity contraction can be absorbed by growing church and foundation maintainer capacity, preserving overall selective-track access and institutional pluralism without a legislative fight.): needs_more_info — No published evidence exists on the elasticity or geographic patterning of church/foundation expansion in response to state contraction; the claim that non-state capacity can reliably and evenly absorb state contraction is speculative and directly contested by A4's opposing voices. A decomposition study tracking maintainer-disaggregated intake shares over several years is needed before this claim can be evaluated.
- A6 (Arbitrary, discretionary capacity cuts (S2) disrupt families who already committed to a selective-track path and provide no predictable, published rationale or schedule for planning.): communication_fixable — The underlying substantive concern (capacity reduction) may proceed either way, but the specific harm named here — unpredictability and lack of published rationale — is addressed by transparent, multi-year-published capacity schedules and cohort-protection guarantees, which several implementation-step proposals already specify as feasible administrative fixes.
- A7 (Delaying academic tracking to age 14+ (S3) addresses the root sorting mechanism and is supported by strong cross-national evidence, but its equity gain depends entirely on simultaneously enacted and enforced anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment.): irreducible_tradeoff — Even with best-effort design, Finland's own post-2000 experience shows anti-sorting enforcement cannot fully guarantee sorting will not migrate to choice/catchment channels; this is a genuine implementation risk baked into the mechanism itself, not merely a funding or design gap — improving structural equity necessarily runs the risk of relocated, not reduced, sorting unless enforcement proves unexpectedly robust, which remains empirically untested in Hungary.
- A8 (Hungary's acute and aging teacher shortage is the binding constraint on any comprehensive-school transition (S3): without front-loaded, statutory teacher retraining and recruitment guarantees, structural delay cannot deliver its promised gains and risks a multi-year instructional-quality dip.): evidence_answerable — The scale and demographic profile of Hungary's teacher shortage is documented and undisputed; what remains open is whether front-loaded retraining can close the gap within a realistic window. A detailed, funded Ministry feasibility study modelling retraining across multiple timeframes and benchmarked against the salary-reform trajectory would directly settle the disputed feasibility question, moving this from assumption to fact.
- A9 (Abolishing the 6/8-year gimnázium entry points via top-down structural reform (S3) erases institutional pluralism, church/foundation maintainer autonomy, and parental choice, and carries high political-reversal risk as shown by Poland's 18-year reform being later reversed.): value_conflict — This is a genuine collision between institutional pluralism/parental choice and centrally-engineered structural equity — no evidence resolves which value should dominate, and the reversal-risk concern is a real, documented empirical pattern (Poland) rather than a communication or design failure that can simply be fixed away.
- A10 (Families with children already in or entering the 6/8-year track need ironclad felmenő-rendszer (grandfathering) protection and no lengthened commutes or unfamiliar catchment reassignment during any phase-out transition.): policy_design_fixable — Every structural-reform scenario's implementation steps already specify felmenő-rendszer (grade-by-grade) phase-in as the legally expected transition mechanism; explicit, statutory cohort protections and local-placement guarantees can directly address this concern without abandoning the underlying reform goal.
- A11 (Non-structural reform (S4) can deliver real, evidence-backed achievement and fiscal gains with lower workload, disruption, and political-reversal risk than structural reform, without touching selection age.): irreducible_tradeoff — Lower disruption and lower reversal risk (S4's genuine strengths) are traded directly against leaving the primary documented driver of segregation untouched (the selection gate); this is not a design flaw fixable within S4's own logic — it is the structural price of choosing the lower-risk path, which several voices in this cluster explicitly accept as a deliberate trade rather than an oversight.
- A12 (Non-structural compensation (S4) leaves the early-selection mechanism that produces near-zero disadvantaged-pupil admission to selective tracks untouched, structurally bounding its equity effect and risking legitimizing segregation while claiming to address it.): value_conflict — Whether compensatory investment within an unchanged selection system counts as genuine progress or as legitimizing inaction is fundamentally a values question about what counts as an adequate policy response to documented inequality — the evidence (Portugal's gains, Hungary's structural bounding) is not in dispute, but its normative interpretation is.
- A13 (Targeted (TEIP-style) funding for disadvantaged schools (S4) is politically fragile unless given statutory cardinal-law funding-floor status, since the scenario's own risk assessment names it as a likely first-line cut during fiscal consolidation.): policy_design_fixable — Multiple voices propose a workable middle path (ordinary-law multi-year commitment with published escape-clause conditions and parliamentary oversight, rather than full cardinal-law entrenchment); this is a legislative-design and political-negotiation question with concrete, testable fixes, not an unresolvable conflict, though full cardinal-law protection may be politically infeasible given coalition fiscal constraints.
- A14 (Passive demographic contraction (S5) without an active equity or network-planning lever is a policy abdication that entrenches existing SES-based gatekeeping, risks uncoordinated and unsafe school closures, and wastes scarce future talent in a shrinking labour-market cohort.): value_conflict — Whether allowing demography to drive outcomes without active equity direction constitutes acceptable policy restraint or unacceptable abdication of duty is a value question about the state's obligation to actively counteract documented inequality versus the legitimacy of low-intervention baselines — this is not resolved by more evidence, since the demographic facts themselves are already well established.
- A15 (A purely passive demographic baseline may not actually exist, since active administrative levers (such as S2's score thresholds) are already operating alongside demographic decline, making 'inaction' framing potentially a disguise for unnamed active policy choices.): evidence_answerable — This is a decomposable empirical question: a dedicated study using regional variation in score-threshold enforcement timing versus demographic decline rates could statistically separate the two drivers' contributions, directly resolving the disputed framing rather than leaving it a matter of interpretation.
- A16 (Demographically-driven contraction (S5) is politically convenient but leaves EU-level equity criticism of Hungary's highly selective school system unanswered unless paired with transparent reporting distinguishing demographic effects from active policy levers.): communication_fixable — The core concern here is transparency and accountability, not a technical or value dispute: publishing a methodologically sound decomposition of demographic versus policy-driven contraction would directly address the legitimacy concern, though whether the European Commission would accept this alone as an adequate substantive response remains an open political question.

- (education_finance) The ~10% learning-gain finding for admitted students represents a genuine tension, not a settled verdict: any policy alternative must weigh the demonstrated (if unreplicated) pedagogical value-added against equity harms, rather than treating equity as automatically dominant, because parental demand and the value-added evidence are not merely artefacts to be dismissed. — education_finance explicitly frames Hungary's 8%-of-cohort elite-track structure, entrenched parental demand, and contested church-funding architecture as making reform politically costly, not merely technically straightforward — implying the value-added finding cannot be dismissed outright.
