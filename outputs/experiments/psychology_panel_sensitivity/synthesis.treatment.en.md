# Synthesis

## Overview
Twelve expert lines converge on a shared empirical core about Hungary's early selection (korai szelekció) into 6- and 8-year gimnázium tracks at ages 10–12: Hungary has the EU's highest socio-economic school segregation, a raw achievement gap that shrinks by roughly three-quarters once family background is controlled for, and international comparative and quasi-experimental evidence (Finland, Poland, Portugal) broadly associating early tracking with amplified inequality without a clear average-achievement dividend. At the same time the record preserves real, unresolved tension rather than a single verdict: some evidence shows a modest (~10%) within-track value-added for admitted students, which the equity-focused experts treat as insufficient to offset segregation costs while others flag it as a genuine, unreplicated finding worth weighing. Demographic contraction (accelerating below-replacement fertility, a measurable one-cohort-ahead 'demographic cliff') is forcing school-network consolidation regardless of selection policy, but government's use of discretionary minimum-score admission thresholds is simultaneously and separately compressing the gimnázium track's cohort share, layering a policy-driven contraction atop a demographic one. Legal and governance analysis finds early selection a narrow, separately codified statutory exception (not the structural default), making it more legally tractable to reform than the broader maintainer architecture, but any change must respect felmenő-rendszer (grade-by-grade phase-in) protections with roughly a one-year statutory lead time. Implementation-planning and political-feasibility experts converge on a sobering constraint set: an aging, contracting teacher workforce, rural/STEM shortages, competing reform bandwidth (NAT, AI-integration), and — since the April 2026 Tisza victory — a live but fragile political opening, immediately met by opposition framing selection reform as a culture-war 'totalitarian' threat, raising high reversal risk absent cross-party consensus or constitutional entrenchment. Historical analogues (Poland's 1999 reform and its 2016–2019 reversal) demonstrate that delayed-tracking reforms can produce measurable achievement and equity gains that are nonetheless politically reversible within a generation. Persistent uncertainty concerns causal attribution (how much of Hungary's inequality is due specifically to the 6/8-year gate versus free school choice, catchment sorting, and church-school expansion operating independently), the transferability of Polish/Finnish effect sizes to Hungary's specific demographic and institutional context, and the unresolved question of whether non-state (church, foundation) maintainers can in practice be restricted from absorbing any phased-out selective capacity — a load-bearing assumption in reform scenarios that several critical reviews across rounds have flagged as directly contradicted by the record's own findings on church-sector growth (10.4%→27% of gimnázium students, 2010–2020/21) and the Tisza coalition's explicit commitment to preserve church maintainers' 'previous operating conditions.'

## Disagreement map
### Whether the ~10% within-track pedagogical value-added for admitted students justifies preserving early selection
- **education_finance, hungarian_education_system**: The measured ~10% larger competency gain in 6/8-year tracks (Horn Dániel) is a real, policy-relevant finding that must be weighed alongside equity costs, not dismissed. Why: Recent statistical-model research controlling for family background found a consistent gain; treating it as irrelevant would upgrade an equity narrative beyond what the evidence supports.
- **equity_and_social_mobility, hungarian_education_system, portuguese_reform, educational_psychology**: The equity case against early selection is stronger than the achievement case for it: value-added is heterogeneous (negative for 6-year track at grade 8, only turning positive by grade 10), near-total exclusion of disadvantaged pupils from elite tracks is documented, and Portugal's non-selective system achieved comparable PISA gains without tracking. Why: Multiple independent evidence lines (HÉTFA panel, Berényi fieldwork, cross-national comparison) show the value-added is not robust across tracks or grades, while segregation effects are large and consistent.

### Whether non-state (church/foundation) maintainers can be effectively restricted from absorbing phased-out selective capacity
- **implementation_planning, political_feasibility** (minority): A phase-out scenario for state-sector selective tracks implicitly or explicitly assumes church/foundation maintainers can be regulated, incentivized, or negotiated into compliance with a contraction of selective capacity. Why: Legal and governance analysis treats maintainer coordination as a negotiable implementation step, and political-feasibility framing describes 'review' commitments that presuppose a controllable maintainer landscape.
- **hungarian_education_system, school_network_planning, political_feasibility** (minority): This restriction assumption is directly contradicted by the record: church-maintained schools' share of all gimnázium students rose from 10.4% (2010) to ~27% (2020/21) and grew fastest (2.5×–5×) in small towns/villages 2010–2022, and the Tisza coalition's own 12-point programme explicitly commits that church maintainers will keep their 'previous operating conditions' — meaning any state-sector contraction is likely to be absorbed, not blocked, by non-state expansion. Why: Empirical maintainer-share trends and the explicit coalition commitment are stronger evidence than the implementation-planning assumption, and this contradiction has been flagged as unresolved across multiple prior rounds (devil_advocate, assumption_checker, coherence_checker, political_risk_checker).

### Whether Poland's and Finland's delayed-tracking gains are transferable to Hungary's specific context
- **polish_reform, finnish_reform, international_comparison**: Delaying tracking to age 14–16 produced substantial, well-documented achievement and equity gains (Poland: 0.25–0.28 SD; Finland: 23% reduction in intergenerational income elasticity) that provide a credible evidence baseline for Hungary. Why: Strong, multiply-replicated quasi-experimental and difference-in-differences findings across independent studies.
- **implementation_planning, hungarian_education_system, educational_psychology** (minority): Transferability is a labelled assumption, not a settled fact: Hungary's higher baseline segregation, denser rural school network, acute teacher shortages, and bundled nature of the Polish/Finnish reforms (welfare expansion, teacher-education upgrade) mean effect sizes may not replicate, and no Hungary-specific costed or quasi-experimental estimate exists. Why: Multiple experts explicitly flag transferability as an unverified assumption requiring Hungary-specific quasi-experimental or costing studies before being treated as a policy baseline.

### Sequencing: should selection reform wait for teacher-workforce stabilization or proceed in parallel
- **implementation_planning**: Hungary cannot feasibly implement structural selection reform before 2030 without prior teacher-workforce stabilization (especially rural/STEM), sequencing away from concurrent NAT/AI-integration reforms, and multi-party consensus — only the first precondition is currently in progress. Why: Acute rural/STEM teacher shortages (2–3× higher unqualified-teacher rates) and fully committed CPD bandwidth make simultaneous implementation operationally implausible.
- **political_feasibility** (minority): Selection reform is now live government policy with a narrow but real political window (post-April 2026 Tisza mandate, ministerial framing as societal choice); waiting for full workforce stabilization risks losing this window entirely to reversal or indefinite deferral. Why: Political windows are empirically short-lived (immediate Fidesz culture-war framing within 48 hours of the minister's statement) and historically reversed on government turnover (Poland).

## What the experts agree on
- Hungary has the EU's/OECD's highest socio-economic segregation between schools, with a parent-education achievement gap far above the EU average. [evidence: strong]
- The raw achievement gap between selective and non-selective tracks shrinks substantially (from ~116 to ~24 points) once family background is controlled for, indicating composition/selection effects dominate the raw gap. [evidence: strong]
- Demographic decline (falling births, shrinking school-age cohorts through at least 2040-2060) will force school-network consolidation independent of any selection-policy choice. [evidence: strong]
- Cross-national difference-in-differences evidence associates earlier tracking ages with greater between-school inequality and compositional sorting, generally without a clear mean-achievement benefit. [evidence: strong]
- Any reform touching enrolled cohorts must proceed via felmenő-rendszer (grade-by-grade phase-in) with a practical minimum lead time of roughly one year, per legal and constitutional-doctrine precedent. [evidence: moderate]
- Free school choice and catchment-area sorting contribute to between-school segregation independently of the 6/8-year selection gate, so abolishing early selection alone would not fully resolve segregation. [evidence: moderate]
- Structural education reforms that lack durable multi-party political consensus are vulnerable to reversal within a generation, as demonstrated by Poland's 2016-2019 rollback of its 1999 reform. [evidence: strong]
- Hungary's teaching workforce is aging and contracting, with acute shortages concentrated in rural areas and STEM subjects, constraining the operational feasibility of any structural reform. [evidence: strong]
