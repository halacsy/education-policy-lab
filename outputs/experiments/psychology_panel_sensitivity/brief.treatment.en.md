# Policy brief — Early academic selection and the 6/8-year gimnázium

This brief synthesizes the expert and stakeholder record on early selection (korai szelekció) into Hungary's 6- and 8-year gimnázium tracks at ages 10-12. It sets out what is known, contested, and unresolved, presents five policy alternatives (S1-S5), and maps every argument cluster raised by stakeholder voices in the discourse layer to an honest response. No option is endorsed as 'the' answer; the brief exists to make the trade-offs and value choices explicit so decision-makers and the public can weigh them.

## Scenario key (full detail: scenarios.en.md)
- S1 — S1 — Admissions and intake-equity regulation: keep the 6/8-year gimnázium structure, but regulate the admission gate (SES-aware quotas, uniform thresholds, coaching-resistant exams). Full detail in scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md.
- S2 — S2 — Supply-side contraction: shrink the state-sector selective track's capacity via score thresholds, without changing admission rules within remaining places. Full detail in scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md.
- S3 — S3 — Structural delay of the selection age to 14+ (Finnish/Polish model), paired with mandatory anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment. Full detail in scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md.
- S4 — S4 — Non-structural compensatory reform (Portugal-style): curriculum clarity, targeted (TEIP-style) funding, retention reduction, leaving the selection age untouched. Full detail in scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md.
- S5 — S5 — Demographically-driven passive contraction: the no-new-policy baseline, in which shrinking birth cohorts force network consolidation regardless of active policy choice. Full detail in scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md.

## What we know
- Hungary has the EU's/OECD's highest socio-economic segregation between schools, with a parent-education achievement gap far above the EU average. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- The raw achievement gap between selective and non-selective tracks shrinks substantially (from roughly 116 to roughly 24 points) once family background is statistically controlled for, indicating that composition/selection effects dominate the raw gap. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Demographic decline (falling births, shrinking school-age cohorts through at least 2040-2060) will force school-network consolidation independent of any selection-policy choice. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Cross-national difference-in-differences evidence (75 countries/regions, 20 years) associates earlier tracking ages with greater between-school inequality and compositional sorting, generally without a clear mean-achievement benefit. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Church-maintained schools' share of all gimnázium students rose from 10.4% (2010) to approximately 27% (2020/2021), growing fastest (2.5-5x) in small towns and villages 2010-2022. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Poland's 1999 delayed-tracking reform produced PISA gains of roughly 0.25 SD (math), 0.28 SD (reading), 0.16 SD (science) within 3-7 years; the reform was later politically reversed (2016-2019), with associated PISA decline. [fact] (evidence: moderate)
- Portugal improved PISA results by approximately 31 points (0.3 SD) between 2000 and 2015-2018 without introducing early tracking, through curriculum standards clarification, increased school autonomy, and regular student assessment; grade-retention reduction alone saved an estimated EUR 112 million over two years alongside a 26% reduction in retained students across 663 schools. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Historical rejection-rate gaps show approximately 46% rejection in low-poverty-share classes versus approximately 22% in high-poverty-share classes, evidencing a documented two-tier discretionary admissions gap between institutions. [fact] (evidence: moderate)
- Hungary's teaching workforce is aging and contracting: approximately 50% of teachers are age 50+, roughly 7.2% are under 30, over 21,000 fewer teachers exist than in 2003/2004, and shortages are 2-3 times more severe in rural areas and STEM subjects. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Any reform touching enrolled cohorts must proceed via felmenő-rendszer (grade-by-grade phase-in) with a practical minimum lead time of roughly one year, per legal and constitutional-doctrine precedent. [fact] (evidence: moderate)
- Finland's post-2000 experience shows that equity gains from de-tracking eroded within a generation: 94% of outcome variation is now within-school, and SES gaps widened again via school choice and residential sorting despite no reintroduction of formal tracking. [fact] (evidence: strong)

## What we consider likely
- Uniform admission rules would reduce the currently documented two-tier discretionary gap between maintainers, but the scale of the SES-skew reduction within remaining selective places is untested in Hungary. [estimate]
- State-sector selective-capacity contraction (S2) is likely to be substantially offset by expansion in church, foundation, or private selective provision, given the documented decade-long growth trend in non-state gimnázium share. [estimate]
- International difference-in-differences evidence suggests delaying selection from age 10-12 to 14 would likely reduce Hungary's socioeconomic achievement gap by approximately 0.15-0.25 SD, based on Polish comparable gains, though this transfer estimate carries meaningful uncertainty given differing baseline segregation levels. [estimate]
- Delaying tracking without simultaneous, enforceable anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment would likely see equity gains substantially eroded by within-system sorting, mirroring Finland's post-2000 trajectory. [estimate]
- Non-structural compensatory reform (S4) is likely to be equity-neutral to equity-negative relative to structural alternatives, because it leaves the admission gatekeeping mechanism fully intact while curriculum and funding gains accrue mainly to the already non-selective majority. [estimate]
- A decree-based or statutory admission-equity or supply-contraction rule would likely be vulnerable to reversal on a change of government, given the demonstrated pattern of rapid reversal of predecessor education reforms in Hungary and Poland's 18-year reversal precedent. [estimate]
- Targeted (TEIP-style) compensatory funding is likely to be a first-line budget cut under Hungary's projected fiscal consolidation (approximately 6.2% deficit) unless entrenched in statute or cardinal law. [estimate]

## Where experts disagree
### Whether the roughly 10% within-track pedagogical value-added for admitted students justifies preserving early selection
- **education_finance, hungarian_education_system**: The measured ~10% larger competency gain in 6/8-year tracks is a real, policy-relevant finding that must be weighed alongside equity costs, not dismissed. [value] Why: Recent statistical-model research controlling for family background found a consistent gain; treating it as irrelevant would upgrade an equity narrative beyond what the evidence supports.
- **equity_and_social_mobility, hungarian_education_system, portuguese_reform, educational_psychology**: The equity case against early selection is stronger than the achievement case for it: the value-added is heterogeneous (negative for the 6-year track at grade 8, only turning positive by grade 10), near-total exclusion of disadvantaged pupils from elite tracks is documented, and Portugal's non-selective system achieved comparable PISA gains without tracking. [value] Why: Multiple independent evidence lines (panel research, fieldwork, cross-national comparison) show the value-added is not robust across tracks or grades, while segregation effects are large and consistent.
### Whether non-state (church/foundation) maintainers can be effectively restricted from absorbing phased-out selective capacity
- **implementation_planning, political_feasibility** (minority): A phase-out scenario for state-sector selective tracks implicitly or explicitly assumes church/foundation maintainers can be regulated, incentivized, or negotiated into compliance with a contraction of selective capacity. [value] Why: Legal and governance analysis treats maintainer coordination as a negotiable implementation step, and political-feasibility framing describes 'review' commitments that presuppose a controllable maintainer landscape.
- **hungarian_education_system, school_network_planning, political_feasibility**: This restriction assumption is directly contradicted by the record: church-maintained schools' share of all gimnázium students rose from 10.4% (2010) to ~27% (2020/21) and grew fastest (2.5x-5x) in small towns/villages 2010-2022, and the Tisza coalition's own 12-point programme explicitly commits that church maintainers will keep their 'previous operating conditions' — meaning any state-sector contraction is likely to be absorbed, not blocked, by non-state expansion. [value] Why: Empirical maintainer-share trends and the explicit coalition commitment are stronger evidence than the implementation-planning assumption, and this contradiction has been flagged as unresolved across multiple prior rounds.
### Whether Poland's and Finland's delayed-tracking gains are transferable to Hungary's specific context
- **polish_reform, finnish_reform, international_comparison**: Delaying tracking to age 14-16 produced substantial, well-documented achievement and equity gains (Poland: 0.25-0.28 SD; Finland: 23% reduction in intergenerational income elasticity) that provide a credible evidence baseline for Hungary. [value] Why: Strong, multiply-replicated quasi-experimental and difference-in-differences findings across independent studies.
- **implementation_planning, hungarian_education_system, educational_psychology** (minority): Transferability is a labelled assumption, not a settled fact: Hungary's higher baseline segregation, denser rural school network, acute teacher shortages, and the bundled nature of the Polish/Finnish reforms mean effect sizes may not replicate, and no Hungary-specific costed or quasi-experimental estimate exists. [value] Why: Multiple experts explicitly flag transferability as an unverified assumption requiring Hungary-specific quasi-experimental or costing studies before being treated as a policy baseline.
### Sequencing: should selection reform wait for teacher-workforce stabilization or proceed in parallel
- **implementation_planning**: Hungary cannot feasibly implement structural selection reform before 2030 without prior teacher-workforce stabilization (especially rural/STEM), sequencing away from concurrent NAT/AI-integration reforms, and multi-party consensus. [value] Why: Acute rural/STEM teacher shortages (2-3x higher unqualified-teacher rates) and fully committed CPD bandwidth make simultaneous implementation operationally implausible.
- **political_feasibility** (minority): Selection reform is now live government policy with a narrow but real political window (post-April 2026 Tisza mandate); waiting for full workforce stabilization risks losing this window entirely to reversal or indefinite deferral. [value] Why: Political windows are empirically short-lived (immediate Fidesz culture-war framing within 48 hours of the minister's statement) and historically reversed on government turnover (Poland).

## What we don't know
- Whether admission-equity rules (S1) can be legally and operationally enforced consistently across state, church, and foundation maintainers given documented maintainer plurality and partial admissions autonomy. [assumption]
- Whether SES-aware quotas or proxies would measurably reduce the family-background gradient in admission once pre-exam self-selection and coaching-driven preparation gaps are accounted for. [assumption]
- Whether the current 40%-toward-30% cohort-share framing used in some reporting is measuring the same population as the 8-12% 6/8-year-entry figure used elsewhere, and which is the correct policy-relevant baseline. [fact]
- Whether anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment (S3's load-bearing component) can be enforced with sufficient rigor and simultaneity to prevent sorting from migrating to those channels, given Hungary's embedded, separately codified school-choice legal architecture. [assumption]
- Whether the teacher-retraining and reallocation timeline for a structural transition (S3) is feasible given Hungary's aging, contracting teacher workforce and already fully committed CPD bandwidth for concurrent NAT and AI-integration reforms. [assumption]
- Whether a statutory funding floor for TEIP-style targeted funding (S4) is achievable given projected fiscal consolidation pressure, or whether compensatory funding will remain contingent on annual budget cycles and vulnerable to cuts. [assumption]
- Whether the interaction between passive demographic contraction (S5) and the government's active score-threshold lever (S2) is accelerating selective-track contraction beyond what demographic decline alone would produce, and whether this compound effect is being measured or monitored at all. [fact]

## What could be done
- **S1 — Regulate the admission gate, keep the track structure**: Keep the 6/8-year gimnázium structure intact but regulate admission itself through SES-aware quotas or proxies, uniform score thresholds across maintainers, and coaching-resistant exam design, aiming to reduce family-capital-driven sorting at the point of entry without touching the selection age.
- **S2 — Shrink the gate rather than fix the rules**: Reduce the state-sector share of 6/8-year places via score thresholds, capacity caps, or demographically-driven consolidation, shrinking the selective track's footprint while leaving admission criteria within remaining places largely unchanged.
- **S3 — Delay the selection age structurally (Finnish/Polish model)**: Raise the statutory age of first academic tracking to 14+ or later, phasing out or converting 6- and 8-year entry points into a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase, paired with explicit, simultaneously enforced anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment.
- **S4 — Compensate within the current structure (Portugal-style)**: Pursue Portugal-style equity and achievement gains through curriculum clarity, targeted (TEIP-style) funding for disadvantaged schools, retention reduction, and school-quality levelling, leaving the 10-12 selection age and track structure fully untouched.
- **S5 — Let demographics do the work (status-quo baseline)**: Serve as the no-new-policy baseline in which shrinking birth cohorts force network consolidation and a natural decline in the selective track's absolute size and share regardless of any active equity or structural intervention, against which S1-S4 are measured.

## What each option costs
- **S1**: SES-proxy infrastructure and data systems, coaching-resistant exam development and administration, and legal/administrative negotiation with state, church, and foundation maintainers all carry unquantified but plausibly multi-year, specialist-staffed costs; no Hungary-specific costing exists for any of these. [estimate]
- **S1**: Winners: children whose ability but not family SES currently determines admission, and the equity narrative more broadly. Losers/risks: church and foundation maintainers may see enforcement as an erosion of constitutionally protected autonomy; disadvantaged pupils outside the SES-proxy definition risk displacement into worse slots if safeguards are absent; teachers risk an unfunded compliance burden. [assumption]
- **S2**: Compensatory funding or capacity controls for church/foundation maintainers to prevent offsetting expansion is flagged as medium-to-high, multi-year, currently unbudgeted; administrative monitoring of cross-maintainer displacement and transitional costs for affected state-sector schools/teachers are also unquantified. [estimate]
- **S2**: Winners: church and foundation maintainers, who are positioned to absorb displaced demand and grow their share. Losers/risks: rural and middle-SES state-sector families facing unpredictable, discretionary capacity cuts; disadvantaged applicants facing intensified competition for shrinking places; teachers in surviving urban schools facing workload intensification; the equity goal itself, if offsetting non-state expansion is not restrained. [assumption]
- **S3**: Early diagnostic/remedial support, teacher retraining and reallocation (against an aging, contracting workforce), facility conversion of 6/8-year buildings, and anti-sorting enforcement infrastructure all carry unquantified costs; the teacher-retraining window has been extended to years 3-9, indicating this is the most resource-intensive of the five scenarios. [estimate]
- **S3**: Winners: pupils across the SES spectrum who would have been sorted out early, especially those near the current selection margin; the system-wide equity picture in the long run, if anti-sorting rules hold. Losers/risks: church and foundation maintainers lose the 6/8-year entry point that anchors part of their identity and growth; the teacher workforce faces an intensive, possibly infeasible retraining burden; families with children already on the 6/8-year track face transition disruption; the reform itself carries high political-reversal risk within a generation (Poland precedent). [assumption]
- **S4**: TEIP-style targeted funding, curriculum-clarity/assessment reform, and school-quality-levelling investment (facilities, staffing parity) are all unquantified for Hungary; Portugal's EUR 112 million two-year retention-reduction saving offers a partial precedent but no direct translation exists. [estimate]
- **S4**: Winners: the non-selective majority of pupils and their teachers, who gain curriculum clarity, retention reduction, and targeted support with the lowest legislative complexity and reversal risk of the five options. Losers/risks: disadvantaged pupils who remain excluded from selective tracks gain nothing from this scenario regarding the gatekeeping mechanism itself; equity advocates risk the mechanism being used rhetorically as if it addressed segregation, when it structurally cannot; the central funding mechanism (TEIP-style) is itself politically fragile under fiscal consolidation. [assumption]
- **S5**: Small-school and dispersed-track closure costs, administrative network-consolidation costs across maintainers, and the implicit, unfunded cost of any equity harm from intensified competition for shrinking places are all unquantified; no new compensatory or equity-targeted spending is budgeted under this baseline by definition. [estimate]
- **S5**: Winners: no political actor bears legislative or reversal risk; the government avoids culture-war exposure. Losers/risks: disadvantaged pupils face intensified competition for a shrinking number of selective places without any admission-equity intervention; rural and dispersed communities face uncoordinated, potentially unsafe school closures; the government risks EU-conditionality exposure for inaction on documented worst-in-EU segregation. [assumption]

## What research could resolve
- A regression-discontinuity or matched-pairs evaluation comparing admission outcomes before and after a piloted SES-aware quota, controlling for prior achievement and coaching investment, would clarify whether S1's admission-equity mechanism actually reduces the family-background gradient.
- Real-time, disaggregated enrollment monitoring by maintainer type across at least 3-5 years following any threshold implementation, with a published displacement-rate estimate, would clarify whether S2's contraction is offset by non-state expansion.
- A dedicated Hungarian pilot or quasi-experimental regional rollout with propensity-matched controls, tracking achievement and segregation measures over 5-7 years, would isolate the effect of delaying selection (S3) from Hungary's specific curriculum, teacher-qualification, and demographic context.
- A decomposition study separating the marginal contribution of the 6/8-year gate from general school-choice segregation, using variation across regions with different kisgimnázium densities, would test whether anti-sorting rules alone (without the structural delay) could achieve comparable equity gains.
- A 5-8 year longitudinal matched-cohort study comparing TEIP-enrolled and non-enrolled students, controlling for baseline SES and prior achievement, tracking assessed competency gain (not just exam pass rates), would clarify whether S4's compensatory approach can close, not merely reduce, achievement gaps.
- A joint KSH/Klebelsberg Center analysis decomposing selective-track enrollment change into a demographic-decline component and an administrative-threshold component over 2024-2030 would clarify how much of the observed contraction under S5 is genuinely policy-neutral.
- A Ministry of Education costing and capacity study modelling teacher-retraining timelines against current CPD commitments and workforce-attrition projections through 2035 would clarify the true feasibility window for any structural reform (S3) or major compensatory investment (S4).

## What people must decide
- Whether Hungary's education system should prioritize protecting institutional and maintainer pluralism (church, foundation, state autonomy) or pursue uniform national equity standards, given that these two values are shown by the record to be in direct, largely unresolved tension. [value]
- Whether the roughly 10% within-track pedagogical value-added for admitted students is judged worth the documented segregation cost it carries — this is fundamentally a value trade-off between talent-development and equality-of-opportunity, not a question research alone can settle. [value]
- Whether to accept a politically fragile, potentially reversible reform now (structural delay, S3) while a narrow post-2026 political window exists, or to wait for teacher-workforce stabilization first, risking the window closing entirely. [value]
- Whether compensatory (TEIP-style) funding for disadvantaged schools deserves cardinal-law entrenchment even at the cost of reduced fiscal flexibility during consolidation — a political-institutional choice about how much future governments should be bound by today's equity commitments. [value]
- Whether society accepts that any single reform (S1-S4) will leave some sorting channel unaddressed (free school choice, catchment, private tutoring) and must therefore be judged as a partial, not complete, answer to segregation — or whether only a combined, multi-front package is an honest response. [value]

## What to verify with real stakeholders
- A1 (SES-aware admission quotas and coaching-resistant exam redesign can reduce entry-gate gatekeeping, but only if exam validity, uniform cross-maintainer enforcement, and non-displacement safeguards for disadvantaged pupils are guaranteed.): needs_more_info — No registry entry documents that a specific SES-proxy quota or coaching-resistant exam design reliably reduces gatekeeping or avoids displacement; the independent research voice itself flags the evidence status as weak. A pre-registered pilot with published displacement and validity metrics is a precondition, not an afterthought, before this mechanism can be assessed.
- A2 (Implementing SES-proxy criteria and monitoring creates new administrative and workload burdens on schools and teachers that are not currently funded, and may fall disproportionately on smaller or less-resourced maintainers.): policy_design_fixable — This is a design gap, not an inherent flaw: dedicated, funded, timetabled administrative hours and a transparent cross-maintainer capacity budget, specified in the implementing decree text itself rather than left to schools to absorb, would directly address the concern.
- A3 (Uniform admission quotas and thresholds risk overriding church and foundation maintainer autonomy over admissions, protected by subsidiarity principles and constitutional/coalition commitments.): value_conflict — This pits equity (uniform, enforceable standards) directly against constitutionally protected institutional autonomy (Article 70/H, concordat-style agreements, coalition commitments). No technical redesign resolves whether the state may bind autonomous maintainers to uniform rules; this is a legitimate values collision requiring political and possibly constitutional resolution, not a design fix.
- A4 (Shrinking state-sector gimnázium capacity via score thresholds without funding commitments or anti-sorting rules simply accelerates consolidation, worsens rural school viability, intensifies competition for remaining places, and does not address — may worsen — SES-based exclusion.): policy_design_fixable — Multiple stakeholders (teacher representatives, government reform-realists) converged in the reciprocity round on the same conclusion: the harms are real if safeguards are absent, but a statutory funding floor, class-size protection, teacher-redeployment guarantees, and enforced anti-sorting rules attached to the decree text itself would contain the risk. The design gap is fixable; whether the political will exists to fund it is a separate, open question.
- A5 (State-sector selective-capacity contraction can be absorbed by growing church and foundation maintainer capacity, preserving overall selective-track access and institutional pluralism without a legislative fight.): needs_more_info — There is no published evidence on the elasticity or geographic patterning of church/foundation expansion in response to state contraction; the independent research voice explicitly flags this as contested and unknown. A three-year, disaggregated enrollment-monitoring commitment would resolve whether this absorption claim holds, but it is currently speculative in either direction.
- A6 (Arbitrary, discretionary capacity cuts disrupt families who already committed to a selective-track path and provide no predictable, published rationale or schedule for planning.): communication_fixable — The underlying concern is about predictability and transparency of process, not the existence of contraction itself. A published, multi-year capacity schedule with explicit cohort protections and guaranteed local-alternative placement — already named as the condition that would shift this stakeholder's opposition to conditional support — directly resolves the legibility problem without requiring a change to the contraction policy's substance.
- A7 (Delaying academic tracking to age 14+ addresses the root sorting mechanism and is supported by cross-national evidence, but its equity gain depends entirely on simultaneously enacted and enforced anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment.): irreducible_tradeoff — Even with best-effort anti-sorting enforcement, Finland's post-2000 experience shows sorting can migrate to choice/catchment/tutoring channels within a generation regardless of enforcement quality; achieving the full equity gain trades directly against the political and administrative cost of simultaneous, enforceable legislation that is legislatively and politically harder than sequential reform. This is a genuine trade-off between ambition and deliverability, not merely a design flaw.
- A8 (Hungary's acute and aging teacher shortage is the binding constraint on any comprehensive-school transition: without front-loaded, statutory teacher retraining and recruitment guarantees, structural delay cannot deliver its promised gains and risks a multi-year instructional-quality dip.): needs_more_info — The scale of the shortage is well-documented (strong evidence), but whether a front-loaded statutory retraining programme could close the gap within a realistic transition window given competing CPD demands is explicitly contested and unresolved in the record; a Ministry of Education costing and capacity study modelling retraining timelines against workforce-attrition projections would answer this before the trade-off between waiting and proceeding can be responsibly decided.
- A9 (Abolishing the 6/8-year gimnázium entry points via top-down structural reform erases institutional pluralism, church/foundation maintainer autonomy, and parental choice, and carries high political-reversal risk as shown by Poland's 18-year reform being later reversed.): value_conflict — This is a direct clash between equity-through-structural-uniformity and institutional pluralism/parental choice as competing legitimate goods, compounded by an empirical (but partly unresolvable) question about durability. No technical fix reconciles preserving maintainer pedagogical autonomy with a genuinely unified comprehensive phase; this requires an explicit political and constitutional choice about which value takes precedence.
- A10 (Families with children already in or entering the 6/8-year track need ironclad felmenő-rendszer protection and no lengthened commutes or unfamiliar catchment reassignment during any phase-out transition.): policy_design_fixable — Legal precedent already establishes felmenő-rendszer (grade-by-grade phase-in) as the standard mechanism for exactly this kind of protection, with roughly a one-year minimum lead time. Explicit statutory cohort-protection clauses specifying zero commute-lengthening and catchment stability for enrolled pupils would directly satisfy this concern; it is a specification detail for the implementing legislation, not an open design question.
- A11 (Non-structural reform (curriculum clarity, grade-retention reduction, TEIP-style targeted funding) can deliver real, evidence-backed achievement and fiscal gains with lower workload, disruption, and political-reversal risk than structural reform, without touching selection age.): irreducible_tradeoff — This scenario genuinely trades lower disruption and reversal risk for a structurally bounded equity ceiling: because it leaves admission gatekeeping fully intact, its gains accrue mainly to the non-selective majority while the segregation mechanism itself is untouched. Both properties are real and cannot be engineered away simultaneously — choosing low-disruption non-structural reform means accepting that the core segregation problem this brief addresses remains, by design, unresolved.
- A12 (Non-structural compensation leaves the early-selection mechanism that produces near-zero disadvantaged-pupil admission to selective tracks untouched, structurally bounding its equity effect and risking legitimizing segregation while claiming to address it.): value_conflict — Whether it is acceptable to pursue incremental, deliverable equity gains within an unchanged selective structure — versus insisting that only structural reform of the selection mechanism counts as a genuine equity response — is a legitimate values disagreement about what equity requires, not a factual dispute resolvable by more evidence. The registry strongly supports the factual premise (early tracking raises inequality); the disagreement is about what follows normatively from that fact.
- A13 (Targeted (TEIP-style) funding for disadvantaged schools is politically fragile unless given statutory cardinal-law funding-floor status, since the scenario's own risk assessment names it as a likely first-line cut during fiscal consolidation.): policy_design_fixable — A concrete institutional design change — statutory or cardinal-law entrenchment, or the middle-path alternative of an ordinary-law multi-year commitment with published escape-clause conditions and parliamentary oversight — directly addresses the identified fragility. This is a design and political-negotiation question with concrete alternatives on the table, not an irreducible conflict.
- A14 (Passive demographic contraction without an active equity or network-planning lever is a policy abdication that entrenches existing SES-based gatekeeping, risks uncoordinated and unsafe school closures, and wastes scarce future talent in a shrinking labour-market cohort.): policy_design_fixable — The demographic decline itself cannot be reversed by policy, but the harms named (uncoordinated closures, worsening gatekeeping, lost talent) are consequences of leaving the response to it undesigned, not of the decline itself. A coordinated national consolidation plan with equity and teacher-workforce-protection safeguards — the exact condition named by multiple stakeholders — converts drift into managed transition without requiring any of S1-S4's structural or admission changes.
- A15 (A purely passive demographic baseline may not actually exist, since active administrative levers (such as S2's score thresholds) are already operating alongside demographic decline, making 'inaction' framing potentially a disguise for unnamed active policy choices.): evidence_answerable — This is a decomposable empirical question: a joint KSH/Klebelsberg Center analysis separating the demographic-decline component from the administrative-threshold component would settle, or at least substantially clarify, how much of S5's observed contraction is genuinely policy-neutral. Until that decomposition is published, the framing concern stands, but the underlying question is answerable with data, not merely a matter of interpretation.
- A16 (Demographically-driven contraction is politically convenient (lowest reversal risk, no legislative cost) but leaves EU-level equity criticism of Hungary's highly selective school system unanswered unless paired with transparent reporting distinguishing demographic effects from active policy levers.): communication_fixable — The core deficit here is transparency, not substance: publishing a methodology that decomposes demographic from policy-driven contraction, on a regular schedule, would directly address the legibility gap this cluster identifies, even though it would not by itself resolve the underlying equity criticism, which remains a separate, substantive question addressed by S1-S4.
