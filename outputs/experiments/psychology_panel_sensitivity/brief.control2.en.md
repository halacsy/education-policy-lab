# Policy brief — Early academic selection and the 6/8-year gimnázium

This brief synthesises expert evidence and a stakeholder stress test on Hungary's early school-selection system (age 10-12 gimnázium entry) and its relationship to the EU's highest socioeconomic school segregation. It sets out five policy alternatives (S1-S5, detailed in scenarios.en.md), what is solidly known, where experts and stakeholder voices genuinely disagree, and what remains undecided. Disagreement and unresolved trade-offs are preserved deliberately: they are the substance of the decision, not a defect to be smoothed away.

## Scenario key (full detail: scenarios.en.md)
- S1 — S1 — Admissions and intake-equity regulation: keep the 6/8-year gimnázium structure but regulate the admission gate (SES-aware quotas, uniform thresholds, coaching-resistant exams). Full detail: scenarios.en.md.
- S2 — S2 — Supply-side contraction of the selective track: shrink state-sector 6/8-year places via score thresholds/capacity caps, leaving admission rules mostly unchanged. Full detail: scenarios.en.md.
- S3 — S3 — Structural delay of the selection age (Finnish/Polish model): raise the tracking age to 14+, phasing 6/8-year entry into a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase, paired with anti-sorting rules. Full detail: scenarios.en.md.
- S4 — S4 — Non-structural compensatory reform (Portuguese model): leave the selection age untouched; pursue equity via curriculum clarity, TEIP-style targeted funding, and retention reduction. Full detail: scenarios.en.md.
- S5 — S5 — Demographically-driven passive contraction: the no-new-policy baseline in which shrinking birth cohorts force network consolidation regardless of active intervention. Full detail: scenarios.en.md.

## What we know
- Hungary has the EU's highest socioeconomic school segregation and one of the OECD's largest SES-achievement gaps (53 PISA points vs. 33 EU average). [fact] (evidence: strong)
- The raw achievement gap between grammar-school and vocational-track students shrinks from 116 to 24 points once family background is controlled for, indicating selection/composition effects, not pure school-quality effects, drive most of the observed gap. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Hungary's accelerating demographic decline (record-low fertility, births at their lowest since 1949) will force school-network consolidation over the 2025–2040 horizon independent of any selection-policy choice. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- International difference-in-differences evidence shows early tracking increases inequality of outcomes without raising average achievement, while delaying tracking (Poland 1999, Finland 1972–77) can produce equity gains without an achievement penalty. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Structural education reforms lacking durable multi-party political consensus are vulnerable to reversal within a single electoral cycle, as demonstrated by Poland's 2016–2019 rollback of its 1999 reform. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Church- and foundation-maintained gimnázium share of total gimnázium students rose from 10.4% (2010) to approximately 27% (2020/2021), expanding independently of state-sector policy. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Teacher shortages (pedagógushiány) are acute, aging, and geographically concentrated in rural and disadvantaged areas (roughly 50% of primary teachers aged 50+, only 7.2% under 30; shortage of at least 16,000 in 2025/26), constraining the pace and sequencing of any region-by-region selection-structure reform. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Admission gatekeeping tracks socio-economic status: rejection rates are approximately 46% in low-poverty-share classes versus 22% in high-poverty-share classes at the 6/8-year gimnázium entry gate. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Physical school-network infrastructure (buildings, transport, dormitories) is not currently the binding constraint on delivering or reforming early selection; the binding constraints are demographic, workforce-related, and political. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Any structural change to the selection age must, for legal and legitimacy reasons, proceed via a felmenő-rendszer (grade-by-grade, cohort-by-cohort) phase-in with a minimum lead time of roughly one year to protect already-admitted students. [fact] (evidence: moderate)
- 6/8-year gimnázium students show approximately 10% larger competency gains than similar-ability peers in 4-year tracks, based on a single statistical-model study not yet independently replicated. [estimate] (evidence: moderate)
- Poland's 1999 reform (delaying tracking from age 12/15 to 15/16) is associated with PISA gains of approximately 0.25 SD in mathematics, 0.28 SD in reading, and 0.16 SD in science (2000-2006); the curated evidence registry tags this as moderate rather than strong evidence. [estimate] (evidence: moderate)
- Finland's 1972-1977 comprehensive reform (delaying tracking from age 11 to 16) reduced intergenerational income elasticity by 23% (0.30 to 0.23), but occurred within a policy bundle including teacher-education reform and welfare-state expansion, not from delayed tracking in isolation. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Portugal improved PISA results by approximately 31 points (0.3 SD) between 2000 and 2015-2018 without introducing early tracking, through curriculum-standards clarification, increased school autonomy, regular assessment, and vocational-pathway development. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Portugal's grade-retention reduction initiative saved EUR 112 million over two years (2016-2018) while reducing retained students by 26% across 663 schools, demonstrating a fiscally self-funding compensatory mechanism, though no Hungary-specific costed estimate exists. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Finland's post-2000 experience shows equity gains eroded by school choice and residential sorting creating within-school segregation (94% of outcome variance within-school), even without formal tracking being reintroduced. [fact] (evidence: strong)

## What we consider likely
- SES-aware admission quotas or proxies at intake (S1) would somewhat reduce sorting at the specific admissions gate they target, but pre-exam self-selection by disadvantaged pupils (information and financial barriers) means a fairer gate may still admit a similar population if outreach and pre-exam barriers are not addressed simultaneously. [estimate]
- State-sector supply contraction (S2) without a parallel lever over church/foundation providers is more likely to intensify SES-skew than reduce it, since the strongest documented growth channel for selective places (church/foundation expansion) is not addressed by this mechanism. [estimate]
- Structural delay of the selection age (S3) is the structurally strongest equity lever in the evidence base, but its equity gain is conditional on anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment being enacted simultaneously — untested and unproven for Hungary's specific, already-high-segregation context. [estimate]
- Non-structural compensatory reform (S4) plausibly improves outcomes for the general-school population but is likely equity-neutral-to-negative regarding the specific selective-track access gap, since it leaves the 6/8-year admissions gate's SES-skewed gatekeeping untouched. [estimate]
- Passive demographic contraction (S5) will mechanically reduce the absolute size of the selective track over time, but this is a scale reduction, not a demonstrated equity improvement — segregation intensity could remain unchanged or worsen if the shrinking pool becomes more geographically and socioeconomically concentrated. [estimate]
- Any regulatory or fiscal lever intended to prevent church/foundation maintainers from absorbing displaced state-sector selective demand is unlikely to succeed without a multi-year negotiated settlement, given documented maintainer autonomy protections and coalition commitments. [estimate]

## Where experts disagree
### Whether the ~10% within-track pedagogical value-added justifies preserving early selection
- **education_finance, hungarian_education_system**: 6/8-year gimnázium students show ~10% larger competency gains than similar-ability peers in 4-year tracks — a genuine (if unreplicated) pedagogical/peer-effect benefit that must be weighed against equity costs, not dismissed. [value] Why: Horn Dániel's statistical-model research (moderate evidence) controls for family background and finds a real gain; the researcher does not conclude this justifies the system, but the finding is treated as a genuine trade-off input.
- **equity_and_social_mobility, international_comparison, portuguese_reform**: The value-added evidence is heterogeneous and confounded, and does not outweigh Hungary's status as the EU's most segregated system; the equity case against early selection is stronger than the achievement case for it. [value] Why: HÉTFA panel data show divergent value-added by track type; cross-national DiD evidence shows early tracking raises dispersion without raising the mean; Portugal achieved comparable PISA gains without any tracking.
### Whether non-state (church/foundation) maintainers can be restricted from absorbing phased-out selective capacity
- **implementation_planning, legal_and_governance** (minority): A national reform can, in principle, be designed to constrain non-state maintainers' selective-track expansion through coordinated legislative and funding-agreement mechanisms, provided consensus-building with maintainer federations occurs. [value] Why: Legal analysis finds the selection age is a codified, narrow exception amendable by statute; implementation planning treats maintainer coordination as a solvable multi-lever problem given adequate lead time.
- **hungarian_education_system, political_feasibility**: Non-state maintainers are structurally positioned to absorb any phased-out selective capacity, making restriction politically and institutionally unlikely. [value] Why: Empirical maintainer-share trend data plus the explicit coalition commitment in Tisza's published programme directly contradict the assumption that restriction is a viable, cost-free lever.
### Near-term political feasibility and durability of structural selection-age reform
- **political_feasibility**: Reform is now live government policy with genuine EU tailwind, but cross-party durability is low-probability in the near term; feasibility exists only if the government narrows scope to pilot-phase rebalancing with explicit quality-levelling and abandons abolition rhetoric. [value] Why: Fidesz's immediate 'totalitarian' framing within 48 hours of the minister's statement, the pattern of rapid reversal of predecessor reforms, and unresolved fiscal-consolidation pressure on funding guarantees all point to high reversal risk.
- **implementation_planning** (minority): Structural reform is not feasible before 2030 at all without prior teacher-workforce stabilization, explicit sequencing away from concurrent NAT/AI reforms, and multi-party legislative consensus — only the first precondition is even in progress. [value] Why: Teacher shortages are acute and geographically uneven, CPD bandwidth is already committed to other reforms through 2026, and Poland's reversal after 18 years shows durability cannot be assumed.
### Whether abolishing the admission examination alone would meaningfully reduce segregation
- **hungarian_education_system, school_network_planning**: Abolishing early selection without simultaneously reforming school choice and catchment rules would leave between-school sorting substantially unchanged, since free school choice and residential/catchment segregation operate independently of the formal exam gate. [value] Why: Kertesi & Kézdi and Berényi-Berkovits-Erőss school-choice studies document strong between-school sorting already at the primary stage; Finland's post-2000 experience shows choice-driven within-school segregation persisting after formal de-tracking.
- **political_feasibility** (minority): Both Nahalka and Szülői Hang leader Miklós argue a single-lever exam abolition is insufficient, but disagree on where the primary lever should be — school-quality variance vs. the exam itself. [value] Why: Both positions are drawn from named Hungarian stakeholders in the current live policy debate (June 2026), representing a genuine within-country disagreement on causal priority, not a resolved empirical question.

## What we don't know
- Whether SES-aware quotas or proxies (S1) would survive legal challenge or be judged compatible with equal-treatment constitutional doctrine in Hungary is untested. [assumption]
- Whether uniform enforcement of admission-equity rules is achievable across state, church, and foundation maintainers without a multi-year negotiated settlement remains unresolved. [assumption]
- Whether any regulatory or fiscal lever can effectively discourage church/foundation offsetting expansion without violating coalition commitments or maintainer autonomy protections is unresolved. [assumption]
- The precise current baseline share of cohort intake attending 6/8-year tracks is contested across sources (8-12% vs. approximately 40%), undermining confidence in stated contraction targets for S2. [fact]
- Whether anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment can, in practice, be enforced strongly and fast enough to prevent sorting migration (S3), given Hungary's higher baseline segregation and legally entrenched school-choice system, is unresolved. [assumption]
- Whether teacher workforce capacity can support full-scale S3 rollout within any politically plausible timeline is highly uncertain, given documented aging, rural/STEM shortages, and CPD bandwidth already committed to other reforms. [assumption]
- Whether cross-party or constitutional entrenchment sufficient to prevent an 18-year-style reversal (as in Poland) is politically achievable in Hungary's current environment is unknown. [assumption]
- Whether TEIP-style compensatory measures (S4) can close competency gaps rather than merely reduce dropout is unresolved because no longitudinal study has tracked student competency trajectories beyond retention reduction. [assumption]
- Whether leaving the 6/8-year admissions gate untouched while investing in general schools (S4) produces any measurable narrowing of the specific selective-track access gap is unknown. [assumption]
- The relative contribution of passive demographic decline versus active score-threshold policy (S2) to observed selective-track contraction is not decomposed in any cited study, meaning S5 cannot cleanly serve as a policy-neutral counterfactual. [fact]
- Whether the shrinking selective-track pool (S5) will become more or less geographically and socioeconomically concentrated as consolidation proceeds is not measured in any cited source. [fact]

## What could be done
- **S1 — Regulate the admission gate, keep the structure**: Keep the 6/8-year gimnázium structure intact but introduce SES-aware quotas or proxies, uniform nationally-binding score thresholds, and coaching-resistant exam design to reduce family-capital-driven sorting at the point of entry, without touching the track structure itself.
- **S2 — Shrink the selective track's footprint**: Reduce the state-sector share of 6/8-year places via score thresholds, capacity caps, or demographically-driven consolidation, shrinking the selective track's footprint while leaving admission criteria within remaining places largely unchanged.
- **S3 — Delay the selection age structurally**: Raise the statutory age of first academic tracking to 14+ or later, phasing out or converting 6- and 8-year entry points into a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase, paired with explicit anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment.
- **S4 — Compensate within the existing structure**: Leave the 10-12 selection age and track structure untouched, but pursue equity and achievement gains through curriculum clarity, targeted (TEIP-style) funding for disadvantaged schools, retention reduction, and school-quality levelling, following the Portuguese model.
- **S5 — Let demographics do the work (status quo baseline)**: The no-new-policy baseline in which shrinking birth cohorts force network consolidation and a natural decline in the selective track's absolute size and share regardless of any active equity or structural intervention, serving as the counterfactual against which S1-S4 are measured.

## What each option costs
- **S1**: SES-proxy data infrastructure, exam redesign/piloting, and cross-maintainer enforcement negotiation costs are all unpublished in magnitude; enforcement negotiation is likely multi-year given documented maintainer autonomy frameworks. Trade-off: families with resources to navigate new criteria may partially recapture advantage, while disadvantaged pupils who self-select out before the formal exam remain unreached unless outreach is addressed simultaneously. [estimate]
- **S2**: Compensatory funding or capacity controls for offsetting church/foundation expansion are flagged medium-to-high cost, multi-year, currently unbudgeted. Trade-off: families with resources to navigate church/foundation alternatives likely retain selective access, while resource-poor families face a genuinely shrunk option set — winners are advantaged families and non-state maintainers, losers are disadvantaged families and rural general schools absorbing peer-drain effects. [estimate]
- **S3**: High-cost, largely unpublished categories: early diagnostic/remedial support, teacher retraining (interacting with the 16,000-teacher shortage), facility conversion, and anti-sorting enforcement infrastructure (identified as the load-bearing but least-costed element). Trade-off: church/foundation maintainers and families valuing early academic differentiation lose institutional autonomy and choice; disadvantaged pupils stand to gain most if anti-sorting enforcement succeeds, but bear the risk of a multi-year instructional-quality dip if teacher capacity fails to materialize in time. [estimate]
- **S4**: TEIP-style targeted funding and school-quality levelling are high-cost, multi-year categories with no Hungary-specific aggregate figure; the scenario's own political-risk analysis names targeted funding as a likely first-line fiscal-consolidation cut. Trade-off: general-school pupils and teachers gain from workload-reducing, fiscally-self-funding retention reduction, but disadvantaged pupils already excluded by the untouched selective gate gain nothing from this path — the scenario risks legitimizing existing segregation while claiming to address inequality. [estimate]
- **S5**: School-network consolidation/closure costs, teacher redeployment/displacement costs, and the opportunity cost of forgoing active equity intervention are all unquantified for Hungary. Trade-off: no fiscal or political cost is borne now, but this shifts the entire cost onto rural communities facing uncoordinated closures and onto disadvantaged pupils whose exclusion from a shrinking selective pool is neither addressed nor tracked — the 'winner' is short-term political convenience, the 'loser' is anyone depending on active equity correction. [estimate]

## What research could resolve
- A legal opinion or Constitutional Court test case assessing SES-based admission criteria against Hungary's equal-treatment and non-discrimination provisions would resolve whether S1's core mechanism is constitutionally viable.
- A decomposition study using regional variation in kisgimnázium density, separating the admissions-gate contribution from school-choice and catchment-driven segregation, would clarify whether S1 alone can produce a measurable equity gain.
- A longitudinal tracking study comparing aggregate selective-track enrollment share (all maintainer types combined) before and after state-sector threshold tightening would resolve whether S2 reduces or merely redistributes selective access.
- An authoritative, single KSH/Oktatási Hivatal published figure reconciling the divergent 6/8-year track baseline share estimates (8-12% vs. ~40%) would remove a major source of contested framing across all scenarios.
- A Hungary-specific pilot (regional or phased) measuring segregation and sorting-migration indicators before and after simultaneous structural delay and anti-sorting rule enforcement would resolve S3's core uncertainty about whether sorting migrates to unregulated channels.
- A detailed Ministry-commissioned costing and capacity study modelling teacher retraining, reallocation, and recruitment timelines against the current shortage severity would resolve whether S3's workforce precondition is achievable within any politically plausible window.
- A 5-8 year longitudinal matched-cohort study comparing TEIP-enrolled and non-enrolled students, controlling for baseline SES and prior achievement, would resolve whether S4's compensatory measures close competency gaps or merely reduce dropout.
- A decomposition study tracking selective-track admission composition by SES before and after compensatory general-school investment, holding admissions rules constant, would clarify whether S4 narrows the selective-track access gap at all.
- A decomposition analysis using KSH/Oktatási Hivatal administrative data separating the demographically-attributable share of selective-track contraction from the policy-attributable (score-threshold) share would resolve S5's central framing problem.

## What people must decide
- Whether Hungary should treat the ~10% within-track pedagogical value-added of the 6/8-year gimnázium as a benefit worth preserving even at a documented equity cost, or whether equity should take precedence given Hungary's EU-highest segregation status — this is a value choice about how to weigh average achievement against distributional fairness, not a question research can settle. [value]
- Whether church and foundation maintainer autonomy (protected by coalition commitments and constitutional Article 70/H) should be preserved as a hard constraint on any reform, or whether equity goals justify overriding or renegotiating that autonomy — this is a political and constitutional value trade-off, not primarily an evidentiary one. [value]
- Whether the political system can and should commit to cross-party or statutorily entrenched durability guarantees for any structural reform, accepting the near-term political cost of such negotiation, or whether short-term feasibility (avoiding coalition conflict) should take priority even at the risk of Poland-style reversal — a decision about how much durability risk is acceptable. [value]
- Whether teacher-workforce stabilization must be a strict precondition completed before any structural reform proceeds, or whether reform and workforce-building can be pursued in careful parallel — this is a sequencing and risk-tolerance decision with no purely technical answer. [value]
- Whether Hungary should pursue a single dominant lever (structural delay, admissions regulation, supply contraction, compensation, or passive drift) or a deliberately combined package — and if combined, which elements are non-negotiable versus adjustable — is a political choice about reform architecture that the evidence base does not itself resolve. [value]

## What to verify with real stakeholders
- A1 (SES-aware admission quotas and coaching-resistant exam redesign can reduce entry-gate gatekeeping, but only if exam validity, uniform cross-maintainer enforcement, and non-displacement safeguards for disadvantaged pupils are guaranteed.): needs_more_info — No registry evidence establishes that SES-proxy quotas or coaching-resistant exam redesign actually reduce gatekeeping or avoid displacement in practice; a pre-registered pilot with published validity and displacement metrics is the precondition several voices name, and none currently exists.
- A2 (Implementing SES-proxy criteria and monitoring creates new administrative and workload burdens on schools and teachers that are not currently funded, and may fall disproportionately on smaller or less-resourced maintainers.): policy_design_fixable — The burden is a design gap, not an inherent feature of SES-aware admission — funded, timetabled administrative hours and a published cross-maintainer capacity budget (as multiple voices propose) would directly address it without abandoning the underlying reform.
- A3 (Uniform admission quotas and thresholds risk overriding church and foundation maintainer autonomy over admissions, protected by subsidiarity principles and constitutional/coalition commitments.): value_conflict — This pits equity (uniform standards to widen opportunity) directly against institutional autonomy protected by constitutional Article 70/H and coalition agreement — no exam or quota design eliminates this tension; only a political negotiation over how much autonomy is preserved can resolve it, and that negotiation is itself a value choice, not a technical fix.
- A4 (Shrinking state-sector gimnázium capacity via score thresholds without funding commitments or anti-sorting rules simply accelerates consolidation, worsens rural school viability, intensifies competition for remaining places, and does not address—may worsen—SES-based exclusion.): policy_design_fixable — Several stakeholders (pedagogus_erdekvedo, kormanyzati_reformrealizmus) explicitly accept that adding statutory class-size floors, teacher-redeployment guarantees, and anti-sorting enforcement to the decree text would contain the harm; the design gap, not the mechanism itself, is what drives opposition — though the feasibility of restraining church/foundation offsetting expansion (a separate value_conflict, see A3/A5) limits how fully fixable this is.
- A5 (State-sector selective-capacity contraction can be absorbed by growing church and foundation maintainer capacity, preserving overall selective-track access and institutional pluralism without a legislative fight.): needs_more_info — The registry has no evidence on the elasticity or geographic patterning of church/foundation capacity expansion in response to state contraction; whether this genuinely preserves equitable access (as egyhazi_fenntartoi claims) or merely relocates advantage (as eselyegyenlosegi_civil and fuggetlen_szakpolitikai_kutatomuhely argue) is an empirical question a decomposition study could resolve, but has not yet.
- A6 (Arbitrary, discretionary capacity cuts disrupt families who already committed to a selective-track path and provide no predictable, published rationale or schedule for planning.): communication_fixable — The underlying concern is procedural transparency, not the policy substance itself; a published, multi-year capacity schedule (already proposed as a condition by kisgyerekes_szuloi and konzervativ_ertekvedo) would resolve most of this cluster's force without changing the contraction mechanism.
- A7 (Delaying academic tracking to age 14+ addresses the root sorting mechanism and is supported by cross-national evidence, but its equity gain depends entirely on simultaneously enacted and enforced anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment.): irreducible_tradeoff — Even with perfect design intent, Finland's post-2000 experience shows sorting migration to school choice/catchment channels is a real risk that enforcement capacity may not fully close, especially given Hungary's already-highest-in-EU segregation and entrenched free-school-choice norm; achieving the achievement/equity gain necessarily requires accepting constraints on school-choice freedom, a genuine trade-off between two legitimate goods (equity and choice), not merely an implementation gap.
- A8 (Hungary's acute and aging teacher shortage is the binding constraint on any comprehensive-school transition: without front-loaded, statutory teacher retraining and recruitment guarantees, structural delay cannot deliver its promised gains and risks a multi-year instructional-quality dip.): evidence_answerable — The factual core (shortage severity, aging profile, rural/STEM concentration) is strongly evidenced and settled; what remains open — whether a funded, front-loaded retraining programme can close the gap within a realistic window — is a costing and capacity-modelling question a Ministry-commissioned study could resolve, moving this from assumption to evidence.
- A9 (Abolishing the 6/8-year gimnázium entry points via top-down structural reform erases institutional pluralism, church/foundation maintainer autonomy, and parental choice, and carries high political-reversal risk as shown by Poland's 18-year reform being later reversed.): value_conflict — This is a direct clash between institutional pluralism/parental choice and centrally engineered structural equity — no enforcement mechanism or entrenchment design eliminates the fact that a unified comprehensive phase removes the specific institutional option of early academic differentiation that some families and maintainers value; the durability risk (Poland) is a real, separate empirical concern, but the autonomy loss itself is a value trade-off with no technical resolution.
- A10 (Families with children already in or entering the 6/8-year track need ironclad felmenő-rendszer (grandfathering) protection and no lengthened commutes or unfamiliar catchment reassignment during any phase-out transition.): policy_design_fixable — All experts agree structural change must proceed via felmenő-rendszer with roughly a one-year minimum lead time — this is an established legal and implementation norm; the specific commitments requested (no commute lengthening, no catchment reassignment for currently enrolled cohorts) can be written into implementation-step design and published multi-year calendars, as several implementation steps already specify.
- A11 (Non-structural reform (curriculum clarity, grade-retention reduction, TEIP-style targeted funding) can deliver real, evidence-backed achievement and fiscal gains with lower workload, disruption, and political-reversal risk than structural reform, without touching selection age.): irreducible_tradeoff — Lower disruption and lower reversal risk are genuine, real advantages of S4, but they are bought at the price of leaving the primary segregation-producing mechanism (the admissions gate) untouched — this is not a design flaw fixable by better implementation; it is the structural nature of choosing a non-structural path, and stakeholders on both sides (oktataspolitikai_reformmozgalom, eselyegyenlosegi_civil vs. konzervativ_ertekvedo, kormanyzati_reformrealizmus) correctly identify this as inherent to the scenario, not incidental to it.
- A12 (Non-structural compensation leaves the early-selection mechanism that produces near-zero disadvantaged-pupil admission to selective tracks untouched, structurally bounding its equity effect and risking legitimizing segregation while claiming to address it.): value_conflict — Whether it is acceptable to pursue visible, deliverable gains for the general-school majority while leaving the selective-track gatekeeping mechanism untouched is fundamentally a question of what equity requires — some hold that any genuine improvement is worthwhile even if bounded, others hold that compensation without structural change legitimizes the underlying injustice; this is a legitimate values disagreement about the meaning of equity, not a gap that more evidence would close.
- A13 (Targeted (TEIP-style) funding for disadvantaged schools is politically fragile unless given statutory cardinal-law funding-floor status, since the scenario's own risk assessment names it as a likely first-line cut during fiscal consolidation.): policy_design_fixable — This is a fundable governance-design problem: a statutory funding floor (cardinal-law, or as kormanyzati_reformrealizmus counter-proposes, an ordinary-law multi-year commitment with published escape-clause conditions and parliamentary oversight) directly addresses the fragility named, though political achievability of cardinal-law status itself remains contested and is separately flagged as an open uncertainty.
- A14 (Passive demographic contraction without an active equity or network-planning lever is a policy abdication that entrenches existing SES-based gatekeeping, risks uncoordinated and unsafe school closures, and wastes scarce future talent in a shrinking labour-market cohort.): policy_design_fixable — Several stakeholders (kisgyerekes_szuloi, oktataspolitikai_reformmozgalom, pedagogus_szakszervezeti_hang) name the specific fix: a coordinated national consolidation plan with advance notice, safe transport guarantees, teacher-redeployment protection, and equity safeguards would convert unmanaged drift into managed transition without requiring any of the structural or admission changes proposed in S1-S4 — the harm is in the lack of coordination, not in demographic contraction itself.
- A15 (A purely passive demographic baseline may not actually exist, since active administrative levers (such as S2's score thresholds) are already operating alongside demographic decline, making 'inaction' framing potentially a disguise for unnamed active policy choices.): evidence_answerable — This is a decomposable empirical question: a KSH/Oktatási Hivatal administrative-data analysis separating the demographically-attributable share of contraction from the score-threshold-attributable share (as multiple uncertainty entries specify) would settle whether S5 can honestly be used as a policy-neutral counterfactual — the data to answer this does not yet exist but the method to produce it is well-specified.
- A16 (Demographically-driven contraction is politically convenient (lowest reversal risk, no legislative cost) but leaves EU-level equity criticism of Hungary's highly selective school system unanswered unless paired with transparent reporting distinguishing demographic effects from active policy levers.): communication_fixable — The core remedy named by both stakeholders who raised this (kormanyzati_reformrealizmus, konzervativ_ertekvedo) is transparent annual reporting clearly separating demographic from policy-driven effects — this is a disclosure and communication fix, not a change to the underlying contraction dynamic, though it does not by itself satisfy the EU's substantive equity-conditionality concerns, which remain a separate political question.
