# Research Notes: Early Academic Selection & the 6/8-Year Gimnázium (Educational Psychology Domain)

**Finding 1 — Hungary's early-selective tracks are socially, not just academically, sorted.** Horn's analysis of Hungarian data shows that 
family background matters even after controlling for previous test scores, and in almost all estimations the effect of SES is comparable in size to, if not larger than, the effect of test score
. Specifically, 
a student with a one standard deviation higher family background than another student with similar test scores from the same school has a 21.6 per cent higher chance of entering a six-year academic track, an 11.4 per cent higher chance of entering a church school
. Source: "Early tracking and competition – A recipe for major inequalities" (Horn), https://real.mtak.hu/42964/1/8_Hungary_final_5_February_2016_hd_u.pdf

**Finding 2 — Hungary is designed as a natural experiment with three selection ages, and outcomes diverge post-selection.** 
The Hungarian system is ideal to test the effect of early-selection on inequality of opportunity, since students are selected at three different ages: the early-selective academic tracks skim off the best students first at age 10, then at age 12, and finally at age 14 all students enter secondary level.
 
Value-added analysis between 6th and 8th grade, and between 8th and 10th grade, shows that mathematical and reading performance diverges across tracks even after controlling for skill and status selection.
 Source: "Chapter 8: Early tracking and competition – A recipe for major inequalities in Hungary," https://www.researchgate.net/publication/327244375

**Finding 3 — Hungary's selection rate and equity outlook, OECD assessment (2015, still the standard reference).** 
Compared to the OECD average (age 15), tracking starts early in Hungary: partially at Grade 5 (age 10-11) or later at Grade 7 (age 12-13); around 85% of Hungarian students are in schools which can select students according to academic performance or recommendations from feeder schools, compared to the OECD average of 43%.
 
The impact of socio-economic background on mathematics performance of 15-year-olds was among the largest in OECD countries, with high variance between schools.
 Source: OECD Education Policy Outlook Hungary, https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/about/projects/edu/education-policy-outlook/379201-Hungary-Profile.pdf

**Finding 4 — Current (2022) PISA equity data confirms the gap has not closed and is comparatively large.** 
In Hungary socio-economically advantaged students (top 25% by SES) outperformed disadvantaged students (bottom 25%) by 121 score points in mathematics — larger than the average difference (93 points) across OECD countries.
 
The performance gap related to students' socio-economic status is widest in Romania and the Slovak Republic, followed by Hungary, Israel and Chinese Taipei.
 Overall national performance also fell: 
mathematics and reading results in 2022 are among the lowest in the history of Hungary's participation in PISA, close to those observed in 2015.
 Source: OECD PISA 2022 Country Notes: Hungary, https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/pisa-2022-results-volume-i-and-ii-country-notes_2fca04b9/hungary_5f1ae58d.html

**Finding 5 — A recent education/labour economics paper (2023) still describes the equity problem as unresolved.** 
Free elementary school choice and early tracking have hindered equity and have caused a high variance of student achievements between schools — a long-standing problem that is still not resolved.
 Source: ScienceDirect, "Longer schooling with grade retention," https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0272775723001346

**Finding 6 — Cross-national causal/quasi-causal literature converges: early tracking raises inequality and can lower mean performance, especially for weaker/low-track students, without clearly raising average achievement.** A difference-in-differences study using PISA/TIMSS/PIRLS found 
early tracking has a negative effect on mean performance of students, particularly in literacy, and a very strong negative effect on low achieving students, suggesting disadvantageous peer- and environmental effects in the lower tracks
. A World-of-Labour synthesis states 
studies using PISA and TIMSS to study early tracking's effect on inequality, though differing in details, commonly conclude that early tracking increases inequality in test scores
, and flags a mechanism concern: 
tracking is usually based on test scores, grades, or teacher recommendations, which are noisy measures especially in early years — raising the concern that early tracking decisions are driven by family background, which could be an obstacle to intergenerational social mobility.
 Sources: ERIC EJ1114208, https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ1114208&pg=2&q=Tracking+in+Education ; IZA World of Labor, https://wol.iza.org/articles/school-tracking-and-intergenerational-social-mobility/long

**Finding 7 — But the literature on social-inequality effects specifically (as opposed to achievement dispersion) is more mixed/contested than popular framing suggests.** A 2021 Springer paper notes 
findings from research on effects of tracking on social inequality have been inconclusive; while some studies provided evidence that tracking perpetuated social inequality, most observed no tracking effect on social achievement gaps.
 A 2026 ScienceDirect paper frames the debate: 
opponents claim that early selection increases achievement variance and social inequality whereas it does not increase achievement level, while the central belief behind between-school tracking is that academically homogeneous schools have advantages for teaching and learning
 — but 
it has yet to be shown whether between-school tracking really increases achievement homogeneity.
 This is an important contested nuance: the achievement-homogeneity assumption underlying the pro-selection efficiency case is itself empirically shaky. Sources: Springer, https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11092-020-09346-4 ; ScienceDirect, https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0276562426000120

**Finding 8 — Big-Fish-Little-Pond Effect (BFLPE): selective/high-achieving school placement can depress academic self-concept even for the "winners" of selection.** A large meta-analysis (33 studies, N=1,276,838) found 
the random effects model led to a mean of the BFLPE at β = −0.28 (p < 0.001)
, and 
moderator analyses revealed that the BFLPE is an age-based process and an intercultural phenomenon, stronger among high school students, in Asia, and when verbal self-concept is considered
 — directly relevant since gimnázium selection happens right at the age band (10-14) where effects intensify entering secondary-type schooling. The original 26-country test established 
the BFLPE posits that a student will have a lower academic self-concept in an academically selective school than in a nonselective school
, and academic self-concept itself causally feeds back into achievement: 
a longitudinal analysis demonstrated that academic self-concept had a direct effect on subsequent school performance beyond the effects of academic ability and prior school performance.
 This is a mechanism-level psychological cost of selection that operates independent of the SES-sorting critique. Sources: Frontiers in Psychology meta-analysis, https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6124391/ ; Marsh cross-cultural study (PubMed), https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12971085/ ; Semantic Scholar summary, https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/The-big-fish-little-pond-effect-on-academic-Marsh/e44089caefaeab780ebd468c985600f40180b0c8

**Finding 9 — Poland's 1999 reform delaying tracking (a concrete structural alternative) is the best-documented natural experiment for "what if selection age is raised" and shows mixed but largely positive results.** The reform 
replaced the former 8-year general primary school with a 6-year general primary and a 3-year general lower secondary school, postponing the age of first selection from age 15 to 16
 (note: this is later-stage tracking than the Hungarian 10/12 debate, but is the closest strong causal evidence on "delay = better/worse"). Achievement effects: 
Poland's PISA score improved from 470 in 2000 to 490 in 2003 and 495 in 2006 in math; in 2000 Poland ranked below the OECD reading average, by 2003 reached it, and by 2006 scored above average, ranking 9th in the world.
 Equity effect: 
the change was likely beneficial for low-track children, as their composition of peers and teachers improved substantially, and results suggest the reform reached its initial goal of decreasing inequalities.
 Labour-market follow-up: 
the reform increased employment probability (by around 3 percentage points) and earnings (by around 4%).
 Important caveat for interpretation: 
the reform was unique in that besides extending comprehensive education, it altered curriculum, standardized examinations, teacher qualification requirements, and financing scheme simultaneously
 — so the "delay tracking" effect is bundled with other reforms, a confound the Polish-reform literature itself flags. Sources: World Bank, https://www.worldbank.org/en/news/opinion/2016/01/22/polands-education-system-leading-in-europe ; Springer labour-market paper, https://labourmarketresearch.springeropen.com/articles/10.1186/s12651-022-00317-4 ; hpatrinos.com working paper, https://hpatrinos.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/03/poland-tracking1.pdf ; Cairn.info, https://shs.cairn.info/journal-revue-francaise-de-sociologie-2014-1-page-127?lang=en

**Finding 10 — Vocational/general track divergence in Hungary compounds the selection story further down the pipeline (context, not core scope, but relevant to "who loses").** 
In PISA 2022, Hungarian students had one of the largest gaps in average mathematics performance between general and vocational programmes, largely due to differences in the socio-economic composition of the two educational paths.
 This suggests the age-10/12 selection is the first of several compounding sorting events in the Hungarian system, consistent with Horn's three-stage selection framing (Finding 2). Source: European Commission Education and Training Monitor 2025, Hungary, https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html

**Finding 11 (uncertainty flag, not a clean finding) — Effect of grade repetition/school starting age as an adjacent Hungarian-specific mechanism.** A Hungarian study using instrumental variables found 
children generally gain from starting school 1 year later, and the effects are much stronger for students from low-educated families
, and 
the hypothesis that delayed entry has a stronger impact on low-status children is supported by robustness checks.
 This is tangential (school-starting-age, not track-selection-age) but relevant to the brief's equity theme and to alternatives discussion (e.g., could later universal school entry reduce the SES gradient that later feeds into 10/12 selection?). Source: Academia.edu, https://www.academia.edu/126047095/Early_tracking_and_competition_A_recipe_for_major_inequalities_in_Hungary
