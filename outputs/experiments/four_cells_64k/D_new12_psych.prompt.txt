# Expert analysis: demography

## Findings (evidence)
- Annual births in Hungary fell from ~125,000 in the early 1990s to 85–95,000 by the early 2020s, and fertility dropped below 1.3 by 2025; this 30–35% cohort collapse mechanically shrinks demand for all secondary tracks including 6/8-year grammar schools (gimnázium) regardless of selection policy design. [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks; Economx Oct 7 2025, Economx May 19 2026]
- System infrastructure is currently calibrated for ~110,000 annual cohorts, but will need to serve only ~72,000 within a few years—a 35% drop that forces school-network consolidation decisions independent of early-selection reform. [evidence: moderate — PSZ president Totyik Tamás, Economx May 19 2026]
- Regional demographic divergence is extreme: some districts record only 55–67 annual births, while Budapest districts and suburban areas show 40%+ divergence between current birth numbers and first-grade enrollment forecasts, making uniform national selection rules structurally unfit for local implementation. [evidence: moderate — Economx May 19 2026]
- Primary-school enrollment has fallen 17.3% in 20 years (from 859,000 in 2005/06 to ~749,000 today); the number of general-school sites (általános iskola) fell from 3,625 in 2021/22 to 3,537 in 2024/25, with accelerating closures. [evidence: strong — Népszava/KSH July 14 2025]
- Kindergarten enrollment fell from 341,000 in 2011/12 to 324,000 in 2024/25; the under-1 cohort is already ~17,000 smaller than the 4-year-old cohort, foreshadowing further primary and secondary contraction through 2040. [evidence: strong — Válasz Online Oct 17 2025; Népszava July 14 2025]
- Kertesi–Kézdi research established that Hungary's free-choice, early-tracking system allows schools discretion to hand-pick students, producing substantial socioeconomic segregation in primary and secondary schooling independent of demographic pressure. [evidence: strong — Kertesi–Kézdi; cited in 'Early tracking and competition – A recipe for major inequalities in Hungary']
- PISA 2022 data confirm that in Hungary socioeconomic status accounts for 25% of variation in mathematics performance versus an OECD average of 15%, indicating that early selection amplifies socioeconomic determinism of outcomes. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2022; MDPI 'How Rare and How Stable?']
- Union analysis shows small-school size is not the primary driver of underperformance; the correlation between test results and social background far exceeds that between results and school size, even though small rural schools disproportionately enroll disadvantaged pupils. [evidence: moderate — PSZ president Totyik Tamás, Economx Oct 7 2025]
- Union and government sources agree that school-network consolidation (mergers and closures) is 'nearly certain' within 5–10 years; currently 80 schools operate with no first grade and 200 operate with ~10 first-graders, making unified selection criteria operationally moot at many sites. [evidence: moderate — Economx July 15 2025; Válasz Online Oct 17 2025]
- In 2024/25, of students transitioning beyond general school, 44.8% (192,000) entered secondary grammar-school track (including 6/8-year routes), 41.9% entered technical/vocational secondary, and 13.3% entered non-matriculation vocational programs. [evidence: strong — KSH 'Oktatási adatok, 2024/2025']
- Hungary's population declined from 9.87 million (2011 census) to 9.6 million (2022 census), a 3.4% drop—the largest intercensal decline ever recorded; trend-based projections place population at 8.5 million by 2050, a further 13% decrease that directly shrinks the school-age population base. [evidence: strong — OSW Centre for Eastern Studies April 2025; Hungarian Central Statistical Office (KSH) 2022 census]
- Cost-function research (Hermann 2005b, OFI synthesis) shows per-pupil costs fall sharply as enrollment rises toward ~200 students, after which economies of scale flatten; consolidation below 200 pupils yields the largest unit-cost savings but aggregate national savings remain uncertain under different closure/merger scenarios. [evidence: moderate — Oktatáskutató és Fejlesztő Intézet (OFI) 'A kistelepülések kisiskolái – társulás?']
- Government framing resists the term 'closure,' instead emphasizing that consolidation ('összevonások') will protect small-settlement schools 'as long as there is demand' (amíg erre van igény), but this framing does not alter the mechanical force of cohort decline. [evidence: moderate — Válasz Online Oct 17 2025]
- Political leadership at all levels has not yet mounted a coordinated, public agenda for managing school-network consolidation despite union and government acknowledgment that it is unavoidable within 5–10 years. [evidence: moderate — Economx July 15 2025; Válasz Online Oct 17 2025; Economx Oct 7 2025]

## Interpretation
Cohort collapse—not selection design—is now the dominant structural force reshaping Hungary's school system through 2040. Births have fallen 30–35% from 1990s levels; the system is calibrated for 110,000 pupils annually but will serve ~72,000 within years. This mechanical shrinkage will force network consolidation (mergers, closures) in nearly every district, with extreme regional divergence meaning that uniform selection rules are becoming operationally moot: many schools already have no first grade. Simultaneously, early selection's documented equity cost (Kertesi–Kézdi, PISA 2022 data showing 25% vs. 15% SES-performance correlation) persists and will not be erased by demographic decline; if anything, concentration of intake in fewer, selective sites will amplify segregation unless deliberate integration safeguards are designed into consolidation. The political consensus—union, government, and researcher voices—is that consolidation is 'nearly certain,' yet no coordinated public agenda for managing it exists. This means the 6/8-year grammar-school decision cannot be isolated from the broader network-consolidation crisis. Reforming selection design alone (e.g., delaying it, widening access, moving to comprehensive lower-secondary) will not solve the system's core problem: too many underutilized buildings serving too few children. Yet consolidation design *without* attention to equity will likely sharpen already steep SES-driven sorting. The critical decision now is not 'early selection vs. comprehensive lower-secondary in the abstract,' but 'how to consolidate the network while protecting access and equity for disadvantaged pupils in shrinking rural regions.'

## Assumptions
- Cohort-decline projections through 2040 will not be reversed by a major unforeseen fertility rebound or policy-driven immigration surge. [assumption]
- School-network consolidation will proceed through some mix of closures, mergers, and distributed-model pilot programs rather than by a reversal to status-quo over-capacity. [assumption]
- The equity effects of early selection are not wholly subsumed by school size or rural–urban geography; they reflect hand-picking discretion by selective schools and remain addressable through policy design (e.g., random-draw admissions, admission ceilings on SES concentration). [assumption]
- Stakeholders (teachers, maintainers, families, local politicians) will need transparent information about consolidation timelines and network-redesign criteria to make informed choices about school transitions and planning. [assumption]

## Position
Cohort collapse through 2040 will force school-network consolidation regardless of whether Hungary retains or reforms early selection (6/8-year grammar schools), but the equity consequences of consolidation depend entirely on whether selection and network-redesign policies are coordinated to protect disadvantaged pupils' access to integrated, quality schooling in shrinking regions.

## Uncertainties
- Will Hungary's fertility rate stabilize at 1.2–1.3 or continue falling below 1.1? Projections are highly sensitive to this parameter and directly affect whether cohort size bottoms out at ~72,000 or falls further to ~60,000 annually by 2035. # Expert analysis: education_finance

## Findings (evidence)
- Hungary's 6- and 8-year grammar schools (hatosztályos and nyolcosztályos gimnáziumok) enroll approximately one in ten students, concentrated in early secondary entry (age 10–12) into the standard 8-grade primary structure. [evidence: strong — Eurydice: Organisation of general secondary education; Index.hu (2019)]
- Hungarian public secondary education is financed through normative per-capita grants that cover only 60–70% of institutional operating costs on average; school maintainers (municipalities, churches, foundations) must provide the remainder, concentrating fiscal burden unevenly. [evidence: strong — Lannert Judit, TÁRKI-TUDOK: A közoktatás finanszírozásának lehetséges megoldásai]
- Church and foundation-maintained grammar schools receive supplementary normative grants (kiegészítő normatíva) on top of base per-pupil funding; historical data indicate church education costs approximately HUF 50 billion/year (~1% of tax revenue), though this covers all church schooling, not selection-specific costs. [evidence: moderate — Lannert Judit, TÁRKI-TUDOK: A közoktatás finanszírozásának lehetséges megoldásai]
- Private shadow costs of admission preparation are substantial and unequally distributed: prep courses cost HUF 140,000; private tutoring can exceed HUF 100,000+ per subject. Among sixth-graders targeting 6-year tracks, nearly 70% use paid preparation (versus <50% for eighth-graders), averaging ~3 hours 40 minutes weekly. [evidence: strong — Eduline (2024, 2023); Privátbankár/Berényi Eszter (2020)]
- Hungary exhibits the highest school segregation by socio-economic status in the EU (PISA 2022); advantaged (top 25%) students outscore disadvantaged (bottom 25%) by 121 points in mathematics versus OECD average of 93 points. Parental-graduate-status achievement gap is 53 points in Hungary versus 33-point EU average. [evidence: strong — European Commission: Education and Training Monitor 2025 — Hungary; OECD PISA 2022 Country Note Hungary]
- Competence-test data show no measurable value-added progress in 6- and 8-year grammar schools once initial ability and family background are controlled, according to Hungary's Education and Child Affairs Minister Lannert Judit (June 2026). [evidence: moderate — Telex (2026-06-06); HVG (2026-06-06); Eduline (2026-06-06, 2026-06-08)]
- Hungary's secondary-school network comprises 880 institutions, densely distributed to ensure accessibility within ~1 hour by public transport; any structural reform (track elimination/restructuring) would require reallocation of existing infrastructure and staff, not new construction, but involves transition and re-accreditation costs. [evidence: strong — KSH (2024/25); Eurydice]
- Earlier (2000s) ministry attempts to counter segregation through targeted funding and pedagogical programs rather than structural change proved insufficient; experts concluded that segregation cannot be addressed without systemic educational reform. [evidence: moderate — Sulinet/EOKIK report: Cigánynak lenni Magyarországon (2002–2006)]
- Per-pupil funding differences between track types are modest; the dominant financial impact of structural reform would be transition costs (staff retraining, building re-accreditation, administrative reorganization), not steady-state per-capita costs. [evidence: weak — Budget chapters; expert estimates (curated source)]
- Demographic decline (shrinking student cohorts) reduces the original structural rationale for early selection — scarce places for many applicants — making the current selective system increasingly difficult to justify on efficiency grounds. [evidence: moderate — Lannert Judit, Education Minister, June 2026 interviews (Telex, HVG, Eduline)]
- Political contestation around potential early-selection reform is high: opposition frames reform as threat to abolish grammar schools entirely; the Government disputes this framing and proposes only a review/social dialogue, not abolition. [evidence: strong — Telex (2026-06-06); HVG (2026-06-06); Eduline (2026-06-08)]
- The central admission examination (felvételi vizsga) system functions as a de facto entry fee even though tuition is free, because preparation is neither universally available nor equally accessible, concentrating advantage among higher-income families. [evidence: strong — Eduline (2024, 2023); Privátbankár/Berényi Eszter (2020)]
- Education Minister Lannert Judit has explicitly stated that the education system should be reviewed through social dialogue, characterizing the current selective structure as a mechanism through which the elite self-selects rather than a system that accelerates learning for high-ability students. [evidence: strong — Telex (2026-06-06); HVG (2026-06-06); Eduline (2026-06-06)]
- No empirical study cited in current research literature demonstrates that grammar schools produce faster learning progress than comprehensive or non-selective tracks once family background and prior achievement are accounted for. [evidence: moderate — Minister Lannert Judit competence-test analysis; European Commission Education and Training Monitor 2025]
- If transition costs are assumed at low-to-moderate scale (staff retraining, building re-use, administrative consolidation), a structural shift away from early selection would cost in the range of tens to low hundreds of millions of Hungarian forints, not billions. [evidence: weak — Expert estimates based on network density (880 schools) and historical reform data (2000s integration programs)]
- Church-maintained selective schools are overrepresented in the 6/8-year track system, receiving supplementary state funding, making them less dependent on municipal budgets and potentially more politically resistant to structural reform affecting their selective status. [evidence: weak — Lannert Judit, TÁRKI-TUDOK; Eurydice; inferred from structural financing data]
- The centralized admission examination (központi írásbeli) system, while formally neutral on socio-economic status, de facto advantages students whose families can afford preparation, making it a mechanism through which economic inequality reproduces school segregation. [evidence: strong — Eduline (2024, 2023); Privátbankár/Berényi Eszter (2020); PISA 2022 segregation data]

## Interpretation
Hungary's 6- and 8-year grammar-school (korai gimnázium) system is financed through the same normative per-pupil grants as comprehensive schools, but maintainers cover 30–40% of actual costs from their own budgets—unevenly. Steady-state per-capita costs differ only modestly. What matters financially is that admission preparation generates substantial private shadow costs (HUF 140,000+ per student, reaching HUF 100,000+ per subject in tutoring) borne disproportionately by higher-income families. This creates a de facto entry fee despite free tuition. The system's structural effect is powerful: Hungary has the EU's highest school segregation by socio-economic status, and achievement gaps are the widest in the EU/OECD—despite (or because of) early tracking. Critically, recent competence-test analysis by the current Education Minister indicates that grammar schools produce no measurable value-added learning progress once prior ability and family background are held constant. They select already-high-performing students; they do not accelerate learning faster than non-selective schools. Demographic decline (falling cohort sizes) has eroded the original structural rationale (scarce places). Politically, the system is contested: the Government (through Minister Lannert Judit, an education-finance researcher) has opened a public dialogue on reform; opposition frames this as abolition risk, which the Government denies. Structural alternatives exist (later selection, comprehensive lower secondary) but require transition costs (staff retraining, building re-accreditation, network reorganization) estimated in the tens-to-low-hundreds-of-millions of forints range, not massive budget outlays. The core disagreement is no longer about affordability but about whether the system's segregation and elite self-selection can be justified when learning value-added is absent and demographic pressure mounts.

## Assumptions
- That Ministry competence-test data on value-added are representative and reliable; no independent external validation was cited. [assumption]
- That formal free tuition constitutes true equality of access; in fact, preparation costs function as a barrier. [assumption]
- That demographic decline will persist, reducing demand for selective capacity and weakening the efficiency justification for early tracking. [assumption]
- That structural alternatives (comprehensive lower-secondary, later selection at 14–16) are educationally and administratively feasible within Hungary's existing school network. [assumption]
- That the current Minister's framing (value-added absent, szelekció is elite self-selection, not acceleration) reflects expert consensus, not a single reformist voice. [assumption]
- That political feasibility of structural reform depends primarily on public dialogue and expert consensus, not on veto power held by church schools or other institutional maintainers. [assumption]
- That transition costs (staff retraining, building re-use, administrative consolidation) can be estimated from historical precedent and current network density, though no detailed costing study was available. [assumption]
- That the school-choice system (szabad iskolaválasztás) provides meaningful choice only to families with transport/information access and school availability; informal catchments remain powerful. [assumption]
- That EU/OECD segregation and achievement-gap data are directly comparable to Hungary's system structure and are not confounded by differences in vocational-track enrollment or immigrant integration policies. [assumption]
- That the 2000s pedagogical-reform failure (targeted funding, non-structural programs) reflects a real constraint—that segregation requires structural (not programmatic) change—rather than a failure of implementation or funding adequacy. [assumption]
- That the Ministry's interpretation of competence-test data (no value-added once family background is controlled) has not been challenged by other researchers, or that such challenges exist but are not yet published. [assumption]
- That any structural alternative (later selection, comprehensive school) would be implemented with political commitment and sufficient transition funding, not as an underfunded or grudging phase-out. [assumption]

## Position
Hungary's early-selection grammar-school system generates substantial private shadow costs and the EU's highest socio-economic segregation but produces no measurable learning value-added once prior ability is controlled, making its continuation politically contestable and structurally replaceable at moderate transition cost.

## Uncertainties
- What is the precise order-of-magnitude cost of a structural transition (e.g., full phase-out of 6/8-year entry points and reorganization into later-entry secondary)? (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Detailed line-by-line costing of staff retraining, building re-accreditation, administrative consolidation, and any new infrastructure needs; pilot re-organization in a single tankerületi központ (state school district center) with transparent costing.)
- How strong is the empirical evidence (beyond the current Minister's statement) that grammar schools produce no value-added? Have independent researchers tested this with rigor comparable to international best practice? (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Independent replication of value-added analysis using longitudinal student data, controlling for prior achievement, family background, and peer composition; comparison with OECD/EU methodologies for value-added estimation; peer review in international journals.)

# Expert analysis: equity_and_social_mobility

## Findings (evidence)
- Hungary's school system explains 19–25% of reading and mathematics achievement variance by socio-economic status, nearly double the OECD average of 12–14%, making it one of the most socially stratified systems in the OECD. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2012–2022 country notes]
- Approximately 8–12% of each cohort enters 6- or 8-year gimnázium tracks, selected at age 10 or 12 via central written admission examination; entrants are strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families. [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics]
- Raw achievement differences between early-selective gimnázium students and non-selected peers are predominantly explained by selection effects (initial ability and family background) rather than by school value-added, which is modest. [evidence: moderate — Horn (2013); Kertesi & Kezdi school-effect studies]
- Hungary has among the most intense school selectivity scores globally (2.85 on PISA Index, ranked 9 of 80 countries in 2022), and the World Bank classifies it as having socio-economic segregation comparable to Latin American systems despite lower income inequality nationally. [evidence: strong — OECD Education GPS 2022; World Bank PISA analysis]
- The 10%-most socio-economically advantaged students score approximately 170 points higher in PISA reading than the 10%-most disadvantaged, and school composition (same-SES clustering) rather than mixing further entrenches this gap. [evidence: moderate — Hungarian Conservative PISA analysis (secondary OECD synthesis)]
- In the Országos Kompetenciamérés (National Competency Assessment), the achievement gap between 6/8-year gimnázium entrants and general-school peers grows sharply from grade 6 (~160–175 points) to grade 10 (~684 in mathematics, ~567 in reading), suggesting peer composition or instructional quality diverges over time. [evidence: moderate — Országos Kompetenciamérés edition summary]
- Value-added analysis by Horn Dániel finds 6/8-year gimnazium students achieve ~10% larger gains relative to similar-ability, similar-background peers in general schools, but this gain is partly attributable to positive peer effects (higher-achieving peers 'pulling up' performance) and pedagogical value-add appears smaller. [evidence: moderate — Horn D. (2026) Telex/G7; Eduline; TARKI publikáció]
- In regions without accessible 8-year gimnazium, SES shows no stronger correlation with grade-6 competency scores than in other regions; by grade 8, regions with an accessible early-selective gimnázium show significantly stronger SES–competency correlation, suggesting the selection mechanism amplifies inequality. [evidence: moderate — Horn D. TARKI publication: 'A kisgimnáziumok szerepe a szelekcióban']
- HÉTFA value-added panel study (1.1M students, 2008–2014) shows the 6-year gimnazium track has negative value-added at grade 8 (smaller gain than general school) and positive value-added only by grade 10, whereas 8-year tracks show positive value-added earlier, suggesting timing and maturation interact with selection mechanisms. [evidence: moderate — HÉTFA Research Institute Working Paper 2015/11]
- The World Bank concludes: 'there are no efficiency gains from introducing early selection of students, and delayed tracking can promote better performance for all students,' positioning early selection as a source of inequity without aggregate learning benefit. [evidence: strong — World Bank: 'Hungary: Skilling up – the next generation' PISA analysis]
- Bavaria (Germany) quasi-experimental reform study: postponing/shifting tracking age lowered performance in both basic and middle tracks by ~13 PISA points (~half a school year), with peer effects identified as the mechanism for basic-track decline, and increased achievement dispersion/inequality of opportunity. [evidence: moderate — ScienceDirect: 'The effects of early tracking on student performance: Evidence from a school reform in Bavaria']
- Northern Ireland de-tracking reform: expanding the elite-track share at age 11 raised overall national examination pass rates but negatively affected non-elite schools' performance—specifically those who lost high-achieving peers—illustrating the peer-loss cost to non-selected pupils. [evidence: moderate — Guyon, Maurin, McNally (2012); ResearchGate economics-of-tracking summary]
- Romania's tracking-delay reform (shifting selection by two years): no aggregate effect on university completion, labour-market participation, or earnings; disadvantaged students showed higher probability of finishing the academic track, but this did not translate to higher university completion, tempering expectations that delayed tracking alone drives mobility gains. [evidence: moderate — Malamud & Pop-Eleches reform evaluation; ResearchGate review]
- Recent meta-analysis across 21 cycles of primary and secondary assessment data found strong evidence that early tracking increases social achievement gaps, contradicting claims that early selection has no inequality consequences. [evidence: strong — Springer Educational Assessment, Evaluation and Accountability: 'Early tracking and different types of inequalities in achievement']
- Within-school between-class ability grouping's effects are contested in the general literature: mixed-ability vs. grouped classes show little average difference, but those placed in lower groups (predominantly disadvantaged students) face reduced peer quality and negative peer effects, while those in higher groups benefit, creating asymmetric harm. [evidence: contested — Oxford Academic European Sociological Review 2025: 'Does within-school between-class ability grouping harm...']
- Government-dependent private/church schools constitute ~25.5% of Hungary's PISA cohort, among the highest in OECD, and this maintainer diversity interacts with gimnázium-selection filters to further stratify students by socio-economic status. [evidence: moderate — OECD Education GPS 2022; Expat Focus PISA summary]
- The Arany János Tehetséggondozó Program (AJTP) is a 15+-year compensatory programme providing talented disadvantaged youth from settlements <5000 population with a preparatory year and access to quality gimnáziums; it has supported ~5000 disadvantaged talented graduates nationally, representing a targeted alternative to structural detracking, though much smaller in scale than the 6/8-year admission cohort (~8–12% of all pupils). [evidence: moderate — Oktatáskutató és Fejlesztő Intézet (OFI) AJTP cost-benefit analysis]
- Researcher Berényi Eszter (ELTE 2019–2024) characterizes Hungary's 6/8-year gimnázium system as producing 'one of the world's most selective education systems', which 'erodes overall education effectiveness' while increasing inequality; the system persists partly due to cross-class political economy—different social groups see it serving different aspirational functions. [evidence: moderate — Berényi E. ELTE research project; Qubit interview 2022]
- Hungary's grade-repetition rate (~6% below OECD average of ~9%) suggests an alternative sorting mechanism may operate: rather than failing students in place, the system channels them into lower tracks, with selection via admission filtering serving as the primary stratification device. [evidence: weak — OECD Education GPS 2022 (inference from rate comparison)]
- No randomised controlled trials exist directly comparing Hungarian early-selection (6/8-year) systems against a counterfactual comprehensive-school scenario with equivalent resources, so causal value-added claims rest on observational designs vulnerable to unobserved confounding. [evidence: weak — Systematic inference from curated evidence base]
- Grade-repetition and ability-grouping research shows students from disadvantaged backgrounds disproportionately enter lower groups/repeated grades; the interaction between early selection and within-school grouping is not well documented for Hungary, making it unclear how much harm compounds at multiple decision points. [evidence: weak — Domain synthesis; no Hungarian-specific study identified]

## Interpretation
Hungary's 6/8-year early-selection gimnazium system is one of the world's most selective and stratifying school structures. The evidence shows: (1) raw achievement differences between selected and non-selected pupils are largely selection effects, not school value-added; (2) selection amplifies the link between family SES and test scores over time, widening inequality; (3) removing high-achieving peers from non-selected schools harms those left behind (peer-loss effect); (4) delayed/compressed tracking in other countries has produced mixed results—some negative, some neutral on aggregate, though potentially beneficial for marginal students entering the academic track. The system persists despite equity costs, partly because different social groups perceive different benefits (domestic prestige, international mobility, aspirational sorting). Targeted compensatory programmes (AJTP) exist but reach far fewer students than the selection cohort. The interaction between early selection and within-school ability grouping is under-researched for Hungary, leaving cumulative stratification effects unclear.

## Assumptions
- We assume the Országos Kompetenciamérés and PISA datasets measure comparable constructs across schools and over time, and that raw score differences reflect real learning differences rather than test artefacts or selection-induced measurement-non-equivalence. [assumption]
- We assume value-added models (Horn, HÉTFA) adequately control for initial ability and family background using available data, but acknowledge that unmeasured family inputs, school-choice endogeneity, and peer-group selection may bias causal estimates. [assumption]
- We assume the international comparison studies (Bavaria, Northern Ireland, Romania) have sufficient contextual similarity to Hungarian policy questions—though school governance, teacher-pay systems, and family choice architecture differ, the core mechanisms (peer effects, ability-grouping harm to low groups) are treated as plausibly transferable. [assumption]
- We assume the AJTP programme's documented reach (~5000 students) and the ~8–12% early-selection admission rate are accurate, but we do not assume that scaling AJTP to replace selection would have identical per-capita effectiveness or political feasibility as the current programme. [assumption]
- We assume the political-economy observation (Berényi) that multiple social groups support the system for different reasons is accurate but do not quantify the distribution of support or model how it would shift under alternative scenarios. [assumption]

## Position
Hungary's early-selection gimnázium system demonstrably amplifies socio-economic achievement inequality over time and generates negative peer effects for non-selected pupils, yet produces only modest causal value-added for those selected; a comprehensive lower-secondary school option with targeted support for high-ability disadvantaged students would plausibly reduce inequality and maintain aggregate learning, though implementation costs, political feasibility, and effects on school choice freedom remain contested.

## Uncertainties
- What share of the observed raw achievement difference (160–175 points grade 6, ~680+ points grade 10) between 6/8-year and general-school pupils is causal value-added, versus selection bias, peer effects, or differential curriculum implementation? The 10% figure cited by Horn is modest but its mechanism (pedagogical quality vs. peer composition) is not definitively separated. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Randomised controlled trial or quasi-experimental design (e.g., discontinuity at admission threshold) directly comparing early-selected pupils against similar non-selected peers; isolating peer composition from pedagogy via sub-group analysis or instrumental variables.)
- Would a shift to comprehensive lower-secondary schooling (age 6–14), retaining within-school academic tracking after age 14, preserve or increase aggregate learning outcomes? The Bavaria and Northern Ireland studies show peer-loss harm but use different educational systems (gymnasium-vs.-Realschule; grammar-vs.-secondary). Hungarian within-school tracking and teacher-pairing architecture is not directly studied under a comprehensive-schooling scenario. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Pilot comprehensive-school programme in 2–3 Hungarian districts, measuring cohort outcomes on Országos Kompetenciamérés and later labour-market/university-completion metrics; teacher preparation and within-school grouping fidelity data; cost accounting vs. status-quo spending.)
- How much of Hungary's high SES–performance correlation is driven by early selection itself (composition sorting), versus pre-selection family investment (tutoring, enrichment), school choice induced by selection, or unequal teacher quality across tracks? Distinguishing these mechanisms would inform whether detracking alone suffices. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Decomposition analysis of PISA/competency-test data by school-selection timing; family-background controls tested against variation in tutor-use/resource-spending by SES; comparison of teacher credentials and classroom observations across tracks before and after selection age.)


----- finnish_reform -----
# Expert analysis: finnish_reform

## Findings (evidence)
- Finland's peruskoulu (1972–1977 phase-in) abolished formal academic selection at age 10–11, replacing a dual-track system (kansakoulu for most, lyseo for the privileged) with a unified 9-year comprehensive school. [evidence: strong — https://grokipedia.com/page/Education_in_Finland]
- The explicit motivation for abolishing early selection in Finland was equity: gymnasium enrollment had grown tenfold (1955–1970) but remained skewed toward privileged groups, driving the 1968 Basic Education Act reform. [evidence: strong — https://grokipedia.com/page/Education_in_Finland]
- Peruskoulu reform is associated with high equity and reduced dependence of student outcomes on family background, though effects are entangled with concurrent teacher education policy and pupil support systems. [evidence: moderate — Pekkarinen, Uusitalo & Kerr 2009]
- Domestic Finnish critics contested the comprehensive reform's effects before international PISA fame: critics argued a 'levelling effect' produced 'unsatisfactory results for all,' representing a genuine debate about equity–quality trade-offs that was only narrative-reversed after PISA 2000 success. [evidence: moderate — https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-59031-4_6]
- Finnish comprehensive schools do not employ formal ability-tracking until age 15, but urban Finnish schools select and track pupils through differential admission criteria and class assignment within nominally comprehensive schools—a 'myth of non-selectivity' documented in empirical research. [evidence: moderate — https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-981-19-8241-5_12]
- Decentralization and the 1998 Basic Education Act in Finland legitimized parental school choice without formally reintroducing tracking; long-term Helsinki research documents that school choice is empirically linked to rising segregation in Finland's largest cities. [evidence: moderate — https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-981-19-8241-5_11; https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/1474904118764938]
- Finland's between-school variance remains low (~9% vs. OECD ~30%) despite overall PISA decline; however, within-school and between-school variance has increased over the past decade, and PISA 2022 shows a 64-point decline from the 2006 peak (548→484). [evidence: strong — https://www.oph.fi/en/blog/pisa-results-reflect-broader-changes-finnish-society; https://okm.fi/en/pisa-2022-en]
- Finland's PISA decline has been steeper than the OECD average; as of PISA 2022, one in four Finnish students performs below Level 2 in mathematics, whereas Finland previously exceeded the OECD average substantially. [evidence: strong — https://okm.fi/en/pisa-2022-en]
- Teacher education is a structural 'carrier' of comprehensive-school reform effects: since 1979, both primary and secondary teachers in Finland must hold a Master's degree requiring ~5 years of rigorous study, with highly selective entry (~10–15% acceptance). This institutional investment preceded and co-occurred with peruskoulu, and is cited as enabling delayed selection without sacrificing quality. [evidence: moderate — https://theeducationist.info/teacher-education-in-finland/; https://www.visitedufinn.com/what-qualifications-are-required-to-teach-in-finnish-schools/]
- Needs-based ('positive discrimination') funding targeted at schools by socioeconomic and immigrant composition can reduce equity gaps: Helsinki's pilot increased secondary continuation rates, particularly for immigrant pupils and Finnish-speaking boys, by ~20% reduction in non-continuation, operated through school hiring of special needs assistants, not raw funding alone. [evidence: moderate — https://vatt.fi/en/-/students-in-helsinki-benefit-from-positive-discrimination-funding]
- Hungary's early selection at age 10–12 (8-year and 6-year grammar schools) is documented by OECD and CEU research as empirically linked to high socioeconomic-performance correlation: Hungary ranks among OECD countries with the strongest correlation between student SES and mathematics achievement (~23.1% vs. OECD 14.8%), with early tracking cited as a contributing structural factor. [evidence: moderate — https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/about/projects/edu/education-policy-outlook/379201-Hungary-Profile.pdf; https://www.etd.ceu.edu/2015/gurzo_klara.pdf]
- The OECD explicitly flags Hungary's early school selection as a structural equity risk and identifies high between-school variance as amplifying the impact of socioeconomic background on student performance, ranking Hungary among the highest internationally. [evidence: strong — https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/about/projects/edu/education-policy-outlook/379201-Hungary-Profile.pdf]

## Interpretation
Finland's peruskoulu reform (1972–77) replaced dual-track selection at age 10–11 with a unified comprehensive school explicitly to reduce inequity. The reform bundled three enabling mechanisms: formal abolition of early tracking, simultaneous investment in five-year research-based teacher education (entry ~10–15%), and later expansion of targeted support funding. Evidence shows peruskoulu is associated with lower between-school variance and reduced family-background dependence; however, this result is not attributable to any single lever—teacher quality, student support systems, and pupil composition all played co-constitutive roles. Critically, Finland's own recent PISA decline (2006–2022: −64 points in mathematics) and rising within-school variance suggest that even without formal selection, decentralization and school choice have reintroduced de facto segregation in urban areas, partially undermining the equity gains. Domestic Finnish critics also questioned the reform's 'levelling effect' in real time, showing this was never a consensus position. For Hungary, the evidence is clearer: early selection (age 10–12) is empirically associated with high socioeconomic-performance correlation and high between-school variance, placing Hungary among OECD leaders in equity gaps. The OECD explicitly identifies early selection as a structural equity risk. Hungary's cost of moving toward a delayed-selection model would not be primarily financial but political and institutional: a teacher-education transformation (to match Finland's depth and selectivity) and a deliberate anti-segregation policy in school choice would be necessary, neither of which is currently in place. Finland's experience suggests that abolishing early selection is necessary but not sufficient; the infrastructure around teaching quality, pupil support, and transparent school allocation must be designed concurrently.

## Assumptions
- The peruskoulu reform's equity gains can be attributed partly to the abolition of early selection itself, rather than being wholly driven by contemporaneous teacher-education investment, support systems, or demographic change. [assumption]
- School choice, if implemented with transparent allocation and anti-segregation safeguards, would not necessarily reproduce the segregation patterns Finland observed in the 1990s–2020s. [assumption]
- Hungary's teacher workforce could be professionalized and made research-literate within a 10–15 year transition (matching Finland's 1979–1995 trajectory), and political will exists to fund this at Finland's per-teacher investment level. [assumption]
- Needs-based funding, such as Finland's positive-discrimination model, would be politically and socially acceptable in Hungary and would not trigger large-scale opt-out into private/denominational schooling. [assumption]
- The relationship between between-school variance and student equity outcomes is monotonic (lower between-school variance implies better equity), independent of within-school heterogeneity and teaching quality in each school. [assumption]

## Position
Early selection at age 10–12 in Hungary is associated with both inequitable and inefficient outcomes (high SES–performance correlation, high between-school variance), whereas Finland's delayed selection to age 15 is associated with lower between-school variance and reduced family-background dependence, though this requires concurrent investment in teacher professionalization, pupil support, and anti-segregation school-allocation policy, none of which is passive or automatic.

## Uncertainties
- What proportion of Finland's peruskoulu equity gains (reduced between-school variance, lower SES–performance correlation) is attributable to abolition of early selection itself versus concurrent teacher-education investment, pupil support systems, or demographic homogeneity? (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Quasi-experimental or natural experiment designs comparing Finnish regions with staggered peruskoulu implementation and different teacher-education timings, or international comparisons of countries adopting delayed selection at different times with different supporting infrastructure.)
- Can Finland's teacher-education model (5-year Master's, ~10% acceptance rate) be replicated in Hungary given different labour-market conditions, training-institution capacity, and political budget constraints, or would alternative professionalization pathways be required? (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Detailed comparative costing of Hungarian vs. Finnish teacher-education systems (instructor qualifications, intake selectivity, stipend/salary scales, institution numbers and capacity), and pilot implementation of a small high-selectivity cohort in Hungary to test workforce behaviour and retention.)
- What level and design of needs-based funding (Hungary's current 10–15% per-school variation vs. Finland's positive-discrimination model) would be necessary to offset remaining inequalities if early selection were abolished without simultaneous teacher professionalization? (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Empirical studies of within-Hungary variation in support funding and its correlation with equity gaps across similar SES-composition schools; simulation or pilot testing of needs-based allocation formulas under Hungarian demography and fiscal constraints.)

----- hungarian_education_system -----
# Expert analysis: hungarian_education_system

## Findings (evidence)
- Approximately 8–12% of each age cohort enters 6- or 8-year grammar school (gimnázium) tracks, selected at age 10 or 12 primarily via central written admission examination; entrants are strongly skewed toward high-socioeconomic-status (SES), urban families. [evidence: strong — KSH / Oktatási Hivatal admission statistics; Eduline 2026 enrollment data]
- Mathematics competency scores in Budapest 8-year grammar schools exceed the national average by roughly 200 points and the Budapest average by ~100 points at Grade 6, reflecting the socioeconomic and prior-ability composition of entrants. [evidence: strong — Eduline 2026; KSH competency-measurement data]
- Students in 6- or 8-year grammar schools show approximately 10% greater value-added (net learning gain controlling for family background and initial ability) than comparable-ability peers in 4-year grammar schools, but the mechanism remains unclear (peer effects, teacher assignment, or pedagogical structure). [evidence: moderate — Dániel Horn (Corvinus), July 2026 Telex/G7 podcast; Horn acknowledges the mechanism is unresolved]
- Free school choice combined with early selective tracks produces strong between-school sorting at the primary stage in cities; abolition of selective tracks alone would not eliminate sorting via catchment area and parental choice. [evidence: moderate — Kertesi & Kezdi 2013; Berényi, Berkovits, Erőss school-choice studies]
- The socioeconomic–mathematics performance association explains 25.1% of variance in Hungary's PISA 2022 results (ESCS index), ranking 3rd strongest among 79 countries, indicating very high inequality of opportunity. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2022 Hungary country profile; OECD Global PISA 2022 report]
- A large-scale study using Hungarian administrative data (Hermann & Kisfalusi 2023/2026) finds that attending a high-poverty school in Grade 8 is negatively associated with reading achievement and secondary education attainment, with substantial direct effects on attainment independent of test-score channels. [evidence: moderate — Hermann & Kisfalusi (2026), Journal of Education and Work; original paper Hunt-Ren 2023]
- A CEU dissertation using event-study methods on the 1990s expansion of early-tracking schools found zero effects on tertiary education rates and labor-market outcomes (employment, unemployment, occupational choice). [evidence: moderate — Gurzo (2015), CEU dissertation]
- Since 2013, most state schools are operated by state school district centres (tankerületi központ); churches and private foundations maintain a growing share, numbering roughly double since 2010, and can freely select students unlike state schools bound by catchment, enabling greater academic selectivity. [evidence: strong — Public Education Act amendments; Dialectical Anthropology 2024; maintainer statistics]
- Long-run academic and residential segregation of Romani pupils persists unresolved after the 2013 European Court of Human Rights judgment and remains subject to EU infringement proceedings. [evidence: strong — European Roma Rights Centre 2024; unresolved 2013 ECtHR judgment and 2016 EU infringement proceedings]
- Recent education policy has instead incentivized vocational training (technikum) after Grade 8 through higher stipends than general-medicine university students receive, moving policy levers away from gymnasium selectivity reform. [evidence: moderate — Eduline 2026; Telex 2026]
- Admission to 6- or 8-year grammar schools is concentrated among children whose parents have the time, skill, motivation, and financial resources to prepare them (tutoring, school open days), not solely on measured ability. [evidence: moderate — Eduline 2026; 2019 study cited therein]
- Demand for 6- or 8-year grammar school places vastly exceeds capacity: e.g., 369 applicants for 38 places (ELTE practice gymnasium) and 300 applicants for 32 places (Radnóti 6+1 track) in 2025/2026, with enrollment numbers unchanged despite oversubscription. [evidence: strong — Eduline 2026]
- Researchers have informally proposed two broad structural alternatives: (a) restructuring the entire system so a 6+6 (primary + secondary) model becomes universal, or (b) creating incentives for upper-middle-class families to keep children in less selective schools without banning elite tracks outright. [evidence: weak — Dániel Horn, 2026 Telex/G7 podcast; preliminary framing, not peer-reviewed proposal]
- Enrollment in 6- or 8-year grammar schools represents only about 8% of each age cohort, versus nearly 2 million children and young people in the broader education system, making the selective sector numerically small but politically and culturally salient. [evidence: strong — Dániel Horn, 2026; Eduline 2026; KSH data]

## Interpretation
Early selection into 6- or 8-year grammar schools operates as a high-stakes sorting mechanism serving a numerically small (8%) but economically and culturally prominent elite. The mechanism works by combining free school choice, examinations, and parental investment (tutoring, aspiration) in ways that strongly favor high-SES, urban families. Evidence shows modest value-added (≈10%) for selected students compared to similar peers in non-selective paths, but this appears partly attributable to composition and resource effects rather than the structure of early selection itself. The system produces measurable between-school segregation by SES and ability at the primary stage, exacerbating a pre-existing inequality of opportunity (PISA shows SES explains 25% of math variance in Hungary, among the world's highest). Rozsdás long-run effects on labor-market outcomes are contested: one study finds zero effect on tertiary and employment, contradicting the assumption that early selection better prepares for higher education. The growing role of church-run schools, which can freely select students, further intensifies academic stratification without uniform structural regulation. Recent policy attention has shifted toward vocational incentives rather than reforming selection mechanisms themselves.

## Assumptions
- The central written examination (felvételi vizsga) and school choice mechanism as currently designed are the primary drivers of SES-skewed selection, and structural or non-structural changes to these mechanisms would meaningfully reduce inequality of opportunity. [assumption]
- The 10% value-added effect attributed to early-selective tracks is stable across different student populations and school contexts, and would persist if the tracks were maintained in a reformed system. [assumption]
- The current capacity constraint (massive oversubscription with unchanged enrollment) reflects real pedagogical or resource limits, rather than a policy choice to limit elite-track expansion. [assumption]
- Abolishing or substantially redesigning early selection would not be offset by intensified segregation through other channels (pure-choice migration, religious-school expansion, new private-school formation). [assumption]
- The measured competency gaps (200-point math gap) and SES-performance correlation persist as stable causal pathways, not temporary manifestations of cohort composition or measurement effects. [assumption]
- The apparent null effect on long-term tertiary and labor-market outcomes (Gurzo 2015) generalizes to the contemporary 6/8-year tracks, even though entry-level composition and school resources may have changed substantially since the 1990s. [assumption]

## Position
Early selection into 6- or 8-year grammar schools in Hungary produces modest peer and pedagogical gains (≈10% value-added) for selected students but concentrates opportunity among high-SES urban families and likely deepens inequality of opportunity net of this small gain; the long-run labor-market and social-mobility effects remain contested, and any reform must account for structural migration to religious and private schools that can freely select.

## Uncertainties
- What fraction of the measured 10% value-added is attributable to school-level teaching quality, pedagogical structure (6+6 vs. 4+4), peer effects, or unmeasured prior ability, and would this decomposition differ for disadvantaged subgroups? (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Randomized controlled trial or matched-cohort analysis isolating the effect of selective vs. non-selective track assignment holding school quality and peer composition constant; longitudinal analysis of value-added separately for low-SES and Roma subgroups.)
- How much of the SES-skewed intake is due to meritocratic selection (differential ability differences by SES that the exam correctly identifies) versus unequal access to exam preparation (tutoring, information, school-choice navigation)? (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Manipulation or discontinuity analysis of exam performance near the admission cutoff, separately analyzing exam-takers from high- and low-SES families; randomized provision of exam-prep support to low-SES students with matched ability.)
- Would abolition or substantial loosening of early selection, if feasible politically, reduce between-school segregation by SES/ability or merely shift it downstream (into school choice, private-school expansion, residential sorting)? (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Longitudinal natural experiment (e.g., jurisdictional variation in track abolition) measuring between-school segregation indices and school-choice enrollment patterns before and after; survey of school-choice motivations among high-SES parents in systems with and without early selection.)
- What are the true causal effects of early selection on tertiary education attendance, labor-market earnings, occupational mobility, and subjective well-being? Do these differ by family background and gender? (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Multi-cohort longitudinal study linking school administrative data, tax/social-insurance records, and survey data through age 30+; instrumental-variable or regression-discontinuity analysis exploiting variation in examination difficulty or cutoff scores over time.)
- What is the cost in money, teaching time, and administrative complexity of implementing each of the proposed alternative models (universal 6+6, incentive-based voluntary de-selection, comprehensive primary education)? What political obstacles would each face? (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Costing study for each reform scenario; interviews with school-maintainer representatives, teacher unions, and political leaders on implementation feasibility and likely opposition; cross-country case studies of transitions in other selective systems.)
- How stable is the current institutional regime (state district centres, church-school autonomy, free choice, entrance exams) to major political or budgetary shocks, and would a reform attempt provoke a counter-reform or structural backlash? (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Historical analysis of past education-system reforms in Hungary (1989–present) and their reversals; stakeholder interviews on red-line conditions and willingness to accept reform; scenario modeling with political-economy experts on coalition dynamics.)

# Expert analysis: implementation_planning

## Findings (evidence)
- Hungary's teaching workforce is aging rapidly, with half of all teachers aged 50 or older and only 7.2% under 30 as of 2024/2025, creating a structural constraint on any reform requiring teacher redeployment or retraining. [evidence: strong — KSH / Education and Training Monitor 2025 (European Commission)]
- A multi-year EU-funded teacher salary reform (EUR 1.8 billion, 2024–2031) is mid-implementation and consumes administrative bandwidth in parallel with any potential selection-system redesign, narrowing the realistic window for structural change. [evidence: strong — European Social Fund Plus / Hungary salary reform timeline 2025]
- A newly launched teacher performance-evaluation bureaucracy was rolled back within one year (June 2026) due to excessive administrative burden, signalling that education administration is already at or near capacity for new requirements. [evidence: strong — Telex, June 9, 2026; education minister Lannert Judit announcement]
- Capacity in the 6- and 8-year grammar school tracks has remained flat (25,962 students in 2024/2025) for over a decade despite strong demand, making both expansion and phase-out scenarios administratively and logistically rigid in the short term. [evidence: strong — KSH / Eduline, February 18, 2026]
- Demand signals shifted in 2026: six-grade grammar school applications rose 11% while eight-grade fell 14%, and demographic decline is reducing the structural urgency of the sorting mechanism itself independent of policy choice. [evidence: strong — Eduline, May 15, 2026; Portfolio.hu, June 11, 2026]
- The current education minister, initially critical of the elite-selection function of the 6/8-year system, retreated to a consultative 'social dialogue' stance within days of political pushback, indicating a low political ceiling for near-term structural abolition. [evidence: strong — Telex, June 6, 2026; WMN, June 8, 2026; Portfolio.hu, June 11, 2026]
- Admissions to 6- and 8-year grammar schools are decentralized to individual schools, which set their own criteria; any reform centralizing or redesigning admissions faces a baseline of thousands of independent school-level procedures. [evidence: strong — Oktatási Hivatal / Eduline, May 15, 2026; Pénzcentrum, November 21, 2025]
- Private preparation costs for 6/8-year admissions average 34,000 forints monthly and 3.5 hours per week, evidence that any redesigned admissions system risks simply relocating the socio-economic sorting mechanism unless explicitly counteracted. [evidence: moderate — Berényi Eszter (2019) cited in Telex, June 8, 2026]
- Compulsory instruction time in Hungary is below OECD average (666 hours/year primary, 796 lower secondary vs. OECD 804/909), constraining any reform extending the common phase without proportional teacher-cost increases. [evidence: strong — OECD Education at a Glance 2025]

## Interpretation
Implementation feasibility for any structural reform of early selection is severely constrained by four overlapping binding realities: (1) administrative bandwidth is saturated by a simultaneous salary reform and rollback of a failed evaluation system; (2) the teaching workforce is aging faster than it is being replaced, making capacity-intensive alternatives unrealistic in the medium term; (3) demand itself is already weakening due to demographic decline, reducing the political pressure for urgent action; and (4) political limits are evident — even a sympathetic education minister retreated immediately to 'consultation' rather than legislative change. The 2019 admission data show that current selection sorts primarily by family investment capacity. Any redesign that claims to address equity or meritocracy would need to explicitly counteract private preparation advantages, which requires new administration at a moment when schools are already struggling with excess bureaucracy. The realistic timeline for substantial reform is post-2031 (after salary reform completion), and the most feasible near-term path is incremental revision of admissions criteria or procedural transparency, not structural abolition or major alternatives.

## Assumptions
- Any structural redesign (e.g., comprehensive lower-secondary, teacher-recommendation admission) would require retraining or redeploying significant numbers of teachers; the current aged workforce makes this capability assumption fragile. [assumption]
- Political feasibility is determined by the stated positions of governing parties and church maintainers; a government changing composition or priority could alter the timeline, but no current legislative proposal for abolition exists. [assumption]
- The 3.5-hour-per-week private preparation figure applies to current admissions; a redesigned system might shift preparation intensity rather than eliminate it, and measurement of this outcome is not yet planned. [assumption]

## Position
Structural reform of early selection is politically and administratively feasible only after 2031, when teacher-salary reform concludes and demographic decline has reduced demand pressure; near-term alternatives are limited to incremental admissions-process transparency and criterion revision, not redesign of the track system itself.

## Uncertainties
- What proportion of the private preparation investment (3.5 hrs/week, 34k HUF/month) reflects parental anxiety about selection-system uncertainty vs. genuine preparation for content demands? (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Qualitative interviews with parents of applicants and non-applicants, plus item analysis of current admissions exams to measure genuine difficulty vs. preparability; comparison with selection systems in peer countries (e.g., Netherlands, Slovakia) where preparation intensity is known.)
- How much of the current demand for 6/8-year tracks is driven by elite-positioning behavior vs. perceived superior pedagogy or outcomes; would administrative redesign (e.g., lottery, teacher recommendation) alone reduce applications, or would demand simply shift to private schools? (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Quasi-experimental analysis of school-choice behavior following admissions-criterion changes in other regions (e.g., track elimination pilots, if any exist); household survey on motivations for 6/8-year choice vs. other secondary options.)
- What is the pedagogical and social-composition difference between 6/8-year gymnasium entry and entry at age 15 with the same pupil cohort; would a delayed-selection alternative deliver comparable academic outcomes with lower segregation? (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Longitudinal follow-up comparing matched cohorts entering grammar school at age 10/12 vs. age 15 in similar-quality institutions, measuring both achievement trajectories and peer-group composition stability over time.)
- Is the administrative decentralization of 6/8-year admissions (school-by-school criteria) a feature that reduces corruption/favoritism or a source of inequality; what is the cost in administrative bandwidth of centralizing admissions vs. the cost of standardizing criteria across 2000+ schools? (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Audit study measuring variance in actual admissions criteria and outcomes across high-demand vs. low-demand grammar schools; cost accounting of a hypothetical centralized admissions bureau (staffing, IT, training); international comparison of centralized vs. decentralized admissions costs and fraud rates.)
- What is the true long-run fiscal cost of a phase-out scenario (capital reallocation, possible teacher redundancy, transition funding) vs. the steady-state cost of maintaining dual tracks; has either been formally modelled? (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Detailed multipart fiscal impact modelling (capital, staffing, transition support, retraining) for three scenarios: status quo, gradual phase-out (5–10 years), accelerated abolition; sensitivity analysis on teacher retirement/attrition rates and building repurposing costs.)


----- international_comparison -----
# Expert analysis: international_comparison

## Findings (evidence)
- Cross-country difference-in-differences studies consistently find that earlier tracking (before age 12–13) increases socio-economic achievement gaps without raising mean performance; meta-analysis of 21 PISA/TIMSS cycles across 75 countries confirms strong evidence of widened social gaps. [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006 (Economic Journal); van Elk et al. 2016 (RCIE); Educational Assessment, Evaluation and Accountability 2021 meta-analysis]
- Hungary's socio-economic achievement gap (19–25% of variance explained by ESCS) is among the OECD's highest, nearly double the average (12–14%); the gap in math between advantaged and disadvantaged students is cited as widest in the EU after Romania and Slovakia. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2022 Country Note Hungary; OECD PISA 2022 Vol. I (2023); ScienceDirect 2025 meta-comparison]
- Poland's 1999 reform delaying vocational tracking (from age 11 to 15) increased test scores by approximately one standard deviation for likely-vocational students, but OECD's 2011 re-evaluation found the underlying achievement gap persisted despite the reform, suggesting the gain was more from extended common instruction than from tracking-age delay alone. [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al. 2016 (Education Economics); OECD 2011 Poland reform evaluation]
- Poland reversed its 1999 tracking-delay reform in 2016, restoring earlier vocational selection despite research evidence of the reform's positive effects on test scores and labor-market outcomes—a direct example of research-supported reform being reversed for political reasons. [evidence: strong — PISA 2022 country reports; Liwiński 2020; Drucker et al. 2022 (cited in ResearchGate comparative)]
- Hungary's school segregation by socio-economic status is among the highest in the EU; free school choice (szabad iskolaválasztás), rather than residential segregation, is the primary driver of inter-school inequality in Hungarian towns. [evidence: strong — EU Education and Training Monitor 2025 Hungary; Kertesi & Kézdi 100-towns study; Hermann & Kisfalusi 2024 (forthcoming)]
- Horn's panel-data causal study finds that early selective tracks (kisgimnázium) provide higher value-added and boost tertiary-education continuation for higher-status students, but students remaining in general tracks lose relative to comprehensive alternatives; early selection increases inequality of opportunity. [evidence: moderate — Horn 2012 ('Early selection in Hungary: A possible cause of high educational inequality')]
- Access to 6/8-year grammar school admission examinations is unevenly distributed: elementary schools with disadvantaged-background pupils rarely offer exam-preparation support, and participation requires substantial family cultural, social, and financial capital investment. [evidence: moderate — Academia.edu 2018/2019 on_education-linked entry-process study]
- OECD explicitly identifies 'tracking students later' as a system feature associated with resilience and equity preservation in PISA 2022 analyses of high-performing, high-equity systems. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2022 Results Volume I (2023)]
- No Hungary-specific natural-experiment study (based on a policy change varying the legal age of entry into 6/8-year grammar school) has been found; the strongest Hungarian causal evidence is observational or matching-based, limiting direct within-country causal identification of tracking-age effects. [evidence: strong — Search audit (round 10, this memo)]
- Hungary's PISA results in mathematics and reading are among the lowest in the country's participation history (2022), and the top–bottom decile gap has not narrowed significantly from 2018–2022, indicating persistent within-cohort inequality. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2022 Country Note Hungary]
- The performance gap between children of graduates and non-graduates is 53 points in Hungary, compared to an EU average of 33 points, reflecting an intergenerational education-inequality marker beyond age-of-selection effects. [evidence: strong — EU Education and Training Monitor 2025, Hungary country report]
- Meta-analysis and natural-experiment evidence (Bavaria, Poland) show early tracking's negative effects are strongest for low-achieving students and students from disadvantaged backgrounds; high achievers show null or near-null effects, implying tracking preserves privilege rather than identifying talent. [evidence: strong — van Elk et al. 2016 (RCIE); Hanushek & Woessmann 2006; Piopiunik 2014 (Bavaria, cited in Springer 2021 meta-analysis)]

## Interpretation
Hungary combines the two strongest risk factors for education inequality: early selection at age 10–12 (via 8- and 6-year grammar schools) and a free school choice system that amplifies rather than mitigates socio-economic sorting. International evidence is unambiguous that early between-school tracking increases socio-economic gaps; Hungary's already-highest OECD inequality gradient (ESCS explains 19–25% of variance vs. 12–14% OECD average) and highest EU segregation suggest the current system is performing as the literature predicts. The mechanism is not mysterious: wealthier families (with higher cultural and financial capital) navigate admissions exams more effectively, and once selected into higher-status tracks, students face cumulative advantage (peer composition, teacher quality, school resources, tertiary access). The Poland reversal demonstrates that even policy-makers who observe positive evidence may retreat under political pressure. Hungary-specific causal studies (Horn; entry-process research) confirm the inequality-amplifying mechanism operates here. What is unknown is whether delaying selection from age 10–12 to age 14–15 would, in the Hungarian context, produce gains comparable to Poland's short-term test-score effects, or whether the underlying ESCS gradient would simply re-manifest at the later gatekeeping point—the OECD's 2011 Poland reassessment suggests the latter risk. The current system solves the problem of directing high-aptitude students into advanced instruction; it creates or amplifies the problem of frozen social mobility and high within-school peer-composition inequality; alternatives exist (later selection, comprehensive lower-secondary with ability-based curricula, targeted early intervention) but each carries fiscal, political, and implementation costs not yet fully quantified for Hungary.

## Assumptions
- That the international tracking-age evidence (Hanushek-Woessmann, van Elk, meta-analyses) is transferable to Hungary's institutional context; Hungary's specific grammar school admissions process, school-choice rules, and teacher-labor-market conditions may mediate or alter the typical tracking-inequality relationship. [assumption]
- That Hungary's high ESCS gradient (19–25% variance explained) persists across the entire age range, not just at the point of 10–12-year selection; if the gradient were concentrated only at entry to grammar school, delaying selection would have different effects than if it re-manifests at age 14–15. [assumption]
- That delaying selection without simultaneous measures to reduce residential/family-background segregation and to equalize teacher quality and resources across schools would not, by itself, eliminate the equality-of-opportunity problem; the Poland experience hints that extended common instruction may matter as much as the age shift. [assumption]
- That school-quality variation (value-added) within tracks is not so large as to offset selection effects; if low-track grammar schools deliver substantially higher value-added than general schools, the inequality harm from selection would be reduced. [assumption]
- That political feasibility constraints in Hungary are not so binding that evidence of inequality harm would motivate structural reform; Poland's reversal suggests that even positive evidence may not overcome political coalitions favoring early selection. [assumption]
- That the mechanisms of inequality (parental cultural/financial capital investment in admissions exam preparation, peer-composition effects, tracking-lock-in) are stable and not substantially mitigated by compensatory policies (gifted-and-talented programs, additional tutorial funding for disadvantaged students in grammar schools). [assumption]
- That no significant measurement error or attenuation bias exists in the observational/matching-based Hungarian causal studies (Horn, entry-process research); if unobserved confounders (e.g., unmeasured student motivation or family support) are large, the true selection effect may differ from reported estimates. [assumption]
- That Hungary's school-choice system and the legal framework for admissions exams will not change substantially during the implementation of any tracking-delay reform; if school choice is simultaneously restricted or exam rules are modified, the effects of age-of-selection change would be confounded. [assumption]
- That costs (fiscal, political, compliance) of alternative approaches (comprehensive lower-secondary plus ability-based in-school differentiation, later selection at 14–15, intensive early intervention to close parental-capital gaps) have been adequately researched in the Hungarian context; they may be substantially under- or over-estimated in the current policy debate. [assumption]
- That 'grammar school quality' (academic rigor, teacher credentials, resource endowment, tertiary placement) genuinely differs across the 6/8-year tracks and general schools at a scale that explains parental demand for selection; if quality differences are smaller than perceived, the selection mechanism would be partially decoupled from academic effectiveness. [assumption]

## Position
Early selection into 6/8-year grammar schools (at age 10–12) amplifies socio-economic achievement inequality in Hungary beyond the baseline set by family background, as indicated by international tracking-age evidence and Hungary-specific observational studies of segregation and selection mechanism, and delaying selection to age 14–15 (with intact comprehensive lower-secondary instruction and no weakening of school-choice amplification) would likely reduce this inequality measurably, though the size of the effect remains uncertain because no Hungarian natural experiment has tested the policy change.

## Uncertainties
- What is the causal effect of delaying selection from age 10–12 to age 14–15 specifically in Hungary's institutional context (school-choice system, school-maintainer structure, teacher labor market, tertiary admissions linkages)? (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A natural experiment (e.g., staggered implementation of a delayed-selection reform across regions or cohorts) combined with administrative data (matched student records, tertiary placement, labor-market earnings) to estimate within-Hungary difference-in-differences; absent this, credible cross-country comparison studies modeling the specific institutional features of the Hungarian system.)
- To what extent would the underlying socio-economic gradient in achievement re-manifest at the age 14–15 gatekeeping point (or later) if selection were delayed, such that delayed selection would not durably reduce inequality? (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Longitudinal data tracking student achievement, family characteristics, and school transitions across multiple age points (e.g., age 10, 14, 15, 16) to estimate whether ESCS-gradients are age-dependent or persistent; OECD's 2011 re-evaluation of Poland hints at this but does not resolve it for Hungary.)

# Expert analysis: legal_and_governance

## Findings (evidence)
- The 6- and 8-year gimnázium tracks are anchored in the Nemzeti köznevelési törvény (2011. évi CXC. törvény, Nkt.) and can be discontinued only through primary legislative amendment, with any phase-out constrained by the felmenő rendszer (ascending system) principle: no cohort already enrolled may be forced out, meaning a full discontinuation takes up to 8 years from legislative change. [evidence: strong — https://net.jogtar.hu/jogszabaly?docid=a1200020.emm; https://www.oktatas.hu/kozneveles/kozepfoku_felveteli_eljaras/]

[evidence: moderate — https://njt.hu/jogszabaly/1994-11-20-67; http://nahalkaistvan.blogspot.com/2010/07/gimnaziumi-felvetelik-indulat-nelkul.html]
- As of June 2026, minister Lannert Judit stated that the mid-level admissions system tied to 6/8-year tracks produces significant social selection and merits societal consultation ('társadalmi egyeztetés'), while explicitly denying a proposal for abolition. She attributed segregation concerns to the selection system itself, not to the track existence. [evidence: strong — https://eduline.hu/kozoktatas/20260608_lannert-judit-gulyas-gergely-vita-gimnaziumok-felveteli-rendszer; https://magyarnemzet.hu/belfold/2026/06/hat-es-nyolcosztalyos-gimnaziumok-reakcio-lannert-judit-vita]

# Expert analysis: polish_reform

## Findings (evidence)
- Poland's 1999 reform delaying tracking by one year (from age 11-12 to age 15) produced large, causally-identified gains: 0.25 standard deviations in mathematics, 0.28 in reading, 0.16 in science; vocational-track students gained approximately one standard deviation. [evidence: strong — Jakubowski, Patrinos, Porta & Wiśniewski (2016), Education Economics; https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/09645292.2016.1149548]
- Decomposition of Poland's reform gains shows two-thirds of the test-score improvement came from increased resources and improved peer composition, while one-third was attributable to the delayed tracking effect itself. [evidence: strong — Jakubowski et al. (2016)]
- Labour-market follow-up (Drucker, Horn & Jakubowski 2022) confirms that low-track students benefited substantially from delayed tracking through improved peer composition and teacher quality, and the reform measurably decreased overall educational inequalities. [evidence: strong — Drucker, Horn & Jakubowski (2022), SpringerOpen; https://labourmarketresearch.springeropen.com/articles/10.1186/s12651-022-00317-4]
- Poland's 2017 reversal of the 1999 reform (abolishing gimnazjum, reinstating 8-year primary + tracked secondary) was explicitly driven by political values and public opinion, not by evidence-based policy analysis. [evidence: strong — Springer chapters: https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-41882-3_7 and https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-59031-4_7]
- Implementation of Poland's 2017 reversal created severe transition costs: a double cohort created excess demand for secondary places (e.g. 3,173 unplaced students in Warsaw alone), and 6,500 teachers were made redundant with another 18,500 facing reduced hours. [evidence: strong — Medium article https://medium.com/@thelambert/education-in-poland-was-dismantling-of-gimnazjum-necessary-8adc9f2e48a8 ; Notes from Poland https://notesfrompoland.com/2020/10/08/the-inequality-epidemic-in-polands-schools/]
- Poland's PISA 2022 results show an unusually large decline: twice the OECD average decline in reading and mathematics, three times the OECD decline in science; all gains from 2003–2012 were reversed by 2022. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2022 Poland country note; https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/pisa-2022-results-volume-i-and-ii-country-notes_ed6fbcc5-en/poland_4a2c28c8-en.html]
- Post-2017 reversal, the share of Polish students in schools reporting staff shortage rose from 3% (2018) to 47% (2022); inadequate/poorly qualified staff rose from 1% to 23%. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2022 Poland country note; https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2023/11/pisa-2022-results-volume-i-and-ii-country-notes_2fca04b9/poland_17b5bcfd/4a2c28c8-en.pdf]
- Parental engagement with schools in Poland fell sharply post-reform: the share of students in schools where at least half of families discussed their child's progress with teachers fell from 49% (2018) to 23% (2022). [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2022 Poland country note; https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/pisa-2022-results-volume-i-and-ii-country-notes_ed6fbcc5-en/poland_4a2c28c8-en.html]
- Hungary's current early-selection system (age 10-12 into 6- and 8-year gimnázium) is documented in Hungarian-language research and World Bank analysis as showing large socioeconomic-background-driven inequalities, with tracking cited as a structural driver of equity loss. [evidence: moderate — OECD Education Policy Outlook Hungary; https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/about/projects/edu/education-policy-outlook/379201-Hungary-Profile.pdf; World Bank PISA Hungary analysis]
- The Polish reform example was explicitly referenced in Hungarian policy discourse via Horn & Drucker (2016) published in Közgazdasági Szemle and in World Bank advisory material to Hungary, indicating direct transferability of the Polish case to Hungarian early-selection debates. [evidence: moderate — Horn & Drucker (2016), Közgazdasági Szemle 0(9), 944-965; https://ideas.repec.org/a/taf/edecon/v24y2016i6p557-572.html; World Bank PISA Hungary document]

## Interpretation
Poland's experience offers a rare natural experiment on early selection's causal effects and political durability. The 1999 reform—delaying selection from age 11-12 to 15—produced large, documented gains in learning outcomes (especially for low-track students), labour-market success, and measured equity. These gains persisted for 18 years despite their visibility. Yet in 2017, a change of government reversed the reform on explicitly non-evidence grounds, citing public opinion and values rather than empirical findings. The reversal imposed immediate transition costs (job losses, unplaced students) and correlates with a sharp subsequent decline in learning outcomes (PISA 2022), staff shortages, and parental disengagement—trends that post-hoc observers plausibly but not definitively link to the structural reversal (COVID-19 presents an alternative explanation). The lesson is twofold: (1) delayed selection causally improves equity and learning, but (2) evidence of success does not ensure political durability if a new majority prioritizes different values (e.g. traditional school structure, parental choice, or decentralization). For Hungary, the Polish case is directly relevant because Hungary's current 8- and 6-year gymnázium system (entry at age 10-12) mirrors Poland's pre-1999 structure and produces comparable equity costs. Hungary's choice is not whether early selection *works*—Poland answered that in 1999-2012—but whether and how the political and implementation risks of change can be managed.

## Assumptions
- The PISA 2022 score decline is substantially attributable to the structural reforms (rather than solely to COVID-19 school closures or other concurrent shocks); the evidence base distinguishes these causes incompletely. [assumption]
- Labour-market and peer-composition improvements in Poland after 1999 were primarily caused by delay of selection rather than by concurrent increases in education spending unrelated to the reform structure. [assumption]
- Hungary's 8/6-year gymnázium system (entry at age 10-12) creates equity effects structurally similar to Poland's pre-1999 system; no major institutional or regional differences would prevent transfer of insights. [assumption]

## Position
Early selection at age 10-12 into the 8- and 6-year gymnázium creates measurable equity loss and competitiveness costs (documented in Hungary's PISA profile and mirrored in Poland's pre-1999 experience); delaying selection to age 15 increases both learning and equity outcomes, though political reversal remains a durable risk.

## Uncertainties
- What share of Poland's PISA 2022 decline is causally attributable to the 2017 structural reversal, versus COVID-19 school closures and pandemic learning loss, versus other factors (teacher stress, demographic change, curriculum implementation)? Current evidence is correlational, not causal. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A quasi-experimental analysis (e.g. difference-in-differences with a control country or regional variation in reversal timing) or a fine-grained decomposition of PISA 2022 loss by school cohort and reform-exposure timing; longitudinal teacher and student surveys on implementation stress and system disruption during the transition.)

# Expert analysis: portuguese_reform

## Findings (evidence)
- Early tracking in international cross-country studies (26-country difference-in-differences) increases educational inequality measured by socioeconomic achievement gaps, with unclear or slightly negative effects on system-wide mean performance. [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Wößmann 2006 NBER/Economic Journal; replicated in 2021 meta-analysis (Lange et al., Springer) and 2024 review (de Gruyter Brill)]
- OECD cross-national analysis confirms that age of first selection correlates with inequity in performance but not with mean performance level across systems. [evidence: strong — OECD 2013, 2020a education indicators; confirmed in 2023 ScienceDirect policy review]
- Socioeconomic achievement gaps already exist at age 10 before formal academic selection, with approximately two-thirds of the age-15 gap and over half of the age-25–29 gap already visible at age 10 across 11 OECD countries. [evidence: strong — OECD Equity in Education 2013]
- Hungary has the highest level of socioeconomic segregation of students into different schools in the EU, with a 53-point parental-education achievement gap compared to the EU average of 33 points, as of 2022 PISA. [evidence: strong — EU Education and Training Monitor 2022 country report for Hungary]
- Hungarian administrative-data causal analysis (matching design) shows that attending a high-poverty school in grade 8 is significantly negatively associated with reading scores and later secondary-education attainment, suggesting school composition operates as a causal mechanism, not merely correlation. [evidence: strong — Hermann & Kisfalusi 2023, HUN-REN administrative-data study]
- Hungary's gimnázium track (general secondary school) achieves world-class performance equivalent to top PISA systems such as Switzerland, Japan, or Korea, coexisting with system-wide segregation and inequality. [evidence: moderate — World Bank PISA analysis of Hungary; publicly available but non-peer-reviewed comparative assessment]
- Portugal abolished academic selection at age 10–12 and adopted comprehensive basic education to grade 9, but substituted grade retention as its sorting mechanism, with 26.6–31.2% of 15-year-olds having been retained at least once by 2015–2018. [evidence: strong — Portugal PISA 2015, 2018; cited in EPAA meta-analysis]
- Meta-analyses of grade retention show null or slightly negative effects on academic competencies, indicating that retention-based sorting is not pedagogically superior to academic-track selection. [evidence: strong — EPAA systematic review, meta-analytic synthesis]
- Portugal's TEIP targeted-support program (15+ years, ~€100 million annually) shows mixed results: early school-leaving rates fell faster in TEIP schools than non-TEIP public schools 2007–2011, but achievement gaps in mathematics and Portuguese widened, and TEIP schools remain above national averages for retention and dropout. [evidence: moderate — MDPI 15-year evaluation 2020; Frontiers in Education follow-up 2026 using DGEEC administrative data]
- Portugal's socioeconomic equity in education worsened from 2015 to 2022 despite its non-selective comprehensive-school structure, with the share of low-SES students performing well falling from 21.0% (2015) to 16.0% (2022), and mathematics underachievement rising from 39.4% to 46.9%. [evidence: strong — EU Education and Training Monitor 2025 country report for Portugal]
- Hungary's free school-choice system (szabad iskolaválasztás) permits schools complete discretion to hand-pick their students in primary education, creating segregation independent of formal academic tracking. [evidence: moderate — Academia.edu historical analysis of Hungarian school-choice system; structural feature confirmed but older documentation]
- Portugal's 2006–2015 PISA improvement (within-country trend without lowering selection age) occurred via curriculum standards alignment, school clustering reform, TEIP targeted support, and retention-rate reduction. [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA trend reports; Crato 2020 policy analysis]
- Early academic selection at age 10–12 and school-choice segregation are structurally separable policy levers: the former is a sorting mechanism by explicit track assignment, the latter operates via admission discretion and catchment-area choice independent of tracking's existence. [evidence: moderate — Synthesis from Hanushek & Wößmann 2006 and Hermann & Kisfalusi 2023; cross-referenced with Hungary school-choice literature]
- Removing early academic tracking without simultaneously addressing retention rates, school-choice discretion, or compensatory-resource distribution may relocate rather than resolve the sorting mechanism. [evidence: moderate — Inference from Portugal case (comprehensive model + high retention = shifted sorting) and Hungarian school-choice architecture]
- International evidence does not distinguish whether early-selection harm operates via earlier age of assignment, via the *existence* of permanent tracks, or via *segregation mechanisms* (admissions discretion, catchment), making optimal alternative designs empirically underdetermined. [evidence: contested — Synthesis of Hanushek & Wößmann; OECD-indicator studies; acknowledged in methodological critiques of tracking literature]

## Interpretation
The evidence converges that early selection (at age 10–12 into 6/8-year gimnázium tracks) increases inequality measured by socioeconomic achievement gaps without raising or raising mean performance. Hungary exemplifies the high-inequality, high-elite-performance tail of this distribution, with the EU's strongest segregation and largest parental-education gap. This is not primarily because selection *creates* the gaps—two-thirds of the age-15 achievement gap and over half of the later-life gap are already visible at age 10, suggesting that sorting at 10–12 locks in pre-existing disparities. However, Portugal's alternative (comprehensive basic school + no age-10 selection) is not a template for painless equity: it substitutes retention-based sorting (equally harmful or harmful-plus) and has *worsened* its own equity trends since 2015 despite the non-selective structure. This implies that the choice between early-academic-track selection and early retention/school-choice segregation is not a choice between equity and inequality, but between *different sorting mechanisms with different trade-offs*. The strongest independent lever identified is not the track's age or existence, but the admissions-discretion and school-choice architecture that allows hand-picking and creates de facto segregation parallel to formal tracking. Portugal's growth in PISA without changing selection age (2006–2015) via curriculum alignment, school clustering, TEIP support, and retention reduction suggests that *how schools are resourced and organized*, not *whether early tracking exists*, may be the primary controllable determinant of both equity and efficacy.

## Assumptions
- The research synthesized (primarily North American and Western European quasi-experimental designs) is generalizable to the Hungarian institutional context (Klebelsberg centre system, school-maintainer mix, selective admissions architecture). [assumption]
- "Inequality" measured by socioeconomic achievement gaps at standardized-test assessment (PISA, national exams) is a sufficient proxy for the broader concept of equity in educational outcomes and life chances. [assumption]
- The absence of documented causal evidence for a claim (e.g., whether abolishing early selection *alone* improves equity without structural changes elsewhere) does not imply the claim is false; it implies it is empirically undetermined and therefore subject to expert disagreement. [assumption]
- School-composition effects observed in administrative-data studies (Hermann & Kisfalusi) are not entirely mediated by selection; some represent true peer or resource-allocation causal effects that would persist even under a comprehensive-school regime. [assumption]
- The timing (2006–2015 for Portugal, 2022 for Hungary PISA) and geopolitical differences (post-crisis EU austerity pressures, COVID-19 impacts not fully captured) do not fundamentally alter the comparability of Portugal's and Hungary's structural choice set. [assumption]

## Position
Early selection at age 10–12 into grammar-school tracks increases socioeconomic inequality without raising system-wide mean performance in Hungary's context, but removing it without simultaneously reforming school-choice admissions discretion, retention rates, and within-sector resource allocation would likely relocate rather than eliminate the sorting mechanism.

## Uncertainties
- Whether the harm from early selection operates primarily via (a) the age at which assignment occurs, (b) the permanence of track assignment, or (c) the segregation mechanisms (admissions discretion, catchment-area choice) that coexist with or without formal selection, remains empirically undetermined and is not resolved by available international evidence. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Quasi-experimental designs or natural experiments isolating the effect of selection-age change (e.g., a regional or cohort-level phase-out of age-10 entry with selection-mechanism controls held constant) or randomized assignment of school-choice discretion vs. centralized-admissions regimes in matched samples.)

Hungary's 6/8-year grammar school network operates as a high-demand, capacity-constrained system superimposed on a shrinking overall student population. The combination of birth-rate collapse (20% in four years), rigid capacity (no expansion despite tenfold oversubscription at elite schools), and administrative consolidation driven by fiscal stress creates a three-layer challenge: (1) structural – the network must shrink overall while early selection maintains bimodal class sizes in feeder primaries; (2) equity – free school choice amplifies socioeconomic sorting, and geography now stratifies access (urban capacity shortage, rural disadvantage concentration); (3) political – closures proceed silently via tankerület decisions rather than through debate about selection's rationale. The rising difficulty of entrance examinations and high performance gaps between selected and non-selected students indicate the system is operating at near-maximum selectivity for a frozen pool of places. Any alternative to early selection must be designed within a demographic-contraction context, not a growth scenario.

## Assumptions
- The 2024/25 network size and enrolment figures are stable proxies for current effective network capacity (i.e., no uncount unoficial private expansion and no shadow market in admissions). [assumption]
- The performance gap measured in the 2024 competency assessment reflects both selection (compositional effect) and school quality; without causal separation, the demand pressure driven by it conflates the two sources. [assumption]
- The administrative consolidation proceeding via tankerület decisions will continue at roughly current pace regardless of selection policy, driven by demographic collapse and municipal fiscal capacity. [assumption]
- The 'under one hour public transport' accessibility standard for grammar schools remains a design goal but is not systematically monitored or enforced as a binding constraint in network-planning decisions. [assumption]

## Position
Under demographic contraction, the 6/8-year grammar school network's capacity constraint is not primarily a selection-policy problem but a network-consolidation problem that selection policy amplifies by concentrating demand at elite schools and leaving shrinking feeder primaries economically unviable.

## Uncertainties
- No recent (2024–2026) quantitative data on actual commuting times and distances for 6/8-year grammar school entrants, versus the stated 'under one hour' accessibility policy aim, limits verification of how network consolidation would affect geographic equity. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Central tracking of commuting patterns via school transport cards (bérlet) or mobile-phone anonymized location data for the 6/8 cohort; comparison across settlement types (urban, suburban, rural) and socioeconomic quartiles.)
- Cost-per-pupil and lifecycle cost data comparing 6/8-year grammar tracks to 4-year grammar school and vocational school alternatives in the same network, adjusted for intake quality and regional context, are not publicly available in comparable form. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Transparent cost accounting per maintainer type (state, church, foundation) for each track over a 3-year rolling window, published by the Klebelsberg centres, enabling scenario-based fiscal impact modelling.)
- No recent (post-2020) quantitative study updates the causal selection-effect estimates from Kertesi–Kézdi (2004–2015) for current conditions, leaving unclear whether the performance gaps reflect mostly selection or early instruction quality differences. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: A quasi-experimental or matched-cohort study comparing value-added trajectories of students admitted vs. not admitted to 6/8 tracks, controlling for prior test scores and background, using 2018–2025 administrative data (if available under GDPR); sensitivity check on exam-difficulty calibration.)
- The political feasibility and implementation cost of any alternative to early selection (e.g., phased phase-out, comprehensive lower-secondary model, later-entry grammar school) depend on unstated negotiation positions of maintainers (churches, foundations, state sector unions), which are not documented in accessible form. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Structured stakeholder interviews (maintainers, teacher unions, parent organisations, municipal leadership) on explicit 'red lines' and cost-sharing model preferences in three selection-policy scenarios; publication of preliminary positions.)
- The long-term student demand for 6/8-year grammar places under continued demographic shrinkage (and whether 'prestige' demand will hold or collapse as cohort size halves) is unknowable without assumptions about parental value-weighting of academic prestige vs. proximity. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Cohort-tracing survey (2025–2030) of applicant and non-applicant families in shrinking and growing municipalities to measure stated preferences for grammar-school access vs. local proximity and community stability; sensitivity analysis of demand elasticity to seat reductions.)

# Expert analysis: educational_psychology

## Findings (evidence)
- Early academic selection in Hungary (grades 5–7, via 6- and 8-year gimnáziums) covers approximately 85% of students through schools with selection power, placing Hungary among the earliest trackers in the OECD. [evidence: strong — OECD Education Policy Outlook: Hungary (2023)]
- Hungary exhibits the largest socio-economic performance segregation in the EU: the socioeconomic achievement gap is 53 PISA points (vs. EU average 33), and 62% of Roma children attend schools where the majority or all pupils are Roma. [evidence: strong — European Commission, Education and Training Monitor 2025, Hungary country report]
- Between-school achievement variance sharply increases after grade 8 (the second major selection point); variance ratios rise from ~5 at primary start to ~25 by secondary end, driven by selection. [evidence: strong — Csapó, Molnár et al., IEA 2008; Hungarian National Assessment data]
- Students in 8-year gimnáziums achieve 0.10–0.25 SD higher scores than comparable peers in other school types, even controlling for socioeconomic status, yet represent only 2–3% of the cohort. [evidence: strong — Horn Dániel, TÁRKI; 'A kisgimnáziumok szerepe a szelekcióban']
- Hungarian 6- and 8-year gimnázium students show approximately 10% larger learning gains than matched peers, yet peer composition and cream-skimming effects (not necessarily pedagogy) appear to drive this premium. [evidence: moderate — Horn Dániel (2026); Telex/G7, Eduline reporting]
- When high-ability students leave ordinary schools via selective gimnázium admission, the average performance of those remaining in the source school decreases measurably. [evidence: moderate — Horn Dániel (2026) research; Telex/G7, Eduline reporting]
- International meta-analytic evidence (Hanushek & Wößmann 2006, 2021 replication across 75 countries) finds that early tracking increases achievement inequality without offsetting gains in mean performance. [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Wößmann, NBER Working Paper 11124 (2006); Springer replication (2021)]
- However, a Polish reanalysis (Jakubowski 2010) of the same TIMSS/PISA datasets found no significant tracking effect, illustrating that evidence on tracking's aggregate effects remains inconclusive across different analytical choices. [evidence: contested — Jakubowski 2010; Hanushek & Wößmann evidence review]
- Poland's 1999 reform delaying comprehensive schooling by one year produced large test-score gains (0.25–0.28 SD), especially for lower-ability students, but Hungary's economists (Horn & Drucker) have noted the reform was later partially reversed. [evidence: moderate — Jakubowski et al., Education Economics 2016; EERA conference paper (Horn & Drucker)]
- The big-fish-little-pond effect (BFLPE) — equally able pupils develop lower academic self-concept in selective schools — is one of the most robustly replicated findings in educational psychology, confirmed across 41 countries and persisting years after graduation. [evidence: strong — Marsh & Hau (2003); Seaton, Marsh & Craven (2009); Marsh et al. (2007); Fang et al. meta-analysis (2018)]
- Teacher-expectancy effects (Rosenthal & Jacobson, 'Pygmalion') exist but are contested in magnitude: later meta-analyses find much smaller average effects than the original claims, yet systematically larger effects for stigmatized and low-SES pupils — the exact groups that early selection labels. [evidence: contested — Rosenthal & Jacobson (1968); Shewach, Sackett & Quigley (2019) meta-analysis]
- Stereotype threat (Steele & Aronson 1995) — performance drops when a negative group stereotype is salient — shows publication-bias-inflated effects in earlier literature; meta-analyses (Flore & Wicherts 2015, Shewach et al. 2019) find smaller, more fragile effects in high-stakes testing contexts. [evidence: contested — Flore & Wicherts (2015); Shewach, Sackett & Quigley (2019) meta-analysis]
- Classroom and system structures that emphasize ranking and selection push pupils toward performance goal orientation (fear of failure, shallow strategies) rather than mastery orientation (persistence); adolescence is the sensitive period for this effect. [evidence: moderate — Dweck & Elliot goal-orientation literature; Marsh (1987+)]
- Self-determination theory (Deci & Ryan 1985, 2000) — intrinsic motivation is supported by autonomy, competence and relatedness — is robustly cross-culturally replicated and provides the standard framework for how school structures sustain or undermine pupil motivation. [evidence: strong — Deci & Ryan (1985, 2000); Zimmerman (2002)]
- Growth mindset interventions (Dweck 2006) show only very small, practically negligible average effects in large preregistered replications and meta-analysis (Sisk et al. 2018), with poor replication of original small-sample studies. [evidence: contested — Sisk et al. (2018) meta-analysis; Psychological Science]
- Hungary's 6- and 8-year gimnázium system was introduced in the 1990s during deregulation without deliberate systemic-impact analysis; early warnings from researchers about selectivity risks were politically minimized due to small enrollment share at the time. [evidence: moderate — Berényi sociological account (Qubit 2022); Csapó et al. retrospective analysis]
- Hungary transitioned from a 1985–1993 comprehensive 8+4 system with catchment areas to a deregulated system with free school choice and early selection; this shift was deliberate policy, not accidental. [evidence: moderate — 'Early tracking and competition – A recipe for major inequalities in Hungary' (Academia.edu); Hungarian policy history]

## Interpretation
Early selection at ages 10–12 in Hungary operates within a deregulated school-choice system that has produced the highest socio-economic and ethnic segregation in the EU. The evidence shows three distinct mechanism layers: (1) selection effects — the top 2–3% removed to selective schools measurably boosts their peer-group and depresses performance among those left behind; (2) self-concept effects — the big-fish-little-pond mechanism is robustly replicated and creates lower academic self-regard in selective cohorts, with documented persistence into adulthood; (3) motivation/identity effects — competitive ranking systems push adolescents toward performance avoidance rather than mastery, and early labeling interacts with stereotype threat and teacher-expectancy effects, especially for stigmatized pupils. International causal evidence (Poland's 1999 delayed-tracking reform, Hanushek & Wößmann's cross-country panel data) suggests that delayed tracking reduces inequality without sacrificing mean achievement, though one reanalysis challenges this. The system's 10% learning-gain premium for selective-school students appears driven primarily by composition effects and cream-skimming, not distinctive pedagogy. The trade-off is neither inevitable (as some defenders claim) nor fully settled (as evidence gaps remain), but the current evidence weight points toward inequality amplification without aggregate gain.

## Assumptions
- That school choice, in the absence of explicit equity safeguards or strong voucher regulation, permits high-SES families to secure selective-school places for their children at higher rates, amplifying composition effects. [assumption]
- That the 10% learning-gain premium found in recent research reflects measurable pedagogy quality differences or is primarily a composition premium that would disappear if peer groups were randomized. [assumption]
- That teacher-expectancy and stereotype-threat effects, which are contested in magnitude across populations, apply to Hungarian selective-school contexts with sufficient force to measurably dampen motivation among non-selected pupils. [assumption]
- That the big-fish-little-pond effect's documented international persistence holds true in the Hungarian secondary context and extends to educational aspirations and subsequent career choices. [assumption]

## Position
Early academic selection at ages 10–12 in Hungary's current deregulated system measurably amplifies socio-economic and ethnic achievement segregation and motivation gaps without a corresponding aggregate learning gain, and evidence-based alternatives (delayed selection with universal lower-secondary coverage, coupled with school-funding reform and teacher-support mechanisms) appear feasible and likely to reduce inequality without sacrificing mean performance.

## Uncertainties
- Whether the measured 10% learning-gain premium for 6/8-year gimnázium students is driven primarily by selection/composition effects (as Horn's 2026 analysis suggests) or reflects genuinely higher pedagogical quality that could be generalized to all schools. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Randomized controlled trials or quasi-experimental designs that isolate pedagogy from peer composition (e.g., comparing matched student cohorts in selective vs. nonselective schools controlling for peer-group average achievement); analysis of value-added (school effect net of intake) by school type.)
- How large and persistent are teacher-expectancy and stereotype-threat effects in Hungarian secondary selective contexts specifically — existing meta-analyses are mostly from North American samples, and generalization to Hungarian adolescents and the Hungarian institutional context remains uncertain. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Hungarian longitudinal studies tracking labeled vs. unlabeled students' motivation, goal orientation, and self-concept trajectories from selection point through secondary completion; direct measures of classroom discourse and teacher feedback in selective vs. nonselective schools.)
- Whether delayed-tracking reforms (like Poland's 1999) would produce comparable learning and equity gains in Hungary, or whether institutional, labor-market, or cultural differences make Hungary's case structurally distinct. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Targeted retrospective analysis of Hungary's own policy experiments (e.g., municipalities that delayed selection or eased selection criteria in the 2000s) if data exist; expert interviews with Hungarian educators and policymakers who lived through partial detracking attempts; economic analysis of labor-market returns to secondary school type in Hungary before and after reforms.)
