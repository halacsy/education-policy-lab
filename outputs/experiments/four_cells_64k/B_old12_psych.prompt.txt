# Expert analysis: demography

## Findings (evidence)
- Annual births in Hungary fell from approximately 125,000 in the early 1990s to 77,500 in 2024, with a further 7.1% decline to ~72,000 in 2025, representing the lowest recorded level since 1949 and forcing school-network consolidation decisions in the 2020s–2030s regardless of selection policy. [evidence: strong — KSH demographic yearbooks; hvg.hu (31 Jan 2025); economx.hu (9 May 2026); nepszava.hu (30 Jan 2026)]
- Primary general school (általános iskola) enrollment declined 17.3% over 20 years, from 859,000 pupils in 2005/2006 to 710,000 in 2024/2025, tracking the shrinking birth cohorts directly, with first-grade intake falling 1.9% year-on-year in 2024/2025 to 95,000. [evidence: strong — Népszava (2025), citing KSH data]
- A demographic cliff is measurable one cohort ahead: Hungary currently has approximately 17,000 fewer one-year-olds than four-year-olds, equivalent to roughly 600–800 kindergarten/class-groups, meaning at least one hundred small-school's worth of children will vanish from the system within a few years. [evidence: moderate — Válasz Online (17 Oct 2025)]
- Government policy is using selection-track admission mechanics (minimum-score thresholds set by the Klebelsberg Center maintainer) to shrink the gimnázium track's share of cohort intake from approximately 40% toward 30% or lower, independent of demand; gimnázium enrollment share was flat-to-slightly-rising before this intervention. [evidence: moderate — Szülői Hang (15 Jan 2025); Index (17 Jan 2025)]
- Minimum-score admission policy for 6- and 8-year grammar schools remains a school-maintainer-level discretionary decision with no national legal basis, creating inconsistent two-tier admissions: some institutions (e.g., Toldy Ferenc Gimnázium, Budai Nagy Antal Gimnázium) enforced 70-point cutoffs in 2025; others initially treated them as non-binding recommendations then retroactively rejected borderline students. [evidence: moderate — 168.hu (9 Sep 2025); eduline.hu (5 Sep 2025); Educational Authority (Oktatási Hivatal) confirmation]
- In 2024/2025 approximately 194,000 students attended gimnázium nationally (45.4% of upper-secondary students); 6- and 8-year tracks serve a numerically small but structurally significant slice of each cohort, geographically concentrated in urban areas, notably Budapest. [evidence: strong — KSH 2024/2025 preliminary data; nool.hu (21 May 2026)]
- Standardized test-score evidence shows a composition gap at selection point: 6th-graders competing for 6-year gimnázium entry averaged 58.6 points (out of 200), 8th-graders competing for 9th-grade entry averaged 51.2 points, with 47.8% of 8th-graders scoring below 50 versus only 23.15% of 6th-graders scoring above 70, indicating two demographically and academically distinct competing populations. [evidence: moderate — Pénzcentrum (20 Jan 2025)]
- Long-run population projections (KSH/HDRI SEEMIG medium-variant) show continuous population decline through at least 2060 under all fertility and migration scenarios, with the under-20 population declining from ~2 million (2012) to approximately 1.4 million by 2060, meaning the school-age cohort available for all secondary tracks shrinks structurally through 2040 and beyond. [evidence: strong — KSH/HDRI SEEMIG projection; demografia.hu (2018)]
- Cross-border analogue (Beregszász, Ukraine's Hungarian-minority region): Ukraine's Ministry of Education mandated from September 2025 that state subsidy no longer covers grammar-school operation below 45 students, forcing local consolidation and busing of older students to larger hub schools, illustrating a hard enrollment floor's effect on small-town selective-track viability under demographic decline. [evidence: moderate — karpataljalap.net (25 June 2026)]
- Total fertility rate in Hungary fell from 1.51 (2023) to 1.38 (2024) to 1.31 (2025); natural population decrease exceeded 52,000 in 2025; population fell to 9,489,000 by 1 January 2026, all representing accelerating decline, not stabilization. [evidence: strong — hvg.hu (31 Jan 2025); economx.hu (9 May 2026); nepszava.hu (30 Jan 2026)]

## Interpretation
Hungary's school-age population is shrinking at an accelerating pace driven by historic low fertility and natural decrease. The annual flow through primary school has fallen 17% over two decades and continues to decline. This forces school-network consolidation regardless of whether early selection (6/8-year grammar schools) exists or not. However, government policy is simultaneously using minimum-score admission thresholds as a supply-side lever to further compress the grammar-school track's share of the remaining cohort from ~40% toward 30%, even though demand for grammar education has remained stable or risen. The impact is twofold: (1) structural — the absolute pool of students shrinks, making small schools and dispersed selective tracks unsustainable; (2) policy-driven — the share available to grammar tracks is deliberately reduced independent of demand, creating a pincer effect. Selection at age 10–12 remains numerically small (6- and 8-year tracks serve fewer students than 9th-grade entry) but carries outsized symbolic and distributional weight, concentrating opportunities in urban and already-advantaged families. The evidence shows composition differences at selection point (higher-performing 6th-graders competing for early entry) but does not settle whether selection causes the gap or merely sorts pre-existing advantage. Cross-border and within-country evidence both confirm that demographic thresholds (45-student viability floors) force closure of small or dispersed schools; the same logic applies to selective tracks.

## Assumptions
- Cohort-size projections (declining under-20 population) are stable across the planning horizon (2025–2040) and do not assume major immigration reversing natural decrease. [assumption]
- School-maintainer sustainability thresholds (minimum 45 students, reference floors for class viability) are a binding constraint; schools below such floors cannot operate financially or pedagogically. [assumption]
- Minimum-score admission policies as applied by maintainers represent government intent to shift supply; they are not merely institutional quality signals. [assumption]
- The 'completion' of demographic decline (population stabilization) will not occur within the planning horizon (2040); the trend is structurally downward. [assumption]
- Test-score differences at selection point reflect both composition (family background, prep investment) and possibly school-level intake quality, but not causal school effect; the direction of selection bias is unknown. [assumption]

## Position
Demographic decline through 2040 will force school-network consolidation and closure of small selective tracks independent of policy choice, but government's simultaneous use of score-threshold levers is accelerating selective-track contraction beyond what demographic decline alone would cause, concentrating remaining grammar-school slots among already-advantaged urban populations.

## Uncertainties
- The net causal effect of early selection (6/8-year grammar-school entry at age 10–12) on individual student achievement and social mobility is not directly measured in Hungarian evidence; test-score gaps at selection reflect composition, not school value-added. # Expert analysis: finnish_reform

## Findings (evidence)

## Interpretation
Finland's abolition of early selection at age 11 and delay of tracking to age 16 produced measurable equity gains—primarily benefiting disadvantaged pupils' cognitive development and intergenerational mobility—alongside gendered and lifecycle trade-offs. However, these gains proved vulnerable to school choice and residential sorting mechanisms that create within-school inequality without formal tracking. The reform's effects were inseparable from parallel investment in university-based teacher education and a broader welfare-support system. Implementation was gradual, regionally sequenced, and faced political resistance in urban areas. For Hungary, the Finnish case demonstrates both that early selection can be reformed and that de-tracking alone is insufficient: structural change must be bundled with teacher-education upgrade and protected against substitution mechanisms (school choice, streaming, catchment-area sorting).

[assumption]

## Position
Delaying academic selection beyond age 12 in Hungary and extending common schooling to age 16, bundled with university-based teacher-education reform and protected against school-choice-driven substitution of formal tracking with within-school segregation, would be expected to reduce achievement gaps and raise intergenerational mobility, but only if the full policy package is implemented coherently.

(confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Natural experiments or quasi-experimental designs in other countries that de-tracked without simultaneous welfare expansion or teacher-education reform, or conversely, countries that made those investments without de-tracking.)

## Interpretation
Hungary's early selection system at age 10–12 into 6- and 8-year grammar schools (affecting ~8–12% of each cohort) has become a major driver of Europe's sharpest increase in school-system social selectivity since 2018. Early selection at age 10–12 into parallel 6- and 8-year grammar schools is the primary structural cause of Hungary's current position as Europe's most socially selective education system and should be phased out by 2035 in favor of single-entry-age (14 or 15) and unified secondary-school admission to reduce inequality of opportunity without sacrificing demonstrable value-added in pedagogy.

# Expert analysis: international_comparison

## Findings (evidence)
- Difference-in-differences evidence from 75 countries/regions over 20 years shows early tracking increases test-score dispersion across PISA, PIRLS, and TIMSS cycles, though effect sizes vary substantially by study pair. [evidence: strong — Educational Assessment, Evaluation and Accountability (2021); https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11092-020-09346-4]
- Early-tracking countries show larger inequality increases between primary and secondary school than late-tracking countries; Germany (+0.71), Greece (+0.30) and Czech Republic (+0.25) contrast with Turkey (−0.63), New Zealand (−0.50), Canada (−0.32). [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann, NBER Working Paper 11124; https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w11124/w11124.pdf]
- Poland's 1999 delayed-tracking reform (replacing early streaming with comprehensive lower-secondary) yielded PISA math gains of 0.25 SD, reading 0.28 SD, science 0.16 SD by 2006, with approximately one-third attributed to instructional time and the remainder to compositional/resource changes. [evidence: strong — Jakubowski, Patrinos, Porta & Wiśniewski, Education Economics 24(6) 2016; https://ideas.repec.org/a/taf/edecon/v24y2016i6p557-572.html]
- Poland's labour-market follow-up (2022) found that vocational-track students who remained an extra year in comprehensive schooling benefited from better peers and teachers, with the reform reducing inequality; however, Poland reversed the reform in January 2017 after 18 years. [evidence: strong — Drucker, Horn & Jakubowski, Journal for Labour Market Research (2022); https://labourmarketresearch.springeropen.com/articles/10.1186/s12651-022-00317-4]
- Hungary has the OECD's highest socioeconomic achievement gaps: 53-point gap between children of graduate and non-graduate parents, compared to 33-point EU average; 37% of Hungarian students were low achievers in 2022. [evidence: strong — European Commission, Education and Training Monitor 2025 — Hungary; https://op.europa.eu/webpub/eac/education-and-training-monitor/en/country-reports/hungary.html]
- Hungary's selection architecture includes at least four formal selection points before higher education; in Central European systems (Czechia, Slovakia, Hungary) the formal primary/lower-secondary unity is a façade, because 6–8 grade secondary schools cream off high-performing, typically middle-class pupils as early as end of grade 5–7. [evidence: strong — CEU Center for Policy Studies (2020); https://cps.ceu.edu/sites/cps.ceu.edu/files/attachment/publication/3258/cps-working-paper-educ-social-selection-education-hungary-2020.pdf]
- Hungarian educational selectiveness shows 2–3 years of measured difference in intellectual development between class means within primary-school cohorts, and Hungary has selection at age 7 as a first point. [evidence: moderate — Csapó, Molnár & Kinyő, IEA; https://www.iea.nl/sites/default/files/2019-04/IRC2008_Csapo_Molnar_etal.pdf]
- 74% of 25–64 year-olds with at least one tertiary-educated parent also attained tertiary qualification in Hungary, indicating strong intergenerational transmission of educational advantage. [evidence: strong — OECD, Education at a Glance 2024 — Hungary; https://www.oecd.org/content/dam/oecd/en/publications/reports/2024/09/education-and-a-glance-2024-country-notes_532eb29d/hungary_faab9f42/1b2a3510-en.pdf]

- Registry-backed: Cross-country difference-in-differences evidence shows earlier between-school tracking increases inequality of outcomes without raising mean performance. [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Woessmann 2006, Economic Journal]
- As of 2024/25, Hungary has 880 secondary general-education (gimnázium) schools nationally, with eight-year programmes starting from grade 5 and six-year programmes from grade 7. [evidence: strong — Eurydice, Organisation of general secondary education — Hungary; KSH (2024/25)]
- A 2025 systematic review of five OECD PISA equity reports (2009–2022) plus academic studies found persistent, not uniformly improving, socioeconomic equity trends across PISA cycles 2000–2018/2022, suggesting cross-national equity gains from single structural levers are contested and cycle-dependent. [evidence: moderate — ScienceDirect systematic review (2024–2025); https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666374024000414]

## Interpretation
Hungary combines early selection (age 7, then age 10–12 into 6/8-year gimnázium), exceptionally high socioeconomic achievement gaps (53 vs 33 EU average), and strong intergenerational educational transmission. International evidence shows that early tracking systems increase inequality without raising mean achievement; Poland's natural experiment of delayed tracking produced gains of 0.25–0.28 SD and reduced inequality, though Poland later reversed this reform. Hungary's inequality is not due to mean underperformance but to the amplification of family background through its multi-point selection system. The transferability of Poland's or other late-tracking systems to Hungary faces political, resource, and institutional constraints not yet quantified; equity gains are robust in DiD literature but cycle-dependent in PISA trends. Whether early selection *solves* problems (e.g., teacher efficiency in heterogeneous classrooms) remains an unresolved trade-off between efficiency and equity.

## Assumptions
- The PISA cycles 2000–2022 are comparable across countries and years in terms of equity measurement methodology. [assumption]
- Selection effects (composition) and school value-added can be sufficiently disentangled in DiD and propensity-score-matching designs to attribute gains to delayed tracking rather than other concurrent reforms. [assumption]
- Poland's institutional and demographic context in 1999 is sufficiently similar to Hungary's current context that the Polish effect sizes could be transferable. [assumption]
- The 6/8-year gimnázium system is the primary driver of Hungarian socioeconomic inequality rather than other institutional factors (e.g., catchment-area segregation, resource allocation, teacher distribution). [assumption]
- The international late-tracking evidence applies to the 10–12 age selection point; earlier selection (age 7) may have different effects that are less studied. [assumption]
- Hungarian politicians and stakeholders would accept a phase-out of 6/8-year grammar schools if evidence of equity gains were sufficiently strong, though this is not certain. [assumption]
- Current data availability permits accurate measurement of selection effects independent of broader resource constraints or pedagogical factors. [assumption]
- The teacher shortage in Hungary does not fundamentally alter the expected benefits of delayed tracking, though it may slow implementation or reduce gains. [assumption]

## Position
International difference-in-differences evidence indicates that delaying selection from age 10–12 to age 14 would likely reduce Hungary's socioeconomic achievement gap by approximately 0.15–0.25 SD (based on Polish comparable gains), but this would require compensatory instructional resources and teacher supply that Hungary currently lacks in demonstrable quantity.

## Uncertainties
- Whether the Polish 1999 delayed-tracking reform's effect sizes (0.25–0.28 SD) would replicate in Hungary given different demographic composition (Roma representation, migration patterns) and school-system institutional differences. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Natural experiments or quasi-experiments within Hungary (e.g., regional pilot phase-outs of 8-year grammar schools with random assignment or propensity-score matching); administrative data on prior achievement, family SES, and post-selection outcomes for Poland and Hungary cohorts.)

(confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Decomposition analyses isolating the contribution of selection (composition) from within-school and between-school resource differences using multi-level models and Hungarian census/administrative data linked to student outcomes.)
- Whether teacher supply and instructional-resource capacity in Hungary would permit full-scale implementation of delayed tracking without reducing quality for higher-achieving or vocational-track students. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Current teacher-shortage forecasts for Hungary (age-cohort, attrition, supply from initial teacher education, cross-border migration); cost estimates for expanding comprehensive-school capacity and teacher development programmes; simulations of class composition and teacher allocation under delayed tracking.)

## Findings (evidence)

[evidence: moderate — AJB nyilatkozat, atlatszo.hu]

[evidence: moderate — Alapvető jogok biztosa jelentés a köznevelési törvény módosításáról, nemzetisegijogok.hu]

The statutory architecture treats early selection as a bounded, separately-justified exception within a system whose default structural principle reserves selection for age 15 (grade 9). This narrow codification makes the 6/8-year track more legally tractable to reform than Hungary's broader institutional architecture — legislative change is possible without restructuring the entire secondary system. However, the 2026 KEKVA precedent and constitutional ombudsman doctrine establish that any change touching enrolled student cohorts must respect felmenő-rendszer protections: phase-in by grade, with roughly one-year minimum lead time. The high EU-reported socio-economic segregation linked to Hungary's selection system underscores the equity stakes, but legal feasibility and legitimacy both point toward cohort-by-cohort implementation, not abrupt abolition.

[assumption]

## Position
Because early selection is codified as a narrow, separately-justified legal exception rather than the structural default of secondary education, reform of the selection age is legally more tractable than reshaping Hungary's broader maintainer or curriculum architecture — but legitimacy and constitutional doctrine require any change to proceed on a felmenő-rendszer (grade-by-grade phase-in) basis with minimum ~1-year statutory lead time, protecting students already admitted under existing rules.

----- polish_reform -----
# Expert analysis: polish_reform

## Findings (evidence)
- Poland's 1999 delayed-tracking reform, which extended comprehensive general education from 8 to 9 years (moving selection from age 12 to age 15), produced PISA score gains of 0.25 SD in mathematics, 0.28 SD in reading, and 0.16 SD in science between 2000 and 2006; students in likely-vocational tracks gained approximately one standard deviation (~100 PISA points). [evidence: strong — Jakubowski et al. (2016), Education Economics 24(6)]
- Between-school equity improved markedly after the 1999 reform: Poland reduced performance disparities between schools from one of the largest in the OECD to one of the smallest. [evidence: strong — OECD synthesis; Jakubowski et al. 2016]
- Individual-level socioeconomic equity did not improve after the 1999 reform: using PISA 2000–2012 data, there was no significant decrease in correlation between students' socioeconomic status and their test results. [evidence: moderate — Academia.edu; Openedition synthesis]
- The 1999 reform's labor-market effects persisted: using EU-SILC data, a 2022 study found the reform decreased inequalities via improved peer and teacher composition for previously low-track students, with gains confirmed by multiple methods (Liwiński 2020; Strawinski & Broniatowska 2021). [evidence: strong — Drucker, Horn & Jakubowski (2022), Journal for Labour Market Research 56:13]
- Poland's 2016–2019 reversal abolished the 3-year gimnazjum, returned to 8-year primary school followed by tracked secondary schools, raised school entry age back to 7, curtailed special-needs inclusion, revised curriculum toward more Polish history/language, ended project-based learning, and disbanded lower-secondary teacher teams. [evidence: strong — World Class Learning Systems (2024)]
- Political opinion on the 1999 comprehensive-school reform became sharply partisan by 2015–2016: a 2016 IPSOS/OKO.press poll showed Law and Justice (PiS) supporters 73% positive / 10% negative and Civic Platform/Modern voters 25% positive / 68% negative, indicating school reform became an identity marker rather than an evidence-driven choice. [evidence: moderate — Medium (@thelambert); Springer chapter (2030-41882-3_7)]
- The 2017 implementation shock from forced overlap of two secondary-school cohorts (final gimnazjum graduates and first primary-school-only graduates competing simultaneously) intensified admission competition; in Warsaw alone, 3,173 students did not gain entry to any school that year. [evidence: moderate — Medium (@thelambert)]
- PISA 2022 results for Poland fell to levels last seen in 2003, erasing gains made since 1999; researchers attribute part of this decline to the full effect of the 2016–2019 reversal (which only fully affected the 2022 cohort, not the 2018 cohort) combined with 26+ weeks of school closures during COVID-19. [evidence: moderate — OECD PISA 2022 Country Notes (Poland); Springer chapter (2031-69284-0_9)]
- Some elements of Poland's 1999 reform proved politically irreversible: increased school autonomy, external examinations, broader access to preschool, and higher-education access changes remained in place even after the broader 2016–2019 reversal, suggesting that decentralized and legally-entrenched reforms resist rollback better than central mandates alone. [evidence: moderate — Springer chapter (2030-59031-4_7)]
- Teacher unions split during the reversal: the larger ZNP union (which had supported the 1999 comprehensive-school reform as international best practice) organized nationwide protests, and 80% of schools participated in a 2019 teacher strike over pay and conditions tied to reform-driven chaos, though the strike did not reverse the policy. [evidence: strong — Balkan Insight (2020); Springer chapter (2030-41882-3_7)]

## Interpretation
Poland's experience demonstrates a powerful learning effect from delayed tracking: eight years of comprehensive schooling followed by age-15 selection produced substantial gains in average achievement (especially for at-risk students) and between-school equity. However, it reveals two critical vulnerabilities: (1) individual-level socioeconomic inequity proved harder to shift—school convergence did not equalize advantage within schools; and (2) structural school reforms, once implemented successfully, can become partisan identity markers and be reversed for political rather than evidence-driven reasons, erasing gains within a single cohort cycle. The 2016–2019 reversal incurred both immediate costs (implementation shock, teacher strikes, student-placement chaos) and measurable long-term costs (PISA decline). Reforms that embed changes in decentralized and legally-entrenched systems rather than relying solely on central mandate appear more durable. For Hungary, this implies early selection cannot be evaluated in isolation: it must account for whether alternatives are politically resilient over a full education cohort's passage through the system.

## Assumptions
- The attribution of Poland's PISA gains primarily to the 1999 timing reform (rather than to confounding policy or demographic changes, or measurement variance) is robust enough for causal inference in a quasi-experimental setting, though alternative explanations (e.g., broader infrastructure investment, curriculum reform, teacher-training expansion) cannot be fully ruled out. [assumption]
- The 2016 IPSOS/OKO.press polling result accurately reflects genuine opinion at that moment, not campaign artefacts; the sharp partisan split reflects genuine value disagreement rather than mere measurement error. [assumption]
- Hungary's institutional and political context is sufficiently similar to Poland's that lessons about reform durability, partisan politicization, and implementation shock transfer meaningfully, even though Hungary's school system differs in governance and school-choice rules. [assumption]
- The 2022 PISA decline can be partially attributed to the 2016–2019 reversal's effects, separable from COVID-19 learning loss, because the full reversal affected only the 2022 cohort; however, the counterfactual outcome under the 1999 reform structure without COVID is unobserved and must be estimated. [assumption]
- The labor-market wage gains observed by Drucker, Horn & Jakubowski (2022) and other studies reflect genuine returns to improved secondary-school composition and teacher quality, not merely selection into higher-paying occupations among the same underlying ability distribution. [assumption]

## Position
Early selection at age 10–12 into the 6- and 8-year grammar school tracks is justifiable only if Hungary can sustain politically durable institutional design, ensure between-school equity gains translate into individual-level SES equalization, and maintain implementation continuity across electoral cycles—outcomes Poland achieved partially and lost entirely, demonstrating that the benefits of delayed selection (1 SD gain for at-risk students, improved labor-market outcomes, reduced school segregation) outweigh the benefits of early tracking only if political reversibility risk and individual-SES gains (not achieved in Poland) are weighted equally with test-score gains.

## Uncertainties
- The precise causal decomposition between the 1999 reform's structural effect (unified intake) versus its resource/composition effects (investment and teacher change) remains uncertain; even Jakubowski et al.'s two-thirds/one-third split is an estimate and could shift with different methodologies. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Controlled variation in implementation timing or intensity across regions, or new difference-in-differences analysis on cohort-level PISA data with explicit separators for resource spending and teacher composition.)
- Whether individual-level SES gaps could have been closed by longer-duration exposure to the 1999 reform (the Polish cohort was measured at age 15, not adulthood) or whether structural timing reform alone is insufficient without targeted SES-based support programs remains untested. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Longitudinal follow-up of Polish cohorts into adulthood (age 25–35), measuring lifetime earnings, intergenerational mobility, and SES-conditional achievement by school-sector composition; similar cohort studies in other countries with delayed-tracking reforms.)

(confidence: low; would be reduced by: Publication of a binding government decision (határozat) with explicit timeline and legislative mandate, or a formal Oktatási Hivatal review-report with policy-option analysis; absence of such documents after six months indicates indefinite deferral.)
- Are statutory (vs annual-budget) funding-floor guarantees politically achievable under the projected fiscal consolidation pressures, or will compensatory funding remain contingent on annual budget cycles? (confidence: low; would be reduced by: The 2027 budget bill text showing either (a) a cardinal-law funding-floor commitment or (b) temporary, deficit-contingent compensation language; ministry communications on funding-guarantee strategy; EC fiscal guidance on permissible spending commitments.)
- Will the Fidesz opposition signal any willingness to negotiate a cross-party consensus on selection reform, or will it maintain the 'totalitarian/communism' framing and signal intent to reverse the reform on return to government? (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Public statements by Fidesz leadership (e.g., via opposition spokesperson or shadow minister) indicating willingness to negotiate or conditions for consensus; polling showing public support for selection reform high enough to make opposition costly; evidence of behind-the-scenes dialogue or negotiation frameworks.)


----- portuguese_reform -----
# Expert analysis: portuguese_reform

## Findings (evidence)
- Portugal maintained a comprehensive (non-selective) school structure through compulsory basic education and improved PISA results by approximately 31 points (0.3 standard deviations) between 2000 and 2015–2018 without introducing early tracking. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA trend reports; Marôco, Portugal: The PISA Effects on Education, Springer]
- Portugal's documented gains in learning outcomes resulted from curriculum standards clarification, increased school autonomy, regular student assessment, and vocational pathway development—not from changes to selection age. [evidence: strong — Springer, Curriculum and Educational Reforms in Portugal; Crato 2020]
- Hungary's early selection system (at ages 10 and 12 into 8-year and 6-year grammar schools) amplifies achievement variance and inequality of opportunity without raising average performance. [evidence: strong — Terrin & Triventi, The Effect of School Tracking on Student Achievement and Inequality: A Meta-Analysis, SAGE; Horn D. (2013), Diverging performances, Research in Social Stratification and Mobility]
- TEIP (Portugal's targeted school support programme) reduced student dropout and grade retention but did not close competency gaps relative to non-TEIP schools, and no rigorous longitudinal evaluation of its long-term student outcomes exists. [evidence: moderate — MDPI, Has the Portuguese Compensatory Education Program Been Successful; ScienceDirect Procedia 2014; CIIE Porto]
- Between 2016 and 2018, Portugal's grade-retention reduction initiative saved 112 million euros while reducing retained students by 26% across 663 schools. [evidence: strong — Público, Nov 12 2019; official Portuguese government reporting]
- Portugal's school selectivity index (PISA 2022) stands at 1.29 on the OECD scale, ranking 75th of 80 countries—among the lowest in the OECD. [evidence: strong — OECD Education GPS – Portugal, 2022]
- Cross-national evidence from multiple studies shows early tracking (age 10, as practiced in Austria, Germany, Hungary, and Slovakia) increases between-school achievement variance and social inequality without raising average achievement. [evidence: strong — Hanushek & Wößmann, foundational cross-national study; ScienceDirect 2026 synthesis]
- Hungarian municipalities that adopted 8-year grammar schools show that socio-economic status is the strongest predictor of mathematical test performance among tracked countries. [evidence: moderate — Gurzo, K., The long term effects of early tracking in schools, CEU eTD 2015]

## Interpretation
Portugal demonstrates that significant learning-outcome improvement is achievable in a comprehensive, non-selective system through targeted non-structural reforms—curriculum alignment, assessment transparency, retention reduction, and equitable school support—without lowering average achievement or restricting access. By contrast, Hungary's early-selection structure (age 10–12) creates inequality and performance stratification without a corresponding efficiency or achievement premium. The evidence suggests that early tracking solves no efficiency problem and creates substantial social-stratification problems; its persistence reflects structural inertia and political interest rather than pedagogical necessity. TEIP-style targeted support shows promise in reducing dropout but requires stronger design and rigorous evaluation to demonstrate competency-gap closure. Retention reduction, Portugal's clearest non-structural success, is both a fiscal and equity win when implemented systematically.

## Assumptions
- Portugal's comprehensive system is demographically and institutionally comparable to Hungary's in relevant dimensions (school resources, teacher training, family educational background distribution) such that its outcomes are transferable. [assumption]
- Curriculum clarity, school autonomy, and regular assessment drive outcomes independently of selection structure; their effects are not interactive with early tracking in ways that would reverse the ordering observed in Portugal. [assumption]
- The PISA index is a valid proxy for school system 'selectivity' and its observed ranking reflects genuine institutional differences rather than measurement artifact. [assumption]
- Reduction in grade retention can be achieved at scale through pedagogical and assessment reform without structural dismantling of the current school-type system. [assumption]

## Position
Hungary's early-selection system at ages 10–12 increases achievement inequality and socio-economic stratification without raising average performance, and similar learning gains to Portugal's are achievable through non-structural reforms (curriculum clarity, assessment transparency, retention reduction, targeted support) within a comprehensive framework.

## Uncertainties
- Whether targeted support (TEIP-type) can close achievement gaps rather than merely reducing dropout rates remains uncertain because no longitudinal study has tracked student competency trajectories beyond retention reduction. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: A 5–8 year longitudinal matched-cohort study comparing TEIP-enrolled and non-enrolled students, controlling for baseline socio-economic status and prior achievement, tracking assessed competency gain (not just exam pass rates).)
- The fiscal and pedagogical cost of phase-out or structural reform of Hungary's 8-year grammar school track (replacing it with comprehensive entry and specialist bands at 14+) has not been modeled in published Hungarian research. (confidence: high; would be reduced by: A scenario analysis prepared by Hungarian education ministry or major research institute, modeling capital reallocation, teacher redeployment, and transition costs over 10–15 years under different phase-in schedules.)
- Whether Hungary's school-choice system (szabad iskolaválasztás) amplifies or mitigates selection effects is empirically unknown; choice may create de facto academic sorting at comprehensive-system entry points. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A cross-municipal comparison of school-choice patterns and outcomes in comprehensive vs. tracked districts, with matching on initial school capacity and socio-economic intake, to isolate choice-driven stratification.)
- The long-term labor-market and civic outcomes (earnings, employment stability, political participation) of students who remained in a comprehensive system versus those tracked into lower academic grades in Hungary remain unquantified. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: A linked administrative register study (tax authority, labor force survey, electoral records) following cohorts 20+ years post-secondary-entry, comparing tracked vs. comprehensive-entry outcomes within Hungary or using a quasi-experimental discontinuity at a reform boundary.)
- Whether a phased shift from 8-year to 6-year grammar school entry (age 12), preserving selection but delaying it, would reduce social stratification or merely postpone it is a structural question not addressed in the current evidence base. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Comparative research on countries that have experimentally implemented age-postponement of tracking (e.g., comparing 10 vs. 12 entry regimes in the same institutional context), ideally with randomized school-network pilots in Hungary.)


----- school_network_planning -----
# Expert analysis: school_network_planning

## Findings (evidence)
- Hungary's secondary network of 880 schools is deliberately dense, with a policy aim that every student reach a gimnázium by public transport within about an hour. [evidence: moderate — Eurydice/KSH 2024/25]
- Dormitory capacity nationally exceeds demand and demand is declining, currently easing rather than solving the commuting-tolerance constraint for thin rural markets. [evidence: moderate — Eurydice 2024/25]
- More than 1,200 of Hungary's 3,155 settlements had no public educational institution at all by 2024, concentrated in peripheral and rural regions. [evidence: strong — ResearchGate spatial-clustering study 2025 (KIR 2021-2024)]
- Birth numbers are falling faster than previously expected—5,979 in October 2025, fertility rate 1.31—meaning the school-age intake base for any selection scenario continues to shrink. [evidence: strong — KSH via IntelliNews November 2025]
- Long-run population projections place Hungary at approximately 8.5 million inhabitants by 2040-2050, requiring school-network planning to account for sustained demographic decline, not a temporary fluctuation. [evidence: moderate — IntelliNews/OSW Centre for Eastern Studies projections]
- Selection is not geographically uniform: approximately 40% of general schools nationally (60% in villages) send zero pupils to 6- or 8-year gimnázium tracks, while urban and county-seat schools lose 20-30% of each cohort. [evidence: moderate — PrivátBankár and Index reporting on Oktatási Hivatal and national competence-measurement data]
- When top-performing pupils depart after grade 4 or 6, the sending general school experiences mildly negative average effects on remaining peers and unplannable class-size volatility that complicates staffing. [evidence: moderate — Horn et al. 2018 cited via qubit.hu]
- Church-maintained school enrollment grew fastest (2.5× to 5×) in small towns and villages 2010-2022; simultaneously, pupils in settlements with no remaining state-run primary school rose from 1% to 4%—a maintainer shift layered atop selection pressures. [evidence: strong — A közoktatás indikátorrendszere 2023 via qubit.hu]
- Gimnázium enrollment reached 192,000 students (44.8% of all secondary enrollment) in 2024/25, with general-school enrollment continuing to decline while secondary and tertiary enrollment grew—consistent with shrinking cohorts at the 10-12 selection point. [evidence: moderate — KSH preliminary 2024/25 data]

## Interpretation
Physical school-network capacity—buildings, dormitories, and transport infrastructure—is not currently the binding constraint on delivering early selection almost anywhere. The dense 880-school grid plus underused dormitory capacity mean most towns can physically access a 6- or 8-year gimnázium. The real town-by-town feasibility problem is compositional and demographic: shrinking birth cohorts mean fewer children to sustain both a full eight-grade general school and a competing gymnázium track locally. Selection is already concentrated in a minority of settlements (mostly urban or church-maintained), while ~40% send no one to tracked programs. Each cohort self-selecting out weakens the remaining general-school class, accelerating consolidation pressure. Maintainer transfers (state to church) are currently absorbing this burden rather than solving it.

## Assumptions
- Current KSH demographic projections (to 2040-2050) will not be substantially reversed by family-policy interventions in the medium term. [assumption]
- Dormitory slack capacity reported at the national level is sufficiently distributed geographically to matter for actual feasibility, rather than concentrated only in a few large urban centers. [assumption]

## Position
Physical school-network capacity is currently sufficient to sustain early selection almost anywhere; the genuine town-by-town feasibility bottleneck is the shrinking and unevenly distributed child population, combined with a maintainer and admissions design that concentrates selection into a minority of settlements.

## Uncertainties
- We lack settlement or county-level data on current dormitory vacancy versus demand trends in specific declining regions; only national aggregate trends are known. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: Settlement- or county-disaggregated KIR/KIFIR dormitory occupancy records by region and time period.)
- We do not have a recent, precise projection of how many settlements would lose their last local secondary program if consolidation continues at current demographic pace. (confidence: low; would be reduced by: A dedicated KSH or Oktatási Hivatal settlement-level school viability projection under current birth-rate scenarios.)
- The causal magnitude of the Horn et al. (2018) 'peer drain' effect on sending general schools rests on a single study cited second-hand; its magnitude and generalizability across settlement types remain uncertain. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Direct access to the original peer-reviewed paper and replication of the analysis across more recent cohorts and settlement-type subgroups.)


----- educational_psychology -----
# Expert analysis: educational_psychology

## Findings (evidence)
- Equally able pupils in selective (high-achieving) grammar school settings develop lower academic self-concept than those in non-selective schools, a robust cross-national effect independent of actual achievement differences. [evidence: strong — Marsh 1987+; Frontiers in Psychology meta-analysis (33 studies, N=1,276,838); β = −0.28, p < 0.001]
- Hungarian family socio-economic background predicts entry into 6- and 8-year grammar school (gimnázium) at rates comparable to or exceeding test-score effects: a student with one standard deviation higher family background has 21.6% higher probability of entering a 6-year track, controlling for prior achievement from the same school. [evidence: strong — Horn, 'Early tracking and competition – A recipe for major inequalities' (https://real.mtak.hu/42964/1/8_Hungary_final_5_February_2016_hd_u.pdf)]
- Mathematical and reading achievement diverges across Hungarian tracks (6-year, 8-year, comprehensive) between grades 6–8 and 8–10 even after controlling for prior skill and socio-economic status, indicating selection itself shapes trajectories beyond initial sorting. [evidence: moderate — Horn, value-added analysis between 6th–10th grade, Hungarian system natural experiment]
- In Hungary, approximately 85% of pupils attend schools with academically selective admissions mechanisms, compared to the OECD average of 43%, positioning early selection at age 10–12 (grades 5–7) as a structural norm rather than exception. [evidence: strong — OECD Education Policy Outlook Hungary, 2015]
- In Hungary (PISA 2022), the mathematics performance gap between students in the top 25% and bottom 25% by socio-economic status was 121 points, exceeding the OECD average gap of 93 points, and mathematics and reading performance declined to among Hungary's lowest recorded PISA results. [evidence: strong — OECD PISA 2022 Country Notes: Hungary]
- Classroom and system structures that emphasise ranking and selection push adolescents toward performance goal orientation (fear of failure, shallow learning strategies) rather than mastery orientation (persistence, deep understanding), with adolescence being the sensitive period for identity consolidation. [evidence: moderate — Dweck & Elliot goal-orientation literature; developmental psychology of adolescence]
- Self-determination theory predicts that intrinsic motivation is sustained by autonomy, competence, and relatedness; selective tracking systems can undermine autonomy (pupil agency in school choice) and relatedness (peer composition shifts by SES and prior achievement). [evidence: strong — Deci & Ryan 1985/2000; self-determination theory applied to school structures]
- Teacher-expectancy and labeling effects on pupil self-concept exist, but are contested in magnitude: average effects are smaller than early Pygmalion claims, yet systematically larger for stigmatised and low-socio-economic-status pupils — exactly the population labelled by early selection. [evidence: contested — Rosenthal & Jacobson 1968 (Pygmalion); Flore & Wicherts 2015 preregistered replications; Steele & Aronson 1995 (stereotype threat) with later meta-analyses]
- Stereotype threat (performance drops when a negative group stereotype becomes salient) has substantial evidence in experimental settings, but preregistered replications and meta-analyses (Flore & Wicherts 2015) find effects smaller and less reliable than early literature, so it should be invoked as a possible mechanism, not a settled empirical fact. [evidence: contested — Steele & Aronson 1995 and successors; Flore & Wicherts 2015 meta-analysis]
- Growth mindset (belief that ability can be developed) interventions are empirically contested: large preregistered replications and the Sisk et al. (2018) meta-analysis found only very small, practically negligible average effects of mindset interventions, and several original studies replicated poorly. [evidence: contested — Sisk et al. 2018 meta-analysis (Psychological Science); Dweck 2006 with subsequent replication literature]
- Cross-national quasi-experimental evidence (PISA, TIMSS, PIRLS difference-in-differences) converges on the finding that early tracking increases within-country inequality in test scores and has a particularly negative effect on low-achieving students, with weaker evidence for raising mean achievement. [evidence: moderate — ERIC EJ1114208; IZA World of Labor synthesis; World Bank opinion pieces on tracking]
- The assumption that between-school tracking improves teaching and learning homogeneity (the efficiency case for selection) is itself empirically contested: some studies find no tracking effect on achievement gaps, and whether between-school tracking actually increases peer-group academic homogeneity is unresolved. [evidence: contested — Springer 2021 paper on tracking and social inequality; ScienceDirect 2026 paper on achievement homogeneity assumption]
- Poland's 1999 tracking-delay reform (postponing selection from age 15 to 16 and extending comprehensive education) coincided with PISA mathematics improvement (470→495 points, 2000–2006) and reading score rise above OECD average; however, the reform bundled curriculum change, examination standardisation, and teacher-qualification reforms, so the 'delay' effect cannot be isolated from broader system change. [evidence: moderate — World Bank 2016; Springer labour-market paper 2022; Cairn.info hpatrinos working paper on Polish reform]
- In Hungary, the socio-economic performance gap is compounded by a vocational/general secondary school divergence: PISA 2022 shows one of the largest mathematics performance gaps between general and vocational programmes in OECD, driven largely by differences in student socio-economic composition, indicating that age-10/12 selection is the first of several sorting events. [evidence: strong — European Commission Education and Training Monitor 2025: Hungary]

## Interpretation
Early academic selection at age 10–12 in Hungary operates as a socio-economically sorted system: family background predicts entry independent of prior ability, and selection amplifies rather than reflects prior achievement differences. The psychological evidence shows two distinct cost mechanisms — the big-fish-little-pond effect (depressed self-concept even among 'winners' in high-achieving environments) and performance goal orientation (selection-driven ranking culture pushes pupils toward fear-of-failure strategies). Cross-national evidence suggests tracking increases inequality in test scores, particularly for lower-track students, without clear evidence of raising mean achievement. The efficiency case for selection (homogenous peer groups improve teaching) rests on an empirically contested assumption. Delayed tracking (Polish reform) shows correlated gains, but cannot be disentangled from bundled system reforms. The trajectory divergence observed between grades 6–10 suggests selection has dynamic sorting effects beyond initial placement. However, the literature on whether tracking directly perpetuates social inequality (vs. merely reflecting and amplifying pre-existing SES gradients) remains genuinely mixed, a nuance often lost in policy debate.

## Assumptions
- A pupil's entry into an 8-year or 6-year grammar school (gimnázium) at ages 10–12 is determined by an admission examination (felvételi vizsga) that aims to measure academic readiness, but the instrument itself is assumed to be largely unbiased by socio-economic status in its design, even if socio-economic differences in results emerge. [assumption]
- The effect of selection on individual achievement trajectories is separable from the effect of selection on inequality: a student might benefit from a high-achieving peer environment for their own learning (positive peer effect) even if the system as a whole increases socio-economic sorting. [assumption]
- Parents' school choice behaviour, driven by reputation and network effects, translates SES gradients into track enrolment even in the presence of academically neutral selection instruments; the counterfactual assumption of the analysis is that free school choice (szabad iskolaválasztás) functions independently of SES in practice. [assumption]
- The value-added effects attributed to selection (students in selective tracks gain more, measured year-to-year) are assumed to result from school processes (curriculum, teacher quality, peer composition) rather than unmeasured selection-into-selective-schools bias or wider system effects (e.g. post-selection resource allocation or parental investment). [assumption]

## Position
Early academic selection (6- and 8-year grammar school admission at ages 10–12) in Hungary increases socio-economic inequality in educational outcomes and reduces academic self-concept for higher-achieving students in selective schools, without clear evidence of raising mean achievement, and therefore imposes psychological and equity costs that are not clearly outweighed by efficiency gains.

## Uncertainties
- What is the current (2024–2025) distribution of Hungarian pupils across the three-tier selection system (6-year, 8-year, comprehensive at age 12)? How has enrolment demand and school-maintainer provision changed since the 2015 OECD baseline, and which regions or maintainer types are most selective? (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Current administrative data from the Klebelsberg centre (tankerületi központ) system and published admissions statistics (percentage of cohort, regional/maintainer breakdown, number of applicants and places by gimnázium type); Hungarian education ministry annual admissions reports.)
- What is the strength and specificity of labeling/expectancy effects and stereotype threat as applied to the Hungarian tracking context? Do teachers' expectations differ systematically between grammar school and comprehensive school pupils with matched prior achievement, and if so, does this mediate subsequent divergence? (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Longitudinal observational study or randomised experiment comparing teacher expectations and pupil self-concept/motivation trajectories in matched-ability cohorts across selective and non-selective schools in Hungary; qualitative interview study of Hungarian teachers' mental models of student potential by track; replication of Steele & Aronson or Rosenthal & Jacobson designs in Hungarian school contexts.)
- Does delaying selection to age 14 (comprehensive schooling through age 14, with track entry at upper secondary) in place of age-10 selection improve mean achievement, reduce inequality, or improve social mobility, holding other system reforms constant? The Polish precedent is confounded by simultaneous curriculum and teacher-qualification reforms. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Natural experiment or quasi-experimental design (e.g. staggered policy rollout across regions, instrumental-variable design using cohort-specific policy variation) comparing comprehensive-through-14 to current age-10/12 selection on achievement, inequality (SES gradients in track placement), and long-term earnings/employment outcomes; if possible, bundle the comparison with standardised curriculum and teacher pay to isolate the 'timing of selection' effect.)
- What is the magnitude of the big-fish-little-pond effect (BFLPE) specifically in the Hungarian context, and does it differ by SES or gender? The global meta-analytic effect (β = −0.28) is large, but replication in Hungarian schools with Hungarian measures of self-concept and achievement is needed. (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Replication of Marsh's BFLPE methodology in Hungarian secondary schools: measure pupils' academic self-concept (via standardised self-concept instruments or longitudinal survey) before and after entry into selective vs. non-selective schools, controlling for prior ability and SES; test moderation by gender, ethnicity, and prior achievement level.)
- Does the effect of selection on achievement inequality operate through change in classroom peer composition (mechanisms: reduced low-achieving peer effect, teacher sorting, curriculum adaptation), through labeling/expectancy channels (mechanisms: self-fulfilling prophecy, stereotype threat), or through some third pathway (e.g. differential parental investment post-selection)? (confidence: high; would be reduced by: Longitudinal mediation analysis in Hungarian data (if available) or cross-national meta-regression on which mechanisms account for tracking effects; randomised or quasi-experimental variation in peer composition (holding track assignment fixed) to test the causal effect of peers independent of selection; qualitative case studies of classroom interactions and teacher planning in selective vs. non-selective schools; measurement of parental investment and aspirations before and after selection.)
- What is the political and implementation feasibility of credible alternatives to age-10/12 selection (e.g. delay to age 14, blind admissions, school-choice reform, teacher-qualification parity)? Which stakeholders (parents, teachers, school maintainers, local politicians) would resist or support each scenario, and on what conditions? (confidence: medium; would be reduced by: Stakeholder survey or interview study mapping preferences and 'dealbreaker' conditions for selection reform; political feasibility assessment by government relations specialists or policy advisors; cost-benefit analysis of each alternative scenario (including transition costs and regional inequality in winners/losers); analysis of Hungarian public opinion trends (via Eurobarometer or Hungarian polling data) on school choice and selection.)
