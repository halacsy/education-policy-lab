## Overview

The expert analyses converge on the significant impact of Hungary's 6- and 8-year gymnasium system, which selects students at ages 10 and 12. A central tension exists between the perceived benefits of early selection for parental choice and social mobility, and the evidence suggesting it exacerbates socio-economic inequalities and is not demographically sustainable in rural areas. While some experts advocate for phasing out early selection to improve equity and address demographic challenges, others emphasize the political feasibility, transition costs, and the importance of parental choice, suggesting alternative approaches or retaining the current system with modifications.

## Disagreement map

*   **demography, equity_and_social_mobility, finnish_reform, international_comparison, school_network_planning, polish_reform**: Hungary must phase out the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system to prevent demographic cohort shrinkage from accelerating the collapse and forced consolidation of rural primary school networks and to reduce socio-economic segregation.
    Why: Demographic decline makes maintaining early selection inefficient, concentrating vulnerability on rural primary schools. Early selection exacerbates socio-economic inequalities by sorting students and depriving non-selected students of positive peer effects. International evidence suggests early tracking increases inequality without raising mean performance.
*   **education_finance, implementation_planning**: Hungary should retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system because the transition costs of structural consolidation (teacher retraining and facility re-adaptation) outweigh any marginal steady-state operational savings, and the current teacher shortage makes redistribution impossible without degrading quality.
    Why: Per-pupil funding differences are modest, and transition costs are high. The existing teacher shortage makes it functionally impossible to redistribute staff without severely degrading quality, and the administrative burden of dismantling the system would collapse educational delivery in disadvantaged regions.
*   **hungarian_education_system, legal_and_governance**: Hungary should retain the 6- and 8-year gimnázium tracks but align their entry requirements and quotas with regional equity targets, while concurrently regulating non-state school admissions to limit socio-demographic sorting.
    Why: Early tracking is deeply entrenched, and simply banning selective tracks within state schools may push selective demand into the non-state sector. Legal complexities with church and foundation schools necessitate a phased approach respecting existing structures.
*   **polish_reform, political_feasibility**: Hungary should phase out early tracking at ages 10 and 12 in favor of a delayed-tracking system at age 15, but must anchor this transition in long-term multi-party pacts and compensatory funding for local schools to prevent political reversal.
    Why: Delaying tracking can improve equity and average performance, but the Polish precedent shows such reforms are vulnerable to political reversal due to concentrated middle-class and institutional resentment.
*   **portuguese_reform**: Hungary should retain the 6- and 8-year gimnazium system but neutralize its socio-economic inequities by deploying non-structural reforms, specifically targeted funding for disadvantaged schools (TEIP-style) and rigorous national curriculum standards.
    Why: Portugal improved outcomes without structural changes by focusing on curriculum, school clustering, and targeted support. This suggests Hungary can mitigate sorting effects and equity deficits through non-structural interventions.
*   **conservative_education**: Maintaining early selective tracks, such as the gymnasium system, is essential for respecting parental rights to choose the best educational path for their children and for fostering institutional diversity, despite evidence suggesting modest value-added gains.
    Why: Parental aspirations and the demand for selective schooling are primary drivers, and existing sorting mechanisms mean simply abolishing tracks would not resolve stratification issues.

## What the experts agree on

*   Hungary's 6- and 8-year gymnasium systems select students at ages 10 and 12, respectively.
*   Early selection into these tracks disproportionately benefits high-SES, urban families.
*   The academic advantage of early-selective gymnasium tracks is largely a selection effect, with modest causal educational gains for admitted pupils.
*   Demographic decline is a significant factor forcing school-network consolidation decisions in the coming decades, with rural areas experiencing faster population loss.
*   Transition costs (retraining, building use, administration) are the dominant financial impact of structural reform, not steady-state operational costs.
*   The fragmentation of school maintainers (state, church, foundations) complicates uniform structural mandates.
*   International evidence suggests early tracking exacerbates social inequalities without necessarily improving overall academic achievement.
*   Poland's experience shows that educational reforms involving delayed tracking, while potentially beneficial for outcomes, are vulnerable to political reversal.
*   Any phase-out of the 6- and 8-year gymnasium system would require legislative amendment and a gradual ("felmenő rendszer") approach for current students.
*   Teacher shortages and administrative capacity are significant constraints on implementing large-scale structural reforms.
