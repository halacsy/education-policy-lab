## Interpretation

*   The current system of early selection (6- and 8-year gimnaziums) is a primary driver of socio-economic segregation in Hungarian education. [interpretation]
*   Demographic decline exacerbates the sustainability issues of the existing school network, particularly in rural areas, and early selection contributes to this problem by concentrating educational resources in selective institutions. [interpretation]
*   While early selection may offer perceived academic benefits for some, it comes at the cost of increased inequality and potentially hinders the overall development of the education system. [interpretation]
*   Structural reforms, such as phasing out early selection and implementing comprehensive schooling, are seen as more effective in addressing deep-seated equity issues, despite significant transition costs and political challenges. [interpretation]
*   Non-structural interventions, like targeted funding and curriculum standardization, offer a less disruptive path to improving equity and outcomes, drawing on international examples, but their effectiveness in fully offsetting the impacts of early selection is debated. [interpretation]
*   The fragmentation of school ownership (state, church, foundations) presents a significant hurdle to implementing uniform, top-down reforms. [interpretation]
*   Political will and durability are critical factors for the success of any reform, as demonstrated by the risk of reversal in similar contexts like Poland. [interpretation]
*   The concentration of high-achieving students in selective schools can negatively impact the peer composition and overall academic environment of general schools, particularly in urban areas. [interpretation]
*   The effectiveness of comprehensive schooling models is contingent on significant investment in teacher training and support systems to manage diverse student needs. [interpretation]
*   The success of international models, such as Finland or Portugal, is context-dependent and requires careful adaptation to the Hungarian socio-economic and institutional landscape. [interpretation]
*   The debate over early selection involves a trade-off between perceived academic specialization benefits and broader equity and social mobility goals. [interpretation]
*   The risk of shifting selection pressures to the non-state sector is a significant concern if state-run selective schools are abolished without parallel regulation of private institutions. [interpretation]
*   The long-term sustainability of any reform hinges on its ability to withstand political changes and secure consistent funding. [interpretation]

## Assumptions

*   A workable SES indicator exists at application time that can be reliably used for admissions. [assumption]
*   The central written admission examination can be reformed to be less reliant on test preparation and more reflective of innate ability or potential. [assumption]
*   Quotas for disadvantaged SES pupils will be effectively implemented and monitored across all selective schools. [assumption]
*   Existing school choice mechanisms will not fully counteract the intended effects of admission reform. [assumption]
*   General schools can absorb and challenge high-achievers if funded for it. [assumption]
*   A declining trajectory of selective places will not lead to a significant increase in demand for non-state selective schools. [assumption]
*   Advanced-programme funding will be sufficient to incentivize families to remain in general schools. [assumption]
*   Postponing academic selection requires a school infrastructure capable of handling high student heterogeneity. [assumption]
*   The Hungarian comprehensive track (general school) currently lacks the equivalent teacher quality and support systems that supported the Finnish transition. [assumption]
*   Sorting does not simply migrate to school choice between general schools and to non-state maintainers. [assumption]
*   Non-structural interventions (standards, targeted funding) are politically easier to implement and sustain than structural changes. [assumption]
*   The mechanisms of Portugal's TEIP and curriculum standards are transferable to the Hungarian socio-economic and institutional context. [assumption]
*   Compensation can meaningfully offset, not just mask, the effects of early sorting. [assumption]
*   Teacher shortages will not prevent the implementation of rigorous curriculum standards and targeted support. [assumption]

## Recommendations

*   **For Scenario S1 (Admission Reform within Current Structure):** Implement reforms to admission processes, including a more holistic assessment and a quota system for disadvantaged pupils, alongside strengthened compliance mechanisms. This should be piloted and evaluated for effectiveness in reducing SES advantage and improving representation.
*   **For Scenario S2 (Gradual Phase-Down of 6/8-Year Entry Places):** Gradually reduce the number of places in 6- and 8-year gimnaziums over a defined period. Simultaneously, introduce and fund advanced programmes in general schools and invest in teacher development to enhance their capacity to support high-achievers.
*   **For Scenario S3 (Comprehensive School to Age 14):** Transition to a unified comprehensive school structure until age 14 by phasing out all 6- and 8-year gimnaziums. This requires substantial investment in nationwide teacher retraining for differentiated instruction and the development of robust diagnostic and remedial support systems.
*   **For Scenario S4 (Keep Structure, Compensate General Schools):** Implement rigorous national curriculum standards across all schools. Introduce targeted support for disadvantaged schools, similar to Portugal's TEIP, and provide compensation and incentives to general schools to mitigate the effects of early selection.
*   **Cross-Scenario Recommendation:** Any reform should be accompanied by robust monitoring and evaluation mechanisms to assess its impact on equity, academic outcomes, and the school network.
*   **Cross-Scenario Recommendation:** Given the political sensitivity and complexity of school maintainers, reforms should consider phased implementation and broad stakeholder engagement to build consensus and ensure durability.
*   **Cross-Scenario Recommendation:** Explore legislative amendments that establish uniform structural rules across all maintainers while guaranteeing gradual teach-out paths for current students, respecting existing bilateral agreements with recognized churches.
*   **Cross-Scenario Recommendation:** To counter the risk of political reversal, consider establishing long-term multi-party pacts and compensatory funding mechanisms for local schools.
*   **Cross-Scenario Recommendation:** If early selection is retained in any form, implement measures to regulate non-state school admissions to limit socio-demographic sorting.
*   **Cross-Scenario Recommendation:** Prioritize investments in teacher training and support systems, regardless of the chosen reform path, as this is a critical factor for the success of any educational intervention.
