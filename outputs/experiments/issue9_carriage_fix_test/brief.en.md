# Policy brief — Early academic selection and the 6/8-year gimnázium

This brief synthesizes the expert evidence record and a stakeholder stress test on reforming Hungary's early academic selection (age 10–12 entry into 6/8-year gimnázium tracks). It is organized to separate what is well established from what is contested, to lay out real policy alternatives without crowning a winner, and to make visible which arguments would change the decision and which mainly consume attention. Every claim below carries an honest evidence tag; disagreements and minority views are preserved, not smoothed away.

## Scenario key (full detail: scenarios.en.md)
- S1 — S1 — Admissions and intake-equity regulation: keep the 6/8-year gimnázium structure, but regulate the admission mechanism (SES-aware proxies, uniform thresholds, coaching-resistant exams). See scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md for the full scenario.
- S2 — S2 — Supply-side contraction of the selective track: shrink state-sector 6/8-year capacity via thresholds/caps, leaving admission criteria within remaining places largely unchanged. See scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md for the full scenario.
- S3 — S3 — Structural delay of the selection age (Finnish/Polish model): raise the statutory tracking age to 14+, phasing 6/8-year entry into a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase, paired with anti-sorting rules. See scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md for the full scenario.
- S4 — S4 — Non-structural compensatory reform (Portugal-style): curriculum clarity, TEIP-style targeted funding, retention reduction, school-quality levelling, without changing the selection age. See scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md for the full scenario.
- S5 — S5 — Demographically-driven passive contraction: the no-new-policy baseline in which shrinking birth cohorts force network consolidation regardless of any active equity intervention. See scenarios.en.md / scenarios.hu.md for the full scenario.

## What we know
- Hungary's socio-economic status explains an unusually large share (19–25%) of PISA achievement variance, roughly double the OECD average, marking Hungary among the most stratified education systems in the OECD/EU. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Only 8–12% of each age cohort enters 6- or 8-year grammar school (gimnázium) tracks, selected via a central written admission examination at age 10 or 12, and entrants are strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Private admission-preparation costs (tutoring, prep courses) are substantial and unequally distributed, functioning as a de facto entry fee despite formally free tuition. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Church and foundation school maintainers, who can freely select students unlike catchment-bound state schools, have grown substantially since 2010 (from roughly 10% to roughly 25-30% of gimnázium enrolment) and complicate any uniform structural reform of the selection system. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Demand for 6/8-year grammar school places vastly exceeds available capacity, which has remained flat for over a decade despite this oversubscription. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- International evidence from multiple independent cross-country and quasi-experimental studies (21-cycle PISA/TIMSS meta-analysis, Hanushek & Woessmann, van Elk et al.) is highly consistent that earlier tracking increases socio-economic achievement gaps without raising mean performance. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Hungary's teaching workforce is aging (half aged 50+, only 7.2% under 30) and is concurrently absorbing an EU-funded salary reform (2024–2031), constraining the administrative bandwidth available for any near-term structural redesign. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- Annual births fell from roughly 125,000 (early 1990s) to 85–95,000 (early 2020s), with 2024 figures around 77,500 and 2025 estimated near 72,000, mechanically shrinking demand for all secondary tracks including 6/8-year gimnázium regardless of selection-policy design. [fact] (evidence: strong)
- The measured value-added of early-selective tracks (~10% larger learning gains than comparable peers) is real but modest, and its causal mechanism (pedagogy vs. peer composition vs. cream-skimming) remains unresolved. [fact] (evidence: moderate)
- Structural reform of early selection faces a genuinely uncertain, low near-term political ceiling: a 2026 ministerial equity critique of the system was quickly walked back to a consultative stance under opposition pressure. [fact] (evidence: strong)

## What we consider likely
- Admission-mechanism reform (S1) likely reduces the correlation between admission scores and family SES at the point of entry, but the magnitude for Hungary specifically is not established. [estimate]
- Shrinking state-sector selective capacity alone (S2) is likely to be substantially offset by continued church/foundation expansion, relocating rather than reducing net selectivity, given the documented threefold growth in non-state selective enrolment share since 2010. [estimate]
- Delayed tracking (S3) would likely reduce Hungary's socio-economic achievement gap over time, but only if anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment are enacted and enforced concurrently — the gain is conditional, not automatic. [estimate]
- Non-structural compensatory reform (S4) is likely to leave low-SES high-performing pupils without improved access to the selective track itself, since it does not touch the age-10-12 admission gate. [estimate]
- Passive demographic contraction (S5), absent explicit equity safeguards, is likely to concentrate selective capacity into fewer, more advantaged-skewed sites rather than passively resolve segregation. [estimate]

## Where experts disagree
### Does early selection itself cause the equity harm, or is it the segregation architecture (school choice, catchment, admissions discretion) that is the real driver?
- **international_comparison, educational_psychology, equity_and_social_mobility, finnish_reform**: Early tracking at age 10–12 is itself the mechanism amplifying socio-economic achievement gaps; delaying selection to 14–15 with intact comprehensive instruction would measurably reduce inequality. [value] Why: Cross-country meta-analyses (Hanushek & Woessmann 2006; van Elk et al. 2016; 21-cycle PISA/TIMSS meta-analysis) find early tracking increases socio-economic gaps without raising mean performance; Hungary's ESCS gradient (19–25% variance) is nearly double the OECD average, consistent with the predicted mechanism.
- **portuguese_reform, hungarian_education_system, school_network_planning**: Early academic selection is structurally separable from segregation-producing mechanisms (free school choice, admissions discretion, catchment migration); removing the track alone without reforming these other channels would likely relocate rather than resolve sorting. [value] Why: Portugal's abolition of age-10-12 selection substituted grade retention as sorting (26.6–31.2% of pupils retained at least once) and equity worsened 2015–2022 despite the non-selective structure; Hungary's free school choice already produces between-school sorting independent of formal tracking.
### Do international tracking-age reforms (Poland, Finland) transfer causally to Hungary's specific institutional context?
- **polish_reform, finnish_reform**: Poland's 1999 reform and Finland's peruskoulu provide causally credible evidence that delayed/abolished early selection improves learning and equity outcomes, and these mechanisms are directly relevant to Hungary's comparable pre-reform structure. [value] Why: Poland's reform produced causally-identified gains (0.25 SD math, 0.28 SD reading) via a natural experiment design; Hungary's 8/6-year gymnázium system structurally mirrors Poland's pre-1999 system.
- **international_comparison, finnish_reform** (minority): Transferability is uncertain because Hungary's school-choice rules, teacher labor market, and maintainer structure may mediate or block the typical tracking-inequality relationship, and no Hungary-specific natural experiment exists to confirm the causal effect size. [value] Why: A search audit found no Hungary-specific natural-experiment study varying the legal age of grammar-school entry; Finland's own teacher-education transformation (5-year Master's, ~10-15% acceptance) that co-occurred with peruskoulu has no Hungarian equivalent, confounding attribution.
### Is political reversal risk (as in Poland 2017) the dominant obstacle to durable reform, or is legal/institutional structure (maintainer veto points, felmenő rendszer) the binding constraint?
- **polish_reform, political_feasibility**: Evidence of a reform's success does not guarantee durability; a change of political majority can reverse structural reforms on values grounds regardless of empirical results, and Hungary's own political dynamics already show this risk is active. [value] Why: Poland's 2017 reversal of the 1999 reform was explicitly driven by political values and public opinion, not evidence-based analysis, despite 18 years of documented gains; Hungary's 2026 minister statement was immediately met with a mobilizing counter-narrative from the opposition, replicating this pattern.
- **legal_and_governance** (minority): The binding constraint on near-term reform is legal-institutional: statutory anchoring of the tracks, the felmenő rendszer (phase-out floor), and constitutionally-entrenched maintainer plurality require multi-stakeholder consent independent of which party holds power. [value] Why: The 6/8-year gimnázium is anchored in primary legislation requiring legislative amendment for discontinuation, constrained by the felmenő rendszer principle preventing forced removal of enrolled cohorts; church-maintained schools hold constitutionally-backed funding agreements creating a legal-political veto point distinct from ordinary electoral risk.
### Does removing selection carry a distinct psychological cost (self-concept, motivation, stereotype-threat mechanisms) beyond the economic/demographic/legal arguments?
- **educational_psychology** (minority): Beyond composition and segregation effects, early selection carries a distinct, robustly-replicated psychological cost: the big-fish-little-pond effect lowers academic self-concept for selected students even as it labels non-selected peers, and ranking-based structures push adolescents toward performance-avoidance rather than mastery orientation. [value] Why: The big-fish-little-pond effect (Marsh & Hau 2003; Seaton, Marsh & Craven 2009; Fang et al. 2018 meta-analysis) is confirmed across 41 countries and persists years after graduation; teacher-expectancy and stereotype-threat effects are systematically larger for stigmatized and low-SES pupils — the exact groups labeled by early selection.
### Is demographic cohort collapse, rather than selection-policy design, now the dominant force reshaping the school network?
- **demography, school_network_planning**: Births have fallen 30–35% from 1990s levels, forcing school-network consolidation independent of any selection-policy choice; the 6/8-year track decision cannot be isolated from this broader demographic-driven consolidation crisis. [value] Why: System infrastructure calibrated for ~110,000 annual cohorts must serve ~72,000 within a few years; 80 schools already operate with no first grade and 200 with ~10 first-graders; this mechanical shrinkage forces consolidation decisions regardless of the selection policy chosen.

## What we don't know
- Whether SES-aware admission proxies (S1) would durably reduce family-capital-driven sorting or whether advantaged families would simply redirect investment toward new preparation or relocation strategies once the exam changes. [estimate]
- Whether church and foundation maintainers can be brought into a uniform admission-equity or capacity regime without a specified legal-political mechanism, given their documented autonomy guarantees and growing enrolment share. [estimate]
- Whether state-sector capacity contraction (S2) produces a net reduction in selective-track segregation or is substantially offset by church/foundation/private expansion. [estimate]
- Whether anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment (S3) can be made simultaneously enforceable with a structural delay, or whether enactment will lag, allowing sorting to migrate before enforcement catches up. [assumption]
- Whether Hungary's teacher workforce, given its current age structure and the concurrent 2024-2031 salary reform, can realistically absorb a comprehensive lower-secondary transition (S3) within the 2-9 year timeline proposed. [estimate]
- Whether the observed Polish 1999 test-score gains would replicate in Hungary's specific institutional context, given that no Hungarian natural experiment has directly tested a tracking-age delay. [estimate]
- Whether TEIP-style targeted funding (S4) can be made durable against fiscal consolidation pressure in Hungary's specific budget environment, or whether it will be cut in a first consolidation round. [estimate]
- Whether non-structural reform (S4) can measurably narrow achievement gaps for low-SES high-performing pupils specifically, given that their primary barrier (exclusion from the selective track at age 10-12) is left entirely unaddressed by compensatory funding to their home schools. [estimate]
- Whether Hungary's fertility rate will stabilize around 1.2-1.3 or continue falling below 1.1, directly affecting whether cohort size bottoms out near 72,000 or falls further toward 60,000 annually by 2035. [estimate]
- Whether demographically-driven consolidation (S5), absent explicit equity safeguards, will concentrate or disperse selective-track segregation across regions, given documented extreme regional divergence in birth-rate decline. [estimate]

## What could be done
- **S1 — Regulate the admission gate, keep the structure**: Introduce SES-aware admission proxies, uniform score thresholds, and coaching-resistant exam design within the existing 6/8-year gimnázium structure, aiming to reduce family-capital-driven sorting at the point of entry without touching the selection age or track structure itself.
- **S2 — Shrink the selective footprint via supply-side caps**: Reduce the state-sector share of 6/8-year places through score thresholds, capacity caps, or demographically-driven consolidation, leaving admission criteria within remaining places largely unchanged, aiming to shrink the selective track's overall footprint.
- **S3 — Delay the selection age structurally (Finnish/Polish model)**: Raise the statutory age of first academic tracking to 14+, phasing out or converting 6- and 8-year entry points into a unified comprehensive lower-secondary phase, paired with explicit, concurrently enforceable anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment.
- **S4 — Compensate without changing the structure (Portugal model)**: Pursue equity and achievement gains through curriculum clarity, TEIP-style targeted funding for disadvantaged schools, retention reduction, and school-quality levelling, without changing the 10-12 selection age or track structure.
- **S5 — Do nothing new: let demographic contraction drive change**: Serve as the no-new-policy baseline in which shrinking birth cohorts force network consolidation and a natural decline in the selective track's absolute size and share, regardless of any active equity or structural intervention.

## What each option costs
- **S1**: Winners: state-school families and disadvantaged pupils if enforcement genuinely covers all maintainers; likely losers: teachers who absorb new item-writing, verification, and compliance workload without dedicated funding or staffing, unless explicitly resourced. The equity gain is bounded and possibly reversed by church/foundation maintainer exemption and preparation-investment relocation. [assumption]
- **S1**: A decree-level reform (EMMI rendelet) is faster to implement than statutory change but also more easily reversed by a future minister without primary legislation, trading durability for speed. [assumption]
- **S2**: Likely losers: state-school families facing longer commutes or costlier alternatives, and teachers facing unquantified redundancy/redeployment risk with no published staffing-transition plan; likely winners (if displacement is real): church/foundation maintainers, who can absorb displaced advantaged demand while remaining unconstrained. [assumption]
- **S2**: The scenario's central equity claim risks being cosmetic: a policy framed as reducing selection may instead relocate it, worsening SES-skew in remaining state-sector selective places without any enforceable lever on non-state maintainer expansion. [assumption]
- **S3**: Highest potential equity payoff among the five options, but also the highest implementation cost and risk: teacher retraining and reallocation for an aging, salary-reform-absorbed workforce, facility conversion, and anti-sorting enforcement infrastructure are all unbudgeted; church-maintained schools may resist conversion without a tested legal compulsion mechanism. [assumption]
- **S3**: Political durability risk: Poland's 2017 reversal of its own successful 1999 reform shows that even well-evidenced structural change is not durable against a change of government, and no cardinal-law or cross-party entrenchment mechanism is specified here to prevent an analogous reversal. [assumption]
- **S4**: Lowest implementation risk and fastest to deliver, but the equity ceiling is documented as lower: low-SES high-performing pupils receive no structural protection against being screened out at the existing age-10-12 gate, so this scenario risks becoming a well-funded way of leaving segregation exactly where it is, especially if the targeted-funding floor is cut in a fiscal-consolidation round as historical patterns suggest. [assumption]
- **S5**: Zero near-term political cost, but absent deliberate equity safeguards, consolidation is documented as likely to concentrate selective capacity into fewer, more advantaged-skewed sites — the losers being Roma and poor families in the hardest-hit regions, while extreme regional divergence in birth-decline means uniform national assumptions about this baseline are themselves unreliable. [assumption]

## What research could resolve
- A discontinuity or matched-cohort analysis of admission outcomes and preparation-spending patterns before and after a pilot exam redesign, tracked over multiple admission cycles, to test whether SES-aware proxies durably reduce sorting or merely displace it (relevant to S1).
- A legal opinion or Constitutional Court guidance on the scope for mandating admission-rule or capacity compliance among non-state (church/foundation) maintainers, plus publication of the full text of church-state funding and autonomy accords (relevant to S1, S2, S3).
- A multi-year tracking study of enrolment shifts across all maintainer types following a state-sector capacity policy change, using disaggregated KSH data by programme length and maintainer, to test whether S2 relocates or reduces net selectivity.
- Publication of a concrete legislative package pairing the tracking-age delay with named, operational anti-sorting enforcement mechanisms and monitoring bodies, plus early-implementation compliance data (relevant to S3).
- A detailed teacher-workforce feasibility study modelling retirement/attrition rates, retraining capacity, and salary-reform bandwidth overlap, published before legislative enactment of a comprehensive lower-secondary transition (relevant to S3).
- A staggered, regional natural experiment or pilot phase-in of delayed tracking in a subset of Hungarian districts, with matched administrative-data outcome tracking against non-pilot regions (relevant to S3).
- A published multi-year budget commitment with cardinal-law or equivalent statutory protection for targeted funding, tracked against actual disbursement through at least one fiscal-consolidation cycle (relevant to S4).
- A longitudinal subgroup analysis tracking low-SES high-performing pupils' access to selective tracks and achievement trajectories before and after targeted-funding implementation, compared against a matched cohort under S1-style admission-equity reform (relevant to S4).
- Longitudinal fertility surveys (five-year updates to Eurostat projections) and direct cohort-component demographic modelling with scenario branching, to resolve whether cohort size bottoms out near 72,000 or continues falling (relevant to S5).
- Detailed school-by-school enrollment forecasts and feasibility studies of mixed school models in low-density regions, plus segregation-index tracking before, during, and after consolidation (relevant to S5).

## What people must decide
- Whether equity in access to academic tracks should take precedence over parental school choice and church/foundation maintainer autonomy where these values conflict, and if so, by how much and through what legal instrument. [value]
- Whether Hungary is willing to accept the multi-year political, legal, and workforce transition costs of structural reform (S3) in exchange for a larger, but conditional and unproven-in-Hungary, equity gain — or whether a faster, lower-risk, lower-ceiling path (S1, S4) is preferred. [value]
- Whether the state should compel or merely negotiate with church and foundation maintainers on admission-equity or capacity rules, given their constitutionally protected autonomy — a decision with direct consequences for whether any of S1-S3 can achieve system-wide (not just state-sector) equity effects. [value]
- Whether policymakers accept that doing nothing (S5) is itself an active policy choice with foreseeable distributional consequences, requiring the same scrutiny and accountability as an active reform, or whether it should continue to be treated as a neutral default. [value]
- How much weight to give to the psychological cost of early ranking and labeling (big-fish-little-pond effect, stereotype threat) as an independent justification for reform, beyond the economic and demographic arguments. [value]

## What to verify with real stakeholders
- A1 (Coaching-resistant exams and SES-aware proxies (S1) can reduce family-capital advantage, but only if enforcement covers church and foundation maintainers too; otherwise displaced demand relocates and the equity gain is cosmetic.): policy_design_fixable — The core mechanism is moderately evidenced and the displacement risk is real but addressable in principle through a policy-design choice — negotiated, binding coverage of non-state maintainers plus independent monitoring — even though no such mechanism currently exists in the record; it is not a value conflict but an implementation gap.
- A2 (Imposing uniform admission-equity thresholds on church and foundation maintainers without their negotiated consent would breach constitutionally protected maintainer autonomy and subsidiarity.): value_conflict — This pits equity/state-mandated uniformity directly against subsidiarity and constitutionally-anchored institutional autonomy — a genuine values collision with no technical fix; the legal question of whether compulsion is even permissible remains separately unsettled, but the underlying value tension does not disappear even with a favorable legal ruling.
- A3 (New exam design, SES-proxy verification, and compliance monitoring create unfunded administrative and psychometric workload for teachers, with no cost category accounting for their time or training.): policy_design_fixable — This is a resourcing gap that can be closed by explicit budget allocation and by locating the new tasks (item-writing, verification, monitoring) in a dedicated ministry/exam-board unit rather than dispersing them onto classroom teachers — a design choice, not an irreducible tradeoff.
- A4 (Shrinking only state-sector selective capacity (S2) while church/foundation maintainers freely expand does not reduce selection but relocates it, since their enrolment share has roughly tripled since 2010 with no legal mechanism to constrain them.): needs_more_info — The direction of the risk is well-argued and widely shared across nearly every stakeholder voice, but the magnitude of actual demand relocation at comparable scale is empirically untested — no pilot or mechanism evidence exists to confirm whether displacement would be full, partial, or largely absent; several stakeholders explicitly flagged this as the decisive unresolved empirical question before taking a final position.
- A5 (Capacity caps in state-maintained selective schools (S2) carry unquantified teacher redundancy and redeployment risk, with no published staffing-transition plan.): policy_design_fixable — A published redeployment guarantee — no forced job loss, retraining support, seniority protection — is exactly the kind of design commitment several stakeholders named as sufficient to move their stance from opposition to conditional support; this is a policy-design gap, not a structural impossibility.
- A6 (Reducing state-sector selective capacity (S2) restricts families' predictable local schooling options and pushes them toward longer commutes or costlier private alternatives without actually reducing selection.): irreducible_tradeoff — If capacity is reduced without an equivalent constraint on non-state expansion, the tradeoff between shrinking the state-sector selective footprint and preserving accessible, affordable local options for state-school families is real and not resolvable by better implementation alone — reducing one side's exposure necessarily changes access for the other unless the whole system (all maintainer types) contracts together, which is not what S2 as designed proposes.
- A7 (Delaying tracking (S3) is the only reform directly targeting the age-10-12 exclusion point, and strong cross-national evidence links delayed selection to reduced socio-economic achievement gaps without loss of average performance.): evidence_answerable — The cross-national mechanism itself is strongly evidenced; what remains open is Hungary-specific transferability, which further research (a staggered regional pilot, workforce feasibility study) could substantially resolve rather than leave as a permanent value dispute.
- A8 (A comprehensive-schooling transition risks eroding equity gains, as in Poland's 2017 reversal, unless anti-sorting rules for school choice and catchment are legislated concurrently with the tracking-age delay, not as an optional add-on.): policy_design_fixable — Multiple stakeholders converged in the reciprocity round on a concrete, implementable fix: legislate both elements in the same act with named, operational compliance mechanisms and a baseline enforcement-capacity audit — this converts an open uncertainty into a specific design requirement rather than an irreducible conflict.
- A9 (A comprehensive-lower-secondary conversion (S3) should not be layered onto a teaching workforce that is disproportionately aging and still absorbing the 2024-2031 salary reform, without a sequenced, funded retraining plan.): policy_design_fixable — The demographic facts are settled and strong; what is contested is only whether a sequenced plan (e.g., phase-in after or alongside the salary reform, cohort-by-cohort felmenő rendszer rollout with protected retraining slots) can be designed and funded — several stakeholders proposed concrete sequencing options in the reciprocity round rather than treating this as an insurmountable barrier.
- A10 (Forcing church-maintained 6/8-year tracks into a unified comprehensive structure without their consent would violate subsidiarity and religious-institutional autonomy, and imported comprehensive models are not settled given Poland's own reversal.): value_conflict — This combines a genuine values collision (institutional/religious autonomy vs. state-mandated structural equity) with an open legal-design question (whether a voluntary opt-out could resolve it); the value tension itself would persist even if a voluntary mechanism were designed, since some equity advocates would regard voluntary participation as insufficient to close the displacement channel.
- A11 (Parents whose children are mid-transition through the existing 6/8-year system need an airtight, published cohort-by-cohort transition calendar so no family is left guessing which rules apply to their child.): not_decision_relevant — This is already addressed in principle by the felmenő rendszer mechanism documented across S1-S3, and a detailed transition calendar is an implementation-communication task that would not change which scenario is chosen — it is an important delivery requirement once a scenario is selected, not a factor that shifts the underlying policy choice; the ledger itself flags this cluster as already answered.
- A12 (Targeted, TEIP-style funding (S4) is the fastest, lowest-disruption way to improve outcomes in disadvantaged schools since it requires no teacher reallocation, retraining, or structural transition.): irreducible_tradeoff — This claim is broadly true and not itself in dispute, but it names a genuine and irreducible tradeoff, not a fixable gap: speed and low disruption (S4) trade directly against equity ceiling and structural reach (S3) — you cannot have the fastest, least disruptive option and the deepest structural fix simultaneously; the choice between them is a values/priorities decision, not an engineering problem.
- A13 (Compensatory funding (S4) is not a substitute for structural admission reform: low-SES high-performing pupils still get screened out at the age-10-12 gate, and Portugal's 2015-2022 experience shows compensatory funding can coexist with worsening equity.): evidence_answerable — The underlying mechanism claim (compensatory funding alone leaves the selective gate untouched) is strongly evidenced and largely conceded even by S4 supporters in the reciprocity round; what remains open — whether S4 as a complement to structural reform, rather than a substitute, can close this gap — is answerable by the specifically proposed longitudinal subgroup study tracking low-SES high-performer track access.
- A14 (Targeted funding floors for disadvantaged schools (S4) must receive durable statutory (cardinal-law) protection, since such lines are typically the first cut in fiscal-consolidation rounds, leaving the equity promise unfulfilled.): policy_design_fixable — A specific, testable design fix (published multi-year budget commitment with statutory protection, tracked through at least one consolidation cycle) was named by multiple stakeholders and by the research-institute voice as the condition that would resolve the concern — this is a design and monitoring gap, not a values collision, though its durability against future qualified-majority amendment remains genuinely uncertain and worth flagging honestly.
- A15 (Demographic-driven school consolidation (S5) is proceeding informally at the tankerület level with no coordinated national costing, staffing-transition plan, or equity safeguard, even though consolidation is documented as nearly certain within 5-10 years.): policy_design_fixable — The demographic driver is settled fact; the absence of coordination is a governance-design gap that a national framework (published regional consolidation calendar, redeployment guarantees, equity-safeguard monitoring) could directly close — several stakeholders named exactly this fix as sufficient to shift their stance from opposition.
- A16 (Calling demographic contraction a 'passive' baseline is a framing error, since active administrative levers already shape its equity outcome, and doing nothing while acknowledging the risk is itself a policy choice with foreseeable disparate impact on Roma and poor families.): communication_fixable — The substantive equity risk is already documented and acknowledged in the scenario's own equity-impact analysis; the remaining problem is that the 'passive'/no-new-policy framing obscures this acknowledged risk from decision-makers and the public rather than adding new uncertainty — renaming and reframing S5 explicitly as an actively-shaped baseline, with visible accountability for the tankerület/threshold levers already in use, would resolve the objection without requiring any new substantive policy.
- A17 (Families facing school merger or closure due to falling enrolment need a published, multi-year regional consolidation calendar with guaranteed minimum notice periods.): policy_design_fixable — This is a concrete administrative-planning request that a coordinated national consolidation framework could deliver directly; the main constraint noted (maintainers' historical preference for case-by-case timing over binding multi-year calendars) is an administrative-capacity and political-will question, not an unresolvable structural conflict.
- A18 (Letting demographic contraction proceed through existing tankerület-level, local decision-making respects subsidiarity and avoids top-down state engineering justified by contested equity theory.): value_conflict — This is a direct values collision between subsidiarity/local self-determination and centrally-coordinated equity safeguarding (A15/A16/A17) — both sides can be internally coherent and evidence-consistent, and no single design fix satisfies both the preference for local autonomy and the demand for centrally accountable equity monitoring simultaneously; the holder itself concedes the empirical uncertainty but the underlying value preference for local control would persist even if that uncertainty were resolved.
- A19 (Church and foundation maintainers (roughly 25-30% of enrolment) are exempt from decree-level admission-equity rules, risking a cosmetic equity gain unless independent monitoring confirms actual admission-rate changes for Roma and disadvantaged pupils.): needs_more_info — The exemption itself is documented, but whether displaced advantaged demand will meaningfully shift toward church/foundation schools at a scale that neutralizes the equity gain, and whether Roma/disadvantaged admission rates will actually change, has not been measured — this requires the independent monitoring data the cluster itself calls for before it can be resolved either way.

## Where the red herrings are
- A11: The demand for an airtight, cohort-by-cohort transition calendar for families mid-way through the current system is a legitimate and important delivery detail, but it does not change which scenario should be chosen: the felmenő rendszer (phase-out floor) principle already addresses this in every structural scenario (S1-S3), and the calendar itself is an implementation task to be resolved after a policy choice is made, not a factor that should move the choice itself.
