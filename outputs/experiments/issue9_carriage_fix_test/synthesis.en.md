# Synthesis

## Overview
The expert record converges on a robust empirical core—Hungary's early selection at age 10–12 into 6/8-year gimnázium tracks coincides with the EU's highest socio-economic segregation and achievement gaps, modest and contested value-added, and international evidence (Poland, Finland, Portugal, Bavaria, Northern Ireland, Romania) that tracking age matters but is neither necessary nor sufficient for equity outcomes—while disagreeing sharply on causal mechanism, on whether comparable jurisdictions' reforms transfer to Hungary's institutional context, and on what is politically and administratively feasible given demographic collapse, maintainer plurality, legal constraints, teacher-workforce aging, and a volatile political environment where a minister's equity critique was immediately met with a 'communist imposition' counter-narrative. Several experts converge on shared findings but attribute them to different mechanisms (selection/composition effects vs. psychological costs vs. legal-institutional veto points vs. sheer demographic mathematics), and the record explicitly preserves contested, moderate, and weak evidence tags rather than resolving them into a single verdict.

## Disagreement map
### Does early selection itself cause the equity harm, or is it selection-effect composition/segregation architecture (school choice, catchment, admissions discretion) that is the real driver?
- **international_comparison, educational_psychology, equity_and_social_mobility, finnish_reform**: Early tracking at age 10–12 is itself the mechanism amplifying socio-economic achievement gaps; delaying selection to 14–15 with intact comprehensive instruction would measurably reduce inequality. Why: Cross-country meta-analyses (Hanushek & Woessmann 2006; van Elk et al. 2016; 21-cycle PISA/TIMSS meta-analysis) find early tracking increases socio-economic gaps without raising mean performance; Hungary's ESCS gradient (19–25% variance) is nearly double the OECD average, consistent with the tracking-age mechanism predicted by this literature.
- **portuguese_reform, hungarian_education_system, school_network_planning**: Early academic selection is structurally separable from segregation-producing mechanisms (free school choice, admissions discretion, catchment migration); removing the track alone without reforming these other channels would likely relocate rather than resolve sorting. Why: Portugal's abolition of age-10-12 selection substituted grade retention as sorting (26.6–31.2% of pupils retained at least once) and equity worsened 2015–2022 despite the non-selective structure; Hungary's free school choice already produces between-school sorting independent of formal tracking (Kertesi & Kezdi), so abolition alone would not close this channel.

### Do international tracking-age reforms (Poland, Finland) transfer causally to Hungary's specific institutional context?
- **polish_reform, finnish_reform**: Poland's 1999 reform and Finland's peruskoulu provide causally credible evidence that delayed/abolished early selection improves learning and equity outcomes, and these mechanisms are directly relevant to Hungary's comparable pre-reform structure. Why: Poland's reform produced causally-identified gains (0.25 SD math, 0.28 SD reading) via a natural experiment design; Horn & Drucker (2016) explicitly referenced the Polish case in Hungarian policy discourse as directly transferable, and Hungary's 8/6-year gymnázium system structurally mirrors Poland's pre-1999 system.
- **international_comparison, finnish_reform** (minority): Transferability is uncertain because Hungary's school-choice rules, teacher labor market, and maintainer structure may mediate or block the typical tracking-inequality relationship, and no Hungary-specific natural experiment exists to confirm the causal effect size. Why: A search audit found no Hungary-specific natural-experiment study varying the legal age of grammar-school entry; the strongest Hungarian causal evidence is observational/matching-based, and Finland's own teacher-education transformation (5-year Master's, ~10-15% acceptance) that co-occurred with peruskoulu has no Hungarian equivalent, confounding attribution of the reform's equity gains to age of selection alone.

### Is political reversal risk (as in Poland 2017) the dominant obstacle to durable reform, or is legal/institutional structure (maintainer veto points, felmenő rendszer) the binding constraint?
- **polish_reform, political_feasibility**: Evidence of a reform's success does not guarantee durability; a change of political majority can reverse structural reforms on values grounds regardless of empirical results, and Hungary's own political dynamics (opposition's 'communist imposition' framing) show this risk is already active. Why: Poland's 2017 reversal of the 1999 reform was explicitly driven by political values and public opinion, not evidence-based analysis, despite 18 years of documented gains, and imposed severe transition costs (unplaced students, teacher redundancies); Hungary's June 2026 minister statement was immediately met with a mobilizing loss-aversion counter-narrative from the parliamentary opposition, replicating this pattern in real time.
- **legal_and_governance** (minority): The binding constraint on near-term reform is legal-institutional: statutory anchoring of the tracks (Nkt.), the felmenő rendszer (8-year phase-out floor), and constitutionally-entrenched maintainer plurality (especially church funding-agreement autonomy) require multi-stakeholder consent independent of which party holds power. Why: The 6/8-year gimnázium is anchored in primary legislation (Nkt.) requiring legislative amendment for discontinuation, constrained by the felmenő rendszer principle preventing forced removal of enrolled cohorts (up to 8-year phase-out); church-maintained schools hold constitutionally-backed funding agreements creating a legal-political veto point distinct from ordinary electoral risk.

### Does removing selection carry a distinct psychological cost (self-concept, motivation, stereotype-threat mechanisms) beyond the economic/demographic/legal arguments?
- **educational_psychology** (minority): Beyond composition and segregation effects, early selection carries a distinct, robustly-replicated psychological cost: the big-fish-little-pond effect lowers academic self-concept for selected students even as it labels non-selected peers, and ranking-based structures push adolescents toward performance-avoidance rather than mastery orientation — a mechanism operating independently of the economic/demographic arguments made elsewhere in the record. Why: The big-fish-little-pond effect (Marsh & Hau 2003; Seaton, Marsh & Craven 2009; Fang et al. 2018 meta-analysis) is confirmed across 41 countries and persists years after graduation; teacher-expectancy and stereotype-threat effects, while contested in magnitude, are found to be systematically larger for stigmatized and low-SES pupils — the exact groups labeled by early selection — representing a psychological causal channel distinct from the composition/selection-effect mechanism emphasized by economics-oriented experts.

### Is demographic cohort collapse, rather than selection-policy design, now the dominant force reshaping the school network?
- **demography, school_network_planning**: Births have fallen 30–35% from 1990s levels, forcing school-network consolidation independent of any selection-policy choice; the 6/8-year track decision cannot be isolated from this broader demographic-driven consolidation crisis, and uniform national selection rules are becoming operationally moot in many low-enrollment districts. Why: System infrastructure calibrated for ~110,000 annual cohorts must serve ~72,000 within a few years; 80 schools already operate with no first grade and 200 with ~10 first-graders; this mechanical shrinkage forces consolidation decisions regardless of whether early selection is reformed, retained, or abolished.

## What the experts agree on
- Hungary's socio-economic status explains an unusually large share (19–25%) of PISA achievement variance, roughly double the OECD average, marking Hungary among the most stratified education systems in the OECD/EU. [evidence: strong] (hungarian_education_system, international_comparison, equity_and_social_mobility, demography, portuguese_reform, finnish_reform, educational_psychology, education_finance)
- Only 8–12% of each age cohort enters 6- or 8-year grammar school tracks, selected via central written admission examination at age 10 or 12, and entrants are strongly skewed toward high-SES, urban families. [evidence: strong] (hungarian_education_system, equity_and_social_mobility, school_network_planning, legal_and_governance, education_finance, implementation_planning)
- The measured value-added of early-selective tracks (~10% larger learning gains than comparable peers) is real but modest, and its causal mechanism (pedagogy vs. peer composition vs. cream-skimming) remains unresolved. [evidence: moderate] (hungarian_education_system, equity_and_social_mobility, educational_psychology, education_finance)
- Private admission-preparation costs (tutoring, prep courses) are substantial and unequally distributed, functioning as a de facto entry fee despite formally free tuition. [evidence: strong] (hungarian_education_system, education_finance, legal_and_governance, implementation_planning)
- Church and foundation school maintainers, who can freely select students unlike catchment-bound state schools, have grown substantially since 2010 and complicate any uniform structural reform of the selection system. [evidence: strong] (hungarian_education_system, legal_and_governance, education_finance, political_feasibility)
- Demand for 6/8-year grammar school places vastly exceeds available capacity, which has remained flat for over a decade despite this oversubscription. [evidence: strong] (hungarian_education_system, school_network_planning, implementation_planning)
- Structural reform of early selection faces a genuinely uncertain, low near-term political ceiling: the June 2026 ministerial equity critique was quickly walked back to a consultative stance under opposition pressure. [evidence: strong] (political_feasibility, implementation_planning, legal_and_governance, education_finance)
- Hungary's aging teaching workforce (half aged 50+, only 7.2% under 30) and concurrent EU-funded salary reform (2024-2031) constrain the administrative bandwidth available for any near-term structural redesign. [evidence: strong] (implementation_planning, demography)
- International evidence, from multiple independent cross-country and quasi-experimental studies, is highly consistent that earlier tracking increases socio-economic achievement gaps without raising mean performance. [evidence: strong] (international_comparison, portuguese_reform, polish_reform, finnish_reform, equity_and_social_mobility, educational_psychology)
