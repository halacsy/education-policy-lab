05 / DECISION PACKAGE PAIR

A tényleges kimenetekInspect the actual outputs

Baseline · 677 words

Hungary's decision on its 6-/8-year gimnázium tracks spans a real option space from full preservation to structural abolition, with several intermediate levers available and combinable. Six transformation paths anchor the space. TP-t1 (status quo with monitoring) changes nothing structurally, relying on existing PISA/ABC reporting infrastructure; its leading constraint is that this same infrastructure has a documented history of not translating into policy correction, so the mechanism produces data without adaptation. TP-t2 (delay selection to age 14-16) targets the earliest and most consequential sorting point directly, drawing on Poland's 1999 comprehensive-tier reform, which is associated with PISA and labour-market gains; its binding constraints are workforce capacity (an aging, undersized teaching corps) and durability, since Poland's reform was substantially reversed after 2016 despite measured success, and even non-abolition proposals in Hungary have triggered high-intensity 'communist unification' framing. TP-t3 (preserve tracks, reform admissions) works within the existing Klebelsberg Centre-administered exam process, leveraging the 2000s precedent that administrative desegregation rules produced temporary segregation reductions; its constraint is coverage — church-maintained and private schools sit outside this authority and can absorb displaced selective demand, and that historical precedent also stagnated over time. TP-t4 (zoning/choice/exemption reform without touching track age) isolates a distinct causal channel — geographic sorting and the 2015 Curia exemption permitting segregated maintainer-run schools — leaving admissions and track age untouched; its constraint is that centralization under KLIK has curtailed the very municipal planning autonomy this lever depends on, and it invites direct legal confrontation with maintainer autonomy. TP-t5 (quotas and outreach within existing tracks) targets compositional access without altering the mechanism or timing of selection; its constraint is a genuine empirical uncertainty distinct from any value question — evidence on whether track value-added exists independent of family background is weak, and at least one study found no measurable tertiary benefit, meaning the 'prize' being redistributed may not durably benefit recipients. TP-t6 (comprehensive pilot with opt-in enrichment) explicitly borrows Finland's 1972-77 mechanism while building in awareness of its confounds and of the post-1990s lesson that choice-driven resegregation can reassert itself absent zoning guardrails, linking itself to T4; its constraint is that even a successful pilot may not secure durable political buy-in, and volunteer regions may be unrepresentative. Across these paths, empirical uncertainty and value choice are distinguishable but entangled. Empirically open questions include: the independent causal effect of track age itself on segregation net of choice and exemptions; where displaced sorting demand would reallocate if early tracking were removed; whether value-added gains from selective tracks persist into labor-market outcomes or reflect background composition; and whether Poland's gains stemmed from tracking delay itself versus concurrent curriculum and workforce investment. Value choices are separate: how much weight to give durability versus ambition (bold structural change risks reversal; incremental change risks entrenching sorting indefinitely); how far central authority should reach into maintainer and religious-institution autonomy that was foundational to the 1989-90 settlement; and whether equal access to prestige institutions has intrinsic worth independent of measured long-run benefit. Material disciplinary mechanisms recur across lenses: centralized entrance-exam admissions and their SES-linked sorting advantage; maintainer exemptions placing church/private schools outside ministerial authority; the 2015 Curia ruling protecting exemption-based segregation; demographic contraction concentrating cohorts in urban selective tracks; and the historical non-translation of monitoring data into corrective policy. Political mobilization capacity — demonstrated by reframing even mild consultation proposals as ideological unification — constrains any path touching track age or admissions directly, while fiscal exposure is low across all options since none requires large per-pupil funding shifts. Decision-makers must weigh: whether to accept near-term political safety at the cost of entrenching documented harm; whether to pursue single-lever tractability or multi-channel bundled reform at higher implementation risk; how much central authority to assert over maintainer autonomy; and whether to gather further pilot evidence before acting nationally, given that harms are already well-documented and actively intensifying under demographic contraction. The research most likely to shift this choice concerns displaced-demand reallocation pathways, the durability determinants behind Poland's reversal, the exempt sector's share of measured segregation, and whether any structural design can achieve lasting cross-coalition support in Hungary's veto-heavy political system.

+ psychology · 664 words

Hungary's decision on whether to keep the 6- and 8-year gimnázium tracks spans six live options, none of which is a clear winner across all lenses. The empirical core is well-documented: Hungary is an international outlier, with roughly 85% of schools capable of selecting students versus a 43% OECD average, selection beginning at age 10, extreme between-school variance in outcomes, and a Ministerial acknowledgment of the problem unaccompanied by corrective action. What remains genuinely uncertain—an empirical rather than a values question—is how much of this segregation is caused by the tracking mechanism itself versus independent channels: geographic settlement patterns, school-choice commuting, and legal exemptions for church- and privately-maintained schools (including a Curia ruling permitting segregated schooling). This matters because it determines which lever, if pulled alone, would actually move the needle. Six directions are on the table. Status quo with monitoring (T1) requires no new legal authority and preserves the fragile 1989-90 cross-ideological coalition, but existing PISA/ABC monitoring has no track record of translating into policy correction, so segregation likely intensifies as shrinking cohorts concentrate further into urban elite tracks. Delaying selection age to 14-16 (T2) directly targets the earliest and most consequential sorting point, drawing on Poland's 1999 reform, but faces high political mobilization (even mild consultation proposals were framed as "communist unification"), an aging and undersized teacher workforce, and Poland's own post-2016 reversal as a durability warning. Reforming admissions mechanisms while preserving track structure (T3) works within existing Ministry authority and could weaken tutoring-dependent sorting advantages, but church/private schools sit outside Klebelsberg Centre authority and could absorb displaced selective demand, and prior administrative desegregation efforts stagnated mid-decade. Structural desegregation via zoning and choice reform (T4) attacks a genuinely distinct causal channel—geographic concentration and maintainer exemptions—without touching the politically explosive track-age question, but invites direct legal confrontation with religious-institution autonomy and is constrained by KLIK centralization curtailing municipal planning capacity. Targeted quotas and outreach (T5) is the lowest-friction, lowest-legal-threshold option, but rests on contested evidence: value-added gains from elite tracks may be substantially attributable to family background rather than institutional effect, with some studies finding no measurable long-run labor-market benefit. Finally, a comprehensive de-tracking pilot paired with zoning controls (T6) is the most methodologically self-aware option, explicitly designed to isolate Hungary-specific effects from the confounds that muddy Finland's 1972-77 comprehensive shift, but faces the same durability risk as T2 and depends on non-representative volunteer regions. The educational-psychology lens materially reshapes how the quota/outreach and admissions-only options should be read. Admitting more disadvantaged students into elite tracks without changing the tracking structure itself risks subjecting them to well-documented big-fish-little-pond dynamics: equally able students placed in a highly selective, high-achieving peer group can develop lower academic self-concept than they would in a less selective setting, even as their measured achievement rises. Combined with contested value-added evidence, this raises a genuine open question of whether expanding access relocates disadvantaged students into a psychologically costly reference group without commensurate benefit, or whether early, kindergarten-stage outreach—consistent with self-determination theory's emphasis on competence-building before the age-10 selection point—can offset this. By contrast, delaying selection (T2) and comprehensive de-tracking with enrichment (T6) more directly reduce exposure to early labeling and performance-orientation pressures tied to high-stakes sorting, aligning with self-determination theory's preference for autonomy-supportive rather than controlling structures, though effects remain context-dependent on implementation fidelity. Decision-makers must choose between structural correction of the core sorting mechanism (T2, T6) against real reversal and mobilization risk, versus narrower, more legally contained reforms (T3, T4, T5) that leave the age-10 funnel intact and thus deliver only partial equity gains by construction. They must also decide how to treat institutional autonomy claims by church and private maintainers against uniform anti-segregation coverage, and whether further piloting is prudent caution or an unaffordable delay given currently-affected cohorts. The research most likely to change this choice: isolating the independent causal effect of track structure itself net of choice and exemptions, and determining whether track-attributed gains persist into labor-market outcomes once background and peer effects are properly accounted for.
