**Official abstract (verbatim):**
> "Even though some countries track students into differing-ability schools by age 10, others keep their entire secondary-school system comprehensive. To estimate the effects of such institutional differences in the face of country heterogeneity, we employ an international differences-in-differences approach. We identify tracking effects by comparing differences in outcome between primary and secondary school across tracked and non-tracked systems. Six international student assessments provide eight pairs of achievement contrasts for between 18 and 26 cross-country comparisons. The results suggest that early tracking increases educational inequality. While less clear, there is also a tendency for early tracking to reduce mean performance."

**Key findings relevant to early school tracking / selection (paraphrased):**
1. **Identification strategy:** country-level differences-in-differences comparing changes in the achievement distribution between primary school (before tracking anywhere) and secondary school (after tracking in early-tracking systems), which nets out fixed country heterogeneity. Data: six international assessments (PIRLS, TIMSS, PISA) yielding eight primary–secondary achievement contrasts across 18–26 countries.
2. **Inequality:** early tracking significantly increases inequality of achievement — the dispersion (standard deviation) of student outcomes grows between primary and secondary school markedly more in systems that track before roughly age 10–12 than in comprehensive systems (main diff-in-diff results, Tables 2–3 of the article/WP).
3. **Efficiency:** there is no performance gain from early tracking; point estimates for the effect on mean achievement are mostly negative (early tracking tends, if anything, to *reduce* average performance at the secondary level), though these estimates are less precise (Table 4 / Section on level effects).
4. **Distributional pattern:** the increase in inequality is driven disproportionately by losses at the bottom of the achievement distribution — low performers fall further behind under early tracking, while there is no offsetting gain for top performers.
5. **Policy implication:** the results indicate no equity–efficiency trade-off in early tracking; early between-school selection worsens equity without raising (and possibly lowering) overall performance — an argument for postponing tracking, directly relevant to systems that select at age 10–11 (e.g. German-style, Hungarian 8- and 6-year gymnasium entry).
