## Key findings (paraphrased)
1. In Hungary's free-school-choice system, selection starts before first grade: elementary schools use "school-readiness" and aptitude screening at enrolment, and kindergartens pre-sort children, so the ostensibly neutral choice process is in practice a mutual selection between schools and families.
2. Middle-class parents possess the information, networks and cultural capital to navigate school choice (special classes, tagozat, catchment-area avoidance), while low-status and Roma families largely end up in the local, residual school — choice therefore functions as a privilege-conserving mechanism.
3. Schools compete for "problem-free," high-status pupils and use ability grouping and specialized classes as tools of covert selection; pedagogical labels (readiness, ability, aptitude) legitimate social sorting.
4. The interplay of parental strategies and school selection produces strong between-school and within-school segregation already at the start of primary school, which later tracking (6/8-year gimnázium) reinforces rather than creates from scratch.
5. Local education governance (municipal maintainers) tolerated or actively supported these selective practices, so segregation is the joint product of family strategies, school interests and local policy — echoing the quantitative findings of Kertesi–Kézdi (2013) stored alongside this pointer.
